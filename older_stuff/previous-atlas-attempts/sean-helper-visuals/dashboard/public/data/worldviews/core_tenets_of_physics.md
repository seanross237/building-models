# Core Tenets of Physics

## Classical Mechanics & General Principles

### 1. Conservation of Energy
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

### 2. Conservation of Momentum
The total momentum of an isolated system is constant. In every interaction, momentum is transferred but never lost.

### 3. Conservation of Angular Momentum
Rotational momentum is conserved in the absence of external torque. This governs everything from spinning electrons to orbiting galaxies.

### 4. Principle of Least Action
Physical systems evolve along paths that extremize the action integral. Arguably the deepest organizing principle in all of physics — classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, general relativity, and the Standard Model can all be derived from an action principle.

### 5. Noether's Theorem
Every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conserved quantity:
- Time translation symmetry → conservation of energy
- Spatial translation symmetry → conservation of momentum
- Rotational symmetry → conservation of angular momentum

This theorem reveals that conservation laws are not independent facts but consequences of deeper symmetries.

## Relativity

### 6. Constancy of the Speed of Light
The speed of light (c) is the same in all inertial reference frames. Nothing with mass can reach it. This is the foundation of special relativity and restructures our understanding of space and time.

### 7. Equivalence Principle
Gravitational mass equals inertial mass. A gravitational field is locally indistinguishable from acceleration. This insight led Einstein from special to general relativity — gravity is not a force but the curvature of spacetime.

### 8. Lorentz Invariance
The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames. Physical equations must be covariant under Lorentz transformations, which mix space and time coordinates.

### 9. Mass-Energy Equivalence
E = mc². Mass and energy are interconvertible. A small amount of mass corresponds to an enormous amount of energy, as demonstrated by nuclear reactions and particle-antiparticle annihilation.

### 10. General Covariance
The laws of physics take the same form in all coordinate systems. Spacetime is a dynamic, curved manifold whose geometry is shaped by the distribution of matter and energy (encoded in Einstein's field equations).

## Quantum Mechanics

### 11. Quantization
Energy, angular momentum, and other physical quantities come in discrete packets (quanta) at microscopic scales. This discreteness is fundamental — not a result of measurement limitations.

### 12. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
Certain pairs of physical properties (position/momentum, energy/time) cannot both be known to arbitrary precision simultaneously. This is not a limitation of measurement instruments but a fundamental feature of nature: ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ/2.

### 13. Superposition Principle
A quantum system can exist in a combination of multiple states simultaneously until measured. The state space of quantum mechanics is a Hilbert space, and any linear combination of valid states is itself a valid state.

### 14. The Born Rule
The probability of a measurement outcome is given by |ψ|², where ψ is the wavefunction amplitude. This is the bridge between the deterministic evolution of the wavefunction and the probabilistic outcomes we observe.

### 15. Pauli Exclusion Principle
No two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously. This principle underpins the structure of atoms, the stability of matter, the periodic table, and all of chemistry.

### 16. Wave-Particle Duality
Quantum entities exhibit both wave-like and particle-like behavior depending on how they are observed. Electrons diffract like waves; light arrives in discrete packets like particles. Neither classical picture alone is complete.

## Thermodynamics & Statistical Mechanics

### 17. Second Law of Thermodynamics
The entropy of an isolated system never decreases. Systems evolve toward states of greater disorder. This defines the arrow of time — the one law of physics that distinguishes past from future.

### 18. Third Law of Thermodynamics
Absolute zero cannot be reached in a finite number of steps. As temperature approaches zero, the entropy of a system approaches a minimum value.

## Gauge Theory & Symmetry

### 19. Gauge Invariance
The fundamental forces of nature arise from local symmetry requirements. The Standard Model of particle physics is built on the gauge symmetry group U(1) × SU(2) × SU(3), which gives rise to electromagnetism, the weak force, and the strong force respectively.

### 20. CPT Symmetry
The combined operation of charge conjugation (C), parity inversion (P), and time reversal (T) is an exact symmetry of all known physical laws. Any violation of CPT would require a fundamental revision of quantum field theory.

## Notes on Unification

- **Tenets 6-10 (Relativity) and 11-16 (Quantum Mechanics) are the two pillars we need to reconcile.** They work independently but break down at their intersection — the Planck scale, black hole singularities, and the initial conditions of the big bang.
- **Tenet 4 (Least Action) and Tenet 5 (Noether's Theorem)** sit above specific theories and may be the right level of abstraction for finding unification.
- **Tenet 17 (Entropy)** keeps surfacing in quantum gravity research — black hole entropy, the holographic principle, entropic gravity — and may serve as a key bridge between the two pillars.
