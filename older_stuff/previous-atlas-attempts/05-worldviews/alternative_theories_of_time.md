# Eight Alternative Theories of What Time Could Be

---

## Idea 1: Time as Cohomological Obstruction

Time is not a dimension but a measure of the failure of local physical descriptions to extend globally. In the language of sheaf theory, physical states form a presheaf over the causal structure of events. When this presheaf fails to be a sheaf — when local consistent descriptions cannot be glued into a single global description — the obstruction class living in the first cohomology group of the presheaf *is* time. The "flow" we experience is the successive failure to reconcile incompatible local patches of quantum information.

This reframes the problem of time in canonical quantum gravity: the Wheeler-DeWitt equation says the total Hamiltonian constraint annihilates the state (H|ψ⟩=0, implying no global time), but cohomological time says that is correct — there is no global time. Time is precisely the *reason* there is no global description. The passage of a Planck moment corresponds to the minimal nontrivial cocycle in the Cech cohomology of the causal presheaf. General relativity's smooth spacetime emerges in the limit where these obstructions become dense and approximately continuous, much as a manifold emerges from a sufficiently fine nerve of an open cover. This framework naturally accommodates contextuality in quantum mechanics, since contextuality is itself a cohomological phenomenon (as shown by Abramsky and Brandenburger). Time and quantum contextuality would share a common origin.

**Testable consequence:** In tabletop experiments probing the Kochen-Specker contextuality of entangled systems, the degree of contextuality (measured by the cohomological fraction) should correlate with the temporal resolution required to distinguish measurement outcomes. Maximally contextual configurations should exhibit a minimum temporal separation between compatible measurement contexts that scales with the number of observables, providing a laboratory signature linking time to cohomological structure without requiring Planck-scale energies.

## Idea 2: Time as Lossy Compression of Entanglement History

Time is the artifact of the universe performing irreversible data compression on its own entanglement structure. At the fundamental level, there is no time — there is only a growing tensor network of entanglement relations among subsystems. But this network grows in complexity faster than any subsystem can track. Each subsystem is forced to compress its record of entanglement correlations into a reduced, lossy summary.

The "present moment" is the current compressed codeword, and the "past" is the information that was discarded during compression. The thermodynamic arrow of time arises because lossy compression is irreversible: you cannot reconstruct the original entanglement data from the summary. The rate of time's passage for a given subsystem equals the rate at which it must discard entanglement information, which connects directly to the rate of von Neumann entropy production.

This recovers the semiclassical relationship between entropy and time's arrow but grounds it in something more fundamental: information-theoretic compression constraints. In quantum gravity, the holographic principle already tells us that boundary data compresses bulk geometry. This theory says that compression *is* the temporal dimension of the bulk. The Ryu-Takayanagi formula relating boundary entanglement entropy to bulk minimal surface area becomes a statement about how the compressive capacity of boundary degrees of freedom determines the depth of the emergent time direction.

**Testable consequence:** If time is lossy compression, then systems with access to quantum error correction — which resists information loss — should experience a measurable anomaly in their internal clocks. A logical qubit encoded in a quantum error-correcting code should exhibit a slightly slower decoherence-implied "internal time" compared to an unprotected physical qubit in the same thermal environment, because the error correction partially halts the compression process. The discrepancy should scale with the code distance.

## Idea 3: Time as the Non-Invertibility of the Physical Monoidal Category

Time is the categorical expression of the fact that physical processes do not, in general, have inverses. Consider the symmetric monoidal category whose objects are physical systems and whose morphisms are physical processes (channels, interactions). In a timeless universe, every morphism would be an isomorphism — every process would be reversible and there would be no meaningful distinction between "before" and "after." Time exists precisely to the extent that this category fails to be a groupoid.

The temporal ordering of events is the partial order induced by factoring morphisms into irreducible non-invertible components. A Planck time unit corresponds to a single irreducible non-invertible morphism — the minimal physical process that cannot be undone. Unitary quantum mechanics lives in the invertible subcategory (a groupoid), which is why the Schrodinger equation is time-reversible. Measurement, decoherence, and gravitational collapse introduce non-invertible morphisms, which is why they generate experienced time.

This cleanly resolves the measurement problem's relationship to time: measurement *creates* time by injecting non-invertibility. In quantum gravity, the path integral should be reformulated as a sum not over histories but over factorization systems in the process category, weighted by the degree of non-invertibility. The Einstein equations would emerge as a mean-field condition on the density of non-invertible morphisms per unit of emergent spacetime volume.

**Testable consequence:** A quantum system maintained in perfect unitarity (no decoherence, no measurement) should be temporally transparent — it should not contribute to gravitational time dilation. An interferometry experiment comparing the gravitational influence of a macroscopic quantum superposition (maintained coherently) versus the same system after decoherence should show a minute difference in the gravitational redshift experienced by a nearby clock, because the decohered system has more "temporal weight" due to its non-invertible morphisms.

## Idea 4: Time as Computational Irreducibility Depth

Time is the depth of the shortest computation that produces the current state of a system from its boundary conditions. Drawing on Wolfram's computational irreducibility but grounding it in circuit complexity, this theory identifies the passage of time with the growth of quantum circuit complexity of the universe's state. A "moment" is one layer of irreducible computational depth — one step of evolution that cannot be shortcut by any algorithm.

This connects to recent work by Susskind, Brown, and others linking the growth of the Einstein-Rosen bridge interior to the growth of quantum complexity of the dual boundary state. Under this interpretation, the interior volume of a black hole grows because complexity continues to increase long after thermal equilibrium is reached — the black hole interior *is* time continuing to deepen computationally even when entropy has plateaued.

This resolves the apparent contradiction between the second law (entropy increases then saturates) and the subjective sense that time continues indefinitely. Entropy measures coarse-grained information loss; complexity measures fine-grained computational depth. Time tracks the latter, not the former. The heat death of the universe is not the end of time but merely the point where entropy is maximized; complexity, and therefore time, can continue growing exponentially beyond it.

**Testable consequence:** Two quantum systems prepared in the same initial state and evolved to the same final state — but via paths of different computational complexity (one a generic chaotic evolution, the other a fine-tuned shortcut) — should produce different gravitational signatures during the evolution. The system taking the computationally deeper path should couple more strongly to a gravitational field during transit, detectable in principle through precision atom interferometry as a path-dependent gravitational phase shift.

## Idea 5: Time as Thermal Equilibration Between Entangled Subsystems

Time is what a subsystem calls its approach to thermal equilibrium with the rest of the universe, experienced from the inside. This is a radicalization of the Page-Wootters mechanism: the universe as a whole is in a static, timeless state (an eigenstate of the total Hamiltonian), but any subsystem, when it uses another subsystem as a clock, will observe dynamics. The new element is identifying *which* dynamics: the observed evolution is always thermalization.

Every subsystem is perpetually equilibrating with its complement, and the rate of this equilibration is the local rate of time. Near a black hole, the enormous density of states accelerates equilibration — hence gravitational time dilation. In empty flat space, equilibration proceeds at the rate set by the vacuum fluctuation spectrum — hence the baseline tick rate of clocks.

The Big Bang was not the "start" of time but the moment of maximum disequilibrium between subsystems, providing the largest possible gradient for equilibration and hence the fastest subjective time. As the universe thermalizes globally, time slows — not because clocks run slower, but because there is less disequilibrium left to generate the *experience* of time. This naturally produces a cosmological arrow of time without needing a special low-entropy initial condition as a brute postulate; the initial disequilibrium is simply the prior that makes the subsystem decomposition meaningful in the first place.

**Testable consequence:** A subsystem in thermal equilibrium with its environment should exhibit suppressed temporal dynamics — its internal clock should effectively freeze. Deeply thermalized quantum systems (e.g., a spin in perfect thermal equilibrium with a bath) should show anomalously low temporal correlations in weak continuous measurements, falling below the quantum Zeno baseline. The suppression factor should depend on the ratio of subsystem entropy to bath entropy.

## Idea 6: Time as the Euler Characteristic of Causal Sets Under Coarse-Graining

Time is not a coordinate but a renormalization group flow parameter over the discrete causal structure of spacetime. Adopting the causal set hypothesis that spacetime is a locally finite partial order, this theory identifies the flow of time with the change in the Euler characteristic of the causal set's order complex as one coarse-grains.

At the finest (Planckian) resolution, the causal set has some topological invariant. As one integrates out short-scale causal links — the discrete analog of renormalization — the topology changes. Time is the parameter tracking this topological flow. A "tick" of time corresponds to one step of coarse-graining that alters the Euler characteristic by one unit.

The direction of time is the direction in which coarse-graining reduces topological complexity (toward fewer holes, handles, and torsion). This grounds the arrow of time in something topologically robust rather than statistically unlikely. It also provides natural discretization: since the Euler characteristic is an integer, time is fundamentally discrete, though it appears continuous at macroscopic scales because the Euler characteristic changes by enormous amounts per second (~10^43 units). General relativity emerges as the hydrodynamic limit, with the metric encoding the local density of Euler characteristic change per coarse-graining step.

**Testable consequence:** Near a topological defect in spacetime (such as a cosmic string), the granularity of time should differ from topologically trivial regions. Precision pulsar timing arrays comparing signals passing near candidate cosmic string locations versus control paths should reveal anomalous temporal jitter with a specific spectral signature: a flat power spectrum up to a cutoff frequency set by the local Euler density, distinct from gravitational wave signatures.

## Idea 7: Time as the Free Energy of the Observer-Environment Boundary

Time is a thermodynamic potential defined on the boundary between an observer and everything else. Drawing on the free energy principle from theoretical neuroscience (Friston) and the holographic principle from quantum gravity, this theory posits that every physical system with a meaningful "observer" status possesses a Markov blanket — a statistical boundary separating internal states from external environment.

The free energy on this boundary (the variational free energy, measuring discrepancy between internal model and actual environmental statistics) *is* the local time coordinate. A moment of experienced time corresponds to one cycle of free energy minimization: the observer updates its internal model (reducing free energy), but the environment simultaneously shifts (increasing it). The perpetual oscillation of this minimize-and-perturb cycle generates the experiential flow of time.

This is observer-dependent by construction, naturally recovering the relativity of simultaneity without assuming a background spacetime. The connection to gravity comes through the holographic bound: the maximum rate of free energy change on any boundary is set by the area of that boundary in Planck units, precisely mirroring the Bekenstein bound. The Einstein field equations would emerge as the condition that free energy dynamics on all possible Markov blankets are mutually consistent — a self-consistency condition on nested observer boundaries.

**Testable consequence:** Disrupting the Markov blanket of a physical system should measurably alter its temporal behavior. Two identical atomic clocks — one in well-defined thermodynamic isolation (clean Markov blanket), one with deliberately "leaky" boundary (controlled noise injection blurring boundary statistics) — should differ. The leaky-boundary clock should show excess Allan variance beyond what the injected noise accounts for, because the degraded Markov blanket undermines the coherence of the free energy cycle constituting the clock's internal time.

## Idea 8: Time as the Imaginary Part of Complexified Causal Structure

Time is what happens when causality acquires a phase. Consider a causal structure — a partial order on events — and complexify it: allow causal relations to carry a complex-valued weight whose magnitude encodes causal strength and whose phase encodes something new. Purely real causal weights correspond to spatial relationships (where "before/after" is conventional). Purely imaginary causal weights correspond to temporal relationships (where ordering is physical and observer-independent). Mixed complex phases correspond to lightlike or near-lightlike relationships, naturally recovering light cone structure.

This is not arbitrary: the Wick rotation (t → -it) already tells us that time and space are related by a factor of i in the deepest formalism. This theory takes that seriously as ontology rather than mathematical convenience. The metric signature (−,+,+,+) emerges because the imaginary direction squares to −1 while real directions square to +1.

Quantum gravity's difficulties arise because we try to quantize a real geometry when the temporal direction is fundamentally imaginary-valued — we are working on the wrong number field. The path integral, usually plagued by oscillatory convergence issues in Lorentzian signature, becomes naturally well-defined because the imaginary causal weights provide their own convergence factor, eliminating the need for Wick rotation as a separate trick.

**Testable consequence:** If temporal causal relations carry an intrinsic imaginary phase, then interference experiments involving causally superposed orders (the quantum switch) should reveal a phase depending on *temporal* separation but not spatial separation. The prediction: sinusoidal modulation of interference visibility as a function of temporal delay between operations, with a period set by the Planck time scaled by degrees of freedom involved — potentially accessible in near-term quantum switch experiments with tunable delays.
