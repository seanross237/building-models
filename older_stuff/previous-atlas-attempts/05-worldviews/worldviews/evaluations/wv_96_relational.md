# Worldview 96: The Relational Universe — Everything is Observer-Dependent — Evaluation

## Scores
| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Empirical Adequacy | Partial | Relational QM (RQM) reproduces the standard quantum formalism's predictions for all existing experiments — double-slit, Bell violations, EPR, delayed-choice — because it is an interpretation of QM, not a modification of it. The extension to all of physics (relational frequency, relational mass, relational constants) goes beyond Rovelli's original proposal and has not been experimentally tested as a total reinterpretation. The specific novel prediction — violations of Absoluteness of Observed Events (AOE) inequalities in extended Wigner's friend setups — is partially supported by recent photonic experiments (Proietti et al. 2019, Nurgalieva & del Rio 2019) but those experiments require auxiliary locality/freedom-of-choice assumptions and do not cleanly discriminate RQM from other no-collapse interpretations. Pass for the QM sector; partial for the extended-to-all-physics program |
| Internal Consistency | 3/5 | The core axiom — that no property is absolute, only relational — is philosophically coherent within the QM sector but develops two distinct tension points in the full extension. First, the claim that "constants of physics are relational invariants characterizing the interface between subsystems" is in tension with the relational ontology itself: invariants are, by definition, the same for all observers, which is a form of absoluteness — the worldview cannot simultaneously claim constants are relational and that they are invariants without more careful specification. Second, the claim that "time is equilibration between subsystems" imports the Page-Wootters mechanism and thermal time hypothesis, neither of which is derivable from the pure relational axiom without additional structure; grafting them on creates an internal heterogeneity. The cosmological extension (self-relational universe with no external observer) is sketched but not formalized, leaving the consistency of that sector unverified |
| Parsimony | 4/5 | This is the worldview's strongest dimension. RQM does not add entities — it removes the notion of absolute state from the ontology entirely. No hidden variables, no branching worlds, no collapse mechanism, no consciousness field. The ontological economy is genuine: the entire framework follows from one axiom (all properties are relational). The extension to all of physics is philosophically continuous with that axiom rather than a new imposition. The only cost is a somewhat counterintuitive ontology in which there is no "view from nowhere," but that is a conceptual cost, not an entity cost. The one modest addition is the thermal time hypothesis for recovering time from equilibration, which is not strictly derivable from the relational axiom alone |
| Mathematical Precision | 3/5 | Within the QM sector, RQM is well-formalized: Rovelli's 1996 paper and subsequent work by Smerlak, Poulin, and others give a consistent Hilbert-space formulation. The cross-perspective consistency conditions (Smerlak-Rovelli theorem) are proven results. However, the extension to all of physics described in this worldview — relational frequency ratios, relational conservation, relational mass-energy, relational curvature — is presented verbally without equations. "Curvature is relational — it describes how one subsystem's causal structure differs from another's" is a qualitative claim that may or may not reduce to standard GR's coordinate-transformation properties; the correspondence is not demonstrated. The self-relational cosmology is explicitly described as "mathematically underdeveloped." Net assessment: solid formalism in the QM core, verbal extrapolation everywhere else |
| Explanatory Scope | 4/5 | The worldview resolves — or at minimum dissolves — several genuine open problems. The measurement problem is eliminated by definition: there is no collapse because states are never absolute to begin with; each observer sees a definite outcome relative to themselves, and the apparent contradiction between their accounts is expected rather than paradoxical. The Wigner's friend paradox is reconceived as confirmation of the framework rather than an objection. The black hole information paradox gets a natural treatment: "information loss" is relative to the external observer, and the internal observer's account is simply inaccessible — not destroyed. Gauge symmetry is reinterpreted as the formal freedom to describe the same relation in different languages, which is elegant and unifying. The framework does not solve quantum gravity (GR is gestured at but the curvature-is-relational claim needs GR-level formalization), so the largest open problem in fundamental physics remains open |
| Novel Predictions | 3/5 | The key prediction — extended Wigner's friend experiments will violate AOE inequalities — is genuine and timely. Brukner's 2018 theorem establishes that quantum mechanics is incompatible with AOE given standard assumptions, and tabletop photonic experiments are approaching the regime where this is testable. RQM predicts these violations because it explicitly denies AOE as a physical principle. The problem is that many other no-collapse interpretations (many-worlds, QBism) also predict AOE violations; the prediction discriminates against collapse models but not between no-collapse interpretations. A sharper prediction unique to RQM would require specifying what the different observers' accounts will actually contain and whether those can be made to interfere — experiments that are not yet technically feasible. The relational extension to frequency ratios and relational conservation could in principle yield novel predictions at extreme precision measurements, but none are specified |
| Unification | 4/5 | The unification achieved here is conceptual but genuinely broad. A single axiom — all properties are relational — is applied coherently across QM (states, superposition, collapse), thermodynamics (entropy, equilibration), geometry (curvature, causal structure), mechanics (mass, inertia), and information theory (gauge redundancy). This is more than verbal analogy: each application cashes out the relational axiom in the native language of the domain. The reinterpretation of gauge symmetry as relational language freedom connects to real foundational work in gauge theory. The weak point is gravity: a fully relational GR would require deriving the Einstein equations from relational subsystem constraints, which is the program of shape dynamics (Barbour, Gomes) — the worldview does not acknowledge that this is a hard, partially unsolved problem rather than a straightforward extension |
| Compatibility | 3/5 | In the QM sector, RQM is fully compatible with standard QM by construction — same formalism, different interpretation. The compatibility with GR is more complicated. Standard GR has observer-dependent quantities (coordinate components of tensors) and observer-independent quantities (scalars, proper times, invariant intervals). The claim that "curvature is relational" must be reconciled with the fact that the Riemann tensor has observer-independent components and that tidal forces are locally measurable in a way that does not depend on a reference subsystem. Shape dynamics provides a partially relational reformulation of GR but it is not straightforwardly equivalent in all regimes. The thermodynamic compatibility is reasonable: relational entropy (always relative to a coarse-graining) is consistent with standard stat mech. The biggest compatibility risk is with Lorentz invariance: if constants are "relational invariants characterizing the interface between subsystems," the speed of light must still be numerically the same for all inertial observers — the worldview must explain how relational properties can be universal invariants without reintroducing an absolute structure |

## Assessment

Worldview 96 is the most disciplined and intellectually grounded entry in the wild-card set. It is not a free invention — it is the systematic extension of a real, respected program (Rovelli's relational QM) to cover all of physics, pressed to its logical conclusion. That grounding gives it a structural advantage over the more exotic worldviews: the QM core is genuinely formalized, the central axiom has independent philosophical motivation, and the key prediction is empirically live rather than speculative. The overall assessment is a mid-range performer that punches above its weight on parsimony and explanatory scope but is let down by the gap between the formalized QM sector and the verbal extrapolation that covers the rest of physics.

The central axiom — no property is intrinsic, all are relational between subsystems — is stated and applied with unusual consistency. The best worldviews in this set tend to have one big idea and apply it everywhere; this one does exactly that. The relational reinterpretation of gauge symmetry is particularly clean: the argument that gauge redundancy is the freedom to describe the same relation in different coordinate languages has real traction in the foundations-of-physics literature and is not merely a verbal move. Similarly, the relational entropy argument connects directly to the debates between Boltzmann and Gibbs entropy, and to the work of Jaynes and more recently Zurek on einselection — these are not idle analogies.

The measurement problem resolution is the clearest win. By treating superposition as a relational predicate — "system A is in superposition relative to system B that lacks information about it" — the worldview eliminates the measurement problem rather than solving it. There is no collapse to explain because absolute state was never in the ontology. The objection that this merely relocates the problem (now we need to explain why different observers' relative facts cohere into a single intersubjective world) is answered by the Smerlak-Rovelli consistency theorem, which shows that cross-perspective consistency is a theorem of the formalism rather than a further assumption. This is a genuine result, not just a promissory note.

The cosmological gap is the worldview's most serious weakness, and to the author's credit it is flagged honestly. If all properties are relational between subsystems, and the universe is the collection of all subsystems, then what describes the universe as a whole? There is no external observer to anchor a relation. Rovelli's sketch of a self-relational cosmology — the universe described in terms of relations among its own parts — is exactly the right approach, but it faces a fundamental difficulty: the relations among parts of a system are part of that system, which means the description of the relational structure is itself a physical object within the universe requiring description. This is not a vicious regress in principle (the Page-Wootters mechanism provides a concrete example of internal time emerging from relational structure without external reference), but it requires that the full cosmological dynamics be derivable from relational subsystem constraints alone — a program that remains incomplete. The risk flagged in the worldview itself — that a fully relational cosmology makes no predictions at all — is real and not defused.

The internal tension between "relational" and "invariant" applied to the constants of physics is underappreciated in the worldview text. The speed of light, Planck's constant, and the gravitational constant are called "relational invariants — characterizing the interface between subsystems, not properties of the universe in isolation." But invariants are precisely what does not depend on the observer or the subsystem pair. If c is the same for all interface pairs, that sameness is an absolute structural property of the universe, not a relational one. The worldview would need to argue that "same for all interfaces" is itself a relational claim — perhaps that c is an invariant of the full network of relations among subsystems — but this requires a more careful formulation than provided. Without it, the constants of physics look like undischarged absolutes inside a framework claiming to eliminate them.

The novel prediction is real but not yet decisive. Extended Wigner's friend experiments testing AOE inequalities will be important regardless of their outcome: violation confirms that no-collapse interpretations are required; non-violation would rule out standard QM in that regime. RQM predicts violation. But so does many-worlds and QBism. What would discriminate RQM from those competitors is less clear. A prediction specific to the relational structure — perhaps involving the interference between two observers' accounts of the same event, or a situation where the relational consistency conditions produce a quantitatively different probability distribution than many-worlds — would significantly raise the value of this criterion. The current prediction is necessary for RQM but not sufficient to confirm it.

## Key Strengths

- Single axiom applied with unusual consistency across all domains — no domain-specific special pleading
- The QM core is genuinely formalized; Smerlak-Rovelli cross-perspective consistency is a proven theorem, not a verbal claim
- Measurement problem is eliminated rather than patched — one of the cleanest dispositions of the problem in the set
- Relational reinterpretation of gauge symmetry connects to real foundational work and reduces an apparent mystery to a tautology
- Parsimony is genuine — entities are removed from the ontology, not added; no hidden variables, no branches, no collapse mechanism
- Extended Wigner's friend prediction is empirically live and currently being pursued experimentally
- Honest identification of the cosmological gap, with a clear statement of what would need to be true to close it

---

## Critical Weaknesses

- The extension from the formalized QM sector to all of physics (relational frequency, relational mass, relational curvature, relational constants) is entirely verbal — no equations are provided, and correspondence with GR tensors is not demonstrated
- Internal tension between "relational" and "invariant": the constants of physics cannot be simultaneously relational properties and universal invariants without additional argument that is not given
- The self-relational cosmology is mathematically underdeveloped, and the acknowledged risk — that it makes no predictions — is not defused
- The novel prediction (AOE inequality violations) is shared by all no-collapse interpretations and does not discriminate RQM from many-worlds or QBism; a prediction unique to the relational structure is not specified
- The claim that "curvature is relational" must be reconciled with locally measurable tidal forces (Riemann tensor components), which are observer-independent in standard GR; the worldview does not address this
- The thermal-time / equilibration-as-time component is imported from Page-Wootters and the thermal time hypothesis without showing it is derivable from the relational axiom; it is an additional assumption dressed as a consequence
