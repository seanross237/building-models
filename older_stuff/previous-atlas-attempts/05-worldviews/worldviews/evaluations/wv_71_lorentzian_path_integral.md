## Assessment

WV71 is an engineering proposal more than a physical theory: its goal is to make an existing formalism (the Lorentzian gravitational path integral) well-defined by importing techniques from a mathematically adjacent domain (Picard-Lefschetz theory for oscillatory integrals). The central technical idea is sound at the level of finite-dimensional analogues. The outstanding question — essentially the whole problem — is whether it survives the passage to infinite dimensions.

The mathematical core, Picard-Lefschetz thimble decomposition, is genuinely powerful. In finite-dimensional integrals of the form ∫ e^{iS/ħ} dμ, the Picard-Lefschetz method deforms the integration contour from the real domain into the complexified space, flowing along steepest-descent contours (thimbles) attached to critical points (saddles) of S. Along each thimble, Im(S) ≥ 0, so the integrand is exponentially damped rather than purely oscillatory, and the integral converges absolutely. The complex phases encode the original Lorentzian causal structure. This is not new — Witten applied Picard-Lefschetz to the Chern-Simons path integral in 2010, and Feldbrugge, Lehners, Turok, and Halliwell-Hartle-Hertog-Janssen have applied related techniques to the minisuperspace no-boundary path integral since 2017. WV71 is proposing to apply this approach to the full (not just minisuperspace) Lorentzian gravitational path integral with a topology sum.

The unresolved problem is severe: the space of Lorentzian metrics is infinite-dimensional, and there is no well-defined measure on it. In finite dimensions, the Picard-Lefschetz theorem requires the integral to converge absolutely on the complexified domain, and the topology of the integration contour is well-defined. In infinite dimensions, the "space of complexified metrics" is not a well-studied object, there is no rigorous notion of holomorphic Morse theory in this setting, and the steepest-descent flow (which defines the thimbles) may not converge or may have pathological fixed points. The worldview does not address this gap. The claim that "the integral over the union of relevant thimbles is absolutely convergent" is a claim about the output of a procedure that has not been defined rigorously in this setting. This is not a mere technical detail — it is the entire substance of the problem.

The five interpretive layers create a fragility problem. The worldview requires: (1) complexified metrics and Picard-Lefschetz contour deformation, (2) a computational complexity cutoff on contributing saddles, (3) information-theoretic justification for that cutoff, (4) topological grading with exponential suppression at each level, and (5) retrocausal fixed future boundary conditions. Each layer imports its own assumptions, and the layers are not shown to be mutually consistent or mutually necessary. The complexity cutoff is the most problematic: it asserts that only saddle-point geometries reachable by bounded-complexity computation from the boundary data contribute to the path integral. This would eliminate exotic saddles (Bolts, topology changes, Giddings-Strominger wormholes). But the criterion is not derived from the Picard-Lefschetz structure itself — it is an additional postulate, and it conflicts with the spirit of the path integral, which sums over all histories. A selection rule that excludes saddle points based on computational complexity is either a new physical law requiring justification, or it is the observation that those saddles have exponentially suppressed thimble weight — in which case the complexity language is redundant and misleading. The worldview does not clearly decide which it is.

Retrocausal boundary conditions are the most ontologically loaded addition. The worldview requires specifying both initial and final quantum states to "anchor the oscillatory integral" and uniquely determine the contributing saddles. This is technically a two-state-vector formalism or a post-selection condition on the universe. In cosmology, fixing a future boundary condition requires either accepting a fundamentally non-unitary evolution (the future boundary is not dynamically evolved, it is imposed) or accepting a block-universe interpretation where both boundaries exist timelessly. Neither is worked out. The interaction of retrocausal boundary conditions with the arrow of time, the second law of thermodynamics, and quantum state preparation is nontrivial and unaddressed.

The key prediction — a calculable correction to the tensor-to-scalar ratio r from subdominant Lorentzian saddles — is in principle the right kind of prediction: it connects to the CMB, which is measurable. The current observational constraint is r < 0.036 (Planck + BICEP/Keck, 95% CL). Future experiments (LiteBIRD, CMB-S4) will reach σ(r) ~ 0.001. Whether the Lorentzian correction is of this magnitude depends entirely on the calculation, which is not done in the worldview description. If the correction is O(e^{-S_saddle}) ~ O(e^{-1/G_N}) — as one would expect from a subdominant saddle — it is negligibly small compared to any foreseeable measurement precision and the prediction is practically void. If the correction is O(G_N) or larger, it might be testable, but this requires the calculation to be done in full minisuperspace or beyond.

The information-theoretic regularization layer — the claim that configurations exceeding the boundary data's information content are "physically meaningless" — is conceptually interesting but not formalized. It echoes the holographic bound (Bekenstein bound) and the idea that the number of independent degrees of freedom in a region is bounded by its boundary area. If this is true, it does provide a natural UV cutoff for the topology sum. But the argument is presented as an interpretation rather than a derivation: why should the path integral exclude histories just because they are too complex to be specified by the boundary data? In a standard path integral, the action itself determines which paths contribute — paths with large action are suppressed by e^{iS/ħ}. The claim that information-theoretically overcomplicated paths are excluded by a separate, non-action-derived principle needs to be grounded in the Picard-Lefschetz structure (perhaps: those saddles lie on thimbles not connected to the physical integration cycle) or it is an independent postulate.

## Key Strengths

- **The Picard-Lefschetz core is mathematically rigorous in the relevant finite-dimensional analogues.** Witten's application to Chern-Simons and the minisuperspace applications to the no-boundary proposal show that this technique works when the integration space is finite-dimensional. The worldview is not proposing an unproven mathematical method — it is proposing to extend a proven method to a harder domain. This is a legitimate and well-motivated research direction.
- **The framework directly addresses the defining technical problem.** The Lorentzian path integral's oscillatory divergence and the topology sum's Hagedorn-like divergence are real obstructions to computing anything in quantum gravity. A worldview that claims to resolve both simultaneously via a unified technique is targeting the right problems.
- **The CMB prediction is a genuine stake in the ground.** Unlike many quantum gravity proposals that predict only Planck-scale effects, the claimed tensor-to-scalar ratio correction connects to observable cosmology. The prediction is incomplete (magnitude unknown) but it at least specifies what to compute and where to look.
- **The topological complexity grading is well-defined for compact 3-manifolds.** Matveev complexity and Betti numbers are rigorous invariants. If exponential suppression by topological complexity can be derived from the thimble structure, this would give a controlled topology sum for the first time.

---

## Critical Weaknesses

- **The infinite-dimensional extension of Picard-Lefschetz is unproven and genuinely hard.** The space of Lorentzian metrics does not carry a natural complex structure, the steepest-descent flow may not be well-defined, and there is no theorem guaranteeing absolute convergence of the complexified integral in infinite dimensions. This is not a minor technical gap — it is the core claim of the worldview. Without it, the framework is an analogy, not a solution.
- **The complexity cutoff is an independent, underivable postulate.** The claim that only computationally simple saddles contribute is either redundant (already captured by thimble suppression) or introduces a new physical principle (complexity as a selection rule) that is not derived from the action, the symplectic structure, or any standard ingredient of the theory. If it is a new physical principle, it needs to be stated axiomatically and its consequences explored systematically.
- **Retrocausal boundary conditions introduce a fundamental tension with unitarity.** Fixing a future boundary state in cosmology overdetermines the system and is incompatible with standard unitary quantum evolution unless the theory is interpreted as a post-selected ensemble or a block universe. The worldview does not confront this tension. If future boundary conditions are taken seriously as physical, they have dramatic consequences for the arrow of time and statistical mechanics that are not discussed.
- **The key prediction lacks a computed magnitude.** Stating that the tensor-to-scalar ratio "should receive a calculable correction" is not a prediction until the calculation is done. The difference between a correction of size 10^{-60} and 10^{-3} is the difference between an unfalsifiable claim and a near-term falsifiable one. Without the calculation, the prediction is aspirational.
- **The five interpretive layers are not shown to be mutually consistent.** The worldview layers complexified causality, computational cutoffs, information-theoretic bounds, topological grading, and retrocausal boundary conditions without demonstrating that these five systems of ideas are compatible, non-redundant, and jointly sufficient. The resulting proposal is susceptible to the criticism that it achieves convergence by stacking assumptions until the integral is squeezed into a narrow enough set of configurations to converge — which is not the same as showing that the integral is well-defined.
- **The information-theoretic regularization argument is circular.** The claim that configurations of complexity exceeding the boundary data's information content "cannot be resolved and are physically meaningless" presupposes a definition of "the boundary data" and an associated information measure. In Lorentzian quantum gravity without a fixed spatial boundary (unlike AdS/CFT), neither is well-defined. The argument bootstraps from a result that depends on the very structure it is supposed to justify.
