## Assessment

Worldview 39 is genuinely interesting as a reframing of the problem of time, and the core intuition — that temporal evolution might be the *measure of failure* of local descriptions to extend globally — is philosophically sharp and connects real mathematics (sheaf cohomology) to real physics (the Wheeler-DeWitt problem). This is not a random metaphor: sheaf theory is precisely the language for asking when locally consistent descriptions fail to globalize, and the observation that H|ψ⟩=0 might correspond to the zero-obstruction case is the kind of structural alignment that occasionally signals a real insight.

The most compelling feature is explanatory scope. Rather than treating the frozen-time problem as a defect to be patched, this worldview recasts it as a theorem: if the global description exists (if the quantum state has no obstruction to globalization over configuration space), then there is no time, by definition. Time is not absent from quantum gravity despite the math — it is absent because the math is working correctly. This reframing is worth taking seriously. It connects to ideas in topological quantum field theory (where time is indeed associated with nontrivial cobordisms rather than background structure) and resonates with the Hartle-Hawking no-boundary proposal, where the distinction between Euclidean and Lorentzian signature plays a roughly analogous structural role.

The cohomological ER=EPR proposal — that entangled systems have correlated obstruction classes, generating temporal nonlocality — is the worldview's most speculative but also most distinctive prediction. Standard ER=EPR ties the geometry of entanglement to Rindler horizons and wormholes; the cohomological version would instead tie it to the gluing failure of local charts, which is a different and arguably more fundamental claim. Whether it is compatible with standard ER=EPR or supersedes it is not addressed.

The fatal weakness is the circularity the worldview itself acknowledges: to define the presheaf whose H¹ represents time, you must specify sections over open sets of configuration space, which means you must know what configuration space is, which is the full quantum-gravity problem. This is not merely a gap to be filled in later — it is the load-bearing step. Without a constructive definition of the presheaf, the claim that time equals H¹ of that presheaf cannot be formalized, tested for consistency, or used to derive anything. The mathematical machinery invoked (sheaves, cohomology, restriction maps) is fully rigorous, but applying it requires specifying inputs that are currently unavailable. In this respect, the worldview is a precise description of where a theory would live rather than a theory.

A second structural problem concerns directionality. Sheaf cohomology classes are algebraic objects — they do not have an intrinsic direction. A nontrivial class in H¹ marks a *failure* of globalization, but it does not distinguish the past from the future, nor does it order events. The worldview claims that the magnitude of the obstruction class gives the local flow of time, but flow implies direction. Recovering the arrow of time — and indeed the ordered structure of a 1-dimensional temporal axis — from an unordered cohomological obstruction would require additional input (possibly a filtration, an ordering on the set of patches, or a connection to entropy), none of which is supplied. Without this, the worldview explains why time might exist somewhere but not why it flows in one direction or why it is one-dimensional.

The discrete proper-time jump prediction near topology-changing regions is the clearest empirical hook, but it is unlikely to be testable in the near term — topology change in spacetime is a regime accessible only at or beyond the Planck scale. Entanglement-based temporal nonlocality is closer to testable in principle, but the prediction is currently too underdetermined to constrain experimental design.

## Key Strengths

- The identification of H|ψ⟩=0 as the zero-obstruction condition gives a structural (not merely notational) account of why quantum gravity is timeless — this is a genuine conceptual advance over treating the Wheeler-DeWitt result as an embarrassment
- Sheaf cohomology is the mathematically correct language for the question being asked (when do local descriptions fail to globalize?), giving the worldview a natural formal home that is not invented for the purpose
- The semiclassical emergence of time is addressed mechanically: time appears at the interface of incompatible local descriptions, which is precisely the regime where semiclassical approximations live
- The cohomological ER=EPR proposal, while speculative, is structurally distinct from existing proposals and points at a concrete research question
- The worldview reduces several independent puzzles (problem of time, frozen-time problem, temporal nonlocality in entanglement) to a single structural claim, which is the right shape for a unifying framework

---

## Critical Weaknesses

- The presheaf is never defined: without specifying sections, restriction maps, and the site topology, H¹ is not computed over anything, and all subsequent claims are hypothetical; this is not a technical detail — it is the entire formal content
- The circularity is not recoverable within the framework: the presheaf requires knowing configuration space, but configuration space is the output of a complete quantum gravity theory, not available as input; this makes the framework definitionally dependent on solving the problem it is meant to address
- Sheaf cohomology classes are unordered algebraic objects and cannot by themselves generate a directed, one-dimensional time flow; recovering the arrow of time and the 1D structure of temporal evolution requires additional structure not supplied
- The claim that the obstruction class "diverges" near singularities is not derived and is likely ill-posed: H¹ of a sheaf is a group, not a number with a norm, and a norm on H¹ over a changing configuration space would require specifying how the presheaf changes as geometry becomes singular — a hard open problem
- The discrete jump prediction at topology change is empirically inaccessible with any foreseeable technology, limiting the worldview's testability to the entanglement sector, where predictions remain too qualitative to constrain experiments
- Lorentz invariance and diffeomorphism invariance are not addressed; it is not obvious that a presheaf construction on configuration space respects these symmetries, and violating them would immediately conflict with established physics
