## Assessment

The Pure Computational worldview is a philosophically coherent and conceptually provocative framework in the tradition of Zuse, Fredkin, Wolfram, and (more rigorously) Tegmark's Mathematical Universe Hypothesis. Its central thesis — that physics is the execution trace of a single bijective update rule on a finite state — is genuinely parsimonious at the level of ontology. It replaces the zoo of fields, particles, and forces with one object: the rule. The subsequent identifications (speed of light as bandwidth ceiling, quantization as compressibility, superposition as lazy evaluation) are inventive and some track real structural features of quantum mechanics.

The critical failure is the absence of any mathematical formalization. Every claim in the worldview is a verbal analogy. "Kolmogorov complexity weighting" for the Born rule is not a derivation — it is a metaphor. The actual Born rule is a specific quadratic functional over complex amplitudes, and no account is given of why a complexity-based measure produces exactly |ψ|² rather than some other function. Similarly, gauge invariance as a "survival criterion for self-compiling rule-sets" is evocative but not a proof of U(1) × SU(2) × SU(3) structure. Until these can be expressed as equations and used to compute something, the framework is a map drawn in words without coordinates.

The compatibility score is particularly damaging. Lorentz invariance from a discrete substrate is one of the hardest open problems in quantum gravity — the theory explicitly concedes it is unresolved. This is not a minor gap: relativistic invariance is confirmed to extraordinary precision and any discrete theory must recover it. The cosmological constant "explanation" (residual computational overhead) is the weakest point — it amounts to giving a name to the problem rather than solving it. The self-reference objection (what executes the computation?) is philosophically serious and echoes Penrose's criticism of purely algorithmic descriptions of physics; the worldview acknowledges it without resolution.

---

## Key Strengths

- Ontological parsimony: one primitive (the update rule) replaces fields, spacetime, and particles
- Bijectivity as a mechanism for information conservation is a clean structural move
- Measurement problem dissolved without collapse or many-worlds — evaluation rather than ontological branching is a genuinely different approach
- Unification claim (forces as scheduling protocols) is among the most ambitious and least ad hoc unification theses in the worldview set
- The novel prediction, while underspecified, points at a real empirical gap: anomalous order in classically chaotic prepared states is in principle testable

---

## Critical Weaknesses

- No mathematical formalism whatsoever — cannot compute a single number from this framework as stated
- Born rule derivation via Kolmogorov complexity is a verbal analogy, not a theorem; Kolmogorov complexity is itself uncomputable in the general case, which is unacknowledged as a problem for probability assignment
- Lorentz invariance recovery from discrete computation is explicitly unsolved and is a known hard problem with no obvious path forward
- Self-reference problem (who or what runs the computation) is a genuine regress and the worldview has no answer
- "Lazy evaluation" as superposition smuggles in observer-dependence, which the worldview aims to eliminate
- Cosmological constant "explanation" is a relabeling, not a derivation of the observed value ~10⁻¹²² in Planck units
- No initial state or rule is specified — the framework is a schema for a theory, not a theory
- Novel prediction lacks quantitative threshold, making it unfalsifiable in practice
