# Hypothesis Assessment: Gravity Is Information Pressure

**Date:** 2026-03-26
**Hypothesis:** "Gravity is what information does when it runs out of room."

**Core claim:** Gravity is not a fundamental force but an information-theoretic pressure. Mass curves spacetime because concentrated information deforms the substrate it is encoded on. The Bekenstein bound sets the ceiling; black holes are the saturation point. As a corollary, consciousness — being informationally dense — would carry a (tiny) gravitational signature.

---

## 1. Plausibility: 6/10

This is more defensible than it first appears. The hypothesis is essentially a poetic restatement of a research program that multiple serious physicists have pursued. Here is the landscape of support:

**Strong support from existing physics:**

- **Bekenstein bound (1981).** The maximum entropy (and therefore information) that can be contained in a region of space is proportional to the surface area of the region, not its volume. This is exactly the kind of constraint the hypothesis invokes — information has a maximum density, and exceeding it produces a black hole. The hypothesis correctly identifies this as the critical boundary condition.

- **Holographic principle (t'Hooft 1993, Susskind 1995).** The degrees of freedom of a volume of space are encoded on its boundary. This strongly implies that spacetime geometry and information content are not independent — geometry *is* a way of talking about information structure. The hypothesis is consonant with this.

- **Jacobson's derivation (1995).** Ted Jacobson derived the Einstein field equations from the Clausius relation (dQ = TdS) applied to local Rindler horizons. This is arguably the single strongest piece of evidence for the hypothesis. Jacobson showed that if you assume (a) the entropy-area relationship holds for all causal horizons and (b) the Clausius relation holds for heat flow across those horizons, then Einstein's equations follow as an equation of state. Gravity literally emerges as a thermodynamic/information-theoretic consequence. This is not fringe — it is published in Physical Review Letters and widely cited.

- **Verlinde's entropic gravity (2011).** Erik Verlinde proposed that gravity is an entropic force arising from changes in the information associated with the positions of material bodies. He derived Newton's law of gravitation and (in a later paper) aspects of MOND-like behavior from information-theoretic considerations. The hypothesis under review is essentially the poetic version of Verlinde's program. Verlinde's work is controversial but not dismissed; it has generated substantial follow-up research and some observational tests.

- **Ryu-Takayanagi formula (2006).** In the AdS/CFT context, the entanglement entropy of a boundary region equals the area of the minimal surface in the bulk divided by 4G. This directly ties geometric quantities (areas, which determine gravitational physics) to information-theoretic quantities (entanglement entropy). The "ER = EPR" conjecture (Maldacena and Susskind, 2013) pushes this further: spacetime connectivity itself may be built from quantum entanglement.

- **"It from bit" (Wheeler, 1990).** John Archibald Wheeler proposed that every physical quantity derives its meaning from information. The hypothesis is a direct descendant of this program.

**Where the support weakens:**

- The phrase "information literally deforms the substrate it's written on" is metaphorical. In Jacobson's and Verlinde's frameworks, the relationship is more subtle: geometry and information are dual descriptions, not a causal chain where information pushes on spacetime. The hypothesis implies a causal direction (information -> geometry) that the actual physics treats more carefully as an equivalence or duality.

- The established results work at the level of thermodynamic/statistical limits. None of them require or imply that the *content* of the information matters — only the *amount*. A hard drive storing Shakespeare and one storing random noise of the same entropy have identical gravitational effects. This matters for the consciousness claim (see below).

---

## 2. Internal Coherence: Mostly Coherent, One Tension

The core structure is internally consistent: information has a maximum density -> exceeding it creates singularities -> therefore information and geometry are coupled -> therefore gravity is information-theoretic. The logical chain holds.

**The tension:** The consciousness corollary strains the framework. The hypothesis says consciousness is "informationally dense" and would therefore have a gravitational signature. But *everything* with mass already has a gravitational signature. The interesting claim would be that consciousness contributes *additional* gravity beyond what the mass of the brain accounts for — that the informational processing itself gravitates independently of the mass-energy doing the processing. This would be extraordinary and is not supported by the frameworks cited. In Jacobson's and Verlinde's pictures, the entropy that generates gravity is the thermodynamic/Bekenstein entropy associated with mass-energy configurations, not some additional "information of consciousness" layered on top. A brain and a rock of equal mass have equal gravitational fields, regardless of whether the brain is thinking.

If the hypothesis means only that the brain's gravity *is* its information-theoretic pressure (because all gravity is), then the claim is trivially true within the framework but says nothing special about consciousness.

---

## 3. Relation to Known Physics

**Compatible:**
- General relativity: The hypothesis does not contradict GR. It reinterprets GR as emergent rather than fundamental, which is a live research direction.
- Quantum mechanics: No direct conflict. The information-theoretic perspective is native to quantum theory.
- Black hole thermodynamics: Fully consistent. The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy formula is a cornerstone of the hypothesis.
- Holography / AdS-CFT: The hypothesis is essentially a verbal summary of insights from this program.

**In tension with:**
- Standard model: Gravity in the standard framework is a gauge interaction (spin-2 graviton). If gravity is emergent from information, the graviton would be a collective excitation rather than a fundamental particle. This is speculative but not ruled out.
- Verlinde's program has difficulty reproducing the full content of GR in all regimes, particularly gravitational waves. LIGO's detection of gravitational waves is consistent with GR's predictions to high precision. Any emergent gravity theory must reproduce this, and it is not yet clear that Verlinde's framework does in full generality.
- The entropy used in these derivations (Bekenstein entropy, entanglement entropy) is not the same as Shannon information or computational information. Conflating them — which the hypothesis does implicitly — is a category error that the serious versions of this program are careful to avoid.

---

## 4. Hypothetical Testability

**The core claim (gravity is entropic/information-theoretic):**

- **Verlinde's predictions for galaxy rotation curves.** Verlinde's 2016 paper predicted galaxy rotation behavior without dark matter, using an emergent gravity framework. This has been tested against observational data with mixed results — some galaxy samples fit well, others do not. Continued astronomical surveys (Euclid, Rubin Observatory) will provide more stringent tests.
- **Gravitational wave signatures.** If gravity is emergent, there may be deviations from GR predictions at extreme scales or energies. Current LIGO/VIRGO/KAGRA observations are consistent with GR, but next-generation detectors could probe subtler deviations.
- **Tabletop experiments.** There have been proposals (e.g., Bouwmeester group) to test whether gravity can entangle quantum systems. If gravity mediates entanglement, it has quantum character; if it does not, it may be classical/emergent. These experiments are at the edge of current technology.

**The consciousness-gravity claim:**

This is effectively untestable with current or foreseeable technology. The claim would require measuring a gravitational contribution from informational processing that is distinct from the gravitational contribution of the mass-energy performing the processing. The predicted signal would be absurdly small — many orders of magnitude below any conceivable detector sensitivity. Furthermore, the claim is underspecified: it does not define what "informationally dense" means for consciousness in a way that connects to Bekenstein entropy, which is the actual quantity that appears in the gravitational equations. A working brain is nowhere near the Bekenstein bound for its volume. A region of space the size of a human brain could contain roughly 10^42 bits before hitting the Bekenstein limit; the brain's actual information content (by any reasonable measure) is perhaps 10^15 to 10^16 bits. The brain is informationally sparse by the standard the hypothesis invokes.

## 5. Steelmanned Version

The strongest version drops the consciousness claim and states:

> Spacetime geometry is not fundamental. It is the thermodynamic/information-theoretic description of a deeper, pre-geometric microstructure. What we call gravity — the curvature of spacetime — is the macroscopic equation of state of this microstructure, analogous to how pressure and temperature are macroscopic descriptions of molecular motion. The Bekenstein bound is the equation of state's critical point: the configuration where the information content of a region saturates the geometric capacity to describe it, producing a horizon. General relativity is the hydrodynamics of information.

This version is genuinely defensible. It is essentially Jacobson's 1995 result, restated. It is compatible with the holographic principle, black hole thermodynamics, and AdS/CFT. It makes no claim about consciousness. It is the research program that Jacobson, Verlinde, Padmanabhan, Van Raamsdonk, and others are actively pursuing. It is speculative in the sense that no complete UV-complete theory of emergent gravity exists, but it is not crackpot — it is a serious and partially successful research direction.

---

## 6. Fatal Flaw

**The conflation of different notions of "information."**

The hypothesis slides between three distinct concepts:

1. **Bekenstein/thermodynamic entropy** — the quantity that actually appears in the gravitational equations. It counts microstates of a system. It is observer-independent and related to area/energy.
2. **Shannon information** — the quantity used in information theory and computer science. It measures surprise/uncertainty in a message. It is observer- and encoding-dependent.
3. **Conscious information** — whatever it is that consciousness processes. This is not even well-defined in physics.

The gravitational physics works with (1). The word "information" in the hypothesis invokes (2) and (3). These are not the same thing. A black hole has maximum Bekenstein entropy for its area, but it is not "storing" information in the Shannon sense — it is in a maximum-entropy state, which in Shannon terms means it contains *no* useful information (maximum entropy = maximum uncertainty = minimum knowledge).

The hypothesis's rhetorical power comes from using "information" as if it means the same thing in all three contexts. It does not. The serious physics (Jacobson, Verlinde, holography) is careful about this distinction. The hypothesis, by blurring it, makes claims (especially about consciousness) that do not follow from the physics it cites.

---

## Summary Verdict

The core intuition — that gravity and information/entropy are deeply related — is correct and supported by serious theoretical physics. The hypothesis is a poetic and somewhat imprecise restatement of a real research program. It earns its 6/10 on the strength of Jacobson, Verlinde, and holography. It loses the remaining points because (a) it implies a causal direction that the physics does not establish, (b) it conflates distinct notions of information, and (c) the consciousness corollary does not follow from the premises and is untestable. Strip the consciousness claim and tighten the language, and you have a legitimate (if incomplete) picture of where theoretical physics is actually headed.
