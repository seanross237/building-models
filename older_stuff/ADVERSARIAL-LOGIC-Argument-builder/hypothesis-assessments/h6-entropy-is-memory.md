# Hypothesis Assessment: Entropy Is the Universe Forming Memories

**Date:** 2026-03-26
**Hypothesis:** "Entropy isn't disorder -- it's the universe forming memories. Every irreversible process is the cosmos writing something it can't unwrite."

**Core claim:** The second law of thermodynamics is not a description of the universe falling apart. It is a description of the universe accumulating records. Entropy increase is identical to the creation of irreversible records. Because memory requires irreversibility (you cannot remember an event that left no trace), the arrow of time points in the direction of memory formation. The universe is not running down -- it is accumulating experience.

---

## 1. Plausibility: 7/10

This is surprisingly defensible. Unlike many poetic reframings of physics, this one tracks a genuine and well-established connection in the foundations of thermodynamics and information theory. The plausibility is high because the core identification -- entropy increase as record creation -- is not a metaphor. It is a result.

### What supports it

**Landauer's principle (1961).** Rolf Landauer proved that erasing one bit of information dissipates at least kT ln 2 of energy as heat, increasing entropy. The converse is equally important: recording information -- writing a bit into a stable physical medium -- requires an irreversible process that increases entropy elsewhere. Every record is thermodynamically costly. Every act of memory formation is an entropy-increasing event. This is not a metaphor for the hypothesis; it is the hypothesis stated in the language of physics.

**The physics of records (Bennett, Zurek, Wolpert).** Charles Bennett's work on the thermodynamics of computation showed that computation itself can be thermodynamically reversible -- *except for the act of recording results*. It is the creation of a record, the writing-down of an outcome, that mandates entropy increase. Wojciech Zurek extended this insight to quantum mechanics: decoherence produces stable classical records (pointer states) from quantum superpositions, and this process is irreversible and entropy-increasing. The hypothesis correctly identifies the deep connection: what we call "entropy increase" and what we call "record creation" are the same physical process seen from two descriptive angles.

**The thermodynamic arrow and the record arrow coincide.** This is a well-known result in the foundations of physics. The direction in which entropy increases is the same direction in which records accumulate, which is the same direction in which we remember. This is not a coincidence -- it is a consequence of the statistical mechanics of records. A record is a subsystem whose state is correlated with a past event. Creating such a correlation requires entropy increase (Landauer). Therefore, records point in the direction of entropy increase. Therefore, memory points in the direction of entropy increase. The hypothesis simply takes this established physics and foregrounds it as the *primary* description rather than a secondary consequence.

**Hawking's analysis (1985).** Stephen Hawking addressed exactly this question: why do we remember the past but not the future? His answer was that psychological time (the direction of memory) is aligned with thermodynamic time (the direction of entropy increase) because the brain is a physical system that forms memories by irreversible processes. A brain in a time-reversed universe would form memories in the time-reversed direction and would experience that direction as "the past." The hypothesis is essentially Hawking's point, promoted from an observation about psychology to a claim about the fundamental character of entropy.

**Zurek's "epiontic" framework.** Zurek argued that quantum Darwinism -- the process by which the environment selects and proliferates certain quantum states (pointer states) into stable, redundant records -- is the physical mechanism by which classical reality emerges. In this framework, the classical world literally *is* the set of records that the environment has written. This is remarkably close to the hypothesis: the universe is not an arena in which records happen to form; the formation of records is what constitutes the universe's classical structure.

### What undermines it

**Entropy increase happens in systems that are not plausibly "remembering" anything.** When a gas expands into a vacuum, entropy increases. But what is being "remembered"? You can construct a formal answer: the final state is a record that the gas was previously confined. But this stretches "memory" to the point where it includes any state that is correlated with a prior state. At that point, the word "memory" is doing no work that "macrostate" was not already doing. The hypothesis risks being a terminological relabeling rather than a substantive insight.

**Heat death undermines the experience framing.** If entropy increase is the universe "accumulating experience," then the heat death of the universe -- maximum entropy, thermal equilibrium -- is the state of maximum experience. But maximum entropy is also the state of maximum homogeneity, minimum structure, and zero free energy. It is hard to see what "experience" means in a featureless thermal bath. The poetic framing ("accumulating experience") breaks down exactly where the physics becomes most extreme.

## 2. Internal Coherence

The argument's logical structure is:

1. Memory requires irreversible record creation.
2. Irreversible record creation requires entropy increase (Landauer).
3. Therefore, entropy increase is a necessary condition for memory.
4. Therefore, entropy increase *is* memory formation.

The move from step 3 to step 4 is the critical step, and it is a logical overreach in its naked form. "A is necessary for B" does not entail "A is B." Oxygen is necessary for fire, but oxygen is not fire. Entropy increase is necessary for record creation, but not all entropy increase constitutes record creation. A star radiating photons into space increases entropy massively, but it is not obvious that this constitutes "memory" in any non-trivial sense.

### Hidden assumptions

**The hypothesis assumes that "record" and "memory" are natural categories at the fundamental level.** In physics, a record is defined operationally: a subsystem whose current state carries information about a past event. This is an observer-relative notion. Whether a given physical configuration counts as a "record" depends on what question you are asking. The hypothesis treats record-formation as an objective, observer-independent feature of entropy increase. This works in some cases (decoherence creates objective records in Zurek's sense) but not in general (most entropy increase creates correlations that no one will ever decode).

**The hypothesis assumes a particular direction of explanation.** Standard physics says: entropy increases, and as a consequence, records accumulate in the forward-time direction. The hypothesis inverts this: records accumulate, and that is what entropy increase *is*. This inversion is not self-contradictory, but it does require justification for why the record-formation description should be treated as more fundamental than the thermodynamic description. The hypothesis asserts this but does not argue for it.

### Does it contradict itself?

Not exactly. But the "accumulating experience" framing sits uncomfortably with the fact that most of the entropy increase in the universe is not experiential in any recognizable sense. If "experience" is a metaphor for "record," the hypothesis is coherent but the word "experience" is misleading. If "experience" is meant literally -- the universe is in some sense having experiences -- then the hypothesis is committed to a form of panpsychism or panexperientialism, which is a significant ontological commitment that is not argued for.

## 3. Relation to Known Physics

### Landauer's principle

The hypothesis is fully compatible with and supported by Landauer's principle. Landauer's principle establishes the physical cost of information processing, particularly erasure. The complementary result -- that recording a bit is an irreversible, entropy-increasing process -- is precisely the foundation the hypothesis builds on. Experimental confirmations of Landauer's principle (Berut et al., 2012, using colloidal particles in optical traps) strengthen the hypothesis's physical grounding.

### The thermodynamic arrow of time

The hypothesis is compatible with the standard account of the thermodynamic arrow. It does not contradict the Past Hypothesis (that the early universe was in a low-entropy state). Instead, it reinterprets what the arrow means: the arrow of time points in the direction of increasing records/memories, which is the direction of increasing entropy. This reinterpretation is not in conflict with the standard account -- it is a re-description of the same facts.

However, the hypothesis inherits a problem. If entropy increase is memory formation, then the low-entropy initial state is a state of *zero* memories -- a blank slate. The arrow of time is then the universe writing on itself from blankness toward maximal inscription. This is an attractive picture, but it raises the same question the Past Hypothesis always raises: why did the universe start blank? The hypothesis does not answer this. It reframes the question (from "why low entropy?" to "why no memories?") but does not dissolve it.

### Quantum decoherence and the emergence of classicality

This is where the hypothesis has its strongest connection to known physics. Zurek's quantum Darwinism and the decoherence program establish that:

1. Quantum systems in isolation are in superposition (no definite records exist).
2. Interaction with an environment causes decoherence: the environment acquires redundant records of the system's state in certain preferred bases (pointer states).
3. This process is irreversible and entropy-increasing.
4. The emergence of a classical, definite, recordable world is *identical* to the process of environmental record-formation.

The hypothesis, applied to decoherence, yields: "the classical world is the set of things the universe has remembered." This is genuinely insightful and not far from what Zurek himself has argued. It is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that the hypothesis captures something real.

### Statistical mechanics

In standard statistical mechanics, entropy measures the number of microstates compatible with a macrostate. High entropy means many compatible microstates, which means less information about which microstate the system is actually in. This creates a tension with the hypothesis: if high entropy means *less* information about the microstate, how can entropy increase be memory *formation*? The answer is subtle. Entropy increase means the *total* entropy (system plus environment) goes up. The *environment* gains information about the system (records are created in the environment), while the system itself becomes less distinguishable. The records are real -- they are in the correlations between the system and its surroundings -- but the system-alone description becomes less informative. The hypothesis is tracking the environmental records, not the system's self-description.

This subtlety is important and often missed. The hypothesis, properly understood, is about the proliferation of records *in the environment*, which is precisely what Zurek's quantum Darwinism describes. It is not about the system's own entropy in isolation.

### What it cannot explain

The hypothesis does not explain *why* entropy increases. It re-describes what entropy increase *is* (record formation). The underlying question -- why does the universe evolve from low entropy to high entropy, from few records to many? -- is still answered by the Past Hypothesis and the statistics of phase space. The hypothesis is a lens, not a mechanism.

## 4. Relation to Known Philosophy

### Whitehead's process philosophy

Alfred North Whitehead proposed that reality consists of "actual occasions of experience" -- momentary events that "prehend" (take in, record) their predecessors and then perish, becoming data for future occasions. The universe, in Whitehead's view, is an accumulation of experience. The hypothesis maps almost perfectly onto Whitehead: each irreversible process is an actual occasion; the accumulated entropy is the accumulated record of past occasions; the arrow of time is the direction of prehension. Whitehead would have recognized this hypothesis immediately.

### Bergson's duration

Henri Bergson argued that time is not a spatial dimension but an accumulation -- that the past is *preserved* in the present, not left behind. The universe carries its history with it. This is strikingly close to the hypothesis: entropy increase means the universe carries an ever-growing record of its past. Bergson would add that this accumulation is qualitatively distinct from spatial extension, which is also consonant with the hypothesis's emphasis on irreversibility (spatial dimensions are traversable in both directions; the temporal record is not).

### Peirce's tychism and evolutionary cosmology

Charles Sanders Peirce proposed that the universe evolves from chaos to habit -- from randomness to regularity. In Peirce's cosmology, the laws of nature themselves are habits the universe has acquired. This rhymes with the hypothesis: the universe accumulates records (habits, regularities, memories) over time. Peirce would frame entropy increase as the universe developing increasingly elaborate habits of correlation.

### Panexperientialism

If the hypothesis means "experience" literally and not metaphorically, it aligns with panexperientialist traditions (Whitehead, Hartshorne, and some readings of Leibniz). This is a defensible but minority position in philosophy of mind. The hypothesis does not require panexperientialism -- you can read "memory" as a purely physical/informational term -- but the "accumulating experience" framing strongly suggests it.

### Pragmatist epistemology

The hypothesis has a pragmatist flavor. It says: the meaningful description of entropy is not the abstract count of microstates but the concrete question of what records exist. This is a shift from a God's-eye view of physics (counting microstates) to an embedded-agent view (what can be read, recalled, known). Pragmatists from James through Rorty would approve of this re-centering.

## 5. Hypothetical Testability

### Can we distinguish this from the standard view?

This is the critical question, and the honest answer is: **probably not, because the hypothesis may be empirically equivalent to the standard view.** It re-describes the same physical facts in different language. If it makes no different predictions, it is not a competing theory -- it is an interpretation.

However, there are places where the choice of description could matter:

**Test 1: Landauer erasure in the quantum regime.** If entropy increase is fundamentally about record creation, then the minimum entropy cost should be associated specifically with the creation of a stable, decodable record -- not with any irreversible process whatsoever. Experiments at the quantum-classical boundary (e.g., partial measurements, weak measurements) could probe whether entropy increase tracks *record stability* specifically or is indifferent to whether a record is produced. If entropy increases even in processes that create no decodable record, the identification of entropy-increase-with-record-creation is not exact.

**Test 2: Loschmidt reversal experiments.** If you could perfectly reverse a macroscopic system (erase all environmental records of a process), thermodynamic entropy would decrease. The hypothesis predicts: record erasure *is* entropy decrease. Spin-echo experiments (Hahn 1950) and their successors do something close to this in NMR systems. The hypothesis predicts that the degree of entropy recovery in a spin-echo experiment should correlate precisely with the degree of record erasure. This is testable in principle, though the predictions may be quantitatively identical to the standard thermodynamic analysis.

**Test 3: The quantum eraser.** Quantum eraser experiments (Scully et al., 1982; Kim et al., 2000) demonstrate that erasing which-path information restores interference patterns. The hypothesis frames this as: when the environment's record is erased, the entropy associated with that record is reversed, and the system returns to a superposition. This is exactly what happens. The hypothesis does not predict anything different from standard quantum mechanics here, but it provides a unifying interpretation: decoherence is record-writing; the quantum eraser is record-erasing; the difference between classical and quantum is the difference between recorded and unrecorded.

### Honest assessment

The hypothesis is likely **empirically equivalent** to the standard view of entropy. It does not predict new phenomena. What it does is organize known phenomena under a different primary concept (records/memory rather than disorder/microstates). This makes it an interpretation rather than a theory. Interpretations are not testable in the standard Popperian sense, but they are evaluable on other grounds: explanatory unification, conceptual clarity, fertility in suggesting new research directions.

Landauer's principle establishes the thermodynamic cost of recording. Zurek's quantum Darwinism establishes that classical reality *is* the set of records proliferated by decoherence. The thermodynamic arrow, the cosmological arrow, and the psychological arrow of time all point in the direction of record accumulation because they are *descriptions of the same process at different scales.*

A completed irreversible process has left a trace that cannot be undone without external work. The universe's entropy is, precisely, the total of all such traces. The past is not gone; it is encoded -- imperfectly, diffusely, but indelibly -- in the current state of the universe.
>
> The low-entropy initial state is a blank page. The second law is the statement that pages get written on. The arrow of time is the direction of writing. This is not poetry -- it is a re-description of the statistical mechanics of records, grounded in Landauer, Bennett, and Zurek.

This version is genuinely strong. It is compatible with all known physics. It unifies several disparate phenomena (decoherence, Landauer erasure, the thermodynamic arrow, the psychological arrow) under a single concept. It makes the same predictions as standard thermodynamics. And it has the virtue of making the connection between thermodynamics and information theory perspicuous rather than mysterious.

The steelman loses the romantic appeal of "the universe accumulating experience," but it gains precision and defensibility. It is, in effect, a pedagogical and conceptual reorientation rather than a new physical theory -- but such reorientations can be genuinely valuable. The shift from "force" to "energy" in 19th-century physics did not change the predictions, but it transformed the field.

## 7. Fatal Flaw

**The hypothesis is not wrong. It is not even really a hypothesis. It is an interpretation -- and it does not clearly distinguish itself from what it claims to replace.**

The single biggest problem is that the hypothesis presents itself as a *revision* of the standard view ("entropy isn't disorder -- it's memory") when it is more accurately a *re-description*. The standard view already contains everything the hypothesis asserts:

- Statistical mechanics already knows that entropy increase creates correlations between systems and their environments.
- The physics of records and memory (Landauer, Bennett, Zurek) already establishes the connection between irreversibility and record creation.
- The alignment of the thermodynamic and psychological arrows of time is already explained by the fact that brains are physical systems that form memories via irreversible processes.

The hypothesis takes these known results and says: "therefore entropy *is* memory." But this "is" is ambiguous. If it means "entropy increase is a necessary condition for memory formation," that is established physics and not a new hypothesis. If it means "every instance of entropy increase constitutes an instance of memory formation," that is a definitional expansion of "memory" that strips the word of its ordinary meaning (a gas expanding into a vacuum is not "remembering" anything in any useful sense of the word). If it means "the best way to understand entropy is through the lens of record-formation," that is a philosophical claim about explanatory priority that cannot be tested.

The hypothesis falls into the gap between "true but already known" and "novel but not testable." It is at its best as a conceptual reorientation -- a way of teaching and thinking about entropy that foregrounds the informational content rather than the energetic content. At its worst, it is a semantic substitution that replaces one word ("disorder") with another ("memory") without changing the underlying physics and then claims to have discovered something.

The genuine kernel of truth -- that entropy, information, and records are deeply and physically linked, that the arrow of time is the arrow of record accumulation, that the classical world emerges through environmental memory -- is real and important. But it is already known. The hypothesis's contribution is rhetorical and pedagogical, not physical.
