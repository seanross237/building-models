# Adversary Attacks — Against Recursive Causal Self-Modeling (RCSM)

**Agent:** Adversary
**Run:** 002-prose
**Date:** 2026-03-25

---

## Target Theory

The Architect proposes that consciousness is identical to "recursive causal self-modeling" — a physical system is conscious when and only when it (a) models its own states and processes, (b) models its own modeling activity (recursion), and (c) this self-model is causally coupled to its first-order processing. The theory claims to explain what consciousness is, why it arises, what determines its presence, and how it addresses the Hard Problem.

Below are my attacks.

---

### Attack 1: The Recursion Criterion Is Fatally Underdefined

**Target:** The definition of "recursive self-modeling" — the claim that the system must "model itself-as-modeling" and represent "the modeling process itself."

**Flaw:** The Architect never specifies what counts as modeling one's own modeling versus merely modeling one's own states. Consider: a rat that monitors its own confidence level (uncertainty monitoring, which the Architect cites as evidence of possible recursive self-modeling in animals) has a state that tracks the reliability of its first-order perceptual states. Is that "modeling its modeling"? Or is it merely a second-order state that tracks a property (confidence) of a first-order state? The Architect provides no principled criterion for distinguishing these. Without one, "recursive self-modeling" is a phrase that sounds precise but does no actual theoretical work — it can be stretched to include anything with metacognition and contracted to exclude anything we intuit is not conscious.

This is not a minor gap. It is the central criterion of the entire theory, and it is undefined at exactly the point where it needs to be most precise.

**Severity: Fatal**

---

### Attack 2: Circularity in the Argument That Self-Modeling Is Necessary

**Target:** The passage arguing that without a self-model, "there is no self to which experience can be attributed, and the notion of phenomenal consciousness loses its anchor."

**Flaw:** This argument smuggles in the conclusion as a premise. It assumes that phenomenal consciousness requires a "perspectival center" or "subject" — and then argues that only self-modeling can provide one. But the claim that consciousness requires a subject is not established; it is precisely one of the things under dispute. Buddhists, Humeans, and certain phenomenologists have argued that consciousness can be "subjectless" — that there is experience without an experiencer. The Architect does not engage this possibility; they simply assert that "something it is like to be X" presupposes an X. But Nagel's phrase is a piece of ordinary language, not a metaphysical proof that consciousness requires a substantial self. The argument is: (1) consciousness requires a self; (2) a self requires self-modeling; (3) therefore consciousness requires self-modeling. Premise (1) is the contested claim, not an established fact.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 3: The Thermostat Objection Is Handled by Intuition, Not Argument

**Target:** The passage dismissing thermostats and GPS units as non-conscious because they lack "recursive" self-modeling.

**Flaw:** The Architect says a thermostat "has a trivial self-model" but "does not model itself-as-modeling." This is asserted but not argued. A thermostat's feedback loop monitors its own output (temperature) and adjusts its own behavior accordingly. One could describe this as the thermostat "modeling its own regulative activity." The Architect's response would be: "but that's not *really* modeling-of-modeling." But what makes it not-really? The Architect is relying on the intuition that thermostats are not conscious and reverse-engineering the criterion to exclude them. This is the exact methodological error the theory was supposed to avoid: using pre-theoretical intuitions about consciousness to calibrate the theory, rather than deriving verdicts from the theory's principles.

If the recursion criterion were genuinely well-defined (see Attack 1), this objection might be answerable. But since it is not, the thermostat dismissal rests on question-begging.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 4: The Identity Claim Is Asserted Without Justification

**Target:** The passage: "the subjective, experiential, phenomenal character of consciousness is identical to the operation of recursive causal self-modeling."

**Flaw:** This is the boldest claim in the entire theory and it receives almost no argument. The Architect simply announces it: "This is not a definition by fiat. It is a claim about identity." But identity claims require justification. Why should recursive causal self-modeling be *identical* to phenomenal consciousness rather than merely *correlated* with it, or a *necessary condition* for it, or a *causal basis* of it? The Architect provides no argument for identity over these weaker relations.

This matters enormously. If RCSM is merely correlated with consciousness, the theory has not explained consciousness at all — it has found a marker. If RCSM is a necessary condition, there may be additional sufficient conditions the theory misses. If RCSM is a causal basis, then there is still an explanatory gap between the basis and the phenomenon it causes. Only identity closes the gap, and the Architect has not earned identity.

The standard way to argue for psychophysical identity is via considerations like theoretical parsimony, causal closure, and the systematic correlation of mental and physical properties. The Architect gestures at some of these but never constructs the actual argument. The identity claim floats unsupported at the center of the theory.

**Severity: Fatal**

---

### Attack 5: The Godel Analogy Is False

**Target:** The passage claiming that the Hard Problem is "analogous to the limits Godel identified in formal systems" — that a system modeling its own modeling will "necessarily find that the model cannot fully capture the modeled process."

**Flaw:** Godel's incompleteness theorems apply to formal axiomatic systems of sufficient expressive power. They do not apply to physical systems, causal models, or self-referential processes in general. The Architect is trading on a superficial resemblance (self-reference! incompleteness!) to borrow the authority of a rigorous mathematical result for a claim that has not been rigorously established. Nothing in the theory demonstrates that a recursive self-model *necessarily* cannot fully capture its own operation. It might be the case that a sufficiently powerful system could construct a self-model that is complete for all practical purposes, or even theoretically complete if the model operates at a different level of description than the process it models. The Architect asserts necessity ("will necessarily find") without proving it.

Moreover, even if there is some structural incompleteness, the Architect has not shown that this incompleteness would manifest as phenomenal consciousness rather than, say, systematic prediction error or computational overhead. The leap from "the model is incomplete" to "the incompleteness is what it is like to be conscious" is entirely hand-waving.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 6: The Evolutionary Argument Confuses Function With Phenomenality

**Target:** The passage arguing that consciousness arises because "natural selection favors systems that model themselves."

**Flaw:** The evolutionary argument explains why recursive self-modeling might evolve. It does not explain why recursive self-modeling should be *conscious*. This is the classic explanatory gap restated. Grant everything the Architect says about the adaptive value of recursive self-modeling: the system can detect errors, inhibit impulses, adapt strategies. All of these are functional benefits. They explain why the *functional capacity* for self-monitoring would evolve. They do not explain why the functional capacity should be accompanied by subjective experience. A "zombie" system — one with all the same recursive self-modeling but no phenomenal consciousness — would reap identical evolutionary benefits. The Architect has not shown that consciousness is the recursive self-modeling rather than something that (contingently, mysteriously) accompanies it.

The Architect claims to avoid epiphenomenalism, but the evolutionary argument actually relies on the functional role of self-modeling, not its phenomenal character. The phenomenality remains unexplained.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 7: The Causal Coupling Criterion Creates a Testability Problem

**Target:** The claim that the recursive self-model must be "causally efficacious" — that removing it would change the system's behavior.

**Flaw:** How would we test this? In a complex biological system like a brain, the "recursive self-model" is not a discrete, removable module. It is distributed across multiple brain regions and interleaved with processes the Architect would classify as "first-order." The Architect cites default mode network disruption and depersonalization as evidence, but these are coarse-grained interventions that disrupt many processes simultaneously. They do not cleanly isolate recursive self-modeling from other forms of self-representation.

More fundamentally, the criterion is operationally empty in its current form. For any system we want to test, we would need to (a) identify the recursive self-model, (b) remove it while leaving everything else intact, and (c) check for behavioral change. Step (a) requires the very criterion that Attack 1 shows is underdefined, and step (b) may be physically impossible in biological systems where the self-model is deeply integrated. The theory is not falsifiable in practice, which severely limits its scientific value.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 8: The Theory Cannot Account for the Unity of Consciousness

**Target:** The theory as a whole — what it fails to address.

**Flaw:** The Architect never explains why consciousness is unified — why your experience at any moment is a single, integrated field rather than a collection of disconnected fragments. If consciousness is recursive self-modeling, and the brain has multiple self-models operating in parallel (body schema, emotional self-model, cognitive self-model, narrative identity), why don't we have multiple consciousnesses? What binds them into one? IIT addresses this with its concept of integrated information and the exclusion postulate. Global Workspace Theory addresses it with the architecture of the global workspace. RCSM addresses it not at all. This is not a minor oversight — the unity of consciousness is one of the most central and puzzling features of the phenomenon the theory is supposed to explain.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 9: Equivocation on "Model"

**Target:** The term "causal model" as used throughout the essay.

**Flaw:** The Architect defines a causal model as "an internal structure within a system whose states systematically covary with features of the modeled domain, and which the system uses to generate predictions, guide behavior, or select among alternatives." This definition is ambiguous between a strong reading (the system explicitly represents the domain and uses the representation in something like inference) and a weak reading (any internal state that correlates with and causally influences behavior counts as a "model"). On the weak reading, virtually everything is a model — a plant's phototropism involves internal states that covary with light direction and guide behavior. On the strong reading, we need a theory of representation that the Architect does not provide.

The theory needs "model" to be strong enough to exclude trivial cases (plants, thermostats) but weak enough to be physically realizable and empirically detectable. The Architect has not shown that there is a principled place to draw this line. The word "model" is doing enormous theoretical work while remaining systematically ambiguous.

**Severity: Major**

---

### Attack 10: The Dismissal of Subjectless Consciousness Is Dogmatic

**Target:** The assertion that "the concept of 'something it is like to be X' presupposes a perspectival center, an X for whom there is a what-it-is-like."

**Flaw:** This rules out by stipulation an entire family of theories and phenomenological reports. Meditative traditions across cultures report experiences of "pure consciousness" — awareness without content, without a subject-object structure, without a self-model of any kind. If such reports are taken at face value, they constitute direct counterexamples to RCSM. The Architect may respond that these are really cases of attenuated self-modeling rather than absent self-modeling, but this is an empirical claim that requires evidence, not an assertion that can be made from the armchair.

More broadly, the assumption that consciousness requires a subject is a substantive metaphysical commitment that many philosophers reject. By building it into the foundation of the theory without argument, the Architect ensures that the theory cannot engage with the most interesting challenges to its framework.

**Severity: Minor** (because the Architect could address this by acknowledging the assumption and providing independent argument for it, but the failure to do so weakens the theory's philosophical credibility)

---

### Attack 11: The Theory Predicts Nothing Novel

**Target:** The section on "What determines whether a system is conscious."

**Flaw:** The theory's verdicts on paradigm cases (humans: conscious; rocks: not conscious; animals: spectrum) are exactly the same as pre-theoretical common sense. The theory adds no predictive value in any case where we did not already have a strong intuition. The most interesting test case — large language models — receives a punt: "it is an open question." A theory that confirms what we already believed and declines to commit on the hard cases is empirically vacuous.

A good theory of consciousness should generate surprising predictions — cases where the theory says "conscious" but intuition says "not," or vice versa. RCSM generates no such predictions. It is, in this respect, a sophisticated re-description of our intuitions rather than an explanation.

**Severity: Minor** (because predictive novelty is a virtue rather than a requirement, but its absence is a significant weakness)

---

### Attack 12: Hidden Commitment to Functionalism Is Unargued

**Target:** The theory's implicit functionalist framework — the claim that consciousness depends on the functional organization (self-modeling, recursion, causal coupling) rather than the specific physical substrate.

**Flaw:** The Architect never explicitly argues for functionalism, but the theory is implicitly functionalist: if a silicon system implemented the same recursive causal self-modeling as a human brain, RCSM would classify it as conscious. This is a significant metaphysical commitment (multiple realizability of consciousness) that many philosophers deny. Biological naturalists like Searle argue that consciousness depends on specific biological mechanisms, not just functional organization. The Architect's implicit functionalism needs defense, not silent assumption.

**Severity: Minor**
