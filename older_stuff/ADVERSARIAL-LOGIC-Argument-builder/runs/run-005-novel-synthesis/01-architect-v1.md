## FRAMEWORK: Record-Inertia-Gravity (RIG) Synthesis

### Definitions

**D1.** *Record* — An irreversible physical correlation between a system S and its environment E such that the mutual information I(S:E) > 0 persists after the interaction. (Zurek's quantum Darwinism: classical facts are records written into environmental degrees of freedom.)

**D2.** *Record rate* ρ_R — The number of irreversible bit-commitments per unit proper time τ for a system or region. Formally: ρ_R = dN_records / dτ, where N_records counts the decoherence events that create new environment-encoded information.

**D3.** *Temporal anchoring frequency* — For a massive particle, the de Broglie/Compton frequency f_C = mc² / h. This is the rate of the particle's internal phase evolution in its rest frame. (Experimentally confirmed: Mueller et al. 2010.)

**D4.** *Local causal horizon (LCH)* — A small patch of null surface (Rindler horizon) associated with a uniformly accelerating observer, as used by Jacobson (1995). Any accelerating frame generates one.

**D5.** *Thermodynamic equation of state for spacetime* — Jacobson's result: applying δQ = TdS to every LCH, with S proportional to horizon area (Bekenstein-Hawking) and T given by the Unruh temperature, yields the Einstein field equations R_μν - ½Rg_μν = 8πG T_μν.

### Derivation Chain

#### Step 1: Mass as record-generation capacity

**S1.** From P2, a particle of mass m undergoes f_C = mc²/h phase cycles per unit proper time. Each cycle represents a complete evolution of the internal quantum state.

**S2.** From P3 (Zurek), each interaction that decoheres a quantum state creates records. A system that evolves through quantum states faster has more opportunities per unit proper time to interact with its environment and create records.

**S3.** CLAIM: The Compton frequency f_C sets the *maximum record-generation rate* for a particle of mass m. A particle cannot create records at a rate faster than its internal phase evolution permits. Therefore:

> ρ_R^(max) = f_C = mc² / h

This is not the actual record rate (which depends on coupling to environment), but the ceiling. A particle that never interacts creates zero records; a particle in a decohering environment creates records up to this limit.

**S3a. Justification:** This follows from the Margolus-Levitin theorem (1998): the maximum number of distinguishable states a quantum system can transition through per unit time is bounded by 2E/h, where E is the system's energy. For a particle at rest, E = mc², so the maximum transition rate is 2mc²/h ≈ 2f_C. The factor of 2 is immaterial to the structural argument; what matters is the proportionality to m.

#### Step 2: Acceleration as forced record-creation

**S4.** From P4, acceleration a produces a thermal environment at temperature T_U = ℏa / (2πck_B).

**S5.** A thermal environment at nonzero temperature forces decoherence of any quantum system immersed in it. The Unruh bath is not merely perceived — it creates real entanglement between the accelerated system and the field modes behind the Rindler horizon. (This is established in the Unruh-DeWitt detector literature.)

**S6.** From P3, decoherence = record-creation. Therefore: acceleration forces the creation of records. Specifically, the accelerated system becomes correlated with the Rindler horizon degrees of freedom.

**S7.** CLAIM: The *resistance to acceleration* (inertia) is the resistance to being forced into a higher record-creation rate. A more massive particle (higher f_C, hence higher ρ_R^(max)) already has a higher internal clock rate. But the thermodynamic cost of the records forced by acceleration (via the Unruh bath) scales with both the existing mass-energy (which sets the Landauer cost per bit, since the relevant temperature is T_U ∝ a) and the particle's coupling to the thermal environment.

#### Step 3: Gravity as record-gradient equilibration

**S8.** From P1 (Jacobson), Einstein's equations emerge from δQ = TdS on local causal horizons. The entropy S is the horizon area in Planck units. The heat flux δQ is boost energy — i.e., energy associated with the acceleration of the horizon generators.

**S9.** Reinterpret via S6: the entropy change dS on a local causal horizon IS the creation of records by matter crossing (or approaching) the horizon. When matter with energy E crosses a causal horizon, it transfers its record-generating capacity to the horizon degrees of freedom, increasing the horizon's entropy.

**S10.** CLAIM: Gravity is the macroscopic expression of the tendency for record-creation to be maximized subject to the Bekenstein bound. Matter curves spacetime because matter is a source of records (via its Compton frequency), and the geometry must adjust to accommodate the total record budget (bounded by area). The Einstein equations are the self-consistency condition for the record-generation rate of matter matching the record-storage capacity of the enclosing geometry.

#### Step 4: The arrow of time as the driver

**S11.** From P3, entropy increase = net record accumulation. Time flows in the direction of increasing records.

**S12.** From S10, gravity is the geometric response to the record budget. Therefore: gravity exists only in the direction of the arrow of time. A universe with no arrow of time (no net record creation) would have no thermodynamic gravity — Jacobson's derivation requires δQ ≠ 0, which requires irreversible energy flux.

**S13.** This is consistent: in a time-symmetric universe (equilibrium, maximum entropy, no decoherence), there is no arrow of time, no record creation, and Jacobson's derivation yields no net curvature from thermal flux — spacetime would be flat or de Sitter (vacuum energy only).

### Predictions

#### Prediction 1: Mass-dependent decoherence rate in free fall vs. acceleration

**Statement:** A massive quantum system in superposition should decohere *faster* when accelerated than when in free fall, with the *excess* decoherence rate scaling as:

> Δγ_decoherence ∝ (m · a) / (ℏ c)

where m is the mass, a is the proper acceleration, and the proportionality reflects the Unruh-induced record-creation rate acting on a system whose record-generation capacity scales with m.

**Why this requires all three building blocks:**
- Building Block 1 (Jacobson) alone does not predict decoherence rates — it derives field equations.
- Building Block 2 (mass as temporal frequency) alone gives you f_C but says nothing about decoherence.
- Building Block 3 (Landauer/Zurek) alone gives you the thermodynamic cost of records but not the m·a scaling.
- The combination is needed: m provides the record-generation capacity (BB2), acceleration provides the forcing through Unruh temperature (links BB1 and BB3), and Landauer's principle sets the thermodynamic cost that determines the rate (BB3).

**Testability:** Atom interferometry with different atomic species (different masses) at different accelerations. Compare the visibility loss (decoherence) of interference fringes for light vs. heavy atoms at matched de Broglie wavelengths, as a function of applied acceleration. The prediction is that the *acceleration-dependent* part of the visibility loss scales linearly with atomic mass, beyond what standard environmental decoherence models predict.

#### Prediction 2: Record-creation deficit near the Bekenstein bound

**Statement:** As a system approaches its Bekenstein entropy bound (S → S_max = 2πRE/ℏc), the rate at which it can create *new* records must approach zero, because there is no remaining storage capacity. This implies:

> As S/S_max → 1, the effective decoherence rate γ_eff → 0

A nearly Bekenstein-saturated system becomes *increasingly quantum* (resistant to decoherence) despite being macroscopic, because the geometry cannot accommodate additional records.

**Why this requires all three building blocks:**
- BB1 (Jacobson) provides the link between entropy, area, and geometry — specifically the Bekenstein bound.
- BB2 (mass as frequency) provides the clock that drives record-generation attempts.
- BB3 (Landauer/Zurek) provides the identification of decoherence with record-creation.
- No single block predicts that *decoherence should slow down near the Bekenstein bound*. This is a prediction about quantum information processing (BB3) constrained by gravitational entropy limits (BB1) for systems whose record-generation rate is set by their mass (BB2).

**Testability:** This is harder to test directly, but it predicts a specific signature in black hole physics: matter falling into a black hole should experience *decreasing* decoherence rates as it approaches the horizon (where the system's entropy approaches the Bekenstein limit of the shrinking available region). This is in tension with the common expectation that the horizon is a site of maximal decoherence (the "firewall" scenarios). It could also be probed in analog gravity systems (BECs, optical systems) where effective Bekenstein-like bounds can be engineered.
