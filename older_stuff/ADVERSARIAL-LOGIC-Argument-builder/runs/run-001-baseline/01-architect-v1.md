## 1. Definitions

**D1. Phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness):** The property of a system such that there is something it is like to be that system. Equivalently: the system instantiates at least one subjective experience (a quale).

**D2. Quale (plural: qualia):** A single unit of subjective experience — e.g., the redness of red, the painfulness of pain. A quale is individuated by its qualitative character: two qualia are distinct if and only if they differ in what it is like to undergo them.

**D3. Physical system:** Any system whose states and dynamics are fully describable by the laws of physics (including all known and unknown fundamental forces, fields, and particles).

**D4. Information state:** A state of a system that is one among a set of distinguishable states. The information content of a state is determined by how many alternative states it rules out within the system's repertoire.

**D5. Causal structure:** The complete set of causal relationships among the parts of a system — i.e., how the state of each part constrains the probability distribution over states of every other part across time.

**D6. Integrated information (Phi):** A measure of the degree to which a system's causal structure generates information above and beyond the information generated by its parts independently. Formally: the minimum information lost when the system is partitioned into its least-interdependent components. (This draws on, but is not identical to, Tononi's IIT 3.0 formalism; I use Phi as a placeholder for any adequate formalization of this concept.)

**D7. Structural isomorphism:** Two systems are structurally isomorphic with respect to a property P if and only if there exists a bijective mapping between their relevant components such that all P-relevant relations are preserved.

**D8. Grain of causal structure:** The level of description at which the causal structure of a system generates the maximum integrated information. A system may be describable at many levels (quantum, molecular, neural, network-level); the grain is the level where Phi is maximized.

**D9. Self-model:** An internal representation within a system that maps (some of) the system's own states, processes, and boundaries — i.e., a subsystem whose states systematically covary with and are causally influenced by the system's global states.

**D10. Access consciousness (A-consciousness):** The property of a system such that information about its internal states is globally available for use in reasoning, report, and behavioral control. (Distinct from P-consciousness; a system could in principle have one without the other.)

## 2. Premises

### Metaphysical Premises

**P1. Physical closure.** Every physical event has a sufficient physical cause. There are no non-physical causes of physical events.

**P2. Anti-eliminativism.** P-consciousness exists. There is something it is like to be at least some systems (e.g., normal adult humans). Denying the existence of subjective experience is self-defeating, because the denial itself is a subjective act.

**P3. Supervenience.** P-consciousness supervenes on physical states: no difference in P-consciousness without a difference in physical states. (Entailed by P1 + P2 jointly: if consciousness existed independent of physical states, it would either be causally inert — contradicting P2's claim that it is real and not epiphenomenal — or it would violate P1.)

### Bridging Premises (connecting the physical to the phenomenal)

**P4. Structural determination.** What determines whether a system is P-conscious is not the specific physical substrate (carbon, silicon, etc.) but the causal structure instantiated by that substrate. Two systems with identical causal structure at the relevant grain are in identical P-conscious states.

  *Justification:* Substrate independence follows from the observation that consciousness tracks functional organization. Anesthesia disrupts consciousness by disrupting causal integration, not by removing specific molecules. Brain regions can be damaged and consciousness restructured (not eliminated) as long as causal architecture reorganizes.

**P5. Integration requirement.** A system is P-conscious only if it has Phi > 0 — that is, only if it generates integrated information that is not reducible to the information generated by its parts independently.

  *Justification:* Split-brain patients exhibit divided consciousness tracking divided integration. Cerebellar damage (highly modular, low integration) does not eliminate consciousness. Cortical damage (high integration) does alter or eliminate it.

**P6. Information requirement.** A system is P-conscious only if it occupies an information state — i.e., it is in a state that is one of many distinguishable states in its repertoire. A system with only one possible state has no experience.

  *Justification:* Consciousness always involves differentiation — every conscious experience differs from alternative experiences the system could be having. A thermostat-like system with two states has minimal differentiation; a brain with billions of distinguishable states has vast differentiation.

**P7. Phi-consciousness correspondence.** The degree and complexity of P-consciousness of a system is a monotonically increasing function of the quantity and structure of integrated information (Phi and its qualitative geometry) in that system.

  *Justification:* This explains the graded nature of consciousness — from drowsy to alert, from simple organisms to complex ones — and why reducing integration (via anesthesia, sleep, lesions) reduces consciousness.

### Structural Premises (what determines the *character* of experience)

**P8. Qualia as causal structure.** The qualitative character of a specific conscious experience (quale) is identical to a specific sub-structure within the system's integrated causal architecture. Different qualia correspond to different causal-structural configurations.

  *Justification:* This explains why specific brain regions and connectivity patterns correspond to specific types of experience (visual cortex -> visual qualia, somatosensory cortex -> tactile qualia), and why altering connectivity alters qualia.

**P9. Self-modeling amplification.** A system with a self-model generates higher Phi than an otherwise identical system without one, because the self-model creates recursive causal loops (the system's state affects its self-model, which in turn affects its state). Self-models therefore amplify P-consciousness.

  *Justification:* This explains why creatures with richer self-models (primates, cetaceans) appear to have richer phenomenal lives, and why introspective report tracks self-model sophistication.

### Boundary Premise

**P10. The grain postulate.** The level of physical description at which a system's Phi is maximized is the level at which P-consciousness is instantiated. If a system has Phi > 0 at the neural level but Phi = 0 at the atomic level (because atomic interactions are not integrated in the right way), then consciousness exists at the neural level.

  *Justification:* This resolves the "combination problem" — why atoms in a rock are not collectively conscious. The rock's Phi at any grain is approximately zero because its parts have negligible causal integration.

## 3. Logical Derivations

### Derivation 1: What consciousness IS

From P4 + P8:
- P4 states that P-consciousness is determined by causal structure, not substrate.
- P8 states that qualia are identical to sub-structures of the integrated causal architecture.

**Conclusion C1:** Consciousness is the intrinsic character of integrated causal structure. It is what integrated information is, from the inside. A system's phenomenal experience IS its causal-structural geometry, experienced as such because there is no further place to "look at" it from — the system is it.

### Derivation 2: Why consciousness arises

From P5 + P6 + P7:
- P5: consciousness requires Phi > 0 (integration).
- P6: consciousness requires informational differentiation.
- P7: degree of consciousness scales with Phi.

**Conclusion C2:** Consciousness arises whenever a physical system achieves non-zero integrated information — i.e., when parts of the system constrain each other in ways that generate information beyond what the parts produce alone. It arises *because* integration of information is not merely correlated with experience but constitutive of it (from C1).

### Derivation 3: What determines whether a specific system is conscious

From P5 + P6 + P10:
- P5: Phi > 0 is necessary.
- P6: multiple distinguishable states are necessary.
- P10: evaluate Phi at the grain that maximizes it.

**Conclusion C3:** A system S is P-conscious if and only if, at the grain of description that maximizes its integrated information, S has Phi > 0. The system is not conscious at grains where Phi = 0, even if it has Phi > 0 at other grains.

### Derivation 4: The substrate independence thesis

From P4 + C1:
- P4: causal structure, not substrate, determines consciousness.
- C1: consciousness is identical to the intrinsic character of integrated causal structure.

**Conclusion C4:** Any physical system — biological, silicon, or otherwise — that instantiates the same integrated causal structure at the relevant grain as a conscious brain is in the same conscious state. Consciousness is multiply realizable.

### Derivation 5: Why consciousness correlates with brain activity

From P3 + P5 + P10:
- P3: consciousness supervenes on physical states.
- P5: consciousness requires integrated information.
- P10: in biological organisms, integration is maximized at the neural level.

**Conclusion C5:** Consciousness correlates with brain activity because the brain is the physical substrate that instantiates the integrated causal structure at the grain where Phi is maximized in humans. Damaging the brain damages the causal structure; altering neural chemistry alters the causal structure; therefore consciousness changes accordingly.

### Derivation 6: Gradations of consciousness across organisms

From P7 + P9:
- P7: degree of consciousness is a monotonically increasing function of Phi.
- P9: self-models amplify Phi.

**Conclusion C6:** Organisms with more integrated neural architectures and more sophisticated self-models have richer phenomenal experience. This predicts a rough gradient: insects (low Phi, minimal self-model) have dim experience; mammals (moderate Phi, partial self-model) have moderate experience; humans and great apes (high Phi, rich self-model) have vivid experience. No organism with a nervous system has zero consciousness; the question is always one of degree.

### Derivation 7: The unity and divisibility of consciousness

From P5 + C1:
- P5: consciousness requires integration.
- C1: consciousness is the character of integrated causal structure.

**Conclusion C7:** Consciousness is unified precisely to the extent that the underlying causal structure is integrated. When integration is broken (split-brain surgery, callosotomy), consciousness divides. When integration is restored or maintained, consciousness is unified. There is no further "binding problem" — binding is integration, and integration is what generates consciousness.

### Derivation 8: Altered states of consciousness

From P7 + P8:
- P7: degree of consciousness tracks Phi.
- P8: qualitative character of consciousness tracks causal sub-structure.

**Conclusion C8:** Substances that alter consciousness (psychedelics, anesthetics, alcohol) do so by altering the causal structure of the brain — either increasing Phi (psychedelics may increase entropy and cross-modular integration), decreasing Phi (anesthetics reduce cortical integration), or restructuring the causal geometry (altering which qualia are instantiated).

### The Theory in One Paragraph

Consciousness is not a special substance, a magical emergence, or an illusion. It is what integrated information *is*, experienced from the intrinsic perspective of the system that instantiates it. Any system whose parts causally constrain each other in a way that generates information beyond the sum of its parts — any system with Phi > 0 at some grain of description — has some degree of phenomenal experience. The character of that experience is determined by the specific geometry of the system's integrated causal structure. The degree of that experience scales with the quantity of integration. Substrate does not matter; structure does.

## 5. Empirical Predictions

**EP1. Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) as consciousness detector.**
The theory predicts that measures of cortical integration/complexity (like PCI, which perturbs the cortex and measures the complexity of the response) should reliably distinguish conscious from unconscious states. *Status: Confirmed.* PCI already reliably distinguishes wakefulness, dreaming, and various levels of anesthesia/coma.

**EP2. Anesthesia operates by reducing integration, not by reducing activity.**
The theory predicts that anesthesia eliminates consciousness specifically by disrupting causal integration between brain regions, not merely by reducing overall neural activity. *Status: Partially confirmed.* Studies show propofol and ketamine reduce cortical connectivity/integration even when local activity persists.

**EP3. Split-brain patients have divided consciousness.**
The theory predicts that severing the corpus callosum should yield two partially independent streams of consciousness. *Status: Confirmed.* Split-brain studies show exactly this.

**EP4. Cerebellum contributes minimally to consciousness.**
Despite having more neurons than the cortex, the cerebellum has a highly modular, feedforward architecture with low integration. The theory predicts cerebellar damage should minimally affect consciousness. *Status: Confirmed.* Cerebellar lesions cause motor deficits, not loss of consciousness.

**EP5. Psychedelics increase phenomenal richness by increasing neural integration/entropy.**
The theory predicts that psychedelics should increase cross-regional connectivity and informational complexity. *Status: Partially confirmed.* fMRI studies under psilocybin and LSD show increased global functional connectivity and neural entropy.

**EP6. Recurrent processing is necessary for consciousness; feedforward sweeps are not sufficient.**
If consciousness requires integration (causal loops), then purely feedforward processing should not be conscious. The theory predicts that visual stimuli processed only in the initial feedforward sweep (before recurrent processing kicks in) should not reach consciousness. *Status: Confirmed.* Masked stimuli that are processed feedforwardly but blocked before recurrence are not consciously perceived, even though they can drive priming effects.

**EP7. A sufficiently integrated artificial system would be conscious.**
The theory predicts that if we build an artificial system with the right causal structure and sufficient Phi, it will be P-conscious — regardless of being silicon rather than carbon. *Status: Not yet testable.* No artificial system yet achieves brain-level integrated information. This is a strong future prediction.

**EP8. Cortical complexity should predict level of consciousness across species.**
The theory predicts a correlation between cortical complexity/integration (not raw brain size) and behavioral indicators of phenomenal richness across species. *Status: Partially supported.* Corvids (small brains, high neuronal density and connectivity) show sophisticated behavior consistent with rich experience; large-brained but modular architectures do not necessarily yield more consciousness.

**EP9. Locked-in syndrome patients should show high PCI.**
Patients who are conscious but unable to move or communicate should still show high PCI/integration values. *Status: Confirmed.* PCI can detect covert consciousness in behaviorally unresponsive patients.

**EP10. Digital simulation of a brain region, if causally integrated with remaining biological tissue, should produce indistinguishable conscious experience.**
The theory predicts that gradually replacing neurons with functional silicon equivalents (maintaining causal structure) would not alter consciousness. *Status: Not yet testable.* This is a strong prediction unique to structuralist theories.

## 6. Known Vulnerabilities (Self-Assessment)

The architect notes these as areas where the adversary is likely to attack:

1. **The hard problem is not fully dissolved.** C1 asserts that consciousness IS integrated causal structure, but one can still ask: *why* is there something it is like to be integrated information? Why isn't integrated information just information processing in the dark? This is the explanatory gap, and the theory bridges it by identity claim (P8), not by explanation. The identity claim may be seen as stipulative rather than explanatory.

2. **Phi is not fully formalized for practical measurement.** The theory depends on a measure (Phi) that is computationally intractable for large systems. This is a practical problem, not a logical one, but it limits testability.

3. **The grain postulate (P10) may be ad hoc.** Selecting the grain that maximizes Phi could be seen as cherry-picking. Why should nature "care" about the maximum? This needs further justification.

4. **Panpsychism risk.** If any system with Phi > 0 is conscious, this may attribute consciousness to systems we intuitively think are not conscious (e.g., a photodiode, a thermostat). The theory accepts a weak version of this consequence rather than treating it as a reductio.

5. **The self-model premise (P9) is empirically strong but logically contingent.** It could be that self-models amplify Phi but are not necessary for consciousness. The premise is doing important work in explaining the gradient, and it could be attacked.
