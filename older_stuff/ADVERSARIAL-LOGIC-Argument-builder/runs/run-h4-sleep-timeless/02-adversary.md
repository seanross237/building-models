### Attack 1 -- The Unfalsifiability Problem: "Absence of report =/= absence of experience" is a shield, not an argument

**Target:** S6, S8, C2.

**Flaw:** The architect's core move is: deep sleep looks unconscious, but the TDTS framework says it is actually conscious, just temporally decoupled. The absence of subjective reports is explained away as an artifact of the measurement method. The architect acknowledges this risk (Honesty Check #2) but does not solve it.

This is the oldest problem in consciousness studies, and it is fatal here. If consciousness without temporal structure *by definition* cannot be reported, remembered, or behaviorally expressed, then the claim "there is consciousness during N3" is empirically empty. The architect's attempt to rescue it via Prediction 3 (dissociable PCI patterns) does not work: PCI measures cortical complexity, not consciousness. Even if NREM and anesthesia show different PCI decompositions (local vs. global), this tells us about *cortical processing patterns*, not about whether there is "something it is like" to be in that state.

The architect cannot have it both ways: either consciousness-without-time has detectable signatures (in which case the theory makes testable predictions and we can evaluate it) or it does not (in which case the theory is metaphysics, not science). The architect gestures toward the former but the core claim -- consciousness during deep N3 -- rests on the latter.

**Severity: Fatal.** The central thesis (consciousness persists in deep sleep) is unfalsifiable in the form presented. Every piece of evidence against it (no reports, no behavioral signs, no memory formation) is explained as a *consequence* of temporal decoupling rather than as evidence against consciousness. This is structurally identical to the problem with panpsychism: you cannot falsify the claim that a thermostat is conscious.

### Attack 2 -- Equivocation on "temporal structure" between phenomenology and neural mechanism

**Target:** D2, D3, S5, S6.

**Flaw:** The architect conflates two very different things under "temporal structure":

(a) **Phenomenological temporal structure** (D2): the subjective sense of succession, duration, and temporal asymmetry.

(b) **Neural temporal dynamics**: the oscillatory structure of cortical activity (gamma, theta, SWA), thalamocortical feedback loops, and temporal binding mechanisms.

These are not the same thing, and neither is a proven cause of the other. The architect's argument implicitly assumes that (b) causes (a), such that disrupting (b) removes (a) while leaving "base consciousness" intact. But this is precisely the claim under dispute. It could equally be that:

- (b) is constitutive of consciousness itself, such that disrupting neural temporal dynamics disrupts consciousness, not just temporal phenomenology.
- (a) is constitutive of consciousness, such that a state without phenomenological time is not a conscious state at all.

The architect needs the specific causal structure "neural temporal dynamics --> temporal phenomenology" with consciousness as an independent variable. But this is an assumption, not a derivation, and it is the very thing that needs to be proved.

**Severity: Major.** The entire Step 2 depends on this equivocation. If neural temporal dynamics are constitutive of (rather than merely correlated with) conscious experience, then disrupting them during NREM *is* disrupting consciousness, not merely decoupling time.

### Attack 3 -- The "consciousness without time" concept may be incoherent

**Target:** D4, S8, S10, C2.

**Flaw:** The architect describes "consciousness without time" as having the following properties (S10): no succession, no duration, no temporal asymmetry, but phenomenal quality present. The critical question, which the architect does not answer: **How do you distinguish "consciousness without time" from "no consciousness"?**

Consider: what would "consciousness without time" be like? The architect says there is "something it is like" but no temporal structure. But phenomenal experience appears to require *at minimum* the following temporal features:

- **Duration:** An experience must persist for some nonzero interval. An instantaneous experience is not an experience -- it has no phenomenal character, because phenomenal character requires a state to *obtain* for long enough to constitute an experience. (The specious present literature, from James 1890 to Dainton 2000, argues that even the "present moment" of experience has duration.)

- **Distinction from prior/subsequent states:** For there to be "something it is like" at time t, there must be a fact of the matter about how the experience at t differs from adjacent moments. If there is no temporal ordering, there is no "at time t," and no experience can be individuated.

The architect's "timeless present" is not coherently described. If it has no duration, it is not an experience. If it has duration, it has temporal structure (at minimum, the structure "this experience persists"). The concept collapses into either: (a) an experience with temporal structure (contradicting the claim), or (b) no experience at all (contradicting the framework).

The meditation reports (P4) do not save this. Meditators who report "timeless awareness" are *reporting after the fact*, which means they had temporal experience of at least the transition into and out of the state. What the state itself is "like" during its occurrence is precisely what cannot be reported -- returning us to the unfalsifiability problem of Attack 1.

**Severity: Major.** This is a philosophical challenge to the coherence of the central concept. If "consciousness without time" is conceptually incoherent, the TDTS framework has no referent.

### Attack 4 -- P7 (within-NREM dreaming) actually undermines the thesis

**Target:** S6, S8, and the interpretation of P7.

**Flaw:** The architect cites Boly et al. 2017 and Siclari et al. 2017 as evidence for consciousness during NREM. But these studies actually show something different from what the architect claims:

- The NREM conscious reports (~30-50%) are correlated with *decreased* SWA over posterior hot zones. That is, consciousness during NREM is associated with *local waking-like activity* -- precisely the areas that escape the slow-wave temporal template.

- This means the NREM consciousness that has been demonstrated is NOT "consciousness without temporal structure." It is *local consciousness WITH temporal structure* (the awake-like posterior zones) embedded in a background of temporally disrupted cortex.

The architect's own evidence shows that wherever consciousness is found in NREM, it is accompanied by local temporal dynamics resembling waking. This is evidence *against* the TDTS claim that consciousness can persist without temporal structure, and evidence *for* the standard view that consciousness requires waking-like temporal dynamics.

The architect should have caught this: the data says "consciousness in NREM correlates with local de-synchronization (i.e., restoration of temporal complexity)," which is the opposite of "consciousness without temporal structure."

**Severity: Major.** The empirical evidence cited in P7 directly contradicts the core claim S8. The architect used the evidence selectively -- citing the frequency of NREM reports without acknowledging that the mechanism involves local *restoration* of temporal structure, not consciousness persisting *despite* temporal disruption.

### Attack 6 -- IIT (P5) does not actually support the separability claim

**Target:** S3, P5.

**Flaw:** The architect claims that IIT supports the separability of consciousness from temporal structure, because "Phi depends on causal structure, not temporal dynamics." This is a misreading of IIT.

In IIT (Tononi & Koch 2015, Oizumi et al. 2014), Phi is computed over a *transition probability matrix* (TPM) -- the probabilities of transitions between states over one time step. The TPM is fundamentally temporal: it describes how the system evolves from one moment to the next. If temporal dynamics are disrupted (e.g., if the system is frozen or if transitions become random), the TPM changes, and Phi changes.

Specifically, during NREM down states, the cortex enters a hyperpolarized state where neurons do not fire. The TPM during down states approaches the identity matrix or a random matrix (depending on interpretation), both of which have low Phi. IIT therefore predicts *reduced Phi during down states*, which is reduced consciousness -- not "consciousness without time."

The architect's claim that "a system can have high Phi in a single frozen state" is wrong within IIT's formalism. A frozen system (no transitions) has an undefined or degenerate TPM, and its Phi is not well-defined in the standard IIT framework.

Furthermore, IIT 4.0 (Albantakis et al. 2023) explicitly uses *intrinsic information* computed over cause-effect structures that assume temporal transitions. Remove the temporal transitions, and there is no cause-effect structure, and no Phi.

**Severity: Major.** The theoretical backing for the separability claim (S3) collapses if IIT is read correctly. IIT makes consciousness *more* dependent on temporal dynamics than the architect claims, not less.

### Attack 7 -- Prediction 1 does not actually test the core thesis

**Target:** Prediction 1.

**Flaw:** Prediction 1 says that post-awakening temporal order judgments should degrade more steeply with prior sleep depth than non-temporal perceptual tasks. But this prediction is equally consistent with the *standard* view (sleep = reduced consciousness) for a simple reason:

If deeper sleep = less consciousness, and temporal processing is more cognitively demanding than simple perceptual detection, then temporal tasks will degrade faster simply because they are harder. No "consciousness without time" hypothesis is needed. The degradation-with-difficulty gradient is a standard finding in any context of cognitive impairment (sleep inertia, alcohol, fatigue).

For Prediction 1 to specifically support TDTS over the standard view, the architect would need to show that the temporal task degradation is *disproportionate* relative to its baseline difficulty -- i.e., that temporal processing is *selectively* impaired beyond what general cognitive impairment would predict. This requires a much more careful experimental design than what is described.

**Severity: Minor.** The prediction is testable and interesting, but it does not discriminate between TDTS and the standard view without additional controls.

## PART B: Novelty Audit

---

### Has this idea been proposed before?

**YES.** The idea that sleep involves altered temporal consciousness rather than absence of consciousness has significant prior art:

1. **Evan Thompson, *Waking, Dreaming, Being* (2015):** Thompson explicitly discusses the idea that deep sleep may involve a form of awareness, drawing on Indian philosophical traditions (Advaita Vedanta's concept of *sushupti* -- the witness consciousness during deep sleep). He discusses this in the context of neurophenomenology and argues that the absence of dream reports from deep sleep does not entail absence of experience.

2. **Jennifer Windt, *Dreaming* (2015):** Windt develops a detailed philosophical framework for "minimal phenomenal experience" during sleep, including the idea that deep sleep may involve a "selfless, worldless, timeless" form of experience. She explicitly addresses the concept of phenomenal experience without temporal structure.

3. **Thomas Metzinger's "minimal phenomenal experience" (MPE) research program (2020):** Metzinger proposes that there is a minimal form of phenomenal experience -- "pure awareness" -- that persists across sleep states and is characterized by the absence of content, including temporal content. This is essentially the architect's thesis.

4. **Tononi & Koch (2015), within IIT:** Tononi himself has speculated that consciousness during deep sleep may be reduced but nonzero, and that the IIT framework allows for this. However, he does NOT frame this as "consciousness without time" -- he frames it as reduced Phi.

5. **Contemplative science literature:** The entire literature on "witnessing sleep" in the TM tradition (Alexander et al. 1987; Mason et al. 1997) and "luminous sleep" in Tibetan Buddhism (Gyatso 2004; Holecek 2016) describes exactly the phenomenon the architect proposes, often using the language of "timeless awareness during deep sleep."

**The specific phrase "consciousness without time" as a description of deep sleep** appears in Thompson (2015) and in various contemplative science publications. The framing of sleep stages as a "temporal decoupling spectrum" may be a somewhat novel organizational contribution, but the core idea is well-established in the intersection of philosophy of mind, contemplative science, and consciousness studies.

### What is novel in the architect's version?

1. **The graded spectrum framing** (S12: waking/REM/NREM/anesthesia as positions on a temporal coupling axis) is a relatively clear organizational contribution, but it is an organizational framework, not a new hypothesis.

2. **The connection to TMS-EEG/PCI data** (using Massimini's results as empirical support for the temporal decoupling interpretation) is somewhat novel in its specificity, though Thompson (2015) and Tononi's group have made similar connections.

3. **Prediction 2 (meditator NREM signatures)** is testable and somewhat novel as a specific experimental prediction, though it draws on existing findings (Ferrarelli et al. 2013) and the general idea has been proposed in the contemplative science literature.

4. **Prediction 3 (anesthesia vs. sleep PCI decomposition)** is the most genuinely novel prediction, as it proposes a specific empirical test (local vs. global complexity decomposition) that discriminates between temporal decoupling and consciousness loss. This extends existing TMS-EEG methodology in a new direction. However, it tests the temporal decoupling interpretation, not the "consciousness without time" claim per se (which remains unfalsifiable).

### Novelty Verdict

**Core thesis (sleep = consciousness without time):** NOT NOVEL. Well-established in Thompson (2015), Windt (2015), Metzinger (2020), and the contemplative science literature. The architect does not cite any of these, which is a significant omission.

**Graded temporal decoupling framing:** MILDLY NOVEL as an organizational scheme. Not novel as a conceptual contribution.

**Specific predictions:** PARTIALLY NOVEL. Prediction 3 (anesthesia vs. sleep PCI decomposition) is the most original contribution and is genuinely testable. Prediction 1 does not discriminate from the standard view. Prediction 2 is an incremental extension of existing work.
