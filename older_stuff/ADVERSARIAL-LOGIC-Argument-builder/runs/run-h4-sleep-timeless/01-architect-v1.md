## HYPOTHESIS

"Sleep isn't unconsciousness -- it's consciousness without time. Waking is what happens when consciousness and time couple. Dreams are famously atemporal (time jumps, loops, compresses). Deep sleep might not be absence of consciousness but consciousness unbound from temporal structure."

## FRAMEWORK: Temporal Decoupling Theory of Sleep (TDTS)

### Definitions

**D1.** *Consciousness* -- The property of a system such that there is something it is like to be that system (Nagel 1974). Operationally: a system is conscious to the degree that it possesses integrated information that is irreducible to the information in its parts (Tononi IIT) or, more neutrally, to the degree that it supports a unified field of subjective experience.

**D2.** *Temporal structure of consciousness* -- The phenomenological feature by which conscious states are experienced as ordered, successive, and durational. This includes: (a) the sense of "now" (temporal presence), (b) the sense that moments follow one another (succession), (c) the sense that events take time (duration), and (d) the capacity to distinguish past from future (temporal asymmetry).

**D3.** *Temporal coupling* -- The binding of a system's conscious states to an external or internal clock such that the temporal structure of consciousness (D2) tracks objective physical time with approximate fidelity. In normal waking, temporal coupling is maintained by thalamocortical feedback loops, hippocampal theta rhythms, and cortical gamma oscillations.

**D4.** *Temporal decoupling* -- The partial or total unbinding of conscious states from temporal structure, such that consciousness persists but temporal features (succession, duration, asymmetry) are degraded or absent. The claim of this framework is that sleep stages represent graded degrees of temporal decoupling, not graded degrees of consciousness loss.

**D5.** *Complexity of cortical response* -- Measured by the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI), introduced by Massimini et al. (2005, refined by Casali et al. 2013). PCI quantifies the spatiotemporal complexity of the cortical response to a TMS pulse by computing the Lempel-Ziv complexity of the EEG after perturbation. High PCI indicates widespread, differentiated cortical processing; low PCI indicates either stereotyped or absent cortical response.

### Premises (Empirical and Philosophical)

**P1. [Massimini et al. 2005, 2010; Casali et al. 2013 -- TMS-EEG studies]** During NREM deep sleep (stages N2-N3), a TMS pulse to the cortex produces a large-amplitude, stereotyped, locally confined response that fades within ~150ms. PCI is low. During REM sleep, the same TMS pulse produces a response resembling waking: complex, differentiated, and spatially distributed. PCI is high. During waking, PCI is highest.

> Key fact: PCI in REM is closer to waking PCI than to NREM PCI. Yet subjective reports from REM (dreams) show profoundly distorted temporal processing, while subjective reports from NREM show either no reports or fragmentary, thought-like mentation.

**P2. [Neuroanatomy of sleep stages]** NREM slow-wave sleep is characterized by high-amplitude, low-frequency (0.5-4 Hz) cortical oscillations (slow-wave activity, SWA). These oscillations involve global bistability: large cortical populations alternate between depolarized "up states" (active, firing) and hyperpolarized "down states" (silent). Crucially, during down states, thalamocortical communication is disrupted.

> Key fact: The cortex is NOT silent during NREM. Up states involve neural firing patterns as complex as waking. What changes is the *temporal organization* -- the alternation between global up/down states imposes a coarse, stereotyped temporal structure that overrides the fine-grained temporal differentiation of waking.

**P3. [Hobson et al. 2000; Nir & Tononi 2010 -- Dream phenomenology]** Dreams during REM sleep exhibit systematic distortions of temporal phenomenology: (a) time compression (hours of subjective experience in minutes of real time), (b) time dilation (moments that stretch), (c) temporal discontinuities (scene changes with no transition), (d) absence of reliable temporal ordering (events occur "out of order"), (e) inability to track elapsed time. Dreamers rarely know how long they have been dreaming.

**P4. [Meditation reports -- jhana/samadhi traditions]** Contemplative traditions across cultures report states of consciousness in which temporal experience is profoundly altered or absent. Key reports include: (a) "cessation" experiences in Theravada Buddhism (nirodha samapatti) -- consciousness without content, described as timeless; (b) jhana absorption states -- consciousness with content (bliss, equanimity) but no sense of temporal passage; (c) "pure consciousness events" during sleep (reported in TM tradition, studied by Mason et al. 1997; Banquet 1973) -- awareness during deep sleep without dream content. These reports describe states that are clearly conscious (there is something it is like) but lack temporal structure (no succession, no duration, no before/after).

> Key fact: These are convergent reports from independent traditions with different conceptual frameworks, suggesting they track a real phenomenological category rather than being cultural artifacts.

**P5. [Tononi IIT -- Phi and consciousness]** Integrated Information Theory proposes that consciousness is identical to integrated information (Phi). A system's Phi depends on its causal structure, not on its temporal dynamics per se. A system with high Phi is conscious even if its temporal processing is disrupted, because Phi measures the irreducibility of the system's cause-effect structure, not the speed or ordering of its state transitions.

> Key fact for this argument: IIT does not require temporal structure for consciousness. A system can have high Phi in a single "frozen" state. Temporal dynamics affect which *experience* the system has, not whether it has experience.

**P6. [Dehaene, Changeux -- Global Neuronal Workspace, GNW]** Global workspace theory holds that consciousness requires information to be broadcast across widely distributed cortical areas via long-range connections. During NREM slow-wave sleep, the down states disrupt this long-range broadcasting. GNW would predict loss of consciousness during NREM.

> Key fact: GNW and IIT make different predictions about NREM consciousness. GNW predicts absence of consciousness (no global broadcast). IIT predicts reduced but nonzero consciousness (cortical causal structure persists during up states). The TDTS framework sides with IIT on this point.

**P7. [Boly et al. 2017; Siclari et al. 2017 -- within-NREM dreaming]** Recent studies using serial awakenings show that subjects report conscious experiences (dreams or thought-like mentation) from NREM sleep approximately 30-50% of the time. These reports are correlated with local decreases in slow-wave activity over posterior cortical hot zones, even while frontal regions show full slow-wave patterns. This demonstrates that consciousness during NREM is more common than previously assumed and is associated with localized islands of waking-like cortical activity within the sea of slow waves.

### Derivation Chain

#### Step 1: Temporal structure as a separable dimension of consciousness

**S1.** From P3 and P4, temporal structure can be profoundly altered (dreams) or reportedly absent (meditation) while consciousness persists. This suggests that temporal structure is not constitutive of consciousness but is a *modifiable parameter* of conscious experience.

**S2.** Formalize: Let C represent the state of consciousness and T represent the temporal structure of that consciousness. The standard assumption is C = f(T) -- consciousness depends on temporal processing. The TDTS claim is that C and T are *separable*: C = C_base * T_coupling, where C_base is a temporally unstructured consciousness and T_coupling is a temporal binding factor that can range from 0 (no temporal structure) to 1 (full waking temporal fidelity).

**S3.** From P5 (IIT), this separability has theoretical backing: Phi depends on causal structure, not temporal dynamics. A system can have high Phi (high consciousness) with disrupted temporal processing. The temporal structure of experience is determined by which cause-effect repertoire is active, not by whether there is an experience at all.

#### Step 2: Sleep stages as graded temporal decoupling

**S4.** From P1 (TMS-EEG), the cortex during NREM is not functionally dead -- it produces large responses to perturbation, indicating active neural processing. But the responses are stereotyped and local, suggesting that the processing is *temporally impoverished* (dominated by the slow-wave bistability) rather than absent.

**S5.** From P2, the slow-wave oscillation during NREM imposes a coarse temporal structure (up/down alternation at 0.5-4 Hz) that overrides the fine-grained temporal differentiation of waking (which operates at gamma frequencies, 30-100 Hz). The ratio of temporal grain sizes is approximately 100:1 (gamma ~60 Hz vs. SWA ~0.6 Hz).

**S6.** CLAIM: Interpret this as temporal decoupling, not consciousness loss. During NREM:
- C_base remains nonzero (the cortex is still active during up states; P7 shows ~30-50% of NREM awakenings yield conscious reports).
- T_coupling drops dramatically (temporal resolution degrades from millisecond-scale gamma to second-scale SWA; thalamocortical loops that normally maintain temporal coherence are disrupted during down states).
- The *product* C = C_base * T_coupling is low, which is why NREM *appears* unconscious: the temporal structure needed to *encode experiences into memory* and to *report them upon awakening* is degraded. The absence of report =/= the absence of experience.

**S7.** From P3, REM sleep is the intermediate case:
- C_base is high (PCI is near waking levels; P1).
- T_coupling is *partially degraded*: temporal structure exists but is distorted (time compression, loops, discontinuities). The hippocampus -- which in waking provides temporal scaffolding via theta rhythms -- is active in REM but operating in a mode decoupled from external temporal input, producing the characteristic atemporality of dreams.
- The product C = C_base * T_coupling is moderate-to-high but temporally scrambled, matching dream phenomenology.

#### Step 3: Deep sleep as the limiting case -- consciousness without time

**S8.** CLAIM (the core thesis): NREM Stage N3 (deepest slow-wave sleep) represents the extreme of temporal decoupling: T_coupling approaches zero while C_base remains nonzero (though possibly reduced). The result is consciousness without temporal structure.

**S9.** Why this is not equivalent to unconsciousness:
- Unconsciousness (e.g., general anesthesia) involves C_base approaching zero: the cortex's causal structure is disrupted (PCI drops to near-zero under propofol/ketamine-induced unconsciousness; Casali et al. 2013).
- In NREM N3, PCI drops but does not reach anesthesia-level lows. More importantly, the *mechanism* is different: anesthesia disrupts causal structure globally; NREM disrupts temporal coordination while preserving local causal structure during up states.

**S10.** The "consciousness without time" state would have the following properties:
- No sense of succession (no "this follows that").
- No sense of duration (no "this lasted X long").
- No temporal asymmetry (no "before/after").
- Presence of phenomenal quality (there is something it is like) but the quality is temporally undifferentiated -- a "timeless present" or, more precisely, a state with no temporal indexing at all.

**S11.** This is consistent with P4 (meditation reports): the contemplative traditions describe exactly this state -- awareness without temporal structure -- and report it as *distinctly different from unconsciousness*. Experienced meditators describe the difference between deep sleep and unconsciousness as palpable, even though neither involves temporal experience.

#### Step 4: Waking as temporal coupling, not consciousness onset

**S12.** The standard model says: waking = consciousness ON, sleep = consciousness OFF (or dimmed). The TDTS reframes this:

- Waking = consciousness + temporal coupling (C_base high, T_coupling high).
- REM = consciousness + partial temporal decoupling (C_base high, T_coupling moderate/distorted).
- NREM = consciousness + severe temporal decoupling (C_base moderate, T_coupling very low).
- Anesthesia = consciousness OFF (C_base near zero, T_coupling irrelevant).

**S13.** The subjective experience of "waking up" is not the onset of consciousness but the *re-engagement of temporal coupling*. What feels like consciousness beginning is actually time beginning -- the moment when the thalamocortical system re-establishes the temporal scaffolding that allows experience to be sequentially organized and encoded into episodic memory.

### Conclusions

**C1.** Sleep is not a spectrum from conscious to unconscious but a spectrum from temporally coupled to temporally decoupled. The variable that changes most dramatically across sleep stages is not the *presence* of consciousness but its *temporal structure*.

**C2.** Deep NREM sleep represents consciousness without temporal structure -- a "timeless" awareness that is phenomenally real but cannot be reported because report requires temporal encoding (forming a memory that is sequentially accessible). The absence of reports from deep sleep is an artifact of the measurement method (retrospective verbal report), not evidence of absence.

**C3.** Dreaming (REM) is the intermediate state where consciousness is present and temporally structured but the temporal structure is *decoupled from external time*, resulting in the characteristic distortions of dream time.

### Predictions

#### Prediction 1: Graded PCI-temporality correlation across sleep stages

**Statement:** If TDTS is correct, the drop in PCI across sleep stages should correlate more strongly with measures of temporal processing (e.g., temporal binding, temporal order judgments tested immediately upon awakening) than with measures of non-temporal conscious content (e.g., presence of visual imagery, emotional valence). Specifically:

> Subjects awakened from different NREM stages and tested with a temporal order judgment task (e.g., "which came first, the tone or the flash?") should show performance that degrades as a function of prior sleep depth MORE STEEPLY than performance on non-temporal perceptual tasks.

**Testability:** High. This is a behavioral prediction testable with serial-awakening paradigms already used in sleep labs (Siclari et al. 2017). The key innovation is adding temporal-order judgment tasks to the standard battery of post-awakening questionnaires.

#### Prediction 2: Meditation-trained subjects should show distinct NREM signatures

**Statement:** If TDTS is correct and experienced meditators have trained access to "consciousness without time" states (P4), then experienced meditators should show distinct EEG signatures during NREM N3 compared to meditation-naive controls:

> (a) Higher-than-expected PCI during N3 (reflecting preserved C_base despite temporal decoupling).
> (b) Increased gamma activity within the NREM up states (reflecting active conscious processing during the windows of cortical activation).
> (c) Increased frequency of conscious reports upon N3 awakening.
> (d) Reports from N3 that are qualitatively different from standard NREM mentation -- specifically, reports of "awareness without content" or "timeless presence" rather than fragmentary thought-like content.

**Testability:** High. Meditator vs. non-meditator sleep studies are already conducted (e.g., Ferrarelli et al. 2013 on gamma in NREM of meditators). The prediction adds specific hypotheses about the correlation between gamma-during-up-states, PCI, and subjective report quality.

**Existing partial evidence:** Ferrarelli et al. (2013) found increased parietal gamma activity during NREM in long-term meditators. This is consistent with TDTS (preserved conscious processing during temporal decoupling) but was interpreted by the authors as "enhanced cortical processing during sleep" without the temporal-decoupling framing.

#### Prediction 3: Anesthesia vs. sleep should show dissociable PCI patterns

**Statement:** If TDTS is correct, the PCI drop during NREM and the PCI drop during anesthesia should arise from *different* mechanisms, and this should be visible in the TMS-EEG response pattern:

> (a) NREM: The TMS response should show *preserved local complexity* (within-up-state processing is complex) but *reduced global integration* (the response does not propagate beyond the local region). The temporal structure of the response should be stereotyped (reflecting the slow-wave template) but the spatial structure within a single up-state should retain some differentiation.
>
> (b) Anesthesia: The TMS response should show *reduced local AND global complexity*. Both the temporal and spatial structure should be impoverished, reflecting a genuine disruption of causal structure, not merely temporal decoupling.

**Testability:** High. This is a direct extension of existing TMS-EEG protocols. Massimini's group already has data from both NREM and anesthesia conditions. The prediction is that a fine-grained analysis of the TMS-EEG response (distinguishing local vs. global complexity, and within-up-state vs. across-cycle complexity) will reveal the dissociation.

**Existing partial evidence:** Massimini et al. (2005, 2010) noted qualitative differences between NREM and anesthesia TMS responses but did not formally decompose them into local vs. global complexity. Pigorini et al. (2015) showed that NREM TMS responses include an initial complex burst followed by a stereotyped slow wave, which is exactly the pattern TDTS predicts (preserved local processing overridden by temporal template).

### Honesty Check

I flag the following concerns with my own argument:

1. **S2 (separability claim):** The formalization C = C_base * T_coupling is a *model*, not a derivation. I have no principled reason for multiplicative rather than additive or some other functional form. The qualitative claim (temporal structure is separable from consciousness) may be correct even if this specific formalization is wrong.

2. **S6 (absence of report =/= absence of experience):** This is methodologically dangerous. Any theory that explains away negative evidence ("they don't report consciousness because they can't, not because there isn't any") risks unfalsifiability. I have tried to address this with Prediction 3 (dissociable PCI patterns), but the concern is real.

3. **S8 (C_base nonzero in N3):** P7 shows conscious reports from NREM ~30-50% of the time, but these may come disproportionately from lighter NREM stages (N1, N2) rather than deep N3. The evidence for consciousness specifically during N3 is thinner than during N2. The strongest N3 evidence comes from the Boly et al. 2017 finding of posterior "hot zone" activation, but this could be interpreted as local dreaming within N3 rather than global timeless consciousness.

4. **S11 (meditation reports):** Relying on first-person contemplative reports as evidence for a neuroscientific framework is controversial. These reports are subject to interpretation, cultural framing, and retrospective construction. I use them as convergent evidence, not primary evidence, but some readers will object to any role for them.

5. **P5 (IIT):** IIT is itself a contested theory. If IIT is wrong about Phi being independent of temporal dynamics, the theoretical backing for S3 (separability of consciousness and temporal structure) weakens considerably. GNW (P6) would predict the opposite: that temporal broadcasting is constitutive of consciousness, making the TDTS framework incoherent.
