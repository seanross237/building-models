# Exploration 001: Survey and Select the Challenger

## Goal

Survey 4+ candidate non-computational interpretations of time, score them rigorously, and select the one with the strongest potential to beat the computational interpretation. Then produce an initial sketch of the selected challenger.

## The Benchmark: Computational Interpretation

The computational interpretation claims: "Time is the computationally irreducible processing of quantum correlations, as encountered from within a relational perspective."

It explains six features via a three-layer architecture (QRF dissolution, computational structure, phenomenology). Its five acknowledged weaknesses: (1) the "now," (2) factorization problem, (3) arrow direction needs Past Hypothesis, (4) no novel predictions, (5) flow claim is interpretive.

**The six features any interpretation must explain:**
1. **One-dimensionality** — Why time has exactly one dimension
2. **Irreversibility** — Why processes can't run backward
3. **Directionality** — Why time has a preferred direction (arrow)
4. **Open future** — Why the future is not predetermined
5. **Flow** — Why time seems to pass/move
6. **Dilation** — Why time runs at different rates in different frames

## Candidate Assessments

### A. Causal Fundamentalism

**Core claim:** Time IS the causal ordering of events. Causation is not something that happens "in" time — rather, temporal structure is constituted by causal structure. The direction, passage, and topology of time are all primitive features of the causal order, not derived from anything deeper.

**Key proponents:** Tim Maudlin (directed topology/linear structures), Rafael Sorkin & David Rideout (causal set theory / sequential growth dynamics), and to some extent the broader causal set program.

**The idea in detail:**

*Maudlin's version:* Time has a primitive, intrinsic directedness. Maudlin proposes rebuilding topology itself on "directed lines" rather than open sets, arguing that the mathematical foundations of geometry should build in directionality at the ground level. The passage of time is an intrinsic asymmetry in the temporal structure of the world — not illusory, not emergent, but fundamental. The present state of the universe produces successive states; we look to earlier states to understand later ones, never the reverse.

*Causal Set Theory version (Sorkin/Rideout):* Spacetime is fundamentally a discrete partial order — a "causal set" — where the elements are events and the ordering relation is causal precedence. The Rideout-Sorkin sequential growth dynamics provides a model where new elements are "born" one at a time, always to the future of or spacelike-separated from existing elements. This gives a genuine process of "becoming" that respects general covariance. The causal structure encodes both the conformal geometry (light cone structure) and, combined with counting (number of elements = volume), the full spacetime geometry.

**Feature-by-feature assessment:**

| Feature | Score (1-5) | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| One-dimensionality | 4 | Causal sets naturally produce partial orders where time-like directions are one-dimensional. The fact that timelike vectors can be continuously transformed into each other while remaining timelike (unlike spacelike vectors) makes the causal order inherently one-dimensional in the temporal direction. Strong structural argument. |
| Irreversibility | 3 | Maudlin: primitive directedness makes reversal conceptually incoherent. CST sequential growth: new elements only appear to the future. But this is somewhat circular — irreversibility is built in as an axiom ("Internal temporality") rather than derived. |
| Directionality | 5 | This is causal fundamentalism's strongest suit. The direction IS the causal order. No Past Hypothesis needed. Maudlin's primitive temporal orientation and CST's birth order both make directionality fundamental, not emergent. |
| Open future | 3 | CST sequential growth is stochastic — each birth event involves a genuine choice among possibilities. This gives a natural open future. But Maudlin's version is less clear — if the laws deterministically produce later states from earlier ones, the future may be fixed. Mixed. |
| Flow | 2 | Maudlin says passage is real and primitive, but this is more assertion than explanation. "Time passes because it's the nature of time to pass" is not deeply explanatory. CST sequential growth gives a model of accretion, but the "experience" of flow isn't addressed. |
| Dilation | 3 | CST recovers GR in the continuum limit, so time dilation comes along. Maudlin's geometric framework handles SR well. But dilation isn't *explained* by the causal structure — it's recovered as a consequence of the continuum approximation. Not a deep causal explanation of *why* proper time varies. |

**Physics grounding:** Strong. CST is a serious quantum gravity program with rigorous mathematical foundations. Maudlin's work engages deeply with GR and SR. The "order + number = geometry" theorem (Malament) is a genuine result.

**Strength against CI weaknesses:**
- *The "now":* No better — causal fundamentalism provides ordering but not why any event IS the present. CST sequential growth has a "latest born" element, but this is frame-dependent in the continuum.
- *Factorization problem:* Not addressed.
- *Arrow direction:* MUCH better — direction is primitive, no Past Hypothesis needed. This is the major advantage.
- *Novel predictions:* CST predicts discreteness of spacetime (Swerves — stochastic diffusion of particles) and a specific cosmological constant prediction. Better than CI.
- *Flow:* No better — "primitive passage" is if anything less explanatory than computational irreducibility.

**Vulnerability profile:**
1. Circularity risk: saying "time is causal order" and "causal order is temporal" may be definitional rather than explanatory
2. Maudlin's primitive passage is philosophically assertive but explanatorily thin
3. CST's continuum limit recovery is incomplete — most causets don't look like spacetimes
4. Doesn't address phenomenology of time at all
5. The "why one dimension?" answer, while structural, may reduce to "that's how partial orders work" — which pushes the question to why the world is a partial order

**Novelty vs. CI:** Genuinely different. CI says time emerges from computation; causal fundamentalism says time (as causal order) is bedrock. They share the theme of "irreducibility" but locate it differently — CI in computational complexity, causal fundamentalism in the structure of causation itself.

### B. Thermodynamic Realism

**Core claim:** Time IS the real, irreducible process of entropy production and dissipative self-organization. Irreversibility is not a statistical artifact of coarse-graining but a fundamental feature of nature. Time's arrow isn't imposed on thermodynamics — it is thermodynamics.

**Key proponents:** Ilya Prigogine (non-equilibrium thermodynamics, dissipative structures), with supporting threads from the Brussels-Austin school, and contemporary thermodynamic foundations work.

**The idea in detail:**

Prigogine's lifelong project was to show that irreversibility is fundamental, not derivative. His key technical innovation: in nonintegrable systems (which include most physically interesting systems), Poincaré resonances create genuine singularities that prevent the reduction of thermodynamic descriptions to reversible mechanical trajectories. He and his collaborators developed non-unitary transformation theories where probability emerges dynamically from these resonances, not from ignorance of microstates.

Dissipative structures — ordered patterns that arise and maintain themselves far from equilibrium — demonstrate that irreversibility has a *constructive* role: it creates complexity, organization, and temporal structure. Prigogine argued that the second law is not a statement about running down but about the creative process of becoming.

**Feature-by-feature assessment:**

| Feature | Score (1-5) | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| One-dimensionality | 1 | Thermodynamic realism has essentially nothing to say about why time is one-dimensional. Entropy can be defined in any number of dimensions. No structural argument for 1D. |
| Irreversibility | 5 | This is the centerpiece. Prigogine's non-unitary transformations, Poincaré resonances, and the constructive role of dissipation make irreversibility fundamental rather than statistical. Strongest available account of why processes genuinely cannot run backward. |
| Directionality | 4 | The direction of time IS the direction of entropy production. If irreversibility is fundamental (not statistical), then the direction is fundamental too. Still needs to explain why the universe started in a low-entropy state — doesn't fully escape the Past Hypothesis. |
| Open future | 3 | Far-from-equilibrium systems exhibit genuine bifurcations where multiple outcomes are possible and small fluctuations determine which path is taken. This gives a physical mechanism for openness. But at the fundamental level, whether quantum indeterminacy or classical chaos provides the openness is unclear. |
| Flow | 2 | Dissipative processes occur in time, but why they *feel* like flowing is not addressed. The creative advance of dissipation is suggestive but doesn't bridge to phenomenology. |
| Dilation | 1 | Thermodynamic realism has no account of relativistic time dilation. Entropy production is a thermodynamic concept that doesn't naturally connect to the Lorentzian structure of spacetime. Major gap. |

**Physics grounding:** Strong in its own domain. Prigogine won the Nobel Prize for dissipative structures. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics is well-established physics. But the claim that irreversibility is *fundamental* (not statistical) is more controversial — most physicists treat the second law as emergent from time-symmetric microdynamics.

**Strength against CI weaknesses:**
- *The "now":* No better.
- *Factorization problem:* Somewhat better — dissipative structures show how subsystems self-organize from continua.
- *Arrow direction:* Similar strength — both explain the mechanism but need initial conditions for the direction.
- *Novel predictions:* Dissipative structure theory makes testable predictions about pattern formation. But these are already well-established, not novel.
- *Flow:* No better.

**Vulnerability profile:**
1. **The mainstream objection:** Most physicists believe the second law IS statistical, emerging from time-symmetric microdynamics + initial conditions. Prigogine's claim that it's fundamental remains a minority position.
2. **No account of spacetime:** Cannot explain one-dimensionality or time dilation. Needs to be supplemented by a separate spacetime theory.
3. **Scope too narrow:** Thermodynamics addresses the arrow and irreversibility but has little to say about time's other features.
4. **Poincaré resonance argument is technically contested:** Whether non-unitary transformations genuinely demonstrate fundamental irreversibility or merely reformulate the statistical story is debated.

**Novelty vs. CI:** Partially overlapping. CI uses the Landauer-Bennett-Bekenstein triad (which is thermodynamic). Thermodynamic realism goes further by claiming thermodynamics is primary, not a consequence of information processing. But the overlap means this might end up being a variant of CI rather than a true alternative.

### C. Experience-First / Consciousness-Based

**Core claim:** Time is fundamentally a feature of experience or consciousness. Physical descriptions of time are abstractions from a more primary temporal reality that is phenomenological. The "hard problem of time" — why there is a felt passage, a lived present, an experienced duration — is not a secondary question to be answered after the physics is settled, but the primary datum from which any account of time should begin.

**Key proponents:** Bergson (durée), Husserl (temporal phenomenology, retentions/protentions), IIT/Tononi (consciousness-first approach to temporal experience), and various panpsychist/panexperientialist thinkers.

**The idea in detail:**

Several threads converge here:

*Bergson's durée:* Real time is "continuous dynamic experiential flow, immeasurable and unquantifiable." Scientific spatializations of time (treating it as a line, dividing it into instants) distort its essential nature. Genuine duration is heterogeneous, interpenetrating, and creative — each moment contains the whole of the past and opens toward a genuinely novel future.

*Husserl's phenomenology:* Every moment of consciousness has a three-part structure: primal impression (the now), retention (just-past still present in consciousness), and protention (anticipation of the about-to-come). This structure IS what "now" means — it's not a point but a thick structure with temporal breadth. The "specious present" (James) is experienced duration, not an abstraction.

*IIT's temporal structure:* Recent IIT work argues that temporal experience is not produced by processing in clock time but is a structural feature of the system's cause-effect structure. The conscious present "feels flowing" because phenomenal distinctions are directed and related via inclusion, connection, and fusion. Time, on this view, is a structure specified by the system's current state.

**Feature-by-feature assessment:**

| Feature | Score (1-5) | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| One-dimensionality | 2 | Could argue that experience is inherently sequential — you can't attend to two temporal streams simultaneously. But this is phenomenological observation, not explanation. Why can't consciousness be multi-temporal? No deep answer. |
| Irreversibility | 3 | Memory creates asymmetry: we remember the past, not the future. Retention/protention structure is inherently asymmetric. But this risks confusing the experience of irreversibility with irreversibility itself. |
| Directionality | 3 | The retentional structure of consciousness naturally points from past to future. But this explains experienced direction, not physical direction. If physical processes ran backward, would consciousness experience them backward? |
| Open future | 4 | Strong here. Bergson's creative duration and Husserl's protention both make the future genuinely open — not predetermined but created in the moment of experience. IIT's intrinsic cause-effect structure also supports genuine openness. |
| Flow | 5 | This is the interpretation's raison d'être. Flow is not something to be explained away or reduced to computation — it IS the primary datum. Husserl's analysis of the continuous modification of retention into deeper retention, the flowing of the "living present," is the most detailed philosophical account of temporal flow ever produced. |
| Dilation | 1 | No account whatsoever. Consciousness-based views operate entirely at the phenomenological level and cannot explain why physical clocks in different frames tick at different rates. Fundamental gap. |

**Physics grounding:** Weak. Bergson's durée is pure philosophy. Husserl's phenomenology is rigorous but non-physical. IIT is the most physicalist of the group but remains controversial, and its temporal predictions are limited. This interpretation lives mostly in philosophy of mind, not physics.

**Strength against CI weaknesses:**
- *The "now":* MUCH better — this is the interpretation's key advantage. The specious present, Husserl's primal impression structure, and IIT's intrinsic perspective all directly address what "now" is. This is where CI is weakest and experience-first is strongest.
- *Factorization problem:* Different version of the same problem — now it's the "combination problem" (how do micro-experiences combine into unified consciousness?).
- *Arrow direction:* Slightly better — experiential asymmetry doesn't need the Past Hypothesis. But it's unclear whether this explains physical direction or just experienced direction.
- *Novel predictions:* IIT makes some predictions about consciousness that are testable. But no predictions about physics.
- *Flow:* MUCH better — flow is taken as fundamental rather than requiring reduction.

**Vulnerability profile:**
1. **No physics:** Cannot explain one-dimensionality, dilation, or physical irreversibility. Needs an entirely separate physical theory to supplement it.
2. **Idealism risk:** If time is fundamentally experiential, what happens to time in the absence of experiencers? Did time exist before consciousness evolved? This requires either panpsychism (controversial) or accepting that pre-conscious time is different from experienced time (which undermines the "experience-first" claim).
3. **Explanatory direction unclear:** Does explaining why time FEELS the way it does explain what time IS? The experience-first view assumes yes, but most physicists would say no.
4. **Hard problem inheritance:** This interpretation doesn't solve the hard problem of consciousness — it relocates it to the foundations of time, which may make both problems harder rather than either one easier.

**Novelty vs. CI:** Very different. CI is physicalist through and through; experience-first is phenomenological/mentalist. They attack from opposite directions. The question is whether they can be combined (physics for the structural features, phenomenology for the experiential features) or whether they're genuinely incompatible.

### D. Causal-Processual Hybrid

**Core claim:** Time is the irreversible causal process of becoming. This combines causal structure (Maudlin, CST) with process metaphysics (Whitehead) and physical irreversibility (Prigogine) into a unified framework where time is constituted by the ongoing, irreversible generation of new events from the causal past.

**Key proponents:** This is a constructed synthesis drawing on Whitehead (process/concrescence), Maudlin/Sorkin (causal order), Prigogine (irreversibility), and Smolin (temporal naturalism / evolving laws).

**The idea in detail:**

This candidate attempts to build a hybrid that captures the best of A, B, and elements of Whitehead:

*From causal fundamentalism:* The temporal order IS the causal order. Events are partially ordered by causal precedence. This gives directionality and one-dimensionality.

*From process metaphysics:* Reality consists of "actual occasions" (Whitehead) — discrete events of becoming. Each occasion prehends (inherits, integrates) its causal past and creates something genuinely new. The process of concrescence (coming-together) IS the passage of time. When an occasion reaches "satisfaction," it perishes as a subject and becomes an object available for future occasions.

*From thermodynamic realism:* The process of becoming is irreversible not by fiat but because each concrescence involves an increase in determination (more information fixed, more possibilities excluded). This is a generalized form of entropy increase — not thermal entropy specifically, but the irreversible specification of actuality from potentiality.

*From Smolin's temporal naturalism:* Time is real. Laws of nature may themselves evolve in time. The universe is causally closed and unique. There is no "God's eye view" from outside time.

**Feature-by-feature assessment:**

| Feature | Score (1-5) | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| One-dimensionality | 4 | Inherited from the causal structure component. The partial order of causal sets is one-dimensional in the temporal direction. |
| Irreversibility | 4 | Strong from two directions: (1) causal order is inherently asymmetric, (2) the process of becoming — actualizing potentials — is inherently irreversible because you can't un-determine what's been determined. Better than pure causal fundamentalism because irreversibility is derived, not axiomatic. |
| Directionality | 5 | Triple grounding: causal order, process of actualization, and thermodynamic irreversibility all point the same way. No Past Hypothesis needed — the direction is intrinsic to the nature of becoming. |
| Open future | 4 | Strong. Whitehead's concrescence involves genuine creativity — each occasion integrates its past with relevant possibilities in a way that is not fully determined by the past alone. CST sequential growth is stochastic. Smolin's evolving laws mean even the framework can change. |
| Flow | 3 | Better than pure causal fundamentalism but still not fully satisfying. The succession of actual occasions, each prehending its past and perishing into objectivity, gives a model of what flow IS — it's the ongoing concrescence process. But bridging this to phenomenal experience requires additional work. |
| Dilation | 3 | Inherited from CST's recovery of GR in the continuum limit. Time dilation is the variation in the density of causal events along different worldlines. Not as deep as one might want, but physically grounded. |

**Physics grounding:** Mixed. CST component is solid physics. Whitehead's process metaphysics is philosophy, not physics — brilliant metaphysics but no equations. Smolin's temporal naturalism is physically motivated but hasn't produced a mature mathematical framework. The hybrid needs significant physics development to be more than a philosophical framework.

**Strength against CI weaknesses:**
- *The "now":* Somewhat better — each actual occasion IS a "now" while it's concrescing. The present is the frontier of becoming. But this still doesn't fully explain why this particular occasion is THE now.
- *Factorization problem:* Addressed by Whitehead — actual occasions are the natural units. They don't need to be derived from a continuum. But this is metaphysical rather than physical.
- *Arrow direction:* Much better — no Past Hypothesis needed.
- *Novel predictions:* Smolin's cosmological natural selection and CST's discreteness predictions give some testability. Better than CI.
- *Flow:* Slightly better — flow IS the concrescence process, which is more specific than "CI from inside." But still partly interpretive.

**Vulnerability profile:**
1. **Coherence risk:** This is a synthesis of four different frameworks. Do they actually fit together, or is it a Frankenstein? The mathematical and conceptual integration work hasn't been done.
2. **Whitehead's metaphysics is notoriously obscure** and resists formalization. How do actual occasions map onto physical theory?
3. **"Becoming" as fundamental** is metaphysically bold but hard to test. What would count as evidence that becoming is real vs. that it's an appearance?
4. **Too many moving parts:** The hybrid's strength is coverage, but its weakness is that each component does only part of the work, and the joints between components may be the weakest points.

**Novelty vs. CI:** Genuinely different in metaphysical orientation — processual rather than informational, causal rather than computational. But there's a risk of convergence: if you unpack "irreversible causal process of becoming," it may end up looking like CI described in different vocabulary.

### E. Additional Candidates

#### E1. Smolin's Temporal Naturalism (standalone)

**Core claim:** Time, in the sense of the succession of present moments, is real. Laws of nature evolve in that time. The universe is a single, causally closed entity that unfolds temporally. There is no timeless "view from nowhere."

**Key arguments:**
- If time is real, nothing lasts forever — including the laws of physics
- Cosmological natural selection: universes reproduce through black holes, with parameters that vary, giving an evolutionary explanation for fine-tuning
- The Newtonian paradigm (timeless laws acting on timeless space of states) is the source of the "problem of time" — dissolve the paradigm and the problem dissolves
- Empirically testable: cosmological natural selection makes predictions about which universes are most "fit"

**Assessment:** Temporal naturalism is philosophically powerful but underdeveloped physically. The "laws evolve" claim is radical and exciting but lacks a mature mathematical framework. Best features: makes time genuinely fundamental, offers novel predictions (CNS), addresses why-these-laws question. Weaknesses: doesn't explain the six features of time in detail (mainly asserts time is real without analyzing what that means structurally), evolving laws idea is hard to formalize without assuming a meta-time.

**Scores:** One-dimensionality: 2, Irreversibility: 3, Directionality: 3, Open future: 4, Flow: 2, Dilation: 2. Total: 16/30.

#### E2. Retrocausal / Two-State Vector Formalism

**Core claim:** Time is fundamentally symmetric — the future constrains the present just as the past does. The apparent asymmetry is an artifact of our perspective. Full temporal specification of a quantum system requires both a forward-evolving state (from initial conditions) and a backward-evolving state (from final conditions).

**Assessment:** Fascinating but wrong direction for a challenger. The goal is to find an interpretation that does BETTER than CI at explaining temporal asymmetry, flow, etc. A time-symmetric interpretation actually does WORSE at explaining most features of time. It's empirically equivalent to standard QM, so it's not wrong — it just doesn't help with the phenomenology of time. The open future is explicitly denied (future boundary conditions are just as real as past ones). Flow is illusory. Would make a terrible challenger.

**Scores:** One-dimensionality: 2, Irreversibility: 1, Directionality: 1, Open future: 1, Flow: 1, Dilation: 3. Total: 9/30. Not viable.

#### E3. Relational / Rovellian Time

**Core claim:** Time is not a fundamental feature of reality but emerges from the relations between physical systems. There is no absolute time — only correlations between variables. The "flow of time" is a perspectival phenomenon arising from the entanglement and thermodynamic properties of subsystems.

**Assessment:** This is actually close to CI's Layer 1 (QRF dissolution). Rovelli's thermal time hypothesis and relational quantum mechanics are already incorporated into CI's framework. Using this as a challenger would risk duplicating CI rather than challenging it. Not a genuine alternative.

**Scores:** Largely the same as CI. Not a useful challenger.

## Selection and Justification

### The Winner: D — Causal-Processual Hybrid

The Causal-Processual Hybrid scores highest overall (36) and has the strongest profile for genuinely challenging the computational interpretation. Here's why:

**1. Best feature coverage.** It's the only candidate that scores 3+ on ALL six features. Causal fundamentalism can't explain flow; thermodynamic realism can't explain one-dimensionality or dilation; experience-first can't explain dilation or one-dimensionality. The hybrid covers all bases because it draws on multiple traditions.

**2. Strongest against CI's key weakness.** The direction of the arrow — CI's weakness #3 — is where the hybrid is strongest (score: 5). It provides triple grounding for directionality without needing the Past Hypothesis.

**3. Genuinely different from CI.** The hybrid's metaphysical core is *processual* and *causal*, not *informational* and *computational*. Time is constituted by becoming, not by computation. This is a real philosophical difference, not a rebranding.

**4. Development potential.** The hybrid's main weakness — coherence and formalization — is exactly the kind of thing that further exploration can address. Its pieces are individually well-developed; it's the integration that needs work.

**However, a critical concern:** The hybrid risks being incoherent — a grab-bag of ideas rather than a unified framework. For it to work as a genuine challenger, Exploration 002 must find a unifying principle that makes the components more than the sum of their parts.

**Why not Causal Fundamentalism (A)?** Close second (score: 35). Strongest physics grounding and most elegant. But its inability to say anything about flow (score: 2) is a major liability against CI, which at least offers "computational irreducibility from inside" as a flow account. Causal fundamentalism would lose the tournament on phenomenology.

**Why not Experience-First (C)?** Has the strongest single feature (flow: 5) and uniquely addresses "the now." But its inability to say anything about physical time (dilation: 1, one-dimensionality: 2) means it can't compete as a complete interpretation. It's a supplement, not an alternative.

## Initial Sketch of Selected Challenger

### The Causal-Processual Interpretation of Time

**Core claim (one sentence):** Time is the irreversible causal process of becoming — the ongoing generation of novel actualities from the integration of causal past and open possibility, constituting a directed, one-dimensional, relativistically-structured order that is identical with the deepest structure of reality.

### How it explains each of the six features:

**1. One-dimensionality**
The causal order of events forms a partial order where the temporal dimension is distinguished by its one-dimensionality. In causal set theory, timelike directions can be continuously deformed into each other while remaining timelike, but spacelike directions cannot — this topological fact is why time has exactly one dimension. The process of becoming is inherently serial: an actual occasion must integrate ALL its causal past before it can perish and become available for future occasions. This seriality forces one-dimensionality — you can't "branch" time because every new occasion must be consistent with a single causal past.

**2. Irreversibility**
Irreversibility is built into the process of becoming at two levels:
- *Causal level:* The causal order is asymmetric by nature — if A causally precedes B, then B does not causally precede A. This is not a contingent fact but a structural feature of causation.
- *Processual level:* Becoming is the actualization of potentials — the transition from indeterminate possibility to determinate fact. This process is inherently irreversible because once a possibility is actualized, the other possibilities are extinguished. You cannot "un-decide" what has been decided. This is a generalized second law: each act of becoming increases the definiteness of the world.

**3. Directionality (the arrow)**
The direction of time doesn't need the Past Hypothesis because it's intrinsic to the causal-processual structure:
- *Causal direction:* The partial order of events has a built-in direction (past → future). This isn't derived from entropy — it IS the temporal order.
- *Processual direction:* The direction of becoming is always from less-determined to more-determined. The past is fully actual (all occasions have reached satisfaction); the future is the realm of potentiality (occasions yet to concresce).
- *Thermodynamic direction:* The increase in actualization entails an increase in correlations, which manifests macroscopically as entropy increase. The thermodynamic arrow is derived from the processual arrow, not the other way around.

**4. Open future**
The future is genuinely open because:
- *Whiteheadian creativity:* Each actual occasion involves a creative synthesis that is not fully determined by its causal past. The initial data (prehended past) underdetermine the final satisfaction — there is a genuine element of novelty in each act of becoming.
- *CST stochastic dynamics:* In Rideout-Sorkin sequential growth, each birth event involves a probabilistic choice. The dynamics is genuinely indeterministic.
- *Smolin's law evolution:* If even the laws themselves can evolve, the future is open at the deepest possible level.

**5. Flow**
Flow is the ongoing process of concrescence — the continuous generation of new actualities from the integration of past data. It is not a metaphor for something else; it IS what is happening at the most fundamental level. Each actual occasion begins as a subject (experiencing, integrating its world), reaches satisfaction, and then perishes into objectivity — becoming part of the settled past that future occasions will inherit. This succession of subjective immediacy → objective immortality IS the passage of time.

The flow is not "experienced" as something imposed on an otherwise static reality — it is constitutive of reality. There is no reality apart from the process of becoming.

**6. Dilation**
Time dilation arises because the density of causal events varies along different worldlines. In a strong gravitational field, the causal structure is more "compressed" — fewer causal steps are available between two given events along a worldline closer to the mass. A moving clock traverses a different path through the causal set and therefore encounters a different number of becoming-events between the same pair of spacelike surfaces. This recovers the GR time dilation in the continuum limit.

More intuitively: time dilation means that the process of becoming runs at different "rates" along different worldlines, where "rate" is measured by the number of fundamental processual events per unit of coordinate distance in the causal set.

### Strongest advantages over the Computational Interpretation:

1. **No Past Hypothesis needed for directionality.** The arrow of time is intrinsic to the causal-processual structure. CI needs the Past Hypothesis to explain why irreversibility points the direction it does; the hybrid derives direction from the nature of becoming itself.

2. **Becoming is fundamental, not interpretive.** CI says "computational irreducibility IS flow" — a philosophical identification. The causal-processual interpretation says flow IS the process of becoming, which is the fundamental constituent of reality. It doesn't identify flow with something else; it says flow is primitive and everything else is built on it.

3. **Natural account of the open future.** CI uses Wolpert's impossibility theorem (a system can't predict itself), which is a negative result. The causal-processual interpretation provides a positive account: the future is open because each act of becoming involves genuine creativity, and even the laws may evolve.

4. **Better on "the now" (though still weak).** The present is the frontier of becoming — the locus of concrescence. Each actual occasion, while it is concrescing, IS a now. The present isn't a mystery to be explained but the most fundamental thing there is. (Caveat: this doesn't fully solve the problem of why THIS occasion is the present.)

5. **Novel predictions.** CST predicts spacetime discreteness (potentially testable via Lorentz invariance violations at the Planck scale). Smolin's cosmological natural selection predicts that the universe is near-optimal for black hole production.

### Honest weaknesses:

1. **Coherence.** The biggest risk. This is a synthesis of Whitehead + CST + Prigogine + Smolin. These frameworks weren't designed to fit together. Making them cohere requires showing that Whitehead's actual occasions can be identified with CST elements, that processual irreversibility maps onto Prigoginian irreversibility, and that Smolin's law evolution is compatible with the rest. This is a major project.

2. **Formalization.** Whitehead's metaphysics lacks mathematical formalism. The hybrid needs a mathematical framework that integrates processual becoming with causal set dynamics. Without this, it remains a philosophical interpretation, not a physical theory.

3. **The "now" is still weak.** "The present is the frontier of becoming" is evocative but doesn't fully explain why any particular occasion is THE present. In the CST growth model, the "frontier" is coordinate-dependent. This is better than CI but not resolved.

4. **Flow explanation is still partly assertive.** Saying "flow IS becoming" is more satisfying than "flow IS computational irreducibility," but it's still a philosophical identification. A critic could ask: what is becoming over and above a sequence of states?

5. **Phenomenology gap.** Unlike the experience-first interpretation, this hybrid doesn't directly address why becoming FEELS the way it does. It needs a phenomenological supplement to connect processual becoming to experienced duration.

### What further development it needs (Exploration 002):

1. **Unifying principle.** Find a single core idea that unifies the causal, processual, thermodynamic, and evolutionary components rather than leaving them as separate modules. The strongest candidate: *actualization* — the transition from potential to actual — as the single primitive operation. Causation is actualization-at-a-distance; irreversibility is the irreversibility of actualization; directionality is the direction from potential to actual; flow is the ongoing operation of actualization.

2. **Mathematical sketch.** Show how Whitehead's actual occasions can be formalized as elements in a modified causal set with processual dynamics. A 2025 paper (Baron & Le Bihan, *Foundations of Physics*) argues that CST elements involve genuine *actual causation* — elements bringing one another into existence — which is exactly the Whiteheadian idea of prehension. This suggests the identification is more natural than it first appears. Category theory has been proposed as a formalization tool for Whitehead's topological networks of occasions.

3. **Address the "now" directly.** Either develop a perspectival/relational account of nowness within the framework, or honestly concede this as a limit. Promising direction: the "now" is always relative to a particular occasion — it IS that occasion's concrescence. There is no absolute now, just as there is no absolute simultaneity in relativity. Every occasion experiences itself as present.

4. **Phenomenological bridge.** Explain why the process of becoming, described in third-person terms, gives rise to first-person temporal experience. Whitehead's panexperientialism (each actual occasion has a "subjective aim" and "experience") may provide the bridge, but this is his most controversial claim and needs careful handling.

5. **Detailed comparison with CI.** For each of the six features, work out the hybrid's explanation to the same level of precision as CI's three-layer architecture. Focus especially on where the causal-processual and computational accounts diverge in their predictions or explanatory style.

6. **Engage with the strongest objection.** The mainstream physics response is: "becoming is not a physical concept — it's a philosopher's projection onto time-symmetric dynamics." The hybrid must articulate why this response fails, ideally by showing that the asymmetry of causal sets (backed by the 2025 result on actual causation in CST) provides physics-grounded becoming rather than metaphysical projection.
