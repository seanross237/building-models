# Exploration 003 Summary: Causal Order as Origin of the Fakeon

## Goal
Develop and evaluate a novel concept: the fakeon prescription in QG+F arises from spacetime's causal partial order.

## What Was Tried
1. **Novelty check**: Searched Anselmi's papers on fakeons + causality, causal set theory + higher-derivative gravity, Epstein-Glaser causal perturbation theory, Sorkin-Johnston vacuum construction, and the causal structure of higher curvature gravity.
2. **Concept development**: Formulated a physical picture, a specific mechanism (causal partial order → SJ construction → Wheeler propagator for negative-residue modes), and a speculative testable prediction (dimensional reduction at the fakeon mass scale).
3. **Devil's advocate**: Attacked the concept on four fronts: classical→quantum gap, unitarity-in-disguise, inability to independently select the fakeon, and the fatal loop-level gap.

## Outcome: Partially Successful — Novel but Incomplete

The concept is **genuinely novel** — nobody has connected causal order to the fakeon prescription. The key discovery is a **stunning unexploited mathematical bridge**: the fakeon propagator equals the Wheeler propagator at tree level, and Belenchia, Benincasa & Liberati (2015) showed that causal set dynamics naturally produce the Wheeler propagator for unstable modes, excluding them from asymptotic states. This is exactly what the fakeon does.

However, the concept has **serious weaknesses**: (1) Anselmi (2020) showed the Wheeler propagator fails at loop level — the full fakeon prescription requires a more subtle procedure; (2) the classical→quantum gap (causal order is classical, fakeon is quantum) is unresolved; (3) the concept is circular in quantum gravity (using causal structure to constrain a mode that generates causal structure); (4) the relevant causal set results exist only for scalar fields, not spin-2.

## Key Takeaway
The tree-level connection **Wheeler propagator = fakeon propagator** combined with the causal set's natural production of the Wheeler propagator for unstable modes is a concrete, publishable observation that nobody has made. But it's a bridge between two programs, not a complete derivation. The loop-level completion problem is well-posed and could be the focus of future work.
