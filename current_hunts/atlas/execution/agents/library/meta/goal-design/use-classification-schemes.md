## Lesson

When asking an explorer to assess predictions, compare frameworks, or evaluate severity, provide an explicit classification scheme (e.g., DISCRIMINATING/NOVEL/INHERITED, or FATAL/SERIOUS/MODERATE/COSMETIC). Without forced categorization, explorers produce lists that sound impressive but lack honest discrimination.

## Evidence

- **strategy-001 exploration 005** — Severity ranking (for devil's advocate) produced the clearest possible output for strategic decision-making.
- **strategy-004 exploration s4-002** — Classification scheme (DISCRIMINATING/NOVEL/etc.) revealed that only 1 of 12 BH predictions was genuinely discriminating. Without it, the list of 12 "predictions" would have looked impressive.
- **strategy-004 exploration s4-005** — Classification continued to be essential for honest assessment across three domains.
- **strategy-004 exploration s4-006** — Severity ratings (FATAL/SERIOUS/MODERATE/COSMETIC) per attack plus 5-tier validation produced clean, actionable devil's advocate results.

- **yang-mills strategy-001 exploration 001** — The status classification scheme (COMPLETED / PARTIALLY COMPLETED / NOT ATTEMPTED) produced a clear two-tier gap structure that stratified difficulties and made the precise stopping point immediately visible. Classification works not just for severity/quality but also for *progress stratification*.

## Variant: Constraint Checklists

A related pattern: provide an explicit list of constraints to check proposals against (e.g., "check against: graviton propagator, Lorentz invariance, spectral dimension, BH entropy, ghost freedom"). This produces structured, point-by-point assessment that is immediately usable for strategic decisions.

- **quantum-gravity-2 strategy exploration 002** — Asking the explorer to check a framework against specific constraints produced structured, useful assessment. The constraint checklist forced systematic coverage rather than selective emphasis.

## When to apply

Any exploration that produces a list of items that need ranking: predictions, attack vectors, evidence assessments, framework comparisons, or **progress/status audits**. Also effective as **constraint checklists** when assessing proposals against known requirements. Especially critical for devil's advocate explorations and prediction extraction, where the tendency toward impressive-sounding lists is strongest. Also effective for gap analysis — asking for difficulty stratification (tractable vs. fundamental obstruction) produces cleaner strategic insight.
