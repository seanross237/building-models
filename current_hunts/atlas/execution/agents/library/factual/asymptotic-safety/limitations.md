# Limitations and Open Problems of Asymptotic Safety

The asymptotic safety program for quantum gravity faces significant technical and conceptual challenges. These are actively debated in the community.

## Technical Challenges

### 1. Truncation Dependence
All existing computations rely on truncating the infinite-dimensional theory space to a finite-dimensional subspace. There is no systematic way to quantify truncation errors. While results for the Reuter fixed point have been stable across increasingly sophisticated truncations, the extrapolation to the full theory space remains uncontrolled.

### 2. Gauge Dependence
Background field methods introduce gauge-fixing choices. Physical predictions must be gauge-independent, but demonstrating this within truncated flows is difficult. Some studies find that gauge dependence increases rather than decreases with higher-order truncations, opposite to expectations.

### 3. Unitarity
Whether asymptotically safe gravity is unitary remains unresolved. Higher-derivative terms generated by the RG flow (R^2, R_mu_nu R^mu_nu, etc.) can introduce ghost-like degrees of freedom in perturbation theory. The nonperturbative resolution of this issue is an active research area. Positive spectral function results for the dynamical graviton (Bonanno et al. 2022) are encouraging but not definitive.

### 4. Background Independence
The functional RG approach uses a background metric, raising questions about background independence -- a key requirement for any theory of quantum gravity. The "background field approximation" conflates the background field with the mean field, and overcoming this remains challenging.

### 5. Lorentzian vs. Euclidean
Most computations are performed in Euclidean signature for technical convenience. The Wick rotation to Lorentzian spacetime is nontrivial in gravity (no fixed background to define it). Recent work has begun addressing Lorentzian flows directly, but the program is still predominantly Euclidean.

## Conceptual Criticisms

### 6. Donoghue's Critique (arXiv:1911.02967)
John Donoghue argued that the running of Lambda and G found in asymptotic safety "are not realized in the real world" -- the RG running of Newton's constant is not observable in the same way as the QCD coupling. He questions whether current practice meets Weinberg's original conditions for asymptotic safety, and notes conflict with established low-energy quantum gravity calculations.

### 7. Black Hole Entropy Argument
If Bekenstein-Hawking entropy (S = A/4G) reflects microscopic degrees of freedom, the area scaling (rather than volume scaling) suggests gravity is not a local quantum field theory at short distances. This would contradict the field-theoretic foundation of asymptotic safety.

### 8. Tension with Holography / AdS-CFT
If the AdS/CFT correspondence is correct, gravity in the bulk is fundamentally dual to a boundary CFT and is not a local field theory. This is in conceptual tension with asymptotic safety, which treats gravity as a local QFT possessing a UV fixed point.

### 9. BRST Symmetry Violation
Rigorous canonical quantization arguments suggest that general covariance cannot be maintained quantum mechanically in d > 2, while path integral calculations reveal persistent gauge parameter dependence, signaling BRST symmetry violation in truncated flows.

### 10. Observables and Predictivity
Computing physical observables (S-matrix elements, correlation functions on dynamical spacetime) from the effective average action remains very difficult. Without this, direct comparison with experiment or observation is limited.

## Status Assessment

Asymptotic safety remains a viable but unproven approach to quantum gravity. The evidence for the Reuter fixed point is substantial but not conclusive. The program's greatest strength is its concrete computational framework; its greatest weakness is the difficulty of establishing results beyond truncated approximations.
