# Jacobson's Thermodynamic Derivation of Einstein's Equations (1995)

Ted Jacobson demonstrated in 1995 that Einstein's field equations can be derived as a thermodynamic equation of state, published in Physical Review Letters 75:1260-1263 (arXiv:gr-qc/9504004). This is the foundational result that inspired all subsequent emergent gravity work.

## Core Derivation

Jacobson derives the Einstein equation from the proportionality of entropy and horizon area together with the fundamental thermodynamic relation:

**delta Q = T dS**

The key idea is to demand this relation hold for all local Rindler causal horizons through each spacetime point, where:
- **delta Q** is the energy flux across a local Rindler horizon, computed from the matter stress-energy tensor
- **T** is the Unruh temperature seen by an accelerated observer just inside the horizon
- **dS** is the entropy change, proportional to the change in horizon area via the Bekenstein-Hawking relation S = A/(4 L_P^2)

## Result

This requires that gravitational lensing by matter energy distorts the causal structure of spacetime in exactly the way that the Einstein equation holds. The result is Einstein's field equations:

**R_ab - (1/2) R g_ab + Lambda g_ab = (8pi G / c^4) T_ab**

Viewed this way, the Einstein equation is an equation of state.

## Key Implication

Jacobson argued that "it may be no more appropriate to canonically quantize the Einstein equation than it would be to quantize the wave equation for sound in air." This suggests spacetime geometry is emergent rather than fundamental, and the microscopic degrees of freedom underlying spacetime may be entirely different from the metric.
