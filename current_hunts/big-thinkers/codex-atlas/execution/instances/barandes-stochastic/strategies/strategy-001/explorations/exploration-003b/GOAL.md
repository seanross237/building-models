# Exploration 003b — Novelty Search: Stochastic Representations and Consistent Histories

## Mission Context

Our prior explorations identified a potentially novel structural connection:

**Claim:** Barandes' phase freedom (multiple Theta matrices for the same transition kernel Gamma) and the realm selection problem in consistent histories (multiple consistent families for the same quantum system) may be the same mathematical structure — the non-uniqueness of projecting a quantum density matrix onto classical probability distributions.

This connection was NOT found in Barandes' papers (he never mentions consistent histories) and was NOT found in the consistent histories literature during our exploration. But we need a dedicated novelty search to confirm this is genuinely new.

## Your Task

Search the literature systematically for any existing work connecting:
1. Stochastic process representations of quantum mechanics ↔ consistent/decoherent histories
2. Phase freedom in quantum channel representations ↔ realm/framework selection in consistent histories
3. Non-uniqueness of classical descriptions of quantum systems (in any formalism)
4. Stochastic matrices and decoherence functionals

## What Counts as a Prior Discovery

The connection we're checking for has several possible levels:
1. **Direct:** Someone has explicitly connected stochastic representations (Barandes-style or Kraus-style) to consistent histories realm selection. This would be a direct prior.
2. **Indirect:** Someone has noted that Kraus decomposition non-uniqueness and consistent set non-uniqueness are both instances of the same underlying mathematical freedom. This would be an indirect prior.
3. **Adjacent:** Someone has studied the non-uniqueness of classical embeddings of quantum states without connecting it to either framework specifically. This would be relevant but not a prior.

## Success Criteria
- At least 20 papers/sources checked across both literatures
- A clear verdict: "This connection IS in the literature (cite)" or "This connection is NOT in the literature (evidence of absence)"
- If found: exact citation, what was claimed, how it compares to our formulation
- If not found: what the closest existing work is, and what specific gap our claim fills

## Failure Criteria
- Checking fewer than 10 sources
- A verdict of "maybe" or "unclear" without a strong evidence base
- Missing obvious places to look (Griffiths' textbook, Gell-Mann & Hartle's review papers)
