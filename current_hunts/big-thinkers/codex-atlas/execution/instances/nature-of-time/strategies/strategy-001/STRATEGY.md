# Strategy 001: Dialectical Construction

## Objective

Build a compelling, original interpretation of what time is by running a structured dialectical process: generate candidate theses about the nature of time, subject each to rigorous adversarial critique, and synthesize the survivors into a unified interpretation that is stronger than any individual piece.

The goal is not to survey what people have said about time. The goal is to arrive at an answer — one that takes real positions, survives logical attack, and leaves a thoughtful reader feeling like they understand something new.

## Methodology

The exploration cycle follows a three-phase dialectical structure:

### Phase 1: Thesis Generation (Explorations 1-3)

Generate three independent, bold candidate interpretations of time — each approaching from a fundamentally different angle. These should not be hedged or balanced. Each should commit to a strong claim and build the best case it can.

Suggested angle diversity (but the Strategizer should choose based on what seems most promising):
- A physics-first angle (what does physics actually tell us time is, stripped of assumptions?)
- A phenomenological angle (what does the experience of time reveal about its nature?)
- A structural/mathematical angle (is time an information-theoretic, computational, or relational structure?)

Each exploration should produce: a clearly stated thesis, the strongest argument for it, what it explains well, and what it struggles with.

### Phase 2: Adversarial Stress-Testing (Explorations 4-6)

Take the most promising theses from Phase 1 and subject them to devil's advocate attacks. Each stress-test exploration should:
- Identify the weakest points of the thesis
- Construct the strongest possible counterarguments
- Test the thesis against the hard questions: Why does time flow? Why can't we go back? What is "now"? Does time exist without observers?
- Determine what survives the attack and what collapses

The Strategizer should design these to be genuinely adversarial — the goal is to break the theses, not to confirm them. Whatever survives is worth keeping.

### Phase 3: Synthesis and Refinement (Explorations 7-10)

Take the surviving elements from Phase 2 and build a unified interpretation. This phase should:
- Exploration 7: Attempt a first synthesis — combine the strongest surviving elements into a coherent whole
- Exploration 8: Stress-test the synthesis against the hardest remaining questions
- Exploration 9: Refine based on what broke, fill gaps, strengthen weak points
- Exploration 10: Final articulation — write the interpretation in its strongest, clearest form. This should be the kind of argument that a smart person walks away from feeling like they genuinely understand something they didn't before.

### Adaptation Rules

The Strategizer should adapt within this framework:
- If all three Phase 1 theses are weak, use exploration 4 to generate a better one before stress-testing
- If the synthesis in Phase 3 reveals a fundamental flaw, go back to the surviving pieces and try a different combination
- If an unexpected insight emerges at any point, follow it — the phases are a guide, not a prison
- Always prioritize depth over breadth. One thoroughly developed idea beats five shallow ones.

## Validation Criteria

Progress should be evaluated against the Mission Validation Guide tiers:

- **Phase 1 success:** Each thesis is internally consistent (Tier 1) and logically rigorous (Tier 2)
- **Phase 2 success:** At least one thesis survives adversarial attack with its core intact. Bold positions are maintained honestly (Tier 4)
- **Phase 3 success:** The final synthesis has genuine explanatory power (Tier 3), engages with the hardest questions (Tier 5), and earns its conclusions through argument (Tier 4)

**Strategy is exhausted if:** All candidate theses collapse under stress-testing and no synthesis path is viable. In this case, document what was tried, why it failed, and what constraints on the answer were discovered — even a well-mapped failure is progress.

**Strategy succeeds if:** The final interpretation holds up under rigorous logical critique, takes real positions, explains something a non-expert would find illuminating, and doesn't dodge the hardest questions.

## Context

This is the first strategy. No prior work exists. The mission is wide open — "What is time?" with no constraints on approach beyond logical rigor and intellectual honesty. The validation guide emphasizes bold positions tested seriously over balanced surveys, and explanatory power over redescription.
