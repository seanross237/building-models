# Exploration 002: Deep Construction of the Causal-Processual Interpretation

## Goal

Build the Causal-Processual Interpretation in full: find the unifying principle, develop all six feature-explanations to full depth with physics grounding, and strengthen the weak points (flow, dilation, the "now"). The result should be a genuine competitor to the Computational Interpretation.

## 1. The Unifying Principle: Actualization

### 1.1 The Problem

Exploration 001 identified the Causal-Processual Hybrid as the strongest challenger but flagged its biggest weakness: it draws on four traditions (Causal Set Theory, Whiteheadian process metaphysics, Prigoginian thermodynamics, Smolin's temporal naturalism) that weren't designed to fit together. Without a single unifying principle, it's a philosophical Frankenstein — a grab-bag of ideas rather than a coherent interpretation.

### 1.2 The Primitive: Actualization

The unifying principle is **actualization** — the irreversible transition from potential to actual. This single operation is the primitive from which everything else in the interpretation derives.

**Definition:** Actualization is the process by which an indeterminate possibility becomes a determinate fact. It is:
- **Irreversible** — once a potential is actualized, the other possibilities are extinguished. You cannot "un-decide" what has been decided.
- **Creative** — the outcome is not fully determined by the antecedent conditions. Genuine novelty enters the world.
- **Relational** — actualization always occurs in the context of a causal past. What becomes actual is a synthesis of inherited data and novel determination.
- **Constitutive of time** — actualization IS what temporal passage consists of. There is no time apart from the ongoing process of actualization.

### 1.3 How the Four Traditions Map onto Actualization

The key insight is that each tradition describes a *different aspect* of the same primitive operation:

**Causal Set Theory (Sorkin, Rideout):** Each birth event in the sequential growth dynamics IS an actualization. A new element is "born" — it transitions from non-existence (potentiality) to existence (actuality). The 2025 paper by Baron & Le Bihan ("Causal Set Theory is (Strongly) Causal," *Foundations of Physics* 55:63) provides the critical link: using interventionist frameworks, they show that CST involves relations of *actual causation* between elements, whereby elements **bring one another into existence**. This is not mere causal connectibility (the kind found in general relativity, where spacetime points are causally related but don't generate each other). It is genuine *productive* causation — one event making another event happen. This IS actualization.

**Whiteheadian Process Metaphysics:** Concrescence IS the process of actualization. Each actual occasion begins with the prehension (inheritance) of its causal past — the many data of predecessor occasions. Through the process of concrescence, these data are integrated into a new unity guided by the occasion's subjective aim. The occasion reaches "satisfaction" — it becomes fully determinate, fully actual — and then "perishes" into objectivity, becoming part of the settled past available for future occasions to prehend. The transition from potentiality (the initial data, not yet unified) to actuality (the satisfied occasion, fully determinate) IS actualization.

**Prigoginian Thermodynamics:** Prigogine's distinction between the "physics of being" and the "physics of becoming" maps directly onto the actualization framework. The "physics of being" describes timeless, reversible dynamics where nothing genuinely becomes. The "physics of becoming" describes irreversible processes where new structures emerge — dissipative structures self-organize, bifurcations create genuinely novel states, and the arrow of time is constitutive rather than statistical. The key mechanism — Poincaré resonances creating genuine singularities in nonintegrable systems, making the dynamics inherently irreversible — describes the *physics* of why actualization is irreversible. Prigogine's non-unitary transformations formalize the transition "from groups to semigroups, from trajectories to processes" — precisely the mathematical structure of an irreversible actualization process.

**Smolin's Temporal Naturalism:** Smolin's framework adds the final layer: even the laws of nature themselves participate in actualization. "All that is real are causal processes that continually make definite facts out of previously indefinite possibilities, thereby producing novel events out of predecessor events." This IS actualization, stated as a cosmological principle. Cosmological natural selection adds that the *parameters* of the laws are themselves products of an actualization process — each universe "born" through a black hole is a new actualization event at the largest possible scale.

### 1.4 The Quantum Connection: Heisenberg's Potentia

The actualization primitive receives independent support from the foundations of quantum mechanics. Heisenberg himself interpreted the quantum state using Aristotle's notion of *potentia*: the wave function describes objective potentialities, and measurement is the transition from potential to actual. As Heisenberg wrote: "The probability function contains statements about possibilities or better tendencies ('potentia' in Aristotelian philosophy), and these statements are completely objective." The transition from possible to actual "is absolutely necessary in the interpretation of quantum theory."

This means quantum measurement IS actualization. The wave function collapse (on collapse interpretations) or decoherence (on other interpretations) is the same kind of event as a CST birth, a Whiteheadian concrescence, and a Prigoginian bifurcation: the transition from indeterminate potential to determinate fact. Kastner and Kauffman (2017) have developed this connection explicitly, proposing an "ontological duality of res extensa and res potentia" where the quantum world traffics in potentialities and the classical world consists of actualities.

### 1.5 The Coherence Test

The test from the GOAL: "If you can explain the entire interpretation in terms of ONE primitive that the four traditions are different aspects of, you've succeeded."

**Time is the ongoing process of actualization — the irreversible transition from potential to actual.**

- **Causal structure** is the network of actualization-relations: A causally precedes B means A's actuality is part of the data from which B actualizes.
- **Concrescence** is the internal structure of each actualization event: the integration of inherited data into a new determinate unity.
- **Irreversibility** is the irreversibility of actualization: you cannot un-actualize what has become actual, because the transition from indeterminate to determinate is one-way.
- **The arrow of time** is the direction of actualization: from potential to actual, from less-determined to more-determined.
- **Flow** is the ongoing occurrence of actualization events.
- **Temporal naturalism** is the claim that actualization is fundamental and universal — not a feature of subsystems within a static universe, but the very constitution of reality.

The four traditions are not separate modules bolted together. They are four perspectives on one thing: actualization as the fundamental process constituting time and reality.

## 2. Feature-by-Feature Construction

### 2.1 One-Dimensionality

**The question:** Why does time have exactly one dimension? Space has three dimensions. Why is the temporal direction singular?

**The causal-processual explanation:**

One-dimensionality arises from the seriality constraint on actualization. Each actualization event must integrate *all* of its causal past into a single determinate outcome. This forces a fundamental asymmetry between time and space:

**(a) The topological argument from CST:** In a Lorentzian manifold, timelike directions have a distinctive property: any two timelike vectors at a point can be continuously rotated into each other while remaining timelike, but spacelike vectors cannot be rotated into timelike vectors without crossing a null boundary. This means the set of timelike directions forms a single connected component (with two halves: future-directed and past-directed), while the set of spacelike directions forms a higher-dimensional connected component. The one-dimensionality of time IS the one-dimensionality of the timelike direction in causal structure. In a causal set, this manifests as: every maximal chain (the discrete analog of a timelike worldline) is linearly ordered — its elements form a total order. Antichains (the analog of spacelike hypersurfaces) can have arbitrarily many elements. One dimension vs. many.

**(b) The processual argument from actualization:** Each actualization event must be consistent with a *single* causal past. If time branched — if an occasion could have multiple independent futures that don't cohere — then the causal past for a later event would be ambiguous. The requirement of a single consistent integration of past data means that the process of actualization is inherently serial. You cannot concresce "in two directions at once" because concrescence requires the integration of ALL available data into ONE satisfaction. This forces a single temporal dimension.

**(c) The information-theoretic argument:** Actualization is the creation of a determinate fact. A fact must be a single, consistent determination. If time were multi-dimensional, there would be multiple independent "directions of determination," and facts could be determinate in one temporal direction but indeterminate in another. This would undermine the concept of actuality itself — something is either actual or it isn't.

**Physics anchor:** Malament's theorem: a bijective map between two past-and-future-distinguishing spacetimes preserving causal structure must be a conformal isomorphism. The causal structure (a partial order) encodes the conformal geometry, which in turn encodes the fact that time is one-dimensional. The one-dimensionality isn't imposed — it's inherent in what a causal partial order IS.

**Comparison with CI:** The Computational Interpretation explains one-dimensionality via the Margolus-Levitin bound: computation is sequential because there is a maximum rate of state transitions, and this sequential processing gives time its one-dimensionality. The causal-processual account is more fundamental: it doesn't reduce one-dimensionality to a processing constraint but derives it from the nature of causal-actualization structure itself. The ML bound is a *consequence* of the fact that actualization is serial, not the *reason* for it.

**Honest limit:** The topological argument explains why causal structure has one temporal dimension *given that* the world is a Lorentzian manifold. It doesn't explain why there is a Lorentzian manifold in the first place. However, CST's goal is to derive manifold structure from the partial order, not the other way around — so the explanatory direction may eventually be reversed.

**Score: 4/5** (same as E001, but now with deeper arguments)

### 2.2 Irreversibility

**The question:** Why can't processes run backward? Why is time's one-way character not merely a statistical tendency but a genuine feature of reality?

**The causal-processual explanation:**

Irreversibility has a single, fundamental source: **actualization is inherently one-way**. The transition from indeterminate to determinate cannot be reversed, because reversal would require "un-deciding" what has been decided — restoring potentiality from actuality.

**(a) The ontological argument:** Consider what reversal would mean. An actualization event takes many potentialities and collapses them into one actuality. To reverse this, you would need to take a single actuality and somehow "re-open" it into many potentialities. But the information about which potentialities existed before actualization is not contained in the actuality — the other possibilities were genuinely extinguished, not merely hidden. This is not a claim about our ignorance; it is a claim about the ontological status of what happened. The past is *settled* (fully actual); the future is *open* (merely potential). This asymmetry is constitutive, not epistemic.

**(b) The causal-structural argument:** In a causal set, the partial order relation ≺ is antisymmetric: if a ≺ b, then not b ≺ a. This is not a convention — it's built into the definition of causal precedence. Causal precedence is asymmetric because actualization is asymmetric: if a's actuality contributed to b's actualization, then b's actuality could not have contributed to a's, because b did not yet exist when a actualized. Baron & Le Bihan's (2025) analysis makes this precise: in CST's sequential growth dynamics, elements bring one another into existence through *actual causation*. If element a causally produces element b, then b comes into being partly because of a — and this productive relation is inherently asymmetric.

**(c) Prigogine's physics of becoming:** The irreversibility of actualization has a specific physical mechanism. In nonintegrable dynamical systems (which include most physically interesting systems), Poincaré resonances create genuine singularities that prevent the construction of unitary transformations diagonalizing the dynamics. This means the evolution cannot be decomposed into time-reversible trajectories — irreversibility is built into the fundamental dynamics, not imposed by coarse-graining or initial conditions. Prigogine's non-unitary transformations formalize this: the algebra of observables for such systems admits only semigroup (one-way) evolution, not group (reversible) evolution. "The inclusion of thermodynamic irreversibility through a non-unitary transformation theory leads to a deep alteration of the structure of dynamics. We are led from groups to semigroups, from trajectories to processes."

**(d) The generalized second law:** Each act of actualization increases the "definiteness" of the world — more facts are settled, more possibilities are extinguished. This is a generalized second law: the total actuality of the world is monotonically non-decreasing. The standard thermodynamic second law (entropy increase) is a *consequence* of this deeper principle: as more possibilities are actualized, the correlations between subsystems increase, and entropy (which measures the number of microstates consistent with a macrostate) increases because the macrostate description loses information about the increasingly specific actual microstate.

**Physics anchor:** Prigogine's non-unitary transformation theory, CST's antisymmetric partial order, quantum measurement irreversibility (the measurement problem). The key physics is that asymmetry is not statistical — it is built into the structure of the dynamics for nonintegrable systems.

**Comparison with CI:** CI explains irreversibility via the Landauer-Bennett-Bekenstein (LBB) triad: information erasure in a finite universe is thermodynamically irreversible. This is a mechanism-level explanation (how irreversibility works) but still requires the Past Hypothesis to explain why the mechanism runs in one direction. The causal-processual account goes deeper: the LBB mechanism is a *consequence* of the fundamental irreversibility of actualization. Information erasure is irreversible because it is a form of actualization — the transition from "information present" (potential for retrieval) to "information absent" (actuality of loss). The Past Hypothesis is not needed because the direction is intrinsic to the process.

**Honest limit:** Prigogine's claim that irreversibility is fundamental (not statistical) remains controversial. Most physicists treat the second law as emerging from time-symmetric microdynamics plus boundary conditions. The causal-processual interpretation sides with Prigogine but must acknowledge this remains a minority position.

**Score: 5/5** (upgraded from E001's 4/5 — the actualization argument gives a deeper, non-circular account)

### 2.3 Directionality (The Arrow)

**The question:** Why does time have a preferred direction? Why does the arrow point from past to future and not the other way?

**The causal-processual explanation:**

The direction of time IS the direction of actualization: from potential to actual, from indeterminate to determinate. This direction is not imposed by initial conditions (the Past Hypothesis) — it is intrinsic to the nature of the process itself.

**(a) The triple grounding:**

- **Causal direction:** The partial order of events has an inherent direction. If a ≺ b, then a is in b's causal past — a's actuality contributed to b's actualization. This ordering is not a coordinate artifact; it is the physical fact that a existed before b was generated. The direction from a to b IS the direction from earlier to later.

- **Processual direction:** The direction of concrescence is always from less-determined to more-determined. Each actualization event takes the settled past (fully actual, fully determinate) and the open future (potential, indeterminate) and creates a new determination. The past grows; the future shrinks. This asymmetry between settled past and open future defines the arrow.

- **Thermodynamic direction (derived):** The increase in actualization — more facts settled, more correlations established — manifests macroscopically as entropy increase. The thermodynamic arrow is *derived from* the processual arrow, not the other way around. This is a crucial reversal of the standard account: instead of explaining the arrow of time by explaining entropy increase, we explain entropy increase by the arrow of actualization.

**(b) Why no Past Hypothesis is needed:**

The Past Hypothesis (that the universe began in a special low-entropy state) is needed in the standard account because the microdynamics are time-symmetric — without a special initial condition, there's no reason entropy should increase toward the future rather than the past. The causal-processual interpretation dissolves this problem: the dynamics are NOT time-symmetric. Actualization is inherently directional. The universe didn't need to start in a special state for the arrow to point the right way — the arrow is built into the nature of becoming itself.

The low-entropy initial state of the universe is not an unexplained brute fact but a *consequence* of the fact that the universe began with minimal actuality — very few facts settled, very few correlations established. The initial state was "simple" because the process of actualization had barely begun. As actualization proceeds, complexity and correlations increase, which is what entropy increase measures.

**Physics anchor:** CST's sequential growth dynamics provides the physical model: elements are born one at a time, always to the future of existing elements. The birth order IS the temporal order, and it is inherently directional. Maudlin's directed topology provides the mathematical framework: topology should be built on directed lines rather than open sets, with directionality as a primitive geometric concept.

**Comparison with CI:** CI explains the arrow via the LBB engine (mechanism) plus the Past Hypothesis (direction). The mechanism without the PH gives irreversibility but not a preferred direction. The causal-processual interpretation gives both mechanism AND direction from a single source: the nature of actualization. This is a clear advantage.

**Honest limit:** The claim that the initial low-entropy state is "explained" by minimal initial actuality is suggestive but not formally derived. It's a philosophical reinterpretation of the initial conditions, not a dynamical explanation of why the universe started simple.

**Score: 5/5** (same as E001, now with deeper argument for why no Past Hypothesis is needed)

### 2.4 Open Future

**The question:** Why is the future not predetermined? Why can't we, even in principle, predict exactly what will happen?

**The causal-processual explanation:**

The future is genuinely open because **actualization is inherently creative** — the outcome of an actualization event is not fully determined by its antecedent conditions. Three layers of openness:

**(a) Processual creativity (Whitehead):** Each actualization event involves the integration of inherited data (prehensions of the causal past) into a new determinate unity. But the data underdetermine the outcome — the same causal past is compatible with multiple possible outcomes. The "subjective aim" of the occasion — its orientation toward a particular satisfaction — involves a genuine decision that is not reducible to the initial data. This is not randomness (which is determination by chance); it is creativity (which is genuine novelty emerging from the integration of the given).

**(b) Stochastic dynamics (CST):** In Rideout-Sorkin sequential growth, each birth event involves a probabilistic choice among possible positions in the partial order. The dynamics is genuinely indeterministic — given the existing causal set, the next element could be born at any of several positions. The probabilities are governed by the dynamics (discrete general covariance and Bell causality), but the specific outcome at each step is not determined.

**(c) Law evolution (Smolin):** If the laws of nature themselves can evolve — if the parameters governing physics are products of actualization events in prior universe-generations — then the future is open at the deepest possible level. Not only are individual events underdetermined; the very rules governing events are contingent products of a historical process.

**(d) The quantum connection:** Quantum indeterminacy, on the actualization reading, is not a mysterious exception to classical determinism but the *natural expression* of the open future at the microphysical level. The wave function describes the space of possibilities (potentia); measurement actualizes one possibility. The Born rule gives the probabilities but does not determine the outcome. The future is open because actualization is creative.

**Physics anchor:** Quantum indeterminacy (empirically established), CST sequential growth dynamics (mathematically rigorous), Prigogine's bifurcation theory (experimentally confirmed for far-from-equilibrium systems).

**Comparison with CI:** CI explains the open future via computational irreducibility (Wolfram) plus Wolpert's impossibility theorem (a system cannot predict itself). These are negative results — they show what can't be done. The causal-processual interpretation provides a positive account: the future is open because each actualization event is genuinely creative. The CI account explains why we can't predict the future; the causal-processual account explains why the future isn't there to be predicted.

**Honest limit:** The Whiteheadian notion of "creativity" is metaphysically rich but physically underdeveloped. How exactly does the "subjective aim" work at the microphysical level? Whitehead's panexperientialism (every actual occasion has a form of experience) provides a framework but remains controversial.

**Score: 4/5** (same as E001, but now with deeper positive account)

### 2.5 Flow (Strengthened)

**The question:** Why does time *feel* like it passes? Why do we experience a continuous movement from past through present to future?

This was scored 3/5 in E001 and identified as a key weakness. The GOAL asks us to incorporate insights from the experience-first tradition (Husserl, Bergson) to build a flow account that combines processual becoming with phenomenological precision.

**The causal-processual explanation (strengthened):**

Flow is not an illusion, not a metaphor, and not an interpretation imposed on an otherwise static reality. **Flow IS the actualization process as encountered by an actualizing subject.** This explanation operates at three levels:

**(a) Third-person level: the structure of actualization**

At the most basic level, flow is the fact that actualization events keep happening. Each event inherits the entire settled past, integrates it, produces a new determination, and then itself becomes part of the settled past for subsequent events. This continuous succession of actualization events — each one carrying forward the achievements of its predecessors and adding something new — IS temporal flow in its most general form. It happens everywhere there are actualization events, whether or not anyone experiences it.

The key structural feature: each actualization event is *internally temporal* even though it is *externally atomic*. Whitehead's concrescence has a genetic analysis — a process of integration, from initial data through intermediate phases to final satisfaction — even though the entire occasion, once complete, is a single indivisible unit in the causal order. Flow is the transition from one complete occasion to the next, but each occasion is itself a micro-process of becoming.

**(b) First-person level: Husserl's temporal phenomenology**

The experience of flow has a specific structure that Husserl analyzed with extraordinary precision: the **retention-primal impression-protention** structure.

- **Primal impression** is the vivid awareness of the *now* — the current moment of actualization as it occurs.
- **Retention** is the immediate, passive, non-objectifying awareness of the just-past — not a memory recalled, but a fading echo of what was just actual. Husserl distinguishes retention from recollection: retention is "automatic" and involuntary, a direct continuation of the experience that has just occurred. It is the phenomenological trace of the immediate causal past.
- **Protention** is the anticipatory awareness of the about-to-come — the felt openness of the imminent future, the sense that something is about to actualize.

This structure IS what flow feels like from inside. Each moment of consciousness is not a static snapshot but a thick temporal structure: it carries the immediate past (retention) and leans into the immediate future (protention) while being centered on the present moment of actualization (primal impression).

**(c) The bridge: concrescence IS the specious present**

Here is where the causal-processual interpretation provides something no other interpretation offers: **a direct identification between the metaphysical structure and the phenomenological structure.**

Whitehead's concrescence — the process by which an actual occasion integrates its causal past and arrives at satisfaction — IS what Husserl describes as the living present with its retentional-protentional structure. The mapping:

- **Prehension of the causal past** → **Retention.** The occasion inherits the data of its predecessors; the subject experiences this as the retentional trail of the just-past still present in awareness.
- **The process of integration (concrescence)** → **Primal impression.** The moment of actualization — the creative synthesis of the inherited data — IS the vivid now, the primal impression.
- **The subjective aim toward satisfaction** → **Protention.** The occasion's orientation toward its own completion, its "lure" toward a particular form of satisfaction, IS the felt anticipation of the imminent future.
- **Perishing into objectivity** → **The fading of retention.** When the occasion reaches satisfaction and becomes part of the settled past, it is no longer a subject; it becomes an object for successor occasions. This transition IS the phenomenological fading of the present into the past.

This is not a metaphor or a parallel. On the causal-processual interpretation, **concrescence and the specious present are the same thing described from two perspectives** — the metaphysical and the phenomenological. The reason time feels like it flows is that **we are the process of actualization**. Each moment of experience IS an actualization event, and the experience of flow IS what actualization is like from the inside.

**(d) Bergson's durée as the qualitative character of actualization:**

Bergson adds something Husserl doesn't: the qualitative, irreducibly *felt* character of temporal flow. Duration (durée) is not clock-time (temps) — it is the lived quality of becoming. Each moment interpenetrates the next; the past is carried forward not as a static record but as a living influence. This heterogeneous, qualitative flow IS what the process of actualization feels like — not from the outside (where it might look like a sequence of discrete events) but from the inside (where it is a continuous creative advance).

The causal-processual interpretation can incorporate Bergson's insight without embracing his anti-scientific conclusions: durée is what concrescence feels like from inside. The "spatialization of time" that Bergson criticizes (treating time as a line, dividing it into instants) IS the abstraction of coordinate time from the processual reality of actualization. Science measures temps because that's what measurement can access. But the underlying reality is durée — the qualitative flow of becoming.

**Physics anchor:** The phenomenological structure maps onto Whitehead's formal metaphysics, which in turn maps onto CST's sequential growth dynamics (where each element's birth is an actualization event inheriting its causal past). The physics of the specious present (its duration ~100-500ms) corresponds to the timescale over which neural integration processes operate — consistent with the idea that conscious experience is a macro-level concrescence integrating micro-level actualization events.

**Comparison with CI:** CI says flow IS computational irreducibility as encountered from inside a bounded subsystem. This is an elegant identification but lacks the phenomenological structure that the causal-processual account provides. CI doesn't explain WHY computational irreducibility feels like flow — it just identifies the two. The causal-processual account explains the structure of the experience: the retentional trail, the vivid present, the protentional anticipation — all of which follow from the structure of concrescence. It also explains why flow is UNDENIABLE (the self-defeating argument): denying flow requires temporal processing, which IS actualization, which IS flow.

**Honest limit:** The bridge between concrescence and phenomenology relies on Whitehead's panexperientialism — the claim that every actual occasion has some form of experience. This is his most controversial claim. Without it, the bridge from third-person actualization to first-person experience is incomplete. However, the interpretation doesn't require full panexperientialism — it only requires that complex organisms' experiences are macro-level concrescence processes built on micro-level actualization events. The panexperientialist move (every occasion has experience) is the strongest form of the argument, but a weaker form (experience emerges from sufficient complexity of actualization) also works.

**Score: 4/5** (upgraded from E001's 3/5 — the Husserlian-Whiteheadian bridge provides genuine phenomenological depth)

### 2.6 Dilation (Strengthened)

**The question:** Why does time run at different rates in different frames? Why do clocks tick slower near massive objects and at high velocities?

This was scored 3/5 in E001 and identified as needing physics grounding. The GOAL asks us to explore CST's "number of elements = volume" and how proper time relates to chain length.

**The causal-processual explanation (strengthened):**

Time dilation is the variation in the *density of actualization events* along different worldlines. A clock near a massive object ticks slower because **fewer actualization events occur along that worldline** per unit of the worldline's coordinate length.

**(a) The CST foundation: proper time as chain length**

In causal set theory, the most direct analog of proper time along a worldline is the **length of the longest chain** (maximal chain) connecting two causally related elements. A chain is a totally ordered subset — the discrete analog of a timelike curve. Myrheim first conjectured that this chain length should be directly proportional to the proper time along the corresponding timelike geodesic. This proportionality has been confirmed by sprinklings into flat spacetimes and is conjectured to hold for curved spacetimes as well.

The key physics: in a Poisson sprinkling of a causal set into a spacetime, the number of elements in a region is proportional to the spacetime volume of that region (the "hauptvermutung" or fundamental conjecture of CST). This means:

- **More elements** in a region = **more spacetime volume** = **more actualization events**
- The number of elements along a worldline between two events determines the **proper time** elapsed along that worldline
- Different worldlines between the same pair of events can traverse different numbers of elements — and this IS time dilation

**(b) Gravitational time dilation:**

Near a massive object, spacetime is curved such that timelike worldlines closer to the mass have *smaller* proper time between two events (compared to worldlines farther away). In CST terms, the causal set is "compressed" near the mass — the same coordinate distance corresponds to fewer causal set elements. Fewer elements means fewer actualization events, which means less proper time elapses. The clock ticks slower because there is literally *less becoming* along that worldline.

The intuitive picture: imagine the causal set as a network of events. Near a massive object, the network is denser in spacelike directions but sparser in timelike directions (due to the curvature of the Lorentzian metric). A worldline threading through this region encounters fewer elements — fewer occasions of actualization — than a worldline at a greater distance.

**(c) Velocity-dependent time dilation:**

For a moving clock, the explanation is analogous. A clock moving at velocity v relative to an inertial frame traces out a different path through the causal set than a stationary clock. By the geometry of Minkowski spacetime, the moving path has less proper time (this is the twin paradox). In CST terms, the moving worldline traverses fewer causal set elements between the same pair of spacelike surfaces. The moving clock experiences fewer actualization events — less becoming — and therefore registers less time.

**(d) The processual interpretation:**

Time dilation is not a mere coordinate effect or a feature of measurement. It is a *real variation in the rate of becoming*. Along a worldline closer to a massive object (or moving at high velocity), the process of actualization genuinely proceeds *more slowly* — fewer events come into being per unit of coordinate distance. This gives time dilation an ontological weight that the standard interpretation (as a feature of spacetime geometry) lacks.

In Whiteheadian terms: the number of actual occasions that concresce along a worldline varies with the gravitational potential and the velocity. A being whose life follows a worldline of lower proper time genuinely experiences *fewer* moments of becoming. They don't just measure fewer ticks — they *live* fewer actualization events.

**Physics anchor:** Malament's theorem (causal structure determines conformal geometry), the hauptvermutung (number = volume), chain length proportionality (confirmed in flat spacetime sprinklings), and the recovery of GR from CST in the continuum limit. The explanation is grounded in real physics: proper time IS chain length in the discrete case.

**Comparison with CI:** CI explains dilation via the Margolus-Levitin bound: the maximum rate of computation is proportional to the energy, and gravitational redshift reduces the effective energy. This is a valid physical argument but it explains dilation as a *processing constraint* — time runs slower because there's less energy available for computation. The causal-processual account is more fundamental: time runs slower because there ARE fewer actualization events. The ML bound is a consequence of this — the maximum rate of computation is limited because the number of physical events (actualization events) is limited.

**Honest limit:** The proportionality between chain length and proper time has been confirmed in flat spacetime sprinklings but remains conjectural for general curved spacetimes. The continuum limit of CST is not fully worked out — it's not proven that generic causal sets reproduce GR. Also, the "density of actualization events" explanation works at the discrete level but requires the CST framework to be correct; it's not a model-independent argument.

**Score: 4/5** (upgraded from E001's 3/5 — the chain-length-as-proper-time result and the processual reading of dilation as varying rate of becoming provide genuine physics grounding)

## 3. The "Now" — Perspectival Account

### 3.1 The Problem

Every interpretation of time struggles with "the now." Why does one particular moment seem to be THE present? Why this moment rather than any other? The Computational Interpretation acknowledges this as its weakest point. E001 scored the causal-processual hybrid's "now" account as "somewhat better than CI but not resolved."

### 3.2 The Causal-Processual Account

The interpretation offers a **perspectival-processual account of the now** that is genuinely stronger than CI's:

**(a) Each actualization event IS a now.**

The key insight: the "now" is not a global feature of the universe — it is a feature of each actualization event individually. While an actual occasion is concrescing (in the process of becoming), it IS the present. The present is not a timeless slice through spacetime but the locus of active actualization. Each occasion, during its process of becoming, experiences itself as NOW.

After an occasion reaches satisfaction and perishes into objectivity, it is no longer "now" — it is past. It has become part of the settled, determinate data available for future occasions. The transition from "present" (active becoming) to "past" (settled fact) is the transition from concrescence to perishing. This transition is not something that happens IN time — it IS time.

**(b) No global now, no problem.**

The relativity of simultaneity shows there is no absolute global "now" — different observers disagree about which events are simultaneous. The causal-processual interpretation embraces this: there is no single global surface of becoming. Instead, each actualization event has its own "now" — its own moment of active becoming. This is fully compatible with special relativity because the "now" is local and perspectival, not global and absolute.

This parallels the resolution of the Rietdijk-Putnam argument: if different observers disagree about what is "now," it doesn't follow that becoming is unreal. It follows that becoming is *local* — each worldline has its own proper time, its own sequence of actualization events, its own series of "nows." The partial order of the causal set is Lorentz-invariant; the "now" is always defined relative to a particular element's position in that order.

**(c) Why THIS now?**

The deepest question — why this particular moment is THE present for me — receives a unique answer from the causal-processual interpretation: **because "now" is not a property of a moment but a feature of a process.** Each actualization event IS a now while it is happening. When I ask "why is this moment the present?", the answer is: because this is the actualization event that I currently AM. My present moment is the occasion that is currently concrescing — the one that is actively integrating its past and creating its satisfaction.

The question "why this now rather than another?" is like asking "why am I this person rather than another?" — it presupposes a view from outside the process. From inside the process, there is no mystery: each actualization event is its own "now," and the one I'm asking from is the one I currently am.

**(d) Advantage over CI:**

CI can say that each computational step has an indexical "present" — the step currently being executed. But CI doesn't explain what makes a computational step THE current one, because in a block universe with computational structure, all steps exist equally. The causal-processual interpretation does better: not all actualization events exist equally. Past events are settled (fully actual but no longer becoming); future events don't yet exist (merely potential). The present event is the one that is ACTIVELY becoming. This ontological distinction between past, present, and future gives the "now" a metaphysical reality that CI lacks.

**Honest limit:** The account is relational and perspectival — there is no absolute "now" that the whole universe shares. Some might find this unsatisfying. Also, the claim that future events "don't yet exist" is metaphysically strong and incompatible with the block universe view that many physicists hold. The interpretation must own this: it is committed to an open future and a growing universe, which is a genuine metaphysical stance, not a neutral observation.

### 5.3 Remaining Weaknesses (Honest)

1. **Physics grounding is mixed.** The interpretation's strongest physics (CST) is speculative — causal set theory has not been experimentally confirmed. Its strongest philosophy (Whitehead, Husserl) is not physics at all. The weakest link is the Prigogine component: his claim that irreversibility is fundamental, not statistical, remains a minority position.

2. **Panexperientialism tension.** The strongest version of the flow argument (the concrescence-specious-present bridge) relies on Whitehead's claim that every actual occasion has a form of experience. This is the most controversial part of his philosophy. The weaker version (experience emerges from complex actualization) avoids the controversy but weakens the bridge.

3. **Mathematical formalization.** The interpretation lacks a mathematical framework that integrates processual becoming with causal set dynamics. Baron & Le Bihan's (2025) work provides a philosophical bridge (actual causation in CST ≈ Whiteheadian prehension) but not a mathematical one. Without formalization, the interpretation remains philosophical rather than physical.

4. **The "physics of becoming" position.** The interpretation is committed to the claim that becoming is physically real — not an illusion, not a perspectival artifact, not merely a feature of our description. This conflicts with the block universe view held by many physicists. While the interpretation has arguments against the block universe (the self-defeating argument, CST's sequential growth, Prigogine), it must own this as a genuine metaphysical commitment, not a proven fact.

5. **Convergence risk with CI.** Despite the genuine philosophical differences, there's a risk that the two interpretations converge when pressed. Is "actualization" really different from "computation"? If actualization is an information-processing event (integrating data, producing a determinate output), it might be a form of computation. The causal-processual interpretation would then be CI redescribed in metaphysical language. This needs to be addressed directly: the key difference is that actualization is *creative* (the outcome is not determined by the input), while computation is *mechanical* (the output is determined by the input and the algorithm). If the causal-processual interpretation is right, then the universe is not a computer — it's a creative process. This is a genuine, testable difference in metaphysical commitment.

### 5.4 The Strongest Version of the Interpretation

**The Causal-Processual Interpretation of Time (Final Formulation):**

*Time is the ongoing process of actualization — the irreversible creative transition from indeterminate possibility to determinate fact. Each actualization event inherits its causal past, integrates this data through a process of concrescence, produces a novel determination, and perishes into objectivity — becoming part of the settled past from which future events will actualize. The causal order of these events constitutes a directed, one-dimensional, relativistically-structured partial order. The experience of temporal flow IS this process of actualization as encountered from within. The "now" is not a slice of time but the locus of active becoming. The arrow of time is the direction from potential to actual. The future is genuinely open because actualization is creative. Time dilation is the variation in the density of actualization events along different worldlines.*

*This interpretation draws on causal set theory (the physics of discrete causal order), process metaphysics (the structure of actualization), non-equilibrium thermodynamics (the irreversibility of becoming), temporal naturalism (the universality of process), and temporal phenomenology (the structure of lived time).
