# Exploration 005: Build the Synthesis — The Meta-Interpretation

## Mission Context

We ran a tournament between two interpretations of time:

**Computational Interpretation (CI):** Time = computationally irreducible processing of quantum correlations. Grounded in LBB triad, ML bound, computational irreducibility, Wolpert's theorem, QRF dissolution. Strong on: physics grounding (+2.5 in tournament), irreversibility (LBB engine), open future (CI + Wolpert). Weak on: "the now" (-1.5), directionality (needs Past Hypothesis), flow (identification, not explanation).

**Causal-Processual Interpretation (CP):** Time = ongoing process of actualization (potential → actual). Grounded in CST causal structure, Whiteheadian concrescence, Prigoginian irreversibility, Husserlian temporal phenomenology. Strong on: directionality (+1.5, no Past Hypothesis), "the now" (+1.5, ontological), explanatory depth (+1.0). Weak on: physics grounding (-2.5, speculative CST + philosophical Whitehead).

**Tournament result:** Near-draw, CP 48.5 vs CI 47.5 out of 65.

**The key finding:** CI and CP CONVERGE on the same deep structure. Both say time is an irreversible, one-way, novelty-generating process that cannot be shortcut. "Computation" and "actualization" are two names for the same thing. The structural isomorphism is near-total, with one genuine fork: the ontological status of quantum potentialities.

## Your Task

**Build the strongest possible synthesis** of CI and CP — a meta-interpretation that takes the best of both and is stronger than either alone.

### What the synthesis must do:

1. **Take CI's physics.** Keep the LBB engine, the ML bound, CI + Wolpert, the QRF dissolution. These are established results that any interpretation must respect.

2. **Take CP's metaphysics.** Keep the actualization ontology (potential → actual), the perspectival "now," the intrinsic directionality. These address CI's acknowledged weaknesses.

3. **Resolve the tensions.** The two interpretations seem to conflict on:
   - Is time fundamentally *computation* (CI) or *actualization* (CP)? → The synthesis should show these are the same thing seen differently
   - Does the arrow need the Past Hypothesis (CI says yes) or is it intrinsic (CP says yes)? → The synthesis should resolve this
   - Is the "now" an unsolved problem (CI) or a perspectival feature of actualization (CP)? → The synthesis should integrate CP's answer

4. **Be more than cherry-picking.** The synthesis must have its OWN core claim — a single statement that subsumes both CI and CP. Not "CI is right about X and CP is right about Y" but "both CI and CP are partial glimpses of Z."

5. **Score higher than either parent.** If the synthesis scores ≤ 48.5 on the same 13 criteria, it has failed.

### Specific questions to answer:

1. **What is the single core claim?** What is Z — the thing that CI and CP are both partially describing?

2. **How does the synthesis explain each of the six features?** For each feature, which parent's explanation does it adopt, adapt, or transcend?

3. **How does it handle the "now"?** This was CI's weakest point and CP's biggest advantage. The synthesis must have a strong answer.

4. **How does it handle the arrow?** CI needs the Past Hypothesis; CP doesn't. What does the synthesis say?

5. **How does it handle the convergence fork?** The one genuine difference between CI and CP is the ontological status of quantum potentialities. The synthesis must take a position or dissolve the fork.

6. **What are its honest limits?** Every interpretation has them.
