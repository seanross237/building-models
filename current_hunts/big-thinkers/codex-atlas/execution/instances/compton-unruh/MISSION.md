# Mission

The ultimate goal of this mission is to produce novel, defensible results — findings rigorous enough that we could present them to the scientific community with confidence. Everything below serves that goal. A fascinating exploration that doesn't produce a citable, stress-tested result has not yet succeeded.

---

Investigate the claimed resonance between a particle's Compton frequency and the Unruh temperature at very low accelerations (a ~ cH_0), and determine whether it modifies inertia in a way that reproduces MOND-like galaxy rotation curves without dark matter.

Every massive particle oscillates at its Compton frequency (f = mc^2/h). The Unruh effect says an accelerating observer sees thermal radiation at temperature T = ha/(2pi*c*k_B). At very low accelerations — like those experienced by stars at the edges of galaxies — the Unruh thermal wavelength becomes comparable to the Compton wavelength. The hypothesis: a resonance at this scale modifies the particle's effective inertia, producing the "extra gravity" we currently attribute to dark matter.

This is distinct from McCulloch's "quantized inertia" (QI) — the physical mechanism here is a Compton-Unruh interplay in QFT-in-curved-spacetime, not an information-horizon effect. Concretely, pursue:
- Set up the QFT-in-curved-spacetime framework: scalar field coupled to an accelerating detector in a de Sitter background
- Identify the specific integral or calculation needed to determine whether a Compton-Unruh resonance exists
- Compute it — or identify exactly where the calculation becomes intractable and what tools would be needed to finish
- If a resonance exists, derive the modified inertia and compute predicted galaxy rotation curves
- Compare predictions against observed rotation curve data (Milky Way, standard benchmark galaxies like NGC 3198, NGC 2403)
- Compare against MOND predictions for the same galaxies — is this distinguishable from MOND?
- Compare against McCulloch's quantized inertia — is the mechanism genuinely different, or do they converge?
- Search for no-go theorems or inconsistencies that would rule out the resonance
- Check consistency with other observations: solar system dynamics, gravitational lensing, CMB, galaxy cluster dynamics

Even a partial result is valuable. Setting up the calculation framework, identifying the key integral, finding a no-go theorem, or confirming the mechanism is distinct from McCulloch all count as progress. But the mission succeeds when a result is strong enough to present — either "the resonance exists and predicts X" or "the resonance cannot work because Y."
