# Mission

The ultimate goal of this mission is to produce novel, defensible results — findings rigorous enough that we could present them to the scientific community with confidence. Everything below serves that goal. A fascinating exploration that doesn't produce a citable, stress-tested result has not yet succeeded.

---

Derive, verify, and stress-test the "classicality budget" — a conjectured hard limit on how much classical reality can exist in a bounded region of space.

The core idea: classical reality, in the sense of quantum Darwinism (Zurek), requires redundancy — the same information copied many times into the environment so that multiple observers can independently agree on what's real. The Bekenstein bound sets a hard cap on the total information in any bounded region. Combining these gives a trade-off: more copies per fact means more objectivity (more observers can agree) but fewer distinct facts. More facts means richer reality but less agreement between observers. Reality has a budget.

The candidate formula is R_delta <= (S_max / S_T) - 1, where R_delta is the redundancy (number of independent copies) and S_T is the information per classical fact.

Concretely, pursue:
- Rigorous derivation of the classicality budget from Bekenstein bound + quantum Darwinism axioms — identify every assumption
- Compute the budget for specific systems: a lab-scale region, a brain-sized region, near a black hole horizon, the observable universe, Planck-scale regions
- Check edge cases and limiting behavior — does it give sensible results? Does it reduce to known results in any limit?
- Thorough prior art search — has this quantity been derived before, even implicitly or under a different name? Check Zurek's quantum Darwinism papers, Bekenstein/Bousso entropy bound literature, Brandão et al. on redundancy, holographic bounds on information
- Stress-test assumptions: does the result survive if you use Bousso's covariant entropy bound instead of Bekenstein? What about different decoherence models? Different redundancy thresholds?
- Identify the strongest objection and address it
- If the budget is real, identify an experimental scenario (even in principle) where it would manifest — what would a "classicality-limited" region look like?

The goal is to determine whether this is a genuine new physical quantity — novel enough and rigorous enough to present as a result — or a trivial repackaging of known results, or wrong. All three outcomes are valuable, but the mission succeeds when the answer is backed by airtight reasoning.
