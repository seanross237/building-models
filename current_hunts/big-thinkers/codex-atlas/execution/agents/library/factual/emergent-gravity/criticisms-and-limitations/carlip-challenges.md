# Carlip's Challenges for Emergent Gravity (2012)

Steve Carlip's paper "Challenges for Emergent Gravity" (arXiv:1207.2504, published in Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics 46:200-208, 2014) provides a systematic assessment of obstacles that any emergent gravity model must overcome to agree with the observational underpinnings of general relativity.

## Two Types of Models

Carlip distinguishes two broad categories of emergent gravity:

**Type I models**: The fundamental degrees of freedom live in some "environment" -- a medium, a lattice, a pre-existing space or spacetime. These include analogue gravity models, condensed matter approaches, and lattice models.

**Type II models**: Spacetime, the metric, and diffeomorphism invariance are present, but the dynamics of gravity is emergent. These include Jacobson's thermodynamic derivation and Verlinde's entropic gravity.

## Key Challenges

1. **Gauge Symmetry Problem**: One of the main problems is explaining how a system that does not contain gauge symmetries ab initio might develop them effectively in some regime. General relativity has diffeomorphism invariance, which must emerge from the underlying theory.

2. **No-Go Theorems**: The Weinberg-Witten theorem and Marolf's theorem are invoked as obstacles for emergent gravity. The Weinberg-Witten theorem constrains the existence of massless spin-2 particles (gravitons) in theories with certain types of stress-energy tensors.

3. **TDiff Invariance**: The emergence of full diffeomorphism invariance from restricted transverse diffeomorphism invariance remains an open problem.

4. **Thermodynamic Consistency**: Ordinary surfaces, including holographic screens, generally do not obey the first law of thermodynamics, which is one of the key assumptions of emergent gravity approaches.

5. **Fluid/Gravity Equivalence**: Finding the correct metric-fluid relation so that the fluid has physically acceptable properties while the emergent metric obeys Einstein's equations remains difficult.

## Significance

Carlip's analysis does not claim emergent gravity is impossible but identifies concrete technical hurdles that any specific proposal must address. The paper serves as a roadmap for what successful emergent gravity theories need to achieve.
