# Observational Tests of Verlinde's Emergent Gravity

Verlinde's emergent gravity framework makes specific predictions about apparent dark matter effects in galaxies without invoking dark matter particles. Several observational tests have been conducted with mixed results.

## The Prediction

Verlinde predicts that dark energy interacts with matter to create an additional gravitational effect that mimics dark matter. The excess gravity (described as an apparent dark matter density) can be estimated from the baryonic mass distribution and the Hubble parameter alone, with no free parameters.

## Test 1: Weak Gravitational Lensing (Brouwer et al. 2017)

Published in Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society 466(3):2547-2559 (arXiv:1612.03034).
- **Data**: ~180 sq deg overlap of Kilo-Degree Survey (KiDS) with Galaxy And Mass Assembly (GAMA) spectroscopic survey
- **Sample**: 33,613 isolated central galaxies in four stellar mass bins
- **Result**: The prediction from emergent gravity, despite requiring no free parameters, is in good agreement with the observed galaxy-galaxy lensing profiles in all four stellar mass bins
- **Caveat**: Authors emphasized this was "only a first step" requiring further theoretical and observational development

## Test 2: McGaugh Radial Acceleration Relation (2016)

Stacy McGaugh's analysis of 153 galaxies revealed a universal law linking observed rotation speeds to visible matter distribution, matching MOND predictions (which Verlinde's theory recovers) without requiring dark matter.

## Test 3: Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxies (Pardo 2017)

Published in MNRAS 477(1):1285.
- **Sample**: 8 classical dwarf spheroidal satellites of the Milky Way (Fornax, Sculptor, Carina, Draco, Leo I, Leo II, Sextans, Ursa Minor)
- **Result**: Verlinde's theory showed 100% deviation from MOND inside the galaxies (vs only 30% outside). Some galaxies required unrealistically high stellar mass-to-light ratios (e.g., Draco: 13.08 solar units vs expected 1-3)
- **Conclusion**: Verlinde's theory struggles with dwarf galaxies, particularly the most massive systems

## Outstanding Challenge

Verlinde's theory has not yet made predictions for the cosmic microwave background (CMB) temperature anisotropy pattern, which standard Lambda-CDM with particle dark matter correctly predicts in exquisite detail.
