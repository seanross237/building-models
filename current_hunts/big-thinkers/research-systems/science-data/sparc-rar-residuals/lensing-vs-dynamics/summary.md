# Lensing vs. Dynamics in MOND / No-Dark-Matter Scenarios

Short answer: **for galaxies, current weak-lensing evidence is broadly consistent with photons and matter feeling the same extra gravity; for galaxy clusters, MOND still needs additional unseen mass or extra fields.**

## Bottom line

The most direct recent result is Mistele et al. (2024), *Radial acceleration relation of galaxies with joint kinematic and weak-lensing data*, which built a **joint kinematic + weak-lensing radial acceleration relation (RAR)** and found that the weak-lensing RAR **smoothly continues** the galaxy-rotation-curve RAR by about **2.5 dex in acceleration**. That is exactly the empirical pattern you would expect if the same effective gravitational field governs both stars/gas and photons. On galaxy scales, this supports **modified gravity / metric modification** and disfavors pure **modified inertia** or any model in which only matter gets the extra force.

That conclusion is strengthened by Mistele et al. (2024) finding that weak lensing implies **flat circular velocities out to nearly 1 Mpc** and a baryonic Tully-Fisher relation consistent with the kinematic one. In other words, lensing does not reveal some clearly different gravity law than dynamics.

## What MOND itself predicts

Strictly speaking, the original MOND formula is a **nonrelativistic** law for dynamics, so by itself it does **not** fully determine gravitational lensing. To predict light bending you need a **relativistic completion**. In Bekenstein’s 2004 TeVeS construction, light deflection in the weak-field limit is governed by the **same physical potential** that controls matter dynamics. That means a relativistic MOND theory of the TeVeS type predicts, at least in the quasi-static weak-field regime, that **lensing should track dynamics**, not decouple from it.

So the clean logic is:

- **Modified inertia**: matter can deviate from Newton/GR while photons still follow the ordinary metric. This generically risks a lensing/dynamics mismatch.
- **Relativistic modified gravity**: the metric itself is modified, so photons and matter should stay tied together.

The newer galaxy weak-lensing results favor the second picture.

## What the data show on galaxy scales

There are two stages in the literature.

First, Brouwer et al. (2021) used KiDS-1000 and found a weak-lensing RAR that agreed reasonably well with modified-gravity expectations overall, but they also reported a strong difference between early- and late-type galaxies at fixed stellar mass. That looked awkward for simple MOND-like universality.

Second, Mistele et al. (2024) revisited the problem with two key changes: a new deprojection method and a more careful matching of stellar and gas masses plus a stricter isolation cut. Their conclusion is much cleaner: **early- and late-type galaxies lie on the same joint RAR**, and the weak-lensing RAR is a continuation of the dynamical one. Their companion 2024 paper, *Indefinitely Flat Circular Velocities and the Baryonic Tully-Fisher Relation from Weak Lensing*, also finds behavior consistent with the same baryonic scaling laws known from rotation curves, and the 2024 review *The empirical laws of galaxy dynamics: from gas kinematics to weak lensing* summarizes the same picture.

So the present best reading is:

- **Galaxy lensing mass and galaxy dynamical mass are consistent at the relation level.**
- There is **no strong evidence** that photons feel only part of the extra gravity.
- The effective lensing/dynamics ratio on galaxy scales is therefore **consistent with unity within current uncertainties**, though the papers generally compare relations and stacked populations rather than deriving one universal single-number ratio for identical galaxies.

An important cross-check is Mistele, McGaugh, and Hossenfelder (2023) on **superfluid dark matter**. That model can imitate MOND in dynamics but not in light bending in the same way. They found it to be **incompatible with the weak-lensing observations**. That is exactly the pattern you would expect if the data prefer a true metric modification rather than a matter-only response.

## Does this vary by galaxy type or mass?

The 2021 KiDS analysis suggested a morphology dependence, but the 2024 reanalysis argues that this was driven largely by sample-definition and baryonic-mass-estimation issues. With stricter isolation and consistent baryonic accounting, **both early- and late-type galaxies appear to follow the same joint RAR**. I did not find a strong current result showing a robust lensing/dynamics mismatch that varies systematically with galaxy mass once those controls are imposed.

So the current answer is: **not convincingly on galaxy scales**.

## Clusters are still the problem

Galaxy clusters remain the major caveat. The long-running MOND review by Famaey and McGaugh (2012) already summarized the basic issue: when MOND is applied to clusters, the inferred dynamical mass still exceeds the observed baryonic mass by roughly a **factor of 2 to 3** overall, with larger discrepancies toward cluster centers.

Recent lensing work does not remove that problem. Kelleher and Lelli (2024), *Galaxy clusters in Milgromian dynamics: Missing matter, hydrostatic bias, and the external field effect*, find missing-to-visible mass ratios of about **1-5** at 200-300 kpc in relaxed clusters and **0.4-1.1** at 2-3 Mpc, while Famaey et al. (2024) use CLASH strong+weak lensing and find a residual MOND missing-mass component with an inner missing-to-hot-gas density ratio of order **10**. Mistele et al. (2025), *Mass models of galaxy clusters from a non-parametric weak-lensing reconstruction*, similarly find that clusters **systematically deviate** from the galaxy BTFR and RAR unless one adds an additional positive baryonic component; they explicitly say the cluster offset depends strongly on the poorly constrained outer gas profile.

So the current cluster-level statement is:

- **No-dark-matter MOND is still not enough for clusters.**
- You need extra baryons, massive neutrinos, dark fields from the relativistic theory, or some other additional component.
- This is not naturally explained by a simple “photons feel less” story; instead it looks like a genuine residual mass problem in clusters.

## Final assessment

1. **Do photons and matter feel the same extra gravity in galaxies?**  
   Best current answer: **yes, approximately**, within present weak-lensing uncertainties.

2. **What does that imply?**  
   It favors **modified gravity / metric modification** over pure **modified inertia**.

3. **Is there a measured mismatch ratio?**  
   Not as a clean universal number across identical galaxies; the strongest result is that the **lensing RAR and dynamical RAR match at the population level**, so any systematic ratio offset is currently small.

4. **Does it differ by galaxy type?**  
   The latest careful reanalysis says **not convincingly** once isolation and baryonic masses are handled consistently.

5. **What about clusters?**  
   **Clusters still fail** in simple MOND-without-extra-mass. The residual discrepancy is still at roughly the traditional **factor-of-few** level in total mass, and recent lensing work confirms a substantial central missing component.
