# Mission

Prove the Riemann Hypothesis — or make concrete, defensible progress toward a proof.

The Riemann Hypothesis states that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have real part equal to 1/2. It has been open for over 160 years and is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems. Our ultimate goal is a proof. We may not get there in one mission, but every strategy should be oriented toward that goal — not just surveying the landscape, but actively working toward closing the gap.

The most promising avenue is the spectral approach: the conjecture that the non-trivial zeros encode the spectrum of an undiscovered operator or physical system. This connects number theory to random matrix theory, quantum chaos, and operator theory. But do not treat this as the only path — if a different approach looks more promising, pursue it.

Concretely, pursue one or more of:
- A novel connection between known results from different domains (number theory, random matrix theory, quantum chaos, operator theory) that hasn't been explicitly identified and that advances a proof strategy
- A computational result — numerical evidence for a specific spectral operator candidate, a pattern in zeta zero statistics that hasn't been published, or a computation that narrows the space of possible proofs
- A new constraint on what a hypothetical "Riemann operator" must look like, derived by combining existing results
- An overlooked proof technique or combination of approaches from the literature that merits investigation
- A partial result that provably eliminates a class of counterexamples or tightens known zero-free regions

Explorers have full shell access and should compute rather than speculate. When papers provide equations, extract them and run them. Prioritize concrete, verifiable results over philosophical arguments. A computed number is worth more than a plausibility argument.

Math explorers can also use Lean 4 + Mathlib for formal verification. When a partial result or lemma can be formalized, do it — a compiled Lean proof is the strongest possible evidence. When formalization fails, report exactly where the proof breaks and what's missing. The gap analysis is often more valuable than the proof attempt itself.
