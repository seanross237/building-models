# Mission

The ultimate goal of this mission is to produce novel, defensible results — findings rigorous enough that we could present them to the scientific community with confidence. Everything below serves that goal. A fascinating exploration that doesn't produce a citable, stress-tested result has not yet succeeded.

---

Investigate whether Jacob Barandes' reformulation of quantum mechanics as "indivisible stochastic dynamics" reveals structure, predictions, or constraints beyond what standard quantum mechanics provides — or whether it is purely a notational reformulation with no new physical content.

Background: Barandes (Harvard) argues that quantum mechanics can be reformulated entirely in terms of stochastic processes — specifically "indivisible" (non-Markovian in a precise sense) stochastic dynamics on configuration space. The claim is that the Hilbert space formalism, wavefunctions, and the measurement postulate are not fundamental — they are derived structures that emerge from a more basic stochastic theory. The reformulation is claimed to be mathematically equivalent to standard QM within its current domain.

Reformulations have a track record of revealing hidden structure. The amplituhedron reformulates scattering amplitudes geometrically and — while equivalent to Feynman diagrams within N=4 SYM — reveals UV finiteness selection, EFT positivity bounds, and hidden zeros connecting different theories. Lagrangian mechanics is equivalent to Newtonian mechanics but naturally extends to general relativity. The question is whether Barandes' stochastic reformulation does something similar.

Concretely, pursue:

- Understand the mathematical framework precisely: what are "indivisible stochastic processes"? How does the Hilbert space structure emerge from them? What is the exact equivalence theorem and what are its scope conditions?
- Identify where the reformulation might diverge from standard QM: does it suggest natural extensions to regimes where standard QM is ambiguous (quantum gravity, the measurement problem, the Born rule, quantum-to-classical transition)?
- Test whether the stochastic lens makes anything newly tractable: are there computations, derivations, or physical insights that are easier or more natural in the stochastic formulation?
- Compare rigorously to SED (Stochastic Electrodynamics): SED also tried to ground quantum mechanics in stochastic processes and failed decisively for nonlinear systems. Is Barandes' approach structurally different? Why doesn't it hit the same wall? (Our SED mission showed the failure is due to the ω³ spectral density feedback — does Barandes avoid this?)
- Investigate the measurement problem: Barandes claims the measurement postulate is derived, not assumed. Is this genuinely new, or equivalent to decoherence-based accounts? Does it resolve or merely reframe the problem?
- Search for a concrete observable or regime where the stochastic formulation makes a prediction that standard QM cannot easily reach — or prove that no such prediction exists within the framework's scope
- Examine the relationship to other reformulation programs: consistent histories (Griffiths/Omnes), decoherent histories (Gell-Mann/Hartle), Nelson stochastic mechanics, 't Hooft's deterministic QM. How does Barandes relate to each? Is it genuinely distinct?

Key papers: Barandes arXiv:2302.10778 (2023), arXiv:2309.03085 (2023), and any subsequent publications. Also check responses, critiques, and discussions in the community.

Even a partial result is valuable. Establishing the exact scope of equivalence, finding where the stochastic lens adds insight, identifying a concrete limitation, or connecting Barandes to our prior Atlas findings (thermal time, classicality budget, SED) all count as progress. But the mission succeeds when a result is strong enough to present — either "the stochastic reformulation reveals X that standard QM cannot" or "it is provably equivalent with no new physical content because Y."
