# The Causal Set Approach to Quantum Gravity: Comprehensive Review

## Overview

Sumati Surya's 2019 review article in Living Reviews in Relativity (78 pages, 22 figures) provides the most comprehensive survey of causal set theory as an approach to quantum gravity. It establishes the foundational principles:

- The spacetime continuum is replaced by "locally finite posets or causal sets."
- The partial order represents a "proto-causality relation" that corresponds to spacetime causality in the continuum limit.
- Local finiteness encodes "an intrinsic discreteness" such that finite volume regions contain only finitely many causal set elements.
- "The assumption of fundamental discreteness does not violate local Lorentz invariance in the continuum approximation."
- The combination of discreteness and Lorentz invariance produces a "characteristic non-locality" that distinguishes CST from other discrete approaches to quantum gravity.

## Founding of the Program

Causal set theory was proposed by Bombelli, Lee, Meyer, and Sorkin (BLMS) in their 1987 paper "Space-time as a causal set" (Physical Review Letters 59:521). Rafael Sorkin, emeritus professor at Syracuse University and senior researcher at the Perimeter Institute, initiated and continues to be the main proponent of the program. He coined the slogan "Order + Number = Geometry."

## The Program's Unique Features

What distinguishes CST from other quantum gravity approaches:

1. It is the only approach that successfully combines fundamental discreteness with Lorentz invariance.
2. It produced the only successful advance prediction from any quantum gravity theory (the cosmological constant).
3. The discrete structure is fundamentally random (Poisson-distributed), not regular.
4. Non-locality is a feature, not a bug -- it is the necessary consequence of combining discreteness with Lorentz symmetry.

## Current State of Research

According to Nature Research Intelligence, the field has seen significant growth in publications, with approximately 48-51 papers per year in recent years. Four major research fronts as of 2024 include: interacting QFT on causal sets, order-type growth patterns, nonlocal spacetime defects, and black hole thermodynamics.
