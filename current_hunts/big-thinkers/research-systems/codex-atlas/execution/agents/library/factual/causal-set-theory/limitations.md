# Limitations and Open Problems in Causal Set Theory

## 1. The Entropy Problem: Non-Manifoldlike Dominance

The most serious structural challenge: the vast majority of causal sets look nothing like continuum spacetimes. If you randomly select a large causal set, you will overwhelmingly likely obtain a Kleitman-Rothschild (KR) order -- a pathological "three-layer" structure with arbitrarily large spatial extent but only three moments of time.

KR orders dominate the counting of finite partial orders: in the limit of large N, non-KR orders have measure zero. Even if KR orders are removed, the next-most-common structures are similarly non-manifoldlike two-layer, four-layer, and five-layer sets, forming an "extended hierarchy" of problematic structures. Manifoldlike causal sets appear to be a vanishingly small fraction of all causal sets.

For the theory to work, the dynamics (path integral) must strongly suppress these non-manifoldlike configurations. There is recent evidence that the Benincasa-Dowker action does provide some suppression, but the remaining unsuppressed non-manifoldlike causal sets are not yet well understood.

## 2. Deriving the Action from First Principles

The Benincasa-Dowker-Glaser (BDG) action was obtained by discretizing the Einstein-Hilbert action of a smooth manifold. But if causal sets are truly fundamental (not derived from a continuum), the action should be derivable from purely order-theoretic principles without reference to any continuum limit. No such derivation currently exists.

## 3. Identifying Manifoldlike Causal Sets

There is no known efficient algorithm to determine whether a given causal set is "manifoldlike" (i.e., faithfully embeddable in some Lorentzian manifold). This creates a circularity problem: the theory claims to derive geometry from causal sets, but checking whether a causal set has geometric content requires external geometric knowledge.

## 4. Quantum Dynamics

Only classical stochastic dynamics (CSG models) have been fully developed. Constructing a consistent quantum dynamics -- a "quantum sequential growth" process with complex amplitudes -- remains the central open problem. Key difficulties include:

- Defining a quantum measure (decoherence functional) compatible with discrete general covariance.
- The tension between causality conditions and quantum interference.
- The absence of a Hilbert space structure in the natural formulation.

## 5. Emergent Locality

The BDG action retains "some memory" of locality properties from the continuum, but how spatial locality emerges from the purely causal (and inherently non-local) discrete structure is poorly understood. The characteristic non-locality imposed by Lorentz invariance (no invariant nearest-neighbor relation) makes this particularly challenging.

## 6. Contact with Observation

Making robust, testable predictions beyond the cosmological constant remains difficult. Proposed phenomenological effects include:

- Swerving of particles (diffusion in momentum space) -- but the predicted magnitude is extremely small.
- Modified dispersion relations -- but CST's Lorentz invariance means these are expected to be absent, unlike in some competing approaches.
- Signatures in the CMB -- under active investigation but not yet decisive.

## 7. Black Hole Entropy

While there are partial results on computing horizon entropy in CST, a complete and satisfactory derivation of the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy S = A/(4*l_P^2) from causal set principles has not been achieved.

## 8. The Continuum Limit

It remains unclear whether a well-defined continuum limit of CST exists and, if so, whether it reproduces general relativity coupled to quantum fields. The relationship between the discrete dynamics and the Einstein field equations in a suitable limit is not fully established.
