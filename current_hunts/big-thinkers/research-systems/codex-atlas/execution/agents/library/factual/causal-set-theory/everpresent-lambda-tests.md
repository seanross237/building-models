# Observational Tests of Everpresent Lambda

## The Model

The Everpresent Lambda model, developed by Ahmed, Dodelson, Greene, and Sorkin (2004), predicts that the cosmological constant is not a fixed value but fluctuates stochastically over cosmic history. In this framework, Lambda "fluctuates between positive and negative values with an average of zero," with a standard deviation at each epoch comparable to the ambient energy density.

## Zwane, Afshordi, and Sorkin (2017) Tests

Zwane, Afshordi, and Sorkin tested Everpresent Lambda against multiple cosmological datasets using Monte Carlo Markov chains:

- **Current cosmological observations**: The model "can fit the current cosmological observations as well as the LCDM model does."
- **Low redshift Hubble constant**: Resolves tensions with low-redshift H_0 measurements.
- **Baryonic Acoustic Oscillations**: Improves agreement with BAO data in the Lyman-alpha forest at z ~ 2-3.
- **Ultramassive black holes**: Does not significantly address their formation at high redshift.
- **Lithium problem**: Fails to resolve the Lithium abundance discrepancy from Big Bang nucleosynthesis.

The authors conclude that high-redshift dark energy measurements will provide critical tests.

## Das, Nasiri, and Yazdi (2023) Tests

A more recent study by Das, Nasiri, and Yazdi confronted Everpresent Lambda with Supernova Ia (SN Ia) and Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) data:

- "For a small fraction of seeds Model 1 is capable of producing realizations that fit SN Ia data better than LCDM."
- Successful realizations showed "relatively small fluctuations at low redshifts (z < 1.5), which do not closely track the matter density."
- The model "struggles to improve on LCDM at describing the CMB data."
- However, "some variation (especially prior to recombination) is possible and in fact can lead to improvement over LCDM and reduce the Hubble tension."

## Significance

Everpresent Lambda is the only dark energy model derived from first principles of a quantum gravity program (causal set theory). While it does not yet outperform LCDM across all datasets, its ability to address the Hubble tension and the cosmological coincidence problem makes it a compelling alternative worth further investigation.
