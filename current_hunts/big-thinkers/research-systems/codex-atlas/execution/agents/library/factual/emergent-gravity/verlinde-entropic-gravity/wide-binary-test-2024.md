# Testing Verlinde's Emergent Gravity with Wide Binary Stars (2024)

A 2024 study published in Physics of the Dark Universe tested Verlinde's emergent gravity against the low-acceleration gravitational anomaly observed in wide binary star systems.

## The Wide Binary Anomaly

Recent binary orbit analysis (by Chae, and independently by Hernandez) shows the breakdown of Newtonian gravity in the weak gravity regime. A gravitational anomaly parameter between the observed acceleration and the Newtonian prediction was measured from a main sample of 26,615 wide binaries within 200 parsecs, with deviations in the same direction representing a 10-sigma significance.

## Why Wide Binaries Matter

Unlike galaxy rotation curves, the wide binary result cannot be explained by dark matter because the scale concerned is much smaller than the galactic scale, which makes the gravitational acceleration contribution of dark matter completely negligible. This provides a clean test environment for modified gravity theories.

## Connection to Verlinde's Theory

The authors propose explaining the binary orbit anomaly using Verlinde's emergent gravity, with a correction to the original theory. Verlinde's theory predicts that gravitational behavior deviates from Newton's law at accelerations below the Hubble acceleration scale a_0 = cH_0 (approximately 1.2 x 10^{-10} m/s^2). This is the same critical acceleration threshold that appears in MOND.

## Theoretical Background

Verlinde first suggested "entropic gravity" by demonstrating that Newton's law of universal gravitation and Einstein's field equations can be derived by assuming that entanglement entropy follows the area law. He later proposed "emergent gravity" and showed that the Tully-Fisher relation can be explained by the assumption that there is a volume term in entanglement entropy due to the accelerated expansion of our universe.
