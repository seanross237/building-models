# The Cosmological Constant Prediction

The most celebrated result of causal set theory is Sorkin's 1990 prediction for the order of magnitude of the cosmological constant, made roughly a decade before supernova observations confirmed the accelerating expansion of the universe. This remains the only successful advance prediction from any quantum gravity program.

## The Argument

The prediction combines two ingredients:

1. **Unimodular gravity**: In this formulation of general relativity, the cosmological constant Lambda is not a fixed parameter but a fluctuating quantity conjugate to spacetime 4-volume V. The uncertainty relation gives Delta(Lambda) * Delta(V) ~ 1 (in Planck units).

2. **Causal set discreteness**: The spacetime volume V is proportional to the number N of causet elements (V ~ N * l_P^4). Poisson fluctuations in the element count give Delta(N) ~ sqrt(N).

Combining these: the cosmological constant fluctuates with magnitude

    Lambda ~ 1/sqrt(V) ~ 1/sqrt(N)

where V is the spacetime 4-volume of the observable universe and N ~ 10^240 is the number of Planck-volume elements.

## The Prediction's Value

Plugging in the spacetime volume of the observable universe gives:

    Lambda ~ H_0^2 ~ 10^{-122} (in Planck units)

This matches the observed value to within an order of magnitude -- precisely the magnitude that was later confirmed by the 1998 supernova observations of accelerating expansion.

## The "Everpresent Lambda" Model

Ahmed, Dodelson, Greene, and Sorkin (2004) developed this into a concrete phenomenological model called "Everpresent Lambda." Key features:

- Lambda is not a constant but fluctuates stochastically over cosmic time.
- Its mean value is zero, but its standard deviation at each epoch is of order the ambient energy density (the critical density).
- This naturally addresses the "coincidence problem" -- why dark energy density happens to be comparable to the matter density today. In this model, Lambda is always comparable to the ambient density by construction.
- The cosmological "constant" can be both positive and negative at different epochs.

## Status and Tests

The Everpresent Lambda model has been tested against cosmological data (CMB, supernovae, large-scale structure). While broadly consistent, the model remains incomplete -- it does not yet fully specify the stochastic dynamics governing Lambda's fluctuations. Ongoing work by Zwane, Afshordi, and Sorkin has refined the phenomenological predictions.
