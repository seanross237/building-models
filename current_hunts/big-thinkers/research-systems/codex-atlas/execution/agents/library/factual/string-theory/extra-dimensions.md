# Extra Dimensions in String Theory

String theory requires spacetime to have a specific number of dimensions for quantum mechanical consistency. Bosonic string theory requires 26 dimensions; superstring theory requires 10. These are called the "critical dimensions."

## Why These Specific Numbers

The requirement arises from cancellation of the conformal (Weyl) anomaly on the string worldsheet. The central charge of the bc ghost system in the bosonic string is -26, so the matter sector must contribute central charge +26, which equals the spacetime dimension d. For the superstring, the matter central charge (including fermionic contributions) is 3d/2, and the superconformal ghost system has central charge -15, requiring d = 10.

Equivalently, one can derive the critical dimension from modular invariance of the one-loop (torus) partition function. The partition function involves the Dedekind eta function eta(tau) = q^(1/24) * product(1 - q^k), and consistency under modular transformations tau -> -1/tau requires (D-2)/24 to be an integer, where D-2 counts the transverse dimensions. This gives D-2 = 24 (bosonic) or D-2 = 8 (superstring).

## Dimensional Requirements by Theory

- **Superstring Theory**: Requires 10 spacetime dimensions (9 spatial + 1 time)
- **M-Theory**: Requires 11 spacetime dimensions (10 spatial + 1 time)

Since we observe only 4 dimensions, 6 (or 7) extra dimensions must be hidden.

## How Compactification Works

The framework uses a Kaluza-Klein approach, splitting spacetime into two pieces: M = M4 x M6, where M4 represents familiar four-dimensional spacetime and M6 describes six compactified extra dimensions. The extra dimensions are "curled up" at every point in our observable 4D space, forming a tiny compact manifold too small to detect with current experimental instruments.

## Compactification on Calabi-Yau Manifolds

In 1985, Candelas, Horowitz, Strominger, and Witten showed that compactification on Calabi-Yau manifolds (Ricci-flat Kahler manifolds with SU(3) holonomy) preserves exactly N=1 supersymmetry in 4 dimensions and maintains the chirality of fermions, both desirable for phenomenology.

## Physics from Geometry

The specific geometry chosen for compactification determines what particles and forces appear in the resulting four-dimensional theory. Different shapes and topologies of the compact space lead to different gauge groups, particle spectra, and coupling constants.

## The Compactification Challenge

A 6-dimensional Calabi-Yau manifold has around 500 topological parameters (cycles), and quantized fluxes through these cycles create an enormous number of possible vacuum configurations -- this is the origin of the string theory landscape problem. There are tens of thousands of known Calabi-Yau threefolds, and string theory currently provides no mechanism to select which one describes our universe.
