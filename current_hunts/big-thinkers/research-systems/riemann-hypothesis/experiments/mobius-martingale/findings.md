## Executive Summary

We investigated whether the Mertens function M(x) = Sum_{n<=x} mu(n) can be shown to satisfy |M(x)| = O(x^{1/2+epsilon}) -- equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis -- by exploiting the near-independence of prime divisibility events and martingale central limit theory.

**Bottom line:** The prime-by-prime filtration gives a genuine martingale decomposition that captures real structure. The heuristic variance calculation produces exactly the right answer. However, this approach has been substantially explored by Harper (2013, 2020) and Gorodetsky-Wong (2024) for *random* multiplicative functions, and the fundamental gap between random and deterministic multiplicative functions remains the unsolved problem. The martingale decomposition of the *actual* M(x) becomes a purely algebraic identity (inclusion-exclusion by primes), and bounding it requires controlling quantities equivalent to RH itself. The approach is **circular for proving RH** but provides **genuine structural insight** and might be combinable with other tools (Matomaki-Radziwill, Sarnak's program) to make partial progress.

We have re-derived known structure, not found a new path. The novel contribution, if any, is the explicit numerical demonstration of the circularity and the quantification of why the deterministic-to-random gap is the true barrier.

## 1. The Prime-by-Prime Filtration

### 1.1 Setup

Define the filtration F_y = sigma(alpha(p), p <= y), generated by values of a multiplicative function at primes up to y. This is exactly the filtration used by:
- Harper (2013, J. Reine Angew. Math.; 2020, Forum of Math Pi)
- Gorodetsky-Wong (2024, arXiv:2405.20311)

For a random multiplicative function f (Rademacher: f(p) i.i.d. in {-1,+1}, or Steinhaus: f(p) uniform on unit circle), the partial sum S(x) = Sum_{n<=x} f(n) decomposes as:

    S(x) = Sum_{p<=x} Z_{x,p}

where Z_{x,p} is the contribution from integers whose largest prime factor is p. The differences Z_{x,p} form a martingale difference sequence with respect to F_p.

### 1.2 For the Actual Mobius Function

We process primes p_1 < p_2 < ... in order. Start with partial_mu(n) = 1 for all n. At each prime p:
- For multiples of p (not p^2): flip sign (multiply by -1)
- For multiples of p^2: set to zero

The "innovation" D_p = change in Sum partial_mu(n) when processing prime p.

After processing all primes, partial_mu(n) = mu(n) exactly. This gives:

    M(x) = x + Sum_p D_p

This is an exact algebraic identity. It is the **inclusion-exclusion principle applied prime-by-prime**, equivalent to the Euler product for 1/zeta(s).

### 1.3 Verification

Our numerical experiments confirm this decomposition is exact:

| x | M(x) | Sum D_p | x + Sum D_p |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | 2 | -998 | 2 |
| 10,000 | -23 | -10,023 | -23 |
| 100,000 | -48 | -100,048 | -48 |

## 2. Numerical Results: The Mertens Function

### 2.1 Basic Statistics (up to 10^7)

| x | M(x) | M(x)/sqrt(x) |
|---|---|---|
| 10 | -1 | -0.3162 |
| 100 | 1 | 0.1000 |
| 1,000 | 2 | 0.0632 |
| 10,000 | -23 | -0.2300 |
| 100,000 | -48 | -0.1518 |
| 1,000,000 | 212 | 0.2120 |
| 10,000,000 | 1,037 | 0.3279 |

**Key observation:** |M(x)|/sqrt(x) stays well below 0.5 for x <= 10^7. The ratio is decreasing in the range [10^4, 10^5] and fluctuating elsewhere, consistent with slow growth. Odlyzko-te Riele (1985) proved the Mertens conjecture |M(x)| < sqrt(x) is FALSE (counterexample exists around x ~ e^{1.59*10^40}), but for moderate x the bound holds easily.

### 2.2 Distribution of mu(n)

For n <= 10^7:
- mu(n) = +1: 3,040,164 (30.40%)
- mu(n) = 0: 3,920,709 (39.21%)
- mu(n) = -1: 3,039,127 (30.39%)

Squarefree density: 0.607929 (theory: 6/pi^2 = 0.607927). Exact match.

All autocorrelations are tiny (< 0.004 in absolute value). The z-scores are mostly within normal fluctuation range, with the exception of lag 60 (z = -3.06), which reflects the divisibility structure (60 = 2^2 * 3 * 5 has many small prime factors).

### 2.4 Conditional Independence Tests

All consistent with zero. The Mobius function really does "look random" at this scale.

## 3. The Second Moment: A Critical Discovery

### 3.1 The Random Model's Second Moment

For a Rademacher random multiplicative function f:

    E[S(x)^2] = Sum_{d squarefree, d<=x} floor(sqrt(x/d))^2

Our numerical computation reveals:

| x | E[S(x)^2] | E[S(x)^2]/x | E[S(x)^2]/(x*ln x) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 100 | 310 | 3.10 | 0.673 |
| 1,000 | 4,344 | 4.34 | 0.629 |
| 10,000 | 57,296 | 5.73 | 0.622 |
| 100,000 | 714,036 | 7.14 | 0.620 |
| 1,000,000 | 8,540,120 | 8.54 | 0.618 |

**Critical finding:** E[S(x)^2] grows like (6/pi^2) * x * ln(x), NOT like x.

The ratio E[S(x)^2]/(x ln x) converges to approximately 0.618, which is close to 6/pi^2 = 0.608. (The slight discrepancy comes from lower-order terms.)

### 3.2 Growth Rate Confirmation

Ratio of E[S^2]/x at consecutive powers of 10:

| Transition | Actual ratio | Predicted if ~ln(x) |
|---|---|---|
| 10^2 -> 10^3 | 1.401 | 1.500 |
| 10^3 -> 10^4 | 1.319 | 1.333 |
| 10^4 -> 10^5 | 1.246 | 1.250 |
| 10^5 -> 10^6 | 1.196 | 1.200 |

The match to ln(x) growth is excellent and improves with x.

### 3.3 Reconciliation with Harper

This seems paradoxical: E[S(x)^2] ~ x*ln(x) suggests std(S) ~ sqrt(x*ln x), but Harper showed E|S(x)| ~ sqrt(x)/(log log x)^{1/4}.

**Resolution:** The distribution of S(x) is **heavy-tailed**:
- The typical value is |S(x)| ~ sqrt(x) / (log log x)^{1/4} (Harper's result)
- But rare large values |S(x)| ~ sqrt(x * ln x) dominate the second moment
- This is analogous to a log-normal distribution: the mean is much larger than the median

This heavy-tailed behavior is a consequence of the multiplicative chaos structure that Harper identifies.

### 3.4 Implication for RH

Even sqrt(x * ln x) = O(x^{1/2+epsilon}) for any epsilon > 0. So the random model actually predicts RH should hold -- even the "large deviations" of random multiplicative functions are consistent with the RH bound.

## 4. The Innovation Structure

### 4.1 Innovation Sizes

When processing primes smallest-first for x = 100,000:

| Prime p | Innovation D_p | |D_p|/sqrt(x) |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | -75,000 | 237.2 |
| 3 | -13,890 | 43.9 |
| 5 | -4,003 | 12.7 |
| 7 | -1,884 | 6.0 |
| 11 | -912 | 2.9 |
| 37 | +8 | 0.025 |
| 127 | -121 | 0.38 |
| 547 | +10 | 0.032 |
| 50,021+ | 0 | 0.000 |

**Key pattern:** D_p ~ -x/p for the first few primes (the inclusion-exclusion terms), then fluctuates at much smaller scale. Primes p > sqrt(x) contribute innovations of exactly +/-2 or 0 (since they affect at most floor(x/p) numbers, and for p > x/2, exactly 1 number).

### 4.2 Energy (Sum of D_p^2)

| x | Sum D_p^2 | Sum D_p^2 / x | M(x)^2 | M(x)^2 / Sum D_p^2 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | 586,290 | 586 | 4 | 0.0000068 |
| 10,000 | 58,428,021 | 5,843 | 529 | 0.0000091 |
| 100,000 | 5,839,929,074 | 58,399 | 2,304 | 0.00000039 |

**The ratio M(x)^2 / Sum D_p^2 is essentially zero.** This means the innovations exhibit massive cancellation. The energy is dominated by D_2 ~ -3x/4 (which alone contributes ~(3x/4)^2 = 9x^2/16 to the energy). The actual M(x) is six orders of magnitude smaller than what the innovation energy would suggest.

### 4.3 The Circularity Made Explicit

The Burkholder-Davis-Gundy (BDG) inequality for martingales gives:
    |Sum D_p| <= C * sqrt(Sum D_p^2)

But Sum D_p^2 ~ x^2 (dominated by D_2), so BDG gives |M(x) - x| = O(x), which is trivially true and says nothing about sqrt(x) bounds.

**The massive cancellation between the D_p is precisely the content of RH.** Proving it from the innovation structure alone is circular.

### 4.4 Reverse Ordering

Processing primes from largest to smallest dramatically reduces energy:

| x | Forward energy | Reverse energy | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | 586,290 | 124,694 | 4.7x |
| 10,000 | 58,428,021 | 7,145,431 | 8.2x |

The reverse ordering has smaller energy because large primes have small innovations (each large prime p affects only floor(x/p) numbers, contributing |D_p| <= 2*floor(x/p)). The small primes are processed last, when most of the cancellation has already occurred.

This suggests the reverse ordering might give better analytic bounds, but the energy is still O(x^2) due to the small primes.

## 5. The Deterministic vs. Random Gap: The True Barrier

### 5.1 The Problem in One Sentence

For random f, the innovations D_p are random variables with known distributions, and probabilistic tools (BDG, martingale CLT) give sharp bounds. For the deterministic mu, the innovations D_p are fixed numbers, and there are no probabilistic tools to apply.

### 5.2 Detailed Breakdown

**Random multiplicative functions (Harper's world):**
- f(p) are i.i.d. random variables
- D_p are random with mean 0 and controllable variance
- Martingale CLT applies: S(x) has a (non-Gaussian) limiting distribution
- E|S(x)| ~ sqrt(x) / (log log x)^{1/4}
- The sum concentrates, and we get bounds

**Deterministic mu (our world):**
- mu(p) = -1 for all primes p (completely determined)
- D_p is a fixed number, not random
- M(x) = x + Sum D_p is an algebraic identity
- No probabilistic concentration to exploit
- The "randomness" comes from the pattern of primes, which is what we're trying to understand

### 5.3 What "mu Looks Random" Means

Our numerical tests confirm mu is indistinguishable from random at moderate scales:
- Autocorrelations are within noise
- Conditional expectations at multiples of primes are consistent with zero
- Distribution closely matches the predicted 30.4% / 39.2% / 30.4% split

But "looking random" at computable scales doesn't prove asymptotic bounds. The Mertens conjecture (|M(x)| < sqrt(x)) looks true for all computable x but is provably false.

### 6.2 Our Approach's Place in the Literature

The prime-by-prime filtration F_y = sigma(f(p), p <= y) that we propose is **exactly** the filtration used by Harper (2013, 2020) and Gorodetsky-Wong (2024). The martingale decomposition over primes is a standard tool in the study of random multiplicative functions.

Our contribution is not the filtration itself but:
1. The explicit numerical computation of innovations D_p for the actual mu
2. The quantitative demonstration of circularity (M(x)^2 / Sum D_p^2 ~ 10^{-6})
3. The observation that the energy depends on prime ordering (forward vs reverse)
4. The precise identification of where the approach breaks for deterministic functions

### 6.3 The Mussardo-LeClair Paper

Mussardo and LeClair (2021) took a similar probabilistic approach, performing statistical tests on the Mobius function and arguing that M(x) exhibits Brownian motion behavior. Their work is published in J. Stat. Mech. (a physics journal). It is **heuristic, not rigorous** -- they acknowledge RH violation remains "not strictly speaking impossible." Their eighteen randomness tests at 99% confidence levels are numerical evidence, not proof. The mathematical community does not consider this a resolution of RH.

## 7. Three Possible Non-Circular Paths

Despite the circularity for a direct proof, there are conceivable ways to make partial progress:

### 7.1 Path A: "mu is Generic" (Sarnak's Program)

Show that mu(n) is orthogonal to all bounded sequences from zero-entropy dynamical systems. This is Sarnak's conjecture. If fully proved, it would imply strong pseudorandomness properties of mu that could potentially be bootstrapped to RH-like bounds.

**Status:** Proved for many specific dynamical systems (horocycle flows, nilsequences, some interval exchange transformations). Not proved in full generality.

### 7.2 Path B: Short Interval Bootstrapping (Matomaki-Radziwill Extension)

Matomaki-Radziwill (2016) showed that Sum_{x<n<=x+h} mu(n) = o(h) for almost all x, with h growing arbitrarily slowly. This gives cancellation in *most* short intervals. If one could show cancellation in *all* short intervals (removing the "almost all"), one could bootstrap to a bound on M(x).

**Status:** The "almost all" to "all" gap is enormous. The exceptional intervals are where the action is, and controlling them likely requires RH or something equivalent.

### 7.3 Path C: Pretentious Distance Bounds (Granville-Soundararajan Framework)

Show that mu cannot "pretend" to be a character chi_{-4}(n) = (n|4) or any other structured function for too long. The pretentious distance D(mu, chi; x) controls how well mu correlates with chi. If mu has large pretentious distance from all characters, its partial sums must cancel.

**Status:** This is an active research area. The pretentious approach has yielded improvements to the Polya-Vinogradov inequality and related results.

## 8. What We Learned

### 8.1 The Variance Puzzle

The initial heuristic suggested Var(M(x)) ~ (6/pi^2) * x ~ 0.608x, giving std(M(x)) ~ 0.78*sqrt(x). This was wrong in a subtle way.

**Corrected calculation:** E[S(x)^2] for random multiplicative functions grows like (6/pi^2) * x * ln(x), not like x. The extra ln(x) factor comes from the pairwise correlations E[f(n)f(m)] being nonzero when n and m share the same squarefree kernel.

However, the *typical* value of |S(x)| is sqrt(x)/(log log x)^{1/4} (Harper), which is slightly below sqrt(x). The second moment is inflated by heavy tails.

For the actual M(x), the empirical values M(x)^2/x are:
- x = 10^3: 0.004
- x = 10^4: 0.053
- x = 10^5: 0.023
- x = 10^6: 0.045
- x = 10^7: 0.108

These are **far below** the random model prediction of ~ln(x), suggesting the actual mu cancels **better** than random, not worse. This is consistent with RH.

### 8.2 The Innovation Structure Reveals Inclusion-Exclusion

The prime-by-prime decomposition M(x) = x + Sum D_p is just inclusion-exclusion in disguise. Processing prime p = 2 removes half the numbers (setting even-indexed mu to -1), which is the Legendre sieve. Processing p = 3 refines further. This is equivalent to the Selberg sieve methodology.

The key insight: the first few primes (2, 3, 5, 7) contribute ~99.7% of the innovation energy. The tail of small innovations from large primes is negligible. This means any approach to bounding M(x) via this decomposition must primarily control the interaction between the small primes -- which is exactly what the zero-free region of zeta(s) controls.

### 8.3 The max|M(x)|/sqrt(x) Bound is Decreasing

Over intervals [10^{k-1}, 10^k], the maximum of |M(x)|/sqrt(x) is:

| Interval | max|M(x)|/sqrt(x) |
|---|---|
| [10, 100] | 0.832 |
| [100, 1000] | 0.567 |
| [1000, 10^4] | 0.472 |
| [10^4, 10^5] | 0.463 |
| [10^5, 10^6] | 0.438 |
| [10^6, 10^7] | 0.418 |

This slowly decreasing trend is consistent with the conjecture that M(x)/sqrt(x) has a limiting distribution (Ng's conjecture), though the convergence is logarithmically slow.

## 9. Honest Final Assessment

### What we did NOT achieve:
- We did not find a new proof strategy for RH.
- We did not find a gap in the literature that could be exploited.
- The martingale approach to RH is circular for deterministic mu.

### What we DID achieve:
- Confirmed that the prime filtration martingale decomposition is well-known (Harper, Gorodetsky-Wong) and fully explored for random multiplicative functions.
- Quantified precisely WHY the approach is circular for the deterministic case: the innovation energy Sum D_p^2 is O(x^2), making BDG useless, and reducing this energy requires knowing M(x) is small, which is RH.
- Verified numerically that mu "looks random" at all testable scales -- autocorrelations indistinguishable from noise, conditional expectations at prime multiples consistent with zero.
- Discovered that E[S(x)^2] ~ (6/pi^2) * x * ln(x) for the random model (not ~x as initially supposed), and that the heavy-tailed distribution reconciles this with Harper's E|S| ~ sqrt(x)/(log log x)^{1/4}.
- Identified that the innovation energy depends on prime ordering (reverse gives ~5-8x lower energy), suggesting analytic approaches might benefit from processing large primes first.

### The fundamental obstacle:
The Riemann Hypothesis is a statement about the deep structure of the primes. Probabilistic methods can tell us what to *expect* (and the expectation matches RH), but cannot bridge the gap from "expected behavior" to "guaranteed behavior" without new structural input about the distribution of primes. This input would itself be equivalent to (or harder than) RH.
