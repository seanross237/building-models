# Verlinde's Emergent Gravity and the Dark Universe (2016)

In his 2016 paper "Emergent Gravity and the Dark Universe" (arXiv:1611.02269), Erik Verlinde extended his 2010 entropic gravity framework to address dark matter and dark energy, arguing that the observed excess gravity in galaxies and clusters arises from an emergent gravitational effect without need for dark matter particles.

## Core Mechanism

Verlinde proposes that spacetime and gravity emerge from entanglement structures in an underlying microscopic theory. The positive dark energy leads to a thermal volume law contribution to entropy that overtakes the area law precisely at the cosmological horizon. Matter displaces this dark energy entropy, producing an additional "elastic" gravitational response beyond standard Newtonian gravity.

## Key Prediction

The apparent dark matter effect depends on three parameters:
- Baryonic mass M_b
- Newton's constant G
- Hubble acceleration scale: **a_0 = cH_0**

where H_0 is the Hubble parameter. This acceleration scale a_0 approximately equals 1.2 x 10^{-10} m/s^2, which is the same critical acceleration that appears in Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND).

## MOND Recovery

Verlinde's calculations rediscover the equations of MOND: at accelerations below a_0, gravity no longer follows Newton's inverse-square law but transitions to a different regime. This emerges naturally from the entropy displacement framework rather than being postulated.

## Observational Tests

**Brouwer et al. (2017)**: First test using weak gravitational lensing with KiDS survey data (180 sq deg overlap with GAMA spectroscopic survey). Measured surface mass density profiles of 33,613 isolated central galaxies in four stellar mass bins. Verlinde's prediction, requiring no free parameters, was in good agreement with observed galaxy-galaxy lensing profiles.

**Pardo (2017)**: Tested against dwarf spheroidal galaxies. Found that Verlinde's theory underpredicted maximum velocities for more massive dwarf systems. Verlinde responded that the theory "hasn't been developed to the point where it can make specific predictions about all dwarf galaxies."
