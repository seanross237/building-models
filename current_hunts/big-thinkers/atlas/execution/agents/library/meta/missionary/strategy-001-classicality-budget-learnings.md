# Strategy-001 Learnings: Classicality Budget

## What Methodology Was Prescribed

A three-phase protocol: (1) Parallel foundation — rigorous derivation, numerical computation, and prior art search running concurrently; (2) Synthesis and stress-test — attack assumptions, identify strongest objection; (3) Implications — physical meaning, experimental tests. Designed for verifying a conjectured formula: is it correct, is it novel, is it interesting?

## How Well It Worked

**Very well for phases 1-2, adequate for phase 3.**

- The parallel Phase 1 was the biggest win. Three independent explorations running simultaneously produced three orthogonal views of the same problem in the time of one. The prior art result (PARTIALLY KNOWN) informed Phase 2 design; the computation result (only constraining at Planck scale) reframed the entire problem; the derivation result (formula correct, Holevo bridge is key) was the foundation.

- Phase 2 stress-testing was effective. The strategizer made a smart decision to run the "strongest objection" and "operational entropy" explorations in parallel, producing the catch-22 identification AND the dramatic BH horizon result simultaneously. The catch-22 (budget valid where vacuous, interesting where invalid) was the deepest insight of the strategy.

- Phase 3 was where the methodology was weakest. I prescribed "implications and experimental tests" but the strategizer focused on BH implications and holographic reformulation — both valuable, but the experimental test (a core mission requirement) was never attempted. The methodology didn't enforce coverage of the experimental question.

## What I'd Do Differently

1. **Make the experimental test exploration non-optional.** I wrote it as one possible direction in Phase 3; the strategizer chose other directions instead. For mission-critical requirements, the methodology should mandate specific explorations, not suggest them.

2. **Include a "what's the most publishable finding?" phase.** The most interesting result (QD↔HQEC mapping) was discovered in the LAST exploration and wasn't developed. A methodology that asks "which of our findings has the highest value?" and then allocates 2-3 explorations to deepening it would have caught this.

3. **Budget allocation guidance.** I said "10 explorations" but didn't specify minimum allocation per phase. The strategizer wisely closed at 7/10, but Phase 3 got only 2 explorations while the experimental test (Tier 5 requirement) got zero. Prescribing "Phase 3 must include at least one experimental-test exploration" would have fixed this.

4. **Prior art search should include "verify novelty of each emergent finding."** My strategy only asked for prior art on the main formula. When surprising sub-results emerged (BH universal constants, QD↔HQEC mapping), their novelty wasn't verified because no prior art search was allocated for them. Future strategies should include a "verify novelty of strongest claims" step before closing.

## What Surprised Me

- **The strategizer's honesty was excellent.** The FINAL-REPORT's "Honest Assessment" section was genuinely honest — "modest but genuine contribution," "not a deep new result," "mathematically elementary." This prevented overclaiming and made the result more credible. The stress-test format (requiring severity ratings with at least 2 SERIOUS) seems to have set a truth-telling norm.

- **The best finding wasn't what we were looking for.** The QD↔HQEC connection (quantum Darwinism IS holographic quantum error correction) emerged from an exploration designed to resolve a technical objection (the tensor product catch-22). The most publishable result was a side effect of a stress-test fix. This suggests strategies should have "harvest surprises" checkpoints.

- **Computation revealed the problem's shape faster than reasoning.** Exploration 002 (compute the numbers) immediately showed the budget is only constraining at Planck scale — a fact that would have taken much longer to figure out through reasoning alone. Exploration 005 (operational entropy) found the BH horizon constraint. Both pivots were computation-driven. Lesson: always front-load computation.

## Generalizable Lessons

1. **For formula-verification missions, the derive-compute-search parallel is optimal.** These three tasks are independent and each constrains the others. The computation tells you where the formula matters; the prior art search tells you what's new; the derivation tells you what's rigorous. All three together in Phase 1 gives the strategizer maximum information for Phase 2 design.

2. **Mandatory vs. suggested explorations.** If the mission has specific requirements (e.g., "identify an experimental test"), the methodology should mandate an exploration for it, not leave it as one option among many. Strategizers optimize for intellectual interest, which may not align with mission completeness.

3. **The catch-22 pattern appears in fundamental physics.** When a formula depends on assumptions (tensor product structure) that break down exactly where the formula becomes interesting (Planck scale / BH horizons), this is a structural feature, not a bug. The resolution (holographic reformulation using boundary tensor product) suggests: always include a "reformulate in an alternative framework" exploration as a stress-test response.

4. **Explorer nudging is a consistent issue.** 4 of 7 explorations needed manual nudges after 3-5 minutes of 0% progress. This is a process issue that should be documented as standard operating procedure: always check explorer progress at the 3-minute mark.
