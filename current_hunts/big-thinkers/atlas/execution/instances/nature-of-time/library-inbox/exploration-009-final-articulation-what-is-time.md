# What Is Time?

---

## The Question

You already know a great deal about time. You know it flows — always forward, never back. You know it has a direction: eggs break but don't unbreak, people age but don't un-age. You know the future is open in a way the past is not. You know that near massive objects, time runs slower — a fact so precisely measured that GPS satellites must correct for it or your phone will tell you you're in the wrong city.

These are not separate facts. They are all expressions of a single underlying structure. This piece is about what that structure is.

The question "What is time?" has been asked for as long as people have been asking questions. Augustine said he knew what time was until someone asked him. Physics has been remarkably successful at *using* time — every equation in physics involves it — while remaining remarkably unable to say what it *is*. The equations describe how things change in time, but they don't explain time itself. They take it as given.

Over the past century, the question has sharpened into a genuine crisis at the foundations of physics. General relativity treats time as dynamic — it bends, stretches, and dilates. Quantum mechanics treats time as a fixed background — an external clock against which quantum states evolve. These two descriptions are incompatible, and reconciling them is the central unsolved problem in theoretical physics.

When you push into the attempt to reconcile them, something strange happens. The most straightforward approach — applying quantum mechanics to the universe as a whole — produces an equation (the Wheeler-DeWitt equation) in which time simply disappears. The equation says: the quantum state of the universe does not change. It just *is*. This is the **problem of time** in quantum gravity, and it has generated decades of debate: Is the universe really static? Is time an illusion? Or is the formalism missing something?

This piece offers an answer. Not a speculative one — an answer built from established physics, rigorously examined, with its limits honestly stated.

The short version:

> **Time is the computationally irreducible processing of quantum correlations, as encountered from within a relational perspective.**

That sentence is compressed. The rest of this piece unpacks it.

## The Wrong Debate

For most of the twentieth century, the debate about time was framed as a binary: either the universe is *really* static (the "block universe," where past, present, and future all exist equally) or the universe is *really* evolving (where becoming is fundamental and the present moment is special).

Each side had serious arguments. The block universe crowd pointed to relativity: since different observers disagree about which events are simultaneous, there cannot be a universal "now," and therefore no universal flow of time. The becoming crowd pointed to experience: the passage of time is the most immediate datum available to any conscious being, and theories that dismiss it as illusory are dismissing the most obvious fact about reality.

This debate is a dead end. Here is why.

In 2021, Philipp Höhn and collaborators proved something that dissolves it. Working within the framework of *quantum reference frames* (QRFs), they showed that for a constrained quantum system — the kind that the Wheeler-DeWitt equation describes — the "timeless" description (where nothing changes) and the "temporal" description (where a subsystem evolves relative to a clock) are **mathematically equivalent**. They are related by a well-defined physical transformation, in exactly the same way that different coordinate systems in general relativity are related by coordinate transformations.

This is not a vague philosophical claim. It is a mathematical theorem, proved for finite-dimensional constrained quantum systems. The "static" description and the "evolving" description are not competing hypotheses about reality. They are different *perspectives* on the same physics — like viewing a landscape from the north or from the south. Neither is privileged. Neither is more fundamental. Asking "is the universe *really* static or *really* evolving?" is like asking "is electromagnetism *really* an electric field or *really* a magnetic field?" The answer depends on your frame of reference, and the frame-dependence is itself part of the physics.

What does this mean for the problem of time? It dissolves it. The Wheeler-DeWitt equation — which says the universe "doesn't change" — is not telling us that time is an illusion. It is giving us one valid description, the perspective-neutral one, in which the total state satisfies a constraint. From the perspective of any internal subsystem used as a clock, the rest of the universe evolves — and this evolution is exactly the quantum mechanics we know. Both descriptions are true. The "problem" was assuming one had to be more fundamental than the other.

This clears the ground. We no longer need to fight about whether time is real. It is as real as any description from within a physical reference frame. The productive question is not *"does time exist?"* but **"why does time have the specific properties it has?"**

Why does it flow in one direction? Why can't we reverse it? Why is the future open? Why does it have one dimension when space has three? Why does it slow down near massive objects?

These questions have answers. Each one traces back to the same source: the structure of information processing in a universe with finite resources.

## The Right Question

Think of the properties of time as a list. Not a philosophical list — a physical one. These are properties that can be measured, that show up in experiments, that any theory of time must account for:

1. **One-dimensionality.** Space has three dimensions; time has one. You can move in any spatial direction, but you can only move forward in time.

2. **Irreversibility.** Broken eggs don't reassemble. Ink dropped in water doesn't un-diffuse. The microscopic laws of physics are time-symmetric (they work equally well forwards and backwards), but the world we observe is not.

3. **Directionality (the arrow).** Time doesn't just fail to reverse — it has a *direction*. The future is different from the past in a systematic way. Entropy increases. Information disperses. Structure degrades.

4. **The open future.** The past is fixed. The future is not. This isn't just a feeling — it shows up in the physics. Quantum measurements have genuinely undetermined outcomes. Chaotic systems amplify tiny differences. No amount of information about the present fully determines the future.

5. **Flow.** Time *passes*. Each moment gives way to the next. This is not just a psychological impression — it is the most basic structural feature of conscious experience.

6. **Gravitational time dilation.** Clocks near massive objects tick slower than clocks far from them. This has been measured to extraordinary precision. It is not a curiosity — it is telling us something deep about the connection between time and energy.

The standard approach in physics is to address each of these separately. Irreversibility gets a thermodynamic explanation (the second law). The arrow gets a cosmological one (the universe started in a low-entropy state). Time dilation gets a geometric one (spacetime curvature). Flow is typically dismissed as psychology. One-dimensionality is accepted as a brute fact.

The interpretation developed here takes a different approach. It traces all six properties to a single framework: **the computational structure of information processing in a finite universe.** The key ingredients are four theorems from physics and information theory — all of them established, none of them speculative. The claim is not that any one of these theorems is new. The claim is that together, they explain why time is the way it is.

## The Engine of Irreversibility

Start with the deepest question: why can't we go backwards?

The textbook answer is the second law of thermodynamics. There are vastly more disordered states than ordered ones, so systems naturally drift toward disorder. This is correct as far as it goes, but it is unsatisfying. It tells you that reversal is *unlikely* — overwhelmingly, astronomically unlikely — but not that it is *impossible*. Given enough time, the Boltzmann argument says, even a broken egg will spontaneously reassemble (Poincaré recurrence). The irreversibility is statistical, not fundamental.

There is a stronger answer. It comes from information theory, and it is built from three results that lock together like gears in an engine.

**Gear 1: Landauer's Principle.** In 1961, Rolf Landauer proved that erasing information has an irreducible physical cost. Specifically: erasing one bit of information — resetting a physical system from an unknown state to a known state — requires dissipating at least *kT* ln 2 of energy as heat, where *k* is Boltzmann's constant and *T* is the temperature. This is not an engineering limitation. It is a law of physics. It has been confirmed experimentally: first in 2012 by Bérut and colleagues using a colloidal particle in a double-well potential, and most recently in 2025 by Aimet and collaborators, who confirmed it holds even in the quantum many-body regime using ultracold atomic gases.

The key insight: erasure is a *logically irreversible* operation. It takes many possible input states (the unknown bit could be 0 or 1) and maps them to a single output state (the bit is now 0). A many-to-one mapping destroys information. Landauer showed that this logical irreversibility has an unavoidable physical consequence: energy must be dissipated. The information isn't just "lost" — it is converted into heat, spread irreversibly into the environment.

**Gear 2: Bennett's Reversibility Theorem.** In 1973, Charles Bennett proved that any computation *can* be performed reversibly — without erasing information and therefore without dissipating energy — but only if you keep a complete record of every intermediate step. Reversible computation is possible in principle. It just requires saving everything.

This sounds like it undermines Landauer. If computation can be reversible, why is the world irreversible? Because of the third gear.

**Gear 3: The Bekenstein Bound.** In 1981, Jacob Bekenstein showed that any physical system with finite energy *E* contained in a region of radius *R* can store at most a finite amount of information: *S* ≤ 2π*kRE*/ℏ*c*. This is not about the technology of storage. It is about the physics of spacetime. A region of finite size and finite energy has a maximum information capacity. The universe, for all its vastness, is finite in this sense.

Now watch what happens when these three gears mesh:

1. The universe processes information — every physical interaction is, at bottom, a computation on quantum states (this was proved by Seth Lloyd in 2002).
2. Bennett says reversible processing requires keeping complete records.
3. Bekenstein says storage capacity is finite.
4. Therefore, the universe *cannot* keep complete records of its own computation. It must erase.
5. Landauer says erasure is irreversible — it dissipates energy as heat.

**Therefore: the universe's computation is necessarily irreversible.** Not statistically, not probably, not given enough time. *Necessarily.* Any finite universe that processes information must erase information, and erasure is physically irreversible. The information that was destroyed no longer exists in any recoverable form.

This is the Landauer-Bennett-Bekenstein engine. It is the mechanism of time's irreversibility — not a statistical tendency but a structural necessity. A broken egg cannot reassemble because the information required to specify its pre-broken state has been physically destroyed, dissipated as heat into an environment whose capacity to store information is bounded by the Bekenstein limit. To reverse the process, you would need information that no longer exists anywhere in the accessible universe.

The LBB engine runs on established physics. Each gear is individually proven or experimentally confirmed. The chain of reasoning is logically valid. And it gives a different kind of answer than the standard thermodynamic account: irreversibility is not about the improbability of spontaneous order but about the *destruction of information* in a finite system.

## Why Time Has One Dimension

Space has three dimensions. You can move north-south, east-west, or up-down. Time has one. You can only move in one direction along it. This is such a basic feature of reality that it's easy to overlook how strange it is. Why one? Why not two time dimensions, or zero?

In standard physics, the one-dimensionality of time is a brute fact — the spacetime manifold has signature (−,+,+,+), one temporal and three spatial dimensions, and that's just how it is. No explanation is given.

The computational interpretation offers one.

In 1998, Norman Margolus and Lev Levitin proved a theorem that puts an absolute speed limit on computation. The **Margolus-Levitin theorem** states: any quantum system with average energy *E* (measured from its ground state) requires at least a time *t* = π*ℏ*/(2*E*) to transition to a state that is completely distinguishable from its starting state. Equivalently, the maximum number of distinguishable states a system can pass through per second is 2*E*/π*ℏ*.

This is a speed limit on change itself. Not just on how fast a car can go or a signal can propagate — on how fast any physical system can become something different. The speed limit is set by energy: more energy means faster transitions. This was experimentally confirmed in 2021 by Ness and colleagues, who directly observed the Margolus-Levitin bound in a trapped-atom system.

Now consider what this means for the structure of time.

A system that processes information — which is to say, any physical system — transitions through distinguishable states one at a time. The Margolus-Levitin bound says it can only do this at a rate proportional to its energy. At any given energy, there is a maximum rate of *one transition at a time.* The computation is sequential. It proceeds step by step.

This is why time has one dimension. **Time is the axis along which irreducible computation proceeds, and computation is sequential — one step, then the next.** Space, by contrast, admits parallelism. Multiple spatial regions can process information simultaneously, independently of one another. This is why space is multi-dimensional: spatial dimensions correspond to independent degrees of freedom that can operate in parallel. But for any given subsystem, the computation threads through a single sequence of states. That sequence is time.

This doesn't prove that a universe *must* have one time dimension from first principles. What it does is provide a structural explanation where the standard account has none: time is one-dimensional because the computation that constitutes physical change is sequential, and it is sequential because the Margolus-Levitin bound limits state transitions to one at a time for any finite-energy system.

## Why the Future Is Open

Here is a thought experiment. Imagine you had unlimited computational power — more than any computer that could ever be built. Could you predict the future?

No. And the reason is not merely practical.

There are two results that, taken together, establish that the future is genuinely open — not because we lack information, but because of the mathematical structure of computation and physics.

**The first is computational irreducibility.** Stephen Wolfram identified this property in the 1980s and developed it over the following decades. Some computations have no shortcut. The only way to find out what a computationally irreducible system will do after *N* steps is to run all *N* steps. There is no formula, no compression, no clever algorithm that lets you skip ahead. This is not a conjecture — it is a proven property of many computational systems, and it follows from deep results in mathematics (the undecidability of Turing's halting problem, Gödel's incompleteness theorems).

Many physical systems are computationally irreducible. A generic quantum many-body system — a collection of interacting quantum particles — evolves in a way that cannot be efficiently simulated even by a quantum computer (this is connected to active research on quantum computational complexity, and is supported by results like the proven undecidability of the spectral gap problem). The universe, which is a quantum many-body system of extraordinary complexity, is almost certainly computationally irreducible in this sense.

What does this mean? It means there is no way to determine the future state of the universe without effectively running the universe forward in time. No subsystem can "look ahead." No prediction machine can outrun reality. The future is not merely unknown — it is not yet *computed*.

**The second is Wolpert's impossibility theorem.** In 2008, David Wolpert proved a result from computational theory that makes this even sharper. He showed that no inference device — no computer, no mind, no physical system of any kind — that is embedded within a universe can correctly predict all the properties of that universe's future states before those states occur. This is not about limitations in computing power or data. It is a mathematical impossibility, structurally similar to Gödel's incompleteness: a system cannot fully model itself.

The key phrase is "embedded within." An external observer, standing outside the universe and watching it unfold, would face no such limitation (in principle). But no real observer is external. Every observer, every computer, every prediction machine is *part of* the universe it is trying to predict. And Wolpert proved that this self-referential situation creates an absolute barrier to prediction.

Together, these two results establish the following: for any subsystem of the universe, the future is genuinely open. Not merely unknowable in practice — unknowable *in principle*, from the inside. No amount of intelligence, no amount of computational power, no trick of any kind can make the future fully accessible from the present. The present moment is a genuine computational frontier: everything before it has been computed; everything after it has not.

This is not a deficiency. It is a structural feature of any finite system embedded in a computationally irreducible whole. And it is, we will argue, the key to understanding what temporal flow *is*.

## Why Time Flows

This is the hardest question and the one where the interpretation makes its most distinctive claim.

Temporal flow — the sense that time *passes*, that each moment gives way to the next, that we are moving through time rather than merely existing at different points in it — is the most universal feature of conscious experience. Every attempt to deny it is self-undermining: you cannot argue that time doesn't flow without engaging in a process that unfolds over time.

Yet physics has had almost nothing to say about it. In the standard block universe picture, flow is dismissed as a psychological illusion — time doesn't really pass, and our impression that it does is something our brains construct. In thermodynamic approaches, flow is reduced to entropy increase — but entropy increase is a statistical tendency, not the vivid, immediate experience of passage that we all have. Neither account is satisfying.

The computational interpretation offers something different. Not a dismissal, not a reduction, but an identification.

Consider what temporal flow consists of, structurally. Strip away the metaphors and describe the phenomenon precisely:

- States succeed one another in a fixed order.
- The future state is inaccessible from the present.
- Each moment gives way to the next — there is no skipping.
- The process is genuinely *happening* rather than already complete.

Now consider what it is like to be a bounded subsystem embedded in a computationally irreducible process:

- The subsystem passes through states one at a time (the Margolus-Levitin bound enforces sequential processing).
- The future states are inaccessible (computational irreducibility means no shortcut exists; Wolpert's theorem means no embedded subsystem can predict them).
- Each computational step must be completed before the next begins — there is no skipping.
- The outcome is generated step by step — it does not exist in any compressed or pre-computed form (this is what irreducibility *means*).

These are not merely similar. The claim is that they are **the same thing**. Temporal flow is not something over and above the sequential, unshortcutable processing of information by a bounded subsystem. It *is* that processing. The experience of flow is what computational irreducibility is like from the inside.

Consider an analogy. Ask: why does water feel wet? "Wetness" is not some additional property layered on top of the molecular interactions between H₂O and your skin. Wetness *is* what those interactions feel like at the macroscopic scale. There is no "extra ingredient" of wetness that needs to be explained. Similarly, temporal flow is not an extra ingredient that needs to be added to sequential irreducible computation. Flow is what sequential irreducible computation is like for a bounded subsystem embedded within it.

This might seem like a dodge — like we're just *calling* computation "flow" and declaring victory. But there's a way to test whether the identification is doing real work: ask what a world *without* computational irreducibility would be like.

Imagine a universe whose dynamics are computationally reducible — where a sufficiently clever subsystem *could* shortcut to the future, computing the state at time *t* + 1000 directly from the state at time *t* without going through every intermediate step. In such a universe, the future would be accessible from the present. There would be no genuine frontier between "computed" and "not yet computed." Every moment would be, in a computational sense, equivalent — you could reach any future state from any present state without traversing the intervening path.

Such a universe would have succession — things would still happen in sequence — but it would not have *flow*. There would be no sense of openness, no genuine present moment, no frontier. It would be the block universe, realized: all moments equally accessible, none privileged. A universe of pure succession without passage.

In our universe, by contrast, computational irreducibility ensures that no subsystem can access the future without going through every step. The present is distinguished from the future by a real computational boundary. This is flow — not as a psychological addition to the physics, but as a structural consequence of how computation works from the inside.

This claim is interpretive. It cannot be *proved* the way a theorem can be proved, because it bridges the gap between mathematical structure and experienced reality. But it is the most well-motivated identification available: the structural features of temporal flow match the structural features of computational irreducibility exactly, and no competing account achieves this correspondence.

## Why Clocks Tick Slower Near Heavy Objects

General relativity tells us that time runs slower in gravitational fields. A clock on the surface of the Earth ticks about 45 microseconds per day slower than a clock in orbit. Near a black hole, the effect is dramatic: time nearly stops at the event horizon, from the perspective of a distant observer.

GR describes this beautifully — it follows directly from the curvature of spacetime. But GR doesn't say *why* curvature should affect the rate of time. It just says it does. The metric determines everything, and the metric is what it is.

The computational interpretation provides a physical picture of what's happening.

The Margolus-Levitin theorem says the maximum rate at which a quantum system can process information — the maximum number of distinguishable states it can pass through per second — is proportional to the system's energy: ν_max = 2*E*/π*ℏ*. More energy means faster processing. Less energy means slower processing.

In a gravitational field, energy is redshifted. A photon climbing out of a gravitational well loses energy. An atom near a massive object has less energy available for state transitions, as measured by a distant observer, than the same atom far from any massive object. The factor by which the energy is reduced is exactly √(1 − 2*GM*/*rc*²) — the gravitational redshift factor.

Apply the ML bound: if the available energy is reduced by the redshift factor, then the maximum rate of computation — the maximum rate at which the system can change its state — is reduced by the same factor. From the distant observer's perspective, the system near the massive object is processing information more slowly. Its computation is running at a reduced rate. And since time *is* the progression of this computation, time runs slower.

From the *local* perspective — from the perspective of an observer sitting next to the clock near the massive object — nothing has changed. The local energy is the same, the local ML bound is the same, the local rate of processing is the same. This is exactly what GR says: proper time is locally unchanged. Time dilation is a relational effect — it shows up in the comparison between two reference frames, not in either frame alone.

This does not independently *derive* time dilation from the ML bound. The ML bound, when generalized to curved spacetime (as Cao, Chen, and Li showed in 2008), *inherits* the dilation from the spacetime metric. The metric is still the fundamental object. What the ML bound provides is a *computational interpretation*: time dilation is not just a geometric curiosity but an expression of the fact that the rate of information processing varies with the gravitational field. Near massive objects, the universe computes more slowly.

This may seem like a mere restatement. But it does something important: it connects time dilation to the same framework that explains irreversibility, one-dimensionality, and the open future. In the standard account, these are completely separate phenomena with completely separate explanations. In the computational interpretation, they are all aspects of one thing: the bounds on information processing in a finite universe.

## Why There Is an Arrow

The LBB engine explains *why* time is irreversible — the mechanism by which physical processes cannot be undone. But it does not, by itself, explain *why there is a global arrow* — why the entire observable universe exhibits irreversibility in the same direction.

This is an important distinction. The engine and the fuel are different things.

The LBB engine runs in any non-equilibrium environment. Whenever a finite system processes information, it must erase, and erasure is irreversible. But this only produces a *local* arrow — irreversibility in the neighborhood of the computing system. For a *global* arrow — for the fact that everywhere in the observable universe, entropy increases in the same temporal direction — you need something more.

You need the universe to have started far from equilibrium.

Consider a universe in thermal equilibrium — maximum entropy, maximum disorder, maximum information storage. Such a universe satisfies all the conditions of the LBB engine (Landauer's principle still holds, the Bekenstein bound still applies, Bennett's theorem is still true), but it has no global arrow. Any local fluctuation that produces a temporary decrease in entropy will exhibit a local arrow, but there is no cosmic direction, no universal "past-to-future" march of increasing disorder.

Our universe is dramatically far from equilibrium. The early universe was extraordinarily smooth, extraordinarily hot, and extraordinarily low in gravitational entropy. This is the **Past Hypothesis** — the observation (or assumption, depending on your philosophical temperament) that the universe began in a very special, low-entropy state.

The computational interpretation doesn't eliminate the Past Hypothesis. It reframes it: the universe began with far fewer correlations than it could hold. Its Bekenstein-bounded storage was nearly empty. The LBB engine has been filling it ever since — processing information, erasing, dissipating, spreading correlations throughout the universe. The arrow of time is the direction in which this filling proceeds.

So the full picture of the arrow requires two components:

- **The engine (LBB):** Explains *why* irreversibility occurs — because computation in a finite universe must erase information, and erasure is physically irreversible.
- **The fuel (Past Hypothesis):** Explains *why* the arrow points the way it does — because the universe started far from its maximum-entropy state, giving the engine a direction to run.

The engine is the novel contribution of the computational interpretation. The fuel is the same initial condition that the standard thermodynamic account requires. But the engine is a different kind of engine than the standard one. The standard account says: irreversibility is because there are more high-entropy states than low-entropy ones, so systems drift toward high entropy. This is true, but it makes irreversibility sound *statistical* — a matter of overwhelming probability. The LBB engine says: irreversibility is because information is *physically destroyed* by erasure, and destroyed information cannot be recovered. This makes irreversibility sound *structural* — a matter of physical law, not probability.

Both accounts are correct. But the LBB account is more explanatory. It identifies a specific physical mechanism (erasure), a specific physical law (Landauer's principle), and a specific constraint that forces the mechanism to operate (Bekenstein-bounded storage). It explains *why* individual physical processes are irreversible — not just that they are, overwhelmingly, likely to be.

## What It's Like From Inside

We have now assembled the computational framework: the LBB engine for irreversibility, the Margolus-Levitin bound for the rate of processing, computational irreducibility for the open future, and the identification of flow with irreducible sequential computation.

But there is a remaining question, more intimate than any of these: what is "now"? What is this knife-edge present, this feeling that one moment is *happening* while the past has already happened and the future has not yet arrived?

The computational interpretation gives a structural answer. "Now" is the **computational frontier** — the boundary between the portion of the irreducible computation that has been executed and the portion that has not.

This frontier is real, not merely psychological. The past — the portion already computed — has a specific character: some of the information it contained has been irreversibly erased (by the LBB engine). The past is partially inaccessible, not because our memories are imperfect (though they are), but because the physical information has been destroyed. The future — the portion not yet computed — is inaccessible for a different reason: computational irreducibility means it has not yet been determined. The present sits between these two genuine asymmetries: the partially erased past and the undetermined future.

Crucially, "now" is local. Different subsystems have different computational frontiers. An observer near a black hole and an observer far from one are at different points in their respective computations — they have different "nows." This is exactly what relativity requires. There is no universal present, no cosmic "now" that divides the universe into past and future. The present is always relative to a subsystem, a perspective, a reference frame. The quantum reference frame framework (Layer 1) and the computational framework (Layer 2) converge on this point: temporal structure is relational, not absolute.

This is the third layer of the interpretation — the phenomenological layer. The first layer (the QRF dissolution) establishes that "static" and "evolving" descriptions are equivalent perspectives. The second layer (computational structure) explains why time has the specific properties it has. The third layer says: **becoming — the experience of temporal passage — is what computational irreducibility is like from within.**

This is not a dismissal of becoming. It is not calling it an illusion, or reducing it to "mere" physics. It is taking it seriously as a *structural feature of the world.* Any system that processes information sequentially through an irreducible computation, bounded by the Margolus-Levitin limit and the Bekenstein bound, will have a temporal structure to its information processing. It will have a past (partially erased), a future (not yet computed), and a present (the frontier). It will experience succession without the ability to skip ahead. It will find each moment giving way to the next. This IS becoming. Not an illusion of becoming, not a mere description of becoming, but the thing itself.

Where the interpretation is honest about its limits: the "now" is the *least* well explained aspect. The framework provides structural past/future asymmetry at any point along the computation, and it explains why the frontier is real. What it does not fully explain is why any particular point *is* the present — why this step of the computation is the one that is *happening*, as opposed to the steps before and after it. This may be where physics reaches the boundary of what it can explain about subjective experience. The "hard problem" of consciousness — the question of why physical processes are accompanied by experience at all — is not a problem this interpretation was designed to solve, and it does not pretend to solve it.

## What We Don't Know

Every honest interpretation of time must acknowledge what it does not explain. Here are the genuine limits of this one.

**1. The "now" remains partially unexplained.** The interpretation provides structural past/future asymmetry — a real computational frontier — but does not explain what makes any particular point the present moment. Why is *this* step of the computation the one that is happening? The framework can say: "Every step is, from its own perspective, 'now.'" This is true but may not fully satisfy. The specialness of the present may be an irreducible aspect of subjective experience, beyond what any physical theory can capture.

**2. The factorization problem.** The entire interpretation presupposes that the universe can be decomposed into subsystems — a clock here, an observer there, an environment over there. In our universe, this decomposition is well-justified: atoms, molecules, cells, planets are all real subsystems with well-defined boundaries. But in fundamental quantum gravity, the decomposition of the universe's Hilbert space into subsystems may itself be *emergent* — not a given but something that arises from deeper structure. If so, the interpretation describes time's properties *within* the regime of well-defined subsystems but does not explain the origin of subsystem structure itself. It describes the house after the foundation is laid, not the laying of the foundation.

**3. The arrow still needs initial conditions.** The LBB engine explains the *mechanism* of irreversibility but not the *direction*. Why the universe started in a low-entropy state — far from the Bekenstein bound, with room for correlations to accumulate — is not explained by the computational framework. This is the same gap that standard thermodynamics has, and no known interpretation fills it.

**4. No novel predictions.** The computational interpretation does not predict any phenomenon that differs from standard quantum mechanics plus general relativity. Its value is explanatory — it unifies disparate phenomena under a single framework, identifies physical mechanisms where the standard account has only statistical tendencies, and provides structural explanations where the standard account has brute facts. But it generates no new experimental signatures. This limits its status to *interpretation* rather than *theory*.

**5. The flow claim is interpretive, not deductive.** The identification "computational irreducibility IS temporal flow" is a philosophical identification — a claim that two descriptions pick out the same phenomenon — not a mathematical derivation. It is the most well-motivated such identification available: the structural match between CI and flow is exact, and no alternative achieves the same correspondence. But it cannot be *proven*. Someone who finds it unconvincing cannot be shown to be making an error. This is the nature of interpretive claims, and honesty requires stating it plainly.

## What This Means

Let's return to the one-sentence version:

> **Time is the computationally irreducible processing of quantum correlations, as encountered from within a relational perspective.**

Unpacked:

- **"Computationally irreducible"** — the processing cannot be shortcut; you must go through every step. This is why the future is open and why time flows.
- **"Processing"** — the universe processes information; every physical interaction is a computation. The processing is necessarily irreversible (LBB engine) and bounded in rate (Margolus-Levitin). This is why time has an arrow, why it is one-dimensional, and why it dilates near massive objects.
- **"Quantum correlations"** — the physical substrate is entanglement, the web of quantum correlations that constitutes the state of the universe. This connects the framework to quantum gravity and to the established physics of entanglement.
- **"From within a relational perspective"** — temporal experience arises from being *inside* the computation, *part of* the universe, occupying a particular reference frame. The "static" and "evolving" descriptions are equivalent (Höhn's QRF theorem); which one you see depends on where you stand. Becoming is what the computation looks like from within.

None of this requires new physics. Every ingredient — Landauer's principle, Bennett's theorem, the Bekenstein bound, the Margolus-Levitin theorem, Wolpert's impossibility result, computational irreducibility, quantum reference frames — is established. What is new is the synthesis: the recognition that these independently proven results, taken together, explain why time has the specific properties it has.

The universe is not a place where things happen in time. The universe is a computation — a physical, irreducible, Bekenstein-bounded computation — and time is what that computation is like from the inside. Not an illusion. Not a background stage on which events play out. Not a brute fact that must be accepted without explanation. Time is the price of irreducibility, the shape of bounded processing, the view from within.
