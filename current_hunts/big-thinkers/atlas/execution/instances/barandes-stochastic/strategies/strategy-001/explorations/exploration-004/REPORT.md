### 1.1 arXiv:2602.01043 — "A Deflationary Account of Quantum Theory and its Implications for the Complex Numbers"

**Main claim:** The Hilbert-space formalism of QM is a Markovian embedding of underlying indivisible stochastic processes. Complex numbers are necessary specifically to ensure that the Markovian embedding works for systems with N>2 states.

**Technical structure:**
- Introduces the concept of "Markovian embedding": a non-Markovian system can be made Markovian by expanding the state space (standard technique from dynamical systems)
- For a quantum system with N-dim Hilbert space, the Strocchi-Heslot formulation shows it maps to a classical system with 2N coupled harmonic oscillators — a Markovian embedding of dimension 2N
- The stochastic-quantum direction: given Γᵢⱼ ≥ 0 (non-negative transition matrix), find Θ with Γᵢⱼ = |Θᵢⱼ|² — for N>2, unistochastic matrices (those arising from unitary evolution) are generally non-orthostochastic (no real orthogonal matrix works). Complex numbers are needed.
- This gives a structural explanation: **complex numbers are the minimal algebraic extension needed to represent quantum transitions as amplitude-squared**

**Does it add physical content?** MIXED. The argument is structurally interesting: it explains WHY QM needs complex numbers from a stochastic-embedding perspective. This is the same explanandum as Renou et al. (2021)'s real-vs-complex experiments, but from a different angle. However:
- The result is not a new prediction
- The "necessity" of complex numbers was already known from the algebraic structure of QM (irreducible representations of the rotation group, etc.)
- The ISP angle is novel: it says "you need complex numbers to represent quantum transitions AS stochastic amplitudes for N>2" — this is a specific claim about the relationship between stochasticity and complexness

**New results:** One formally claimed new result: the Hilbert space is actually defined over "pseudo-quaternions" (generated by i, K, iK satisfying i² = K² = (iK)² = 1), not merely complex numbers — where K is complex-conjugation operator needed for time-reversal. The "pseudo-quaternion" observation is a structural insight about the algebra of QM that is obscured in the standard Hilbert-space formulation.

**Testable predictions:** None. This is structural/interpretational.

### 1.2 arXiv:2602.10569 — "Pilot-Wave Theories as Hidden Markov Models"

**Main claim:** De Broglie-Bohm pilot-wave theory is best understood not as ontological (wave = real physical field) or nomological (wave = law of nature) but as a **hidden Markov model** — the pilot wave is a collection of latent variables.

**Technical structure:**
- A latent variable framework characterizes structures with 7 properties: abstract, non-unique, unobservable in principle, non-spatial, no backreaction, multivariate, contingent initial conditions
- The de Broglie-Bohm wave function satisfies ALL 7 properties
- The generic construction: for any stochastic process ρ(q,t), introduce a field r(q,t) with ∂(r²)/∂t = ∂ρ/∂t; this gives a deterministic HMM where r is the latent variable
- Particle trajectories Q̇ᵢ = Jᵢ/r²|_{Q(t)} emerge from the latent field
- Key: the pilot-wave trajectories are NOT gauge-invariant under Foldy-Wouthuysen transformations e^{iλ(q,t)/ℏ}Ψ → Ψ; trajectories change but predictions don't — this is the Deotto-Ghirardi ambiguity (1998)

**Does it add physical content?** Yes, modestly. It resolves a genuine debate in Bohmian mechanics foundations (ontological vs. nomological interpretations) by proposing a third option — latent variables. It explains the Deotto-Ghirardi ambiguity as gauge freedom: non-unique trajectories are expected for latent variables, not problematic. This is a genuine conceptual contribution to the foundations of Bohmian mechanics.

However: it makes no new predictions. It addresses a DIFFERENT community (Bohmians) than the main ISP program. And it inherits the same finite-particle limitation: "The proposal applies specifically to pilot-wave theories of fixed, finite numbers of non-relativistic particles."

**Key limitation noted in paper:** Extension to variable-particle systems, relativistic theories, or field theories remains unexplored. This applies to the broader ISP program.

### 1.5 arXiv:2512.18105 — "The CHSH Game, Tsirelson's Bound, and Causal Locality" (Barandes, Hasan, Kagan)

**Main claim:** The postulates of causally local indivisible stochastic processes are precisely strong enough to allow violations of Bell inequalities up to — but not beyond — Tsirelson's bound.

**Technical structure:**
- Defines **causal locality** stochastically: system R does not causally influence system Q if p(qₜ|q₀,r₀) = p(qₜ|q₀). This is dynamical (constraining the underlying stochastic kernel) rather than just marginal (constraining observable statistics).
- In the ISP framework, conditional probabilities are expressed as p(qₜ|q₀) = Tr[𝒫_{qₜ} Θ(t←t₀) 𝒫_{q₀} Θ†(t←t₀)]
- Causal locality → factorization of joint Alice-Bob amplitude matrices: (𝒜ℬ)_{xy} = 𝒜_x ⊗ ℬ_y
- This factorization → CHSH operator satisfies |⟨𝒞⟩| ≤ 2√2 (Tsirelson's bound)
- Key distinction: **No-signaling alone permits PR boxes** (violations up to 4). Causal locality of the *underlying dynamics* (not just statistics) restricts to the quantum Tsirelson bound.

**Does it add physical content?** THIS IS THE STRONGEST CLAIM in the Feb 2026 cluster. The argument structure is: causal local ISP → Tsirelson's bound, without assuming Hilbert space axioms. This gives a *physical reason* for the bound: it's not just an algebraic accident of quantum operators, but a consequence of causal local stochastic dynamics.

**Assessment of the claim's strength:**
- The proof uses amplitude matrix Θ, which is already quantum-mechanical in structure (complex matrix, amplitude-squared = probability)
- So the derivation is essentially: complex-amplitude stochastic mechanics + causal locality → Tsirelson's bound
- This is analogous to how the amplituhedron derives loop amplitudes without Feynman diagrams — same result, different starting point, different "reason"
- Whether this is "circular" (assuming QM implicitly through Θ) or genuinely explanatory (deriving the bound from fewer, more basic axioms) is debatable

**The most honest assessment:** The CHSH/Tsirelson paper identifies causal local indivisible stochasticity as a *characterization* of the quantum bound — not a new prediction, but a structural equivalence. PR boxes are excluded not because they're empirically ruled out (they are) but because they violate causal locality OF THE STOCHASTIC DYNAMICS. This is potentially a stronger foundational statement than "QM gives this bound" because it explains which postulate does the work.

## Section 2: Value Propositions

### Test A — Selection Principle

**Question:** Does indivisibility select a proper subset of quantum theories? Are there quantum systems outside the ISP framework?

**Finding:** The ISP framework is NOT a selector within finite-dimensional QM — it covers all finite-dimensional quantum systems in the forward direction (QM → ISP via Γᵢⱼ = |Uᵢⱼ|²). The reverse direction (ISP → QM) uses Stinespring dilation, which works for all finite-dimensional ISPs.

**The actual selection runs the other way:**
- ISP framework selects FROM stochastic processes: not all stochastic matrices are ISPs. CTMCs are excluded (E001). The ISP is a specific class within the universe of stochastic processes.
- From the stochastic side: ISP is the class of stochastic processes that CAN BE embedded as quantum subsystems. This is a genuine selection — not all probability dynamics is quantum.

**Limitations of the selection:**
- The selection is only interesting if you're asking "which stochastic processes are quantum?" not "which quantum systems are covered?"
- All finite-dim QM is covered. The selection is: among stochastic theories, only those with indivisible (non-Markovian) transition matrices can be quantum.
- This is related to the known result that quantum channels are non-Markovian (the Gorini-Kossakowski-Sudarshan-Lindblad (GKSL) equation is the Markovian limit, and non-Markovianity is a genuine quantum resource)
- The selection does NOT exclude any quantum system within finite-dim QM. The only exclusions are: infinite-dimensional systems (technical gap), and systems where indivisibility "fails" — but what would this mean for a quantum system? A quantum system always has indivisible evolution.

**Verdict:** NEGATIVE as a selection principle WITHIN QM. PARTIAL POSITIVE as a characterization of which stochastic theories are quantum: those with indivisible (non-Markovian) transition kernels. But this isn't a new result — non-Markovianity of quantum dynamics is well-known in quantum information (Breuer-Laine-Piilo measures, etc.).

### Test B — Computational Advantage

**Question:** Is there any computation more natural or efficient in the stochastic formulation?

**Investigated cases:**

**Open quantum systems (Lindblad):** Lindblad master equation ρ̇ = -i[H,ρ] + Σₖ(LₖρLₖ† - ½{Lₖ†Lₖ,ρ}) describes dissipation. In the ISP framework, this corresponds to a CPTP map evolving the transition matrix. But: the Lindblad equation is already formulated at the quantum level. Converting to ISP requires computing Γᵢⱼ = |Θᵢⱼ|² which loses the Lindblad structure entirely. Calculations of decoherence RATES require the Hamiltonian and Lindblad operators — the ISP language adds a layer of representation without simplifying anything.

**Classical limits and thermalization:** The stochastic language might seem natural for the classical limit (large decoherence → Γ approaches a classical stochastic matrix). But this is just decoherence expressed in stochastic language. The calculation of thermalization rates (e.g., Fermi's golden rule) is much more naturally performed in the Hilbert space. The ISP language would require computing all the Θᵢⱼ matrix elements, which is more work, not less.

**Interference and diffraction:** The ISP framework represents interference as "non-divisibility" — you can't factorize the multi-step transition. But computing WHERE interference constructively adds requires knowing the phases (the elements of Θ). The ISP framework discards these phases in Γ; to compute interference you need to reconstruct them, which means going back to the Hilbert space.

**Bell inequalities:** The CHSH/Tsirelson paper offers a derivation from ISP + causal locality. But this is a foundational derivation, not a computational tool. Working physicists compute Bell correlations using the standard quantum formalism.

**Verdict:** NO COMPUTATIONAL ADVANTAGE. The ISP framework is a coarser description (erases coherences/phases). Any calculation requiring phase information (interference, specific decoherence rates, quantum dynamics) must be done in Hilbert space. The ISP representation of the output is a reduction — it strips out exactly the information needed for quantum computations.

### Test C — Structural Insight

**Question:** Does the stochastic formulation reveal structural features of QM that are obscured in Hilbert space?

**Finding 1 — Complex numbers from indivisibility (GENUINE INSIGHT):**

The deflationary account (2602.01043) provides a specific structural insight: complex numbers are the minimal algebraic extension needed to ensure that quantum transitions (unistochastic matrices) can be represented as amplitude-squared for N>2. For N=2, real orthogonal matrices suffice (connecting to the qubit = real case argument). For N>2, you need complex numbers.

This is a structural "explanation" for why QM uses ℂ rather than ℝ. It's not the only such explanation, but it's the one natural from the ISP perspective. Barandes also notes the actual algebra is "pseudo-quaternions" (generated by i, K, iK) — Hilbert spaces over these, not just ℂ. This is a minor structural finding about the algebra underlying QM.

**Limitation:** This structural insight was partially known. The inadequacy of real Hilbert spaces for full QM has been known since Stueckelberg (1960). The experimental confirmation (Renou et al. 2021, arXiv:2101.10870) showed real QM fails specific entanglement tests. Barandes' angle (you need complex numbers for the Markovian embedding) is novel as a derivation path, but not as a result.

**Finding 2 — Causal locality characterizes Tsirelson's bound (MODERATE INSIGHT):**

The CHSH/Tsirelson paper (2512.18105) provides a structural insight: among theories satisfying causal locality OF THE UNDERLYING DYNAMICS (not just of observable statistics), exactly those satisfying the Tsirelson bound are permitted. No-signaling (a weaker constraint) allows PR boxes. Causal local ISP does not.

This identifies which postulate does the work: it's not "quantum mechanics" as a whole that gives the bound, but specifically the causal locality of the underlying stochastic evolution. This is analogous (in spirit) to the amplituhedron identifying UV finiteness as a consequence of positive geometry.

**Limitation:** The derivation uses amplitude matrices Θ (quantum objects). So it's not deriving the bound from *purely* classical stochastic axioms; it's showing that quantum mechanics + causal locality → Tsirelson. The genuinely new claim would be: you can DERIVE that Θ must be complex (unitary evolution) FROM causal locality + ISP axioms. That stronger claim is not made.

**Finding 3 — Indivisibility as quantum-classical discriminator (PARTIAL INSIGHT, known in QI):**

The ISP framework identifies "indivisibility" (non-Markovianity of the transition kernel) as the key property that distinguishes quantum from classical evolution. CTMCs are excluded from ISPs because they're infinitely divisible. Quantum dynamics is non-divisible.

This is structurally true. BUT: in the quantum information literature, non-Markovianity of quantum channels has been studied extensively (Breuer, Laine, Piilo 2009; Rivas, Huelga, Plenio 2010). The ISP framework uses the same concept under a different name and with a slightly different technical definition (transition matrix non-factorizability vs. BLP/RHP non-Markovianity measures). The insight is real but not new to the ISP framework.

**Finding 4 — Phase freedom = realm selection (from E003b, NOVEL):**

Established in E003b: Barandes' Schur-Hadamard gauge freedom (multiple Θ for the same Γ) is structurally analogous to the consistent histories realm selection problem (multiple consistent families for the same density matrix). This is NOT in the literature — Barandes never cites consistent histories, and Griffiths/Gell-Mann-Hartle never discuss ISP. The closest prior is Brun (2000), which connects quantum trajectory unravelings to consistent history frameworks, but uses unitary Kraus freedom (different mathematical object).

**Verdict on Test C:** MIXED POSITIVE. There are genuine structural insights: the complex numbers derivation from indivisibility, the Tsirelson characterization via causal local ISP, and the phase-freedom/realm-selection connection. These are real structural insights that are obscured in the Hilbert space formulation. However, none of them are completely without prior art (Stueckelberg knew about complex numbers, non-Markovianity in QI is well-studied, Tsirelson bound is derivable from standard QM). The ISP framework provides a *different starting point* from which these structural features emerge naturally, rather than having to be imposed axiomatically.

## Section 3: The "What Does It Buy Me?" Question

If you could explain Barandes' framework to a working physicist and they asked "What does it buy me?", here is the honest answer:

**The one-paragraph answer:**

"Barandes' indivisible stochastic dynamics buys you exactly one scientifically useful thing: a principled explanation for *why* Tsirelson's bound is 2√2 rather than the no-signaling maximum of 4 — specifically, because causal local indivisible stochastic evolution is precisely the class of dynamics that achieves this bound. It also gives a structural derivation of why QM needs complex numbers (they're needed to represent quantum transitions as amplitude-squared for systems with more than 2 states). Beyond these two structural insights, the framework adds nothing for working physics: no new predictions, no easier calculations, no access to new experiments, and no coverage of field theories or relativistic systems. Every calculation you'd want to do is either harder (you lose the coherences that carry phase information) or identical (you need Hilbert space anyway for the actual dynamics). The value is foundational/philosophical: if you care about the *conceptual architecture* of quantum mechanics — why it has the structure it has — the ISP framework offers a different and sometimes illuminating starting point. If you care about computing observables, it's a dead end."

## Section 4: Gap Analysis

### Gap 1 — Entanglement Structure Completely Obscured

**What is obscured:** Entanglement is the central resource in quantum information and computing. Entanglement entropy, Schmidt rank, entanglement witness operators, the separability/inseparability of bipartite states — all of these are defined at the level of the density matrix ρ (specifically, the off-diagonal elements in the product basis). The ISP transition matrix Γᵢⱼ = |Uᵢⱼ|² is the MODULUS SQUARED of the amplitude, which erases precisely the phase information needed to compute entanglement.

**Why the stochastic language fails:** Given two parties A and B with joint ISP, the transition matrix ΓᴬᴮᵢⱼΩ is a matrix on the product space. Whether the corresponding quantum state is entangled depends on the phases of Θ, which are not fixed by Γ. Two ISPs with the same Γ can correspond to separable and maximally entangled quantum states respectively (the multiple-Θ freedom means the stochastic data says nothing about entanglement).

**Concrete example:** A two-qubit system in the singlet state |Ψ⁻⟩ = (|01⟩ - |10⟩)/√2 has the SAME diagonal density matrix elements as the maximally mixed state ρ = I/4. The ISP for both would have the same transition matrix Γ. The ISP framework cannot distinguish maximally entangled from maximally mixed.

**Fundamental or technical?** FUNDAMENTAL. The ISP erasure of phases is deliberate — it's the feature that makes ISP "classical" in spirit. But quantum entanglement is entirely encoded in phases. This is not a technical gap; it's an inherent feature of the coarse-graining.

---

### Gap 2 — Dynamics Are Pair-Wise, Not Continuous Generators

**What is obscured:** In Hilbert space QM, the Schrödinger equation ∂ₜ|ψ⟩ = -iH|ψ⟩ gives a *local generator* H that specifies the dynamics everywhere and can be written as H = T + V (kinetic + potential energy). This makes eigenvalue problems natural, perturbation theory straightforward, and conservation laws (via Noether's theorem on H) transparent. The energy spectrum E is the central organizing object of quantum mechanics.

**Why the stochastic language fails:** The ISP framework specifies Γ(t←t₀) for pairs of times. There is no natural generator. To recover H, you must take the log of U = Θ (assuming Θ is unitary), which requires choosing a branch of the matrix logarithm — a non-unique operation, compounding the phase freedom. The ISP description of a quantum harmonic oscillator with energy levels Eₙ = ℏω(n+1/2) would require specifying ALL transition matrices Γ(t←0) for all t, from which you could in principle reconstruct H — but this is harder than the original Hilbert space problem, not easier.

**Barandes' response:** He acknowledges this as a feature of the framework — ISPs are specified by their transition maps, not generators. But this means the ISP framework is a description of *quantum processes* (what happens from t₀ to t), not of *quantum systems* (what structure does the system have). For most physics problems, you want the latter.

**Fundamental or technical?** PARTIALLY TECHNICAL. In principle, one could develop a "generator" formalism within the ISP framework (a matrix derivative of Γ(t←t₀) at t=t₀). But this generator would have the same phase non-uniqueness as Θ, making it much more complicated than the Hermitian H of standard QM. The Hilbert space formulation's Hermitian generator H is a profound simplification that ISP has no natural analog for.

---

### Gap 3 — Quantum Field Theory and the Standard Model Are Out of Reach

**What is obscured:** Virtually all of high-energy physics, condensed matter at a deep level, and quantum gravity requires quantum field theory: creation/annihilation operators, Fock space, renormalization, gauge invariance of a very different kind (Yang-Mills), spontaneous symmetry breaking, the Higgs mechanism, the Standard Model Lagrangian. None of this has any formulation in the ISP framework.

**Why the stochastic language fails:** The ISP framework requires a *finite* configuration space (N states). QFT operates in infinite-dimensional Fock space with arbitrary particle number, continuous field configurations, UV divergences requiring renormalization, and Lorentz invariance as a fundamental symmetry.

Three specific failures:
- **Particle creation/annihilation:** When an electron and positron annihilate to produce two photons, the particle number changes. The "configuration space" before and after are different. ISP has no mechanism for changing N.
- **Lorentz symmetry:** The ISP framework uses a non-relativistic configuration space (positions of particles). Making this Lorentz-covariant would require a stochastic process on spacetime, not space alone. This runs into the relativistic causality problem (spacelike-separated "conditioning times" in the ISP definition).
- **Renormalization:** UV divergences arise from the infinite-dimensional nature of QFT. The ISP framework avoids this by working only in finite N. This is not a solution to renormalization — it's an avoidance of it.

**Fundamental or technical?** FUNDAMENTAL limitation, explicitly acknowledged by Barandes. He calls QFT extension "future work" but the obstacles are not merely technical — the ISP framework's ontological commitment to a classical configuration space conflicts with the field-theoretic picture where fields (not particles) are fundamental.

---

### Gap 4 — Superposition Cannot Be "Seen" in the Stochastic Description

**What is obscured:** In Hilbert space QM, superposition is explicit and vivid: a particle in (|L⟩ + |R⟩)/√2 is literally in a superposition of left and right, with interference fringes computable directly from |ψ|². The double-slit experiment has a standard calculation: amplitudes from two slits add, and |amplitude₁ + amplitude₂|² gives the interference pattern.

**Why the stochastic language fails:** In the ISP framework, a quantum particle going through a double slit is described by a NON-MARKOVIAN stochastic process — the particle is "at the slits" at time t₁, and the conditional probability at the screen at time t₂ cannot be written as the product of the slit-conditional and source-conditional probabilities. The interference is present, but it's encoded in the non-factorizability of the transition matrix. You cannot *look at* the ISP transition matrix and say "interference is happening here" — you can only say "this matrix can't be factored, which is a sign of indivisibility."

This is analogous to knowing that a physical process is non-Markovian (it has memory) without knowing why or how the memory is structured. The ISP framework correctly identifies that interference is a source of non-Markovianity but gives no insight into the mechanism. The Hilbert space formulation makes the mechanism (amplitude superposition) transparent.

**Fundamental or technical?** FUNDAMENTAL. The stochastic description deliberately suppresses the information (phases) that makes interference intelligible. This is the price of working with probabilities rather than amplitudes.

## Section 5: The Amplituhedron Comparison

The amplituhedron adds genuine value beyond Feynman diagrams in three ways:
1. **UV finiteness as selection principle** — new physics criterion
2. **EFT-hedron constraints** — concrete inequalities on Wilson coefficients (new calculations)
3. **Hidden zeros** — inter-theory structure invisible from any Lagrangian

How does Barandes compare?

| Amplituhedron Value | Barandes Analog | Present? |
|--------------------|-----------------|----------|
| UV finiteness as selection principle | Causal local ISP → Tsirelson bound (characterizes quantum theories among stochastic) | PARTIAL — reproduces known bound, doesn't predict new physics |
| EFT-hedron constraints: new inequalities on Wilson coefficients | New constraints on quantum dynamics parameters | NO — no new inequalities derived |
| Hidden zeros: inter-theory structure | Phase freedom = realm selection (structural connection to CH) | POSSIBLE — but not developed or proven as a computational tool |
| Derivation without Feynman diagrams → simpler computations | Stochastic language → simpler calculations | NO — calculations are harder in stochastic language |

**Key asymmetry:** The amplituhedron's most important contribution is the EFT-hedron (concrete measurable inequalities on Wilson coefficients). Barandes has NO analog of this. The framework produces no new inequalities, no new constraints on physical parameters, no new predictions. The Tsirelson bound is reproduced, not extended.

The amplituhedron adds value by making something *calculable* that wasn't (or by revealing new structure that leads to new calculations). Barandes adds value by making something *conceptually intelligible* that wasn't — specifically, the structural role of indivisibility and complex numbers. These are both valuable, but they're different kinds of value. The amplituhedron's value is operational. Barandes' value is interpretational.

## Section 6: Summary Verdicts

**Feb 2026 papers overall:**
- The CHSH/Tsirelson paper (2512.18105) is the most physically significant: it provides a structural characterization of the Tsirelson bound via causal local ISP. Not a new prediction, but a new *reason* for a known constraint.
- The Deflationary Account (2602.01043) provides a structural derivation of why complex numbers are needed for N>2 systems. Genuine structural insight, not completely without prior art.
- The Pilot-Wave/HMM paper (2602.10569) makes a real contribution to Bohmian mechanics foundations by resolving the ontological-nomological fork, but adds nothing to physics predictions.
- The critique papers (Weak Values, ABL Rule) are consistent with the deflationary program but add no positive content.

**Test A (Selection Principle):** NEGATIVE within QM. The ISP framework characterizes which stochastic theories are quantum (those with indivisible kernels), but this is not a new physics result.

**Test B (Computational Advantage):** NEGATIVE. ISP is a coarser description; every quantum calculation is harder or equivalent.

**Test C (Structural Insight):** POSITIVE in three ways: complex numbers derivation, Tsirelson characterization, and (from E003b) phase-freedom/realm-selection structural connection. These are genuine insights, though none are completely without prior art.

**Amplituhedron comparison:** Barandes is significantly weaker. The amplituhedron adds operational value (new computations, new inequalities). Barandes adds conceptual/foundational value (new explanations for known results). Both are legitimate, but the amplituhedron's contribution is more scientifically impactful.

**Overall verdict:** Barandes' framework is a Level 2 reformulation (as classified in E002) that adds genuine conceptual value at the foundational level — it explains structural features of QM (complex numbers, Tsirelson bound, measurement) from a different starting point. It adds NO predictive or computational value. The Feb 2026 cluster deepens the program coherently but does not break through to new physical content. The gap analysis reveals that the framework has inherent limitations (no entanglement, no generators, no QFT) that are fundamental, not technical.
