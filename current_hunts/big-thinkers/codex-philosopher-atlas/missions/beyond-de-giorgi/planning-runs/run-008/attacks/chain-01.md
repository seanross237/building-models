# Attack on Chain 01

## Bottom line

This chain is disciplined enough to produce a useful obstruction memo, but it is still much better at curating a portfolio than at earning a live proof route. Its central weakness is unchanged: it treats "destroyed by Tao averaging" as the main early discriminator before it has shown that this discriminator is measurable at one stable comparison level and correlated with theorem-facing leverage rather than with mere exact-NS distinctiveness.

The chain is strongest when it forces candidates to name one proof architecture and one first missing lemma. It is weakest where it assumes a common scoring language exists across very different mechanisms and where it relies on prior mission filters (`Step 010`, `Step 011`, fixed-geometry audit) without re-auditing how those negatives interact with the new comparison protocol.

## Step-by-step critique

### Step 1 - Freeze the comparison granularity and output schema

This step is necessary, but it still hides the hardest choice under procedural language.

- "Define exactly what counts as preserved by Tao averaging and destroyed by Tao averaging" is only meaningful after fixing the level of comparison. Is the chain comparing identities, transport laws, closure estimates, compactness-compatible quantities, or actual contradiction mechanisms? Those are different tests. A property can fail as an exact identity and still survive at the estimate level that matters for regularity.
- The schema mixes descriptive and evaluative fields too early. "Exact property," "Tao verdict," and "nearest prior-art family" are descriptive; "possible proof architecture" and "first theorem-facing burden" are already strategic judgments. If the Tao verdict is not pinned down first with a worked standard, later fields will be backfilled to justify whatever survives.
- The expected output asks for one pass example and one fail example, which is good, but the kill condition is still too soft. A team can always produce a superficial protocol memo that sounds concrete. The step should fail unless the pass and fail examples are both nontrivial and the distinction is robust under small reformulations of the same candidate.
- Hidden assumption: that there is one comparison granularity suitable for all candidate families. That is doubtful. Stochastic transport, deformation observables, and pressure identities do not naturally live at the same evidentiary level.

Net: Step 1 can easily manufacture a clean-looking rubric that does not actually constrain later decisions. If this step is loose, every downstream ranking becomes discretionary.

### Step 2 - Build the candidate property menu from mission-live sources only

This step is more biased than it looks.

- The candidate examples already preload the outcome: commutator-ready scalarization, deformation constraints, stochastic circulation, vorticity-direction persistence, pressure/stress identity. Those are familiar mission motifs, not a neutral search over "exact NS structures." The chain says "for example," but examples shape selection in practice.
- Restricting to "mission-live sources only" is defensible for workflow discipline, but it creates a structural blind spot. If the mission's live sources are already biased toward Tao-sensitive geometric or transport-flavored mechanisms, this step cannot detect that the whole menu is path-dependent and incomplete.
- The instruction to reject anything already killed by `Step 010`, `Step 011`, or the fixed-geometry audit is sensible as anti-redundancy, but it also imports old calibration choices as hard vetoes. If Step 1 changes the comparison level, some earlier "dead" branches may deserve reevaluation. The chain never says when cross-run negatives are allowed to be reopened.
- The expected output is too compressed for the burden. A one-sentence novelty note and a one-sentence prior-art note are not enough when several candidate families are close to known literature or to earlier internal dead ends. This is exactly how renamed prior-art overlap slips through.
- The kill condition is under-calibrated. "Mostly renamed dead branches" still leaves room to keep one cosmetically differentiated branch alive without showing that it escaped the earlier obstruction for a mathematically relevant reason.

Net: Step 2 risks giving the appearance of a bounded, disciplined menu while in fact serving as a selective re-presentation of the mission's existing favorites.

### Step 3 - Attach each survivor to one proof architecture or one bounded negative

This is the chain's best step, but it still does not force enough contact with actual proof mechanics.

- Requiring one architecture per candidate is a strong anti-handwaving move. The problem is that the listed architectures are still broad labels: local criterion, transport-continuation, concentration exclusion, stochastic contradiction, critical-element rigidity. Each label hides several incompatible technical routes and bottlenecks.
- "State one exact burden the property would need to reduce" is directionally correct, but the chain does not require a metric of reduction. Reduce by what: derivative count, localization loss, exponent gap, coercive defect, compactness obstruction, or contradiction input? Without a typed notion of gain, an attachment can remain slogan-level.
- The option of attaching a candidate to "one bounded negative" is sensible for honesty, but it creates an escape hatch. A weak candidate can survive long enough to generate prose about why it fails rather than being killed immediately once no quantitative burden reduction is visible.
- Hidden assumption: every live candidate should plug into exactly one architecture. Some real mechanisms are hybrid, but the larger problem here is opposite: the chain may force artificial single-host attachments that flatter a candidate by choosing its most favorable narrative frame.
- The demotion rule is still too permissive. "Cannot be attached to one theorem-facing burden" is a necessary reason to demote, but not a sufficient standard for promotion. A burden can be named very precisely without any evidence that the property interacts with it in the needed environment.

Net: Step 3 improves the chain materially, but it still certifies narrative placement more easily than operational leverage.

### Step 4 - Rank by operationality, not by slogan-level novelty

The intention is right, but the ranking architecture is not well founded.

- The four axes are not commensurable. Tao sensitivity, endpoint closeness, prior-art distance, and narrowness of the first missing lemma measure different things and can point in opposite directions. A candidate close to an endpoint is often near prior art; a candidate far from prior art often has a very wide missing lemma. The chain offers no rule for handling these tradeoffs.
- "Keep only candidates that clear all four axes at a nontrivial level" sounds tough, but "nontrivial" is undefined and highly manipulable. This is exactly the sort of threshold that produces an apparently principled shortlist while hiding evaluator judgment.
- Prior-art distance is especially dangerous as a ranking axis. Distance from prior art is not evidence of truth or usefulness; often it is evidence that the route is underdeveloped. This axis can reward novelty theater unless heavily subordinated to bottleneck relevance.
- Narrowness of the first missing lemma can be misleading. Some "narrow" missing lemmas are narrow only linguistically and are effectively as hard as the full problem. Conversely, a broader but structurally clear missing lemma may be the more honest and tractable research target.
- The output requirement of one primary, one secondary, and one bounded-negative branch builds a conclusion template into the process. The chain should allow zero promoted branches without feeling like a process failure. As written, it subtly pressures the evaluator toward a balanced portfolio outcome.

Net: Step 4 is where the chain most strongly risks manufacturing pseudo-comparability and a predetermined "one leader, one backup, one no-go" storyline.

### Step 5 - Convert the ranking into execution-ready admissions rules

This step is useful for mission hygiene, but it exposes the chain's real nature: portfolio management, not theorem discovery.

- If the earlier ranking is soft, then the admissions rules will simply codify soft judgments into harder-sounding workflow constraints.
- Recording which closed branches may not re-enter under new names is good discipline, but the chain still has not solved the opposite problem: when a branch is legitimately reopened because the comparison level or architecture attachment has changed in a mathematically substantive way.
- The downgrade option to "descriptive map only" is honest, but it highlights the asymmetry of the whole chain. Negative and administrative outputs are tightly calibrated; positive outputs are not.
- Hidden assumption: the right next move after ranking is a selector-ready memo. That may be wrong. The honest next move may instead be one stress test on the top candidate to see whether the named first burden is even exposed to the proposed exact property in a toy model or localized setup.

Net: Step 5 is operationally clean, but it can turn under-earned comparative judgments into sticky mission policy.

## Structural weaknesses of the whole chain

### 1. The Tao filter is still doing too much work

The chain says it wants "operational" exact properties, not just Tao-sensitive ones. But the workflow still lets Tao discrimination set the agenda before theorem-facing leverage is established. That is dangerous because many exact NS features are genuinely scrambled by averaging and still useless for regularity.

### 2. Cross-architecture ranking is not really justified

The chain compares objects that pay in different currencies: localized estimate gain, transport control, rigidity input, stochastic representation, concentration exclusion. Without a common evidentiary unit, a ranked atlas is mostly a polished preference ordering.

### 3. The process is heavily path-dependent on prior mission framing

Mission-live sources only; reject anything killed by `Step 010`, `Step 011`, or the fixed-geometry audit; examples drawn from existing mission vocabulary. That is efficient, but it is not neutral. If earlier runs overfit the Tao screen or underexplored one mechanism class, this chain inherits the bias and presents it as disciplined memory.

### 4. Positive outcomes are weaker than negative ones

The chain is well designed to say, "nothing here survives a serious screen." It is much less well designed to say, "this survivor is genuinely promising." That asymmetry matters. A clean obstruction memo is credible; a primary-branch promotion is not equally earned by the current workflow.

### 5. There is a built-in bias toward a tidy middle conclusion

The output shape strongly favors a familiar ending: one main survivor, one backup, one bounded negative, plus some closure rules. That is narratively satisfying and operationally convenient, but it can be manufactured even when the evidence only supports either total obstruction or unresolved incomparability.

### 6. Prior-art overlap remains under-policed

The chain asks for prior-art notes, but not for explicit overlap accounting at the level of theorem mechanism. A candidate can be "not already closed" and still be functionally the same old program with renamed observables and a new Tao-sensitive gloss.

## Fair strengths

- Step 3 is a real improvement over purely slogan-driven mechanism hunting because it demands contact with one named proof architecture.
- The chain is honest that an obstruction memo is a valid terminal output.
- The anti-reentry rule in Step 5 is useful protection against recycled dead branches.
- The comparison-first posture is reasonable if the goal is to cleanly falsify a family of exact-NS hopes rather than to certify a winning route.

## Hard recommendation

Keep this chain only if it is treated primarily as a falsification chain. Its honest success condition is not "produce a ranked atlas with a primary branch." Its honest success condition is: determine whether any candidate survives a strict Tao-comparison protocol, survives prior-art overlap scrutiny, and exposes one typed theorem-facing burden in a way that is more than narrative attachment.

If the planning loop insists on keeping a positive-output option, then the bar should be raised: no branch should be promoted without one explicit model subproblem or localized bottleneck where the proposed exact property yields a clearly typed gain that the averaged model route cannot replicate. Without that, the most likely output is a polished atlas with weak epistemic content.
