## HYPOTHESIS

"Entanglement isn't spooky action at a distance -- it's two particles sharing a single moment of time. They're not connected across space; they're the same event experienced from two locations. Space separates them; time doesn't."

## FRAMEWORK: Temporal Identity Interpretation of Entanglement (TII)

### Definitions

**D1.** *Spacetime event* -- A point (or compact region) in the 4-dimensional spacetime manifold M, specified by coordinates (t, x, y, z) in some frame.

**D2.** *Temporal identity* -- Two spatially separated particles possess temporal identity if and only if there exists a frame-invariant sense in which their quantum states are determined by a single preparation event, such that no independent degrees of freedom were introduced between preparation and measurement. The particles carry forward the *same* quantum information, not copies of it.

**D3.** *Entanglement as identity, not correlation* -- Standard QM treats entanglement as a correlation between subsystems described by a non-factorizable state vector |psi_AB> != |psi_A> x |psi_B>. TII reinterprets this: the state is non-factorizable because the subsystems are not genuinely independent. They are two spatial projections of a single temporal structure.

**D4.** *Spatial separation vs. temporal separation* -- In TII, "spatial separation" means the particles occupy distinct spatial locations in every reference frame. "Temporal separation" means the particles' quantum identities originate from distinct, causally disconnected preparation events. TII claims entangled particles have spatial separation but zero temporal separation.

---

### Premises

**P1. [Standard QM -- Entanglement from common origin]** In the paradigmatic case, entangled particles are produced by a single quantum event: a decay, a parametric down-conversion, a collision vertex. The entangled state is prepared at one spacetime region and the two particles propagate outward. The quantum state was written once, not twice. This is not controversial.

**P2. [No-signaling theorem]** Entanglement cannot be used to transmit information. Local measurements on one particle of an entangled pair produce a marginal probability distribution identical to what would be obtained if the other particle did not exist. The correlations are only visible upon classical comparison of results. No causal influence propagates between the particles at or above the speed of light.

**P3. [Bell's theorem, 1964]** No theory of local hidden variables can reproduce all the predictions of quantum mechanics for entangled systems. Specifically, the Bell-CHSH inequality S <= 2 is violated by quantum mechanics (Tsirelson bound: S <= 2sqrt(2)). Experimentally confirmed (Aspect 1982, Hensen et al. 2015 loophole-free).

**P4. [Special relativity -- no preferred simultaneity]** There is no absolute time. For spacelike-separated events, different inertial frames disagree on their temporal ordering. The relativity of simultaneity is a consequence of the Lorentz transformation.

**P5. [ER=EPR conjecture, Maldacena & Susskind 2013]** Every pair of entangled particles is connected by a non-traversable Einstein-Rosen bridge (microscopic wormhole). The wormhole does not permit signal transmission (consistent with P2) but means the particles are geometrically closer than the ambient spatial metric indicates. Entanglement IS geometric connectivity.

**P6. [Retrocausal interpretations, Price 1996, Wharton 2018, Adlam 2022]** The correlations in entanglement can be explained by models in which future boundary conditions (measurement settings/outcomes) constrain past states, or in which influences propagate both forward and backward in time. These models are consistent with all known experimental data, satisfy no-signaling, and provide local accounts of Bell-inequality violation at the cost of accepting retrocausality.

**P7. [Block universe / eternalism]** In the block universe picture (the natural ontology of relativity), all spacetime events -- past, present, future -- exist tenselessly. There is no "flow" of time. Correlations between spacelike-separated events need not be explained by causal influences; they can be structural features of the 4D block.

### Argument Structure

#### Step 1: The wrong question

**S1.** The standard framing of the entanglement puzzle is: "How does particle B 'know' the outcome of measurement on particle A?" This assumes that information must travel from A to B.

**S2.** From P2, no information travels. Every physicist agrees on this. Yet the question persists because the correlations demand explanation, and our causal intuitions insist that correlations require either a common cause or a direct causal link.

**S3.** CLAIM: The question is wrong not because the answer is "nothing travels" but because the question presupposes that A and B are two independent entities that need to be connected. TII proposes that they are not independent entities. They are a single quantum event that has spatial extent.

> Analogy (heuristic, not proof): Asking "how does the north pole of a magnet communicate with the south pole?" is ill-posed. The magnet is one object. The poles are not separate things in communication; they are aspects of a single structure. Similarly, TII claims the entangled pair is one quantum event, and asking how its parts communicate mistakes a structural feature for a causal process.

#### Step 2: What "sharing a moment of time" means formally

**S4.** The hypothesis states the particles "share a single moment of time." In TII, this is formalized as follows:

> **Temporal Identity Condition (TIC):** Two particles A and B share temporal identity if their joint quantum state rho_AB is entirely determined by a single preparation event E, such that:
> (i) The state rho_AB is pure and non-factorizable (entangled).
> (ii) No additional quantum information was introduced into either subsystem between E and the measurements.
> (iii) The entanglement is a *conserved quantity* inherited from E -- it was not created by any interaction after E.

**S5.** When TIC holds, the quantum state of the pair was written exactly once, at E. Everything that can be known about the joint measurement statistics is determined at E. The particles do not "carry" separate pieces of information that need to be coordinated; they carry no independent information at all. The information exists only in the joint state, which was established at E.

**S6.** "Sharing a moment of time" means: the quantum identity of the pair has a single temporal origin and has not been modified since. The particles are temporally co-located (their quantum state was fixed at one time) even though they are spatially separated. In TII, entanglement is the persistence of this temporal co-location through spatial propagation.

#### Step 3: Compatibility with no-signaling

**S7.** If the particles are aspects of a single event (per S3), then there is nothing to signal between. The no-signaling theorem (P2) is not a constraint on TII; it is a *prediction* of TII. If two things are actually one thing, of course you cannot send a message from one to the other -- there is no sender and no receiver, only a single structure.

**S8.** This is a genuine explanatory advantage over interpretations that posit entanglement as a connection-between-two-things and then must explain why this connection cannot carry signals. TII dissolves the question: there is no connection because there are not two separate things.

#### Step 4: Compatibility with Bell's theorem

**S9.** Bell's theorem (P3) rules out local hidden variable theories. TII is not a local hidden variable theory. The "hidden variable" in a local hidden variable theory is a property that each particle carries independently, determined at creation. TII denies that the particles carry independent properties at all. The joint state rho_AB is irreducibly relational -- it cannot be decomposed into "A's properties" and "B's properties."

**S10.** TII is non-local in the following precise sense: it posits a single entity (the joint quantum event) that has spatial extent exceeding any limit. But it is non-local in a way that is immune to signaling (S7-S8), which is exactly the kind of non-locality that quantum mechanics exhibits.

**S11.** TII does not resolve Bell's theorem (it does not recover local realism). It *reframes* what Bell non-locality means: it is not "spooky action at a distance" but "spooky spatial extent of a single event."

#### Step 5: Connection to ER=EPR

**S12.** The ER=EPR conjecture (P5) states that entangled particles are connected by non-traversable wormholes. In the wormhole geometry, the particles are "at the same place" in the interior geometry even though they are far apart in the exterior geometry.

**S13.** TII can be read as the temporal analog of ER=EPR's spatial claim:
- ER=EPR: "They look spatially separated but are geometrically connected through the interior."
- TII: "They look like two events but are temporally one event."

Both share the structural claim that the apparent separation is misleading about the true relationship. ER=EPR makes this precise through geometry; TII makes it through temporal identity.

**S14.** CLAIM: TII and ER=EPR are complementary perspectives on the same underlying insight. ER=EPR explains the geometric mechanism by which two spatially separated particles can be "the same place." TII explains the temporal mechanism by which two spatially separated particles can be "the same event." If both are correct, entanglement is a structure that is neither purely spatial nor purely temporal but a topological feature of spacetime that can be projected onto either axis.

#### Step 6: Connection to retrocausal interpretations

**S15.** Retrocausal interpretations (P6) explain Bell correlations by allowing future measurement settings to constrain past states. In the block universe (P7), this is natural: the global 4D structure simply has the correlations built in, and the "retrocausal" language is an artifact of describing a timeless structure in temporal terms.

**S16.** TII is compatible with and extends the retrocausal picture. If the particles share temporal identity (their quantum state was fixed at a single event E), then the correlations between future measurements are constraints on the global structure emanating from E. In the block universe, E is a structural node that constrains all future measurement events on the particles it produced. This is not retrocausation in the sense of backward-in-time signaling; it is a global consistency condition.

**S17.** TII adds a specific claim to the retrocausal picture: the reason future measurements are correlated is not that influences travel backward from the measurements to E, but that E *never stopped being the relevant event*. The particles did not "leave" E in any temporal sense. They left E in a spatial sense (they propagated outward) but their quantum identity remained at E. The measurements reach back to E not because causation runs backward, but because the quantum state of the particles IS E, experienced at two later spatial locations.

#### Step 7: Addressing entanglement swapping (the hard case)

**S18.** In entanglement swapping:
1. Source A produces entangled pair (1, 2).
2. Source B produces entangled pair (3, 4).
3. A Bell-state measurement (BSM) on particles 2 and 3 produces entanglement between particles 1 and 4.
4. Particles 1 and 4 never interacted and share no common origin event.

**S19.** This is the strongest objection to TII. I address it by extending the temporal identity concept:

> **Extended Temporal Identity Claim:** The BSM on (2, 3) is itself a quantum event E'. At E', particles 2 and 3 are jointly measured, projecting them into a Bell state. This projection retroactively determines the joint state of (1, 4). In TII, E' becomes the "shared moment" for particles 1 and 4 -- not their creation events (which were separate), but the BSM event which is the moment at which the (1,4) entanglement comes into being.

**S20.** The cost of this extension: "sharing a moment" no longer means "sharing an origin moment." It means "having a quantum identity that is determined by a single event," where that event can be a measurement on intermediary particles. The temporal identity of (1, 4) is established at E', not at their respective creation events.

**S21.** Is this circular? The concern: entanglement is explained by sharing a moment, and the shared moment is defined as the event that creates entanglement. RESPONSE: It is not circular if the TII adds explanatory content beyond "entanglement exists because entanglement was created." The added content is: after E', particles 1 and 4 have zero temporal separation (their quantum identity is fixed by E') even though they have never interacted spatially. The BSM at E' is a topological operation on the temporal structure of the quantum state: it fuses the temporal identities of the two original pairs into a single identity for (1, 4). This is analogous to how ER=EPR would describe entanglement swapping as the fusion of two wormholes into one (Bao et al., 2015).

**S22.** Admitted weakness: The entanglement swapping extension makes TII more complex and less parsimonious than the simple version. The original intuition -- "they share their creation event" -- was clean. The extended version -- "they share whatever event most recently determined their joint state" -- is correct but less striking. I flag this as the main structural tension in TII.

### Conclusions

**C1.** Entangled particles are best understood not as two connected entities but as a single quantum event with spatial extent. The non-factorizability of the joint state is not a "connection" between separate things; it is the unity of one thing.

**C2.** "Sharing a single moment of time" is formalized as the Temporal Identity Condition: the joint quantum state is entirely determined by a single preparation (or projection) event, with no independent information introduced since.

**C3.** TII is compatible with no-signaling (dissolves the puzzle rather than constraining it), Bell's theorem (reframes non-locality as spatial extent of a single event), ER=EPR (provides the temporal complement to ER=EPR's spatial mechanism), and retrocausal interpretations (the shared event persists as the determinant of future correlations in the block universe).

**C4.** TII handles entanglement swapping through extended temporal identity: the BSM becomes the shared event for the newly entangled pair. This extension is logically consistent but reduces the parsimony and intuitiveness of the framework.

**C5.** The core insight, which survives all extensions: the question "how does information travel between entangled particles?" is a category error. There is no information transfer because there are not two independent information-bearing entities. There is one entity with spatial extent.

### Predictions / Distinguishing Features

TII is an interpretation, not a modification of quantum mechanics. It predicts the same experimental outcomes as standard QM for all currently feasible experiments. However, it makes the following structural claims that could in principle be distinguishing:

#### Feature 1: Temporal coherence under time dilation

If entangled particles truly share temporal identity, then subjecting one particle to extreme time dilation (via gravity or velocity) introduces an asymmetry: one particle's local time advances much further than the other's. Standard QM predicts the entanglement is unaffected (the state is Lorentz-invariant). TII makes the same prediction, but interprets it differently: the temporal identity is defined at the preparation event, which is Lorentz-invariant, so differential aging of the particles after preparation is irrelevant.

If an experiment ever found that entanglement degrades with differential proper time -- which standard QM forbids -- it would REFUTE TII (and standard QM). If no degradation is found (as expected), it is consistent with both.

#### Feature 2: Entanglement monogamy as temporal uniqueness

Entanglement monogamy (a qubit maximally entangled with one system cannot be entangled with another) has a natural TII interpretation: temporal identity is unique. An event is one event; it cannot simultaneously be two different events. If particle A shares temporal identity with B, it cannot also share temporal identity with C (for maximally entangled states). Monogamy is not a constraint imposed on entanglement; it is a consequence of events being self-identical.

#### Feature 3: Entanglement and the arrow of time

In TII, the creation of entanglement is the creation of shared temporal identity; the destruction of entanglement (decoherence) is the loss of shared temporal identity through interaction with the environment. Decoherence is when a single event fractures into multiple independent events -- when the environment writes so many records that the unity of the original event is destroyed. This suggests that entanglement is the primordial state and classical separateness is derived, which aligns with the quantum-to-classical transition narrative but reframes it in temporal terms.

### Honesty Check

1. **"Sharing a moment of time" is not Lorentz-invariant as phrased.** The phrase suggests absolute simultaneity. I have tried to formalize it in a Lorentz-invariant way (the preparation event is frame-independent), but the natural-language version of the hypothesis is in tension with relativity. The formal version is compatible; the poetic version is not.

2. **Entanglement swapping weakens the framework.** The extension to handle entanglement swapping (S19-S22) works logically but removes the elegance of the original claim. "They share their creation event" is compelling. "They share whatever event most recently determined their joint state" is correct but sounds like a definition of entanglement restated in new words.

3. **TII may be unfalsifiable.** It makes no predictions that differ from standard QM. This places it in the same category as many-worlds, Copenhagen, and Bohmian mechanics -- metaphysically distinct, empirically identical. Whether this is a flaw depends on whether one values interpretive clarity or predictive novelty.

4. **The analogy with ER=EPR is suggestive but not rigorous.** ER=EPR provides a concrete geometric mechanism (wormholes). TII provides a conceptual reframing (temporal identity). These are not at the same level of formal development.
