# Judge Ruling — Evaluating Adversary's Attacks on RCSM

**Agent:** Judge
**Run:** 002-prose
**Date:** 2026-03-25

---

## Theory Under Review

The Architect proposes **Recursive Causal Self-Modeling (RCSM)**: consciousness is identical to the process by which a physical system builds a causally efficacious model of itself that includes its own modeling activity. Three jointly sufficient and individually necessary conditions: (a) self-modeling, (b) recursion in the self-model, (c) causal coupling of the self-model to the system's dynamics.

## Adversary's Attacks

12 attacks were filed: 2 rated Fatal, 7 Major, 3 Minor.

---

### Attack 1: Recursion Criterion Is Fatally Underdefined

**Did it land?** Yes.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is correct that the recursion criterion — "models its own modeling" — is the load-bearing joint of the entire theory and is not operationally defined with sufficient precision. The Architect distinguishes a thermostat's self-monitoring from a human's metacognition by invoking recursion, but the essay does not provide a formal or even a rigorous informal criterion for what counts as modeling-one's-modeling versus merely monitoring a property of one's own processing. The rat uncertainty-monitoring example the Adversary raises is apt: reasonable people could classify it either way under the current definition.

However, I reduce the severity from Fatal to **Major**. The criterion is underdefined, not incoherent. The Architect has a real distinction in mind — there is a difference between a system that tracks its own temperature and a system that represents its own representational processes — but the essay fails to cash out that distinction precisely. This is fixable. A fatal flaw would be one that cannot be fixed without abandoning the theory's core; this one can be fixed by providing clearer necessary and sufficient conditions for "modeling one's modeling."

**Ruling: Landed. Severity: Major. Must be addressed.**

---

### Attack 2: Circularity — Consciousness Requires Self

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is right that the Architect assumes consciousness requires a "perspectival center" without arguing for this claim, and that the Buddhist/Humean tradition of subjectless consciousness is not engaged. However, the Adversary overstates the damage. The Architect's theory does not strictly require a *substantial* self — a persisting, unified subject. It requires only that the system have an internal model that represents its own processing from a perspective. This is compatible with the self being a construction, a process, or even an intermittent phenomenon. The circularity charge would be more damaging if the Architect claimed consciousness requires a soul or a Cartesian ego; as it stands, the claim is more modest — consciousness requires self-representation — and while this does need defense, it is not obviously circular.

The Architect should acknowledge that the claim "consciousness requires a subject" is substantive and provide positive argument for it, rather than treating it as obvious from Nagel's phrasing.

**Ruling: Partially landed. Severity: Moderate (between Major and Minor). Should be addressed.**

---

### Attack 3: Thermostat Dismissed by Intuition

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** This attack is parasitic on Attack 1. If the recursion criterion were well-defined, the thermostat case could be handled principally rather than intuitively. The Adversary is correct that, as written, the dismissal of the thermostat relies more on the intuition that thermostats are not conscious than on the theory's own resources. But the Adversary also overstates: there is a genuine structural difference between a thermostat's feedback loop and a human's metacognitive architecture, even if the Architect has not articulated it precisely. The fix here is the same as for Attack 1 — tighten the recursion criterion — and once that is done, this attack is likely resolved.

**Ruling: Partially landed, but derivative of Attack 1. Severity: Moderate. Addressed implicitly by fixing Attack 1.**

---

### Attack 4: Identity Claim Unjustified

**Did it land?** Yes.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is correct and this is the most serious attack. The Architect claims that phenomenal consciousness is *identical to* recursive causal self-modeling, but the essay provides no argument for identity over weaker relations (correlation, necessary condition, causal basis, supervenience). The Architect simply asserts the identity and says it is "not a definition by fiat," but assertion without argument is functionally equivalent to fiat.

This is particularly damaging because the identity claim is what distinguishes RCSM from a mere correlational theory. Without it, the theory becomes: "recursive causal self-modeling is associated with consciousness." That's an interesting empirical hypothesis, but it doesn't answer the Hard Problem, it doesn't explain what consciousness IS in any deep sense, and it doesn't earn the Architect's confidence that the theory makes consciousness "entirely physical, entirely functional, and entirely real."

I affirm the severity as **Fatal** in the sense that the theory's strongest claims collapse without it. However, I note that the Architect *could* construct an argument for identity — perhaps via an inference to the best explanation, or by arguing that every feature of consciousness is predicted and explained by RCSM such that positing a further "consciousness property" would be explanatorily idle. The theory is not doomed; the argument is missing.

**Ruling: Landed. Severity: Fatal. Must be addressed with an actual argument for identity.**

---

### Attack 5: Godel Analogy Is False

**Did it land?** Yes.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is right that the Architect's appeal to Godel is loose and potentially misleading. Godel's theorems are about formal axiomatic systems, not physical causal systems, and the structural analogy has not been established. The Architect's claim that a recursive self-model "necessarily" cannot fully capture its own operation is stated without proof, and it is not clear that anything like Godelian incompleteness applies here.

That said, the Adversary slightly overstates. There may be genuine computational or information-theoretic limits on self-modeling that are analogous in spirit (if not in formal detail) to Godel's result. A system of fixed computational capacity cannot construct a model of itself that is as complex as itself. This is a real constraint, but it comes from computational complexity theory, not Godel's theorems, and the Architect should cite the right source.

The more damaging part of this attack is the second paragraph: even granting structural incompleteness, the Architect has not explained why incompleteness would feel like something rather than just being a computational limitation. This links to Attack 6.

**Ruling: Landed. Severity: Major. Must be addressed — either fix the analogy or replace it with a rigorous argument.**

---

### Attack 6: Evolution Explains Function, Not Phenomenality

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** This is the zombie objection restated, and it has real force. The evolutionary argument does explain why recursive self-modeling would evolve; it does not, on its own, explain why recursive self-modeling should be conscious. However, the Architect has a built-in response: if consciousness *is identical to* recursive self-modeling (the identity claim from the theory's core), then explaining why recursive self-modeling evolves just is explaining why consciousness evolves. The zombie objection only has force if you reject the identity claim.

This means Attack 6's force is entirely dependent on Attack 4. If the Architect can successfully defend the identity claim, Attack 6 dissolves. If the identity claim falls, Attack 6 is devastating. This is another reason the identity claim is the most important thing the Architect needs to address.

**Ruling: Partially landed — its force depends on the identity claim. Severity: Conditional (Major if identity fails; Minor if identity is established). Should be addressed in tandem with Attack 4.**

---

### Attack 7: Causal Coupling Criterion Creates Testability Problem

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** The Adversary raises a legitimate concern about operationalization but overstates the conclusion. Many scientific theories posit entities or properties that are difficult to isolate in biological systems (e.g., specific gene functions in complex regulatory networks). The difficulty of cleanly isolating the recursive self-model does not make the theory unfalsifiable — it makes it difficult to test, which is a different and less damning charge. Modern neuroscience is increasingly capable of targeted interventions (optogenetics, transcranial magnetic stimulation, lesion studies) that could test the theory's predictions with reasonable specificity, even if perfect isolation is impossible.

The Adversary is right that the theory needs to specify what would count as evidence for and against it. The Architect should describe concrete experimental predictions, even if the experiments are currently difficult to run.

**Ruling: Partially landed. Severity: Moderate. Should be addressed with concrete empirical predictions.**

---

### Attack 8: Unity of Consciousness Unaddressed

**Did it land?** Yes.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is correct that the theory does not address the unity of consciousness, and this is a significant gap. The binding problem — why there is one consciousness rather than many in a single brain — is a central explanandum for any theory of consciousness, and RCSM as presented has no account of it. If the brain maintains multiple self-models, RCSM as stated would seem to predict multiple consciousnesses, which is not what we observe (except perhaps in split-brain cases, which are instructive).

The Architect likely has resources to address this — perhaps by arguing that the multiple self-models are unified by being components of a single recursive structure, or by adding a unification criterion — but the essay does not do the work.

**Ruling: Landed. Severity: Major. Must be addressed.**

---

### Attack 9: Equivocation on "Model"

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is right that "model" is doing a lot of theoretical work and that the definition admits of strong and weak readings. This is a real problem, particularly when combined with Attack 1: if both "model" and "recursive" are underdefined, the theory's central criterion is doubly vague. However, the Adversary's specific example (plant phototropism) is somewhat strained — most readers would not classify a plant's growth response as a "model" even on the Architect's definition, because the plant does not use the internal state to "generate predictions, guide behavior, or select among alternatives" in any rich sense. The Architect's definition is imprecise but not vacuous.

Still, the theory would benefit significantly from a more rigorous account of what counts as a model — ideally drawing on the substantial literature in philosophy of science on models and representation.

**Ruling: Partially landed. Severity: Moderate. Should be addressed by tightening the definition.**

---

### Attack 10: Dismissal of Subjectless Consciousness Is Dogmatic

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** This overlaps substantially with Attack 2. The Adversary is right that meditative reports of "pure awareness" without self-structure are at least prima facie counterexamples. The Architect should engage with them. However, the Adversary's own severity rating of Minor seems appropriate — these reports are interpretively contested, and the Architect could argue that even "pure consciousness" involves a minimal self-model (the awareness of being aware, which is itself a form of recursive self-modeling). This is not a knock-down counterexample, but it deserves engagement.

**Ruling: Partially landed. Severity: Minor. Can be addressed with a brief discussion.**

---

### Attack 11: No Novel Predictions

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is right that the theory's explicit verdicts align with common sense, which limits its interest. However, the theory does implicitly generate some predictions the Adversary overlooks: (1) it predicts that artificial systems with genuine recursive self-models would be conscious regardless of substrate, which is not common sense; (2) it predicts specific correlations between metacognitive capacity and consciousness level, which are testable; (3) it predicts that disrupting recursive self-modeling should diminish consciousness even if other information processing is intact, which is distinguishable from IIT's predictions. The Architect should make these predictions explicit rather than leaving them implicit.

**Ruling: Partially landed. Severity: Minor. Should be addressed by making implicit predictions explicit.**

---

### Attack 12: Hidden Functionalism Is Unargued

**Did it land?** Partially.

**Assessment:** The Adversary is right that the theory is implicitly functionalist and that this commitment deserves explicit acknowledgment and defense. However, this is a relatively standard commitment in contemporary philosophy of mind, and the burden of arguing for it is lower than the Adversary implies. A brief explicit acknowledgment that the theory is functionalist, with a short defense (e.g., appealing to multiple realizability arguments and the lack of evidence for substrate-specific consciousness), would suffice.

**Ruling: Partially landed. Severity: Minor. Can be addressed briefly.**

---

## Overall Assessment

The theory is ambitious, clearly written, and makes a genuine contribution by synthesizing insights from Higher-Order Theories, IIT, and self-model theories. The prose format makes the reasoning accessible and allows the Architect to develop nuanced positions that might be obscured in formal notation. However, the theory has two critical structural weaknesses (the underdefined recursion criterion and the unjustified identity claim) and several significant gaps (unity of consciousness, the Godel analogy, the Hard Problem treatment).

The theory is not beyond repair. The core idea — that consciousness involves recursive causal self-modeling — is plausible and interesting. But the current version does not do enough work to establish its central claims.

## Prioritized Fix List

### MUST FIX

1. **The identity claim** (Attack 4): Construct an explicit argument for why consciousness is *identical to* recursive causal self-modeling, not merely correlated with or caused by it. This is the single most important fix; many other attacks' force depends on it.

2. **The recursion criterion** (Attack 1, with implications for Attacks 3 and 9): Provide a rigorous, operationally meaningful criterion for what counts as "modeling one's own modeling." This should be precise enough to yield determinate verdicts in test cases (rat uncertainty monitoring, thermostat feedback, etc.) without relying on pre-theoretical intuitions about consciousness.

3. **Unity of consciousness** (Attack 8): Add an account of why a brain with multiple self-models has one consciousness rather than many. This is a central explanandum that the current theory ignores entirely.

### SHOULD FIX

4. **The Godel analogy** (Attack 5): Either replace the loose Godel analogy with a rigorous argument from computational complexity theory, or drop the analogy and address the Hard Problem differently.

5. **The evolutionary argument** (Attack 6): Strengthen the evolutionary argument by explicitly connecting it to the identity claim — show that explaining why recursive self-modeling evolves is explaining why consciousness evolves, because they are the same thing.

6. **Testable predictions** (Attacks 7 and 11): Lay out specific empirical predictions that distinguish RCSM from competing theories (IIT, GWT, HOT) and could in principle be tested.

7. **The subject requirement** (Attacks 2 and 10): Provide positive argument that consciousness requires a perspectival center, and engage with the possibility of subjectless consciousness (whether from Buddhist phenomenology or philosophical argument).

8. **Definition of "model"** (Attack 9): Tighten the definition of "causal model" to clearly exclude trivial cases while remaining physically realizable.

### CAN IGNORE

9. **Hidden functionalism** (Attack 12): A brief acknowledgment would be nice but is not essential. The functionalist commitment is standard and defensible.
