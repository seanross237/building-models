## What Happened

### v1 Theory: Temporal Identity Theory (TIT)
- 10 definitions, 10 premises, 8 conclusions, 10 empirical predictions
- Core claim: consciousness is the intrinsic nature of sufficiently organized temporal structure (Russellian monism + panprotopsychism, applied specifically to time)
- Key innovation: unifies the hard problem of consciousness with the puzzle of temporal passage
- Introduces OTC (Organized Temporal Complexity) as the critical measure

### Adversary produced 11 attacks + novelty audit
- 2 rated Fatal, 6 Major, 2 Moderate, 1 Minor

### Judge ruled: remand for revision
- Downgraded both Fatal ratings to Major
- Identified 8 mandatory fixes, 2 recommended, 1 ignorable
- Key insight: the "constitutive universality" argument for P5 (time is the one physical property present in ALL experience) was available but missing from v1
- Validated the adversary's novelty assessment while noting the theory could become more novel with formalized OTC and qualia-mapping principle

## Verdicts

### Plausibility: 5/10
The theory is internally coherent after v2 revisions. The core idea — that the hard problem and the passage problem are the same problem, and Russellian monism about time resolves both — is genuinely interesting and not obviously wrong. However, it rests on an axiom (P5) that is well-motivated but unproven, and its central measure (OTC) is unformalized. The non-temporal qualia account (P11/C9) is plausible but speculative. The theory is more defensible than most consciousness theories but less defensible than the architect's framing suggests.

### Novelty: 3/10
The theory is primarily a synthesis of existing positions: Russellian monism (Chalmers, Goff), panprotopsychism (Chalmers), temporal constitution of consciousness (Husserl, Dainton), dual-aspect monism about time (Bergson, Whitehead). The specific combination is somewhat new, and the OTC concept could become a genuine contribution if formalized. But the existing literature covers the vast majority of TIT's conceptual territory. The theory adds approximately three things the literature does not have: (a) the constitutive universality argument for singling out time, (b) the OTC concept (informal), and (c) the specific prediction about temporal vs. spatial isomorphism (EP8, EP10). None of these are yet developed enough to constitute a major independent contribution.

### Testability: 3/10
The theory's testable predictions (EP1-EP7) are shared with rival theories and do not distinguish TIT. Its distinctive predictions (EP8-EP11) require future technology. EP11 (temporal vs. spatial disruption of qualia) is the most promising near-term distinctive prediction, as optogenetic temporal perturbation is approaching feasibility. But currently, TIT is empirically indistinguishable from rival theories on all available evidence. The theory is in the "metaphysical interpretation" category rather than the "testable scientific theory" category.

### Worth Pursuing Further?
**Conditionally yes.** The theory has one genuine insight that may be original: the constitutive universality of temporality in experience makes time the uniquely motivated candidate for the Russellian experiential ground. This is a real argument that strengthens Russellian monism by narrowing its scope from "all physical structure has experiential intrinsic nature" to "temporal structure specifically has experiential intrinsic nature." If this argument can be sharpened, and if OTC can be formalized into a computable metric distinct from IIT's Phi, TIT would become a genuine contribution to the philosophy-neuroscience intersection. The most productive next step is NOT further philosophical elaboration but rather: (1) formalizing OTC, (2) designing an experiment to test EP11 (temporal vs. spatial disruption), and (3) developing the constitutive universality argument into a standalone philosophical paper. Without at least one of these, TIT remains a sophisticated synthesis that says nothing the existing literature has not already said more carefully.
