# Hypothesis Assessment: Entanglement as Shared Temporal Identity

**Date:** 2026-03-26
**Hypothesis:** "Entanglement isn't spooky action at a distance -- it's two particles sharing a single moment of time. They're not connected across space; they're the same event experienced from two locations."

**Core claim:** Entangled particles are not two things linked by some mysterious channel. They are a single temporal event manifesting at two spatial locations. No information travels between them because there is nothing to travel between -- there is only one event. What we call "distance" between entangled particles is a misapprehension: space separates them, but time does not. The correlations are explained not by communication but by identity.

## 1. Plausibility: 5/10

This is more interesting than it looks on first reading, because it accidentally converges on ideas that serious physicists have explored -- but it also conflates several concepts that the serious versions keep carefully distinct. The 5/10 reflects a genuine kernel embedded in a frame that does not quite work.

**What supports it from existing physics:**

- **The no-communication theorem already says nothing travels.** Quantum mechanics itself insists that entanglement cannot be used to send information. The correlations between entangled particles are only visible when measurement results are compared through classical (sub-luminal) channels. The hypothesis's core intuition -- that the search for "what travels between them" is the wrong question -- is exactly right. Mainstream quantum mechanics agrees: nothing travels.

- **Entanglement originates at a common spacetime event.** Entangled particles are typically created together -- produced in the same decay, the same parametric down-conversion event, the same interaction vertex. The hypothesis's language of "sharing a single moment" is literally true of their creation: they were one event that produced two particles. The quantum state was prepared at a single spacetime point (or region) and the particles carry that shared preparation into the future. This is not controversial. It is the standard account of how entanglement arises.

- **The ER=EPR conjecture (Maldacena and Susskind, 2013) resonates strongly.** ER=EPR proposes that every pair of entangled particles is connected by a non-traversable Einstein-Rosen bridge (wormhole). The wormhole does not allow information transfer (consistent with no-signaling), but it means the two particles are, in a geometric sense, not as "far apart" as they appear in the ambient spacetime. The hypothesis's claim that "distance is our misunderstanding" maps surprisingly well onto ER=EPR: if entangled particles are connected by a wormhole, then the spatial separation in the external geometry is misleading about the true geometric relationship. The particles are "closer" than they look -- possibly, in the internal wormhole geometry, at the same point.

- **Relational and perspectival interpretations of QM.** Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics (1996) holds that quantum states are not absolute properties of systems but relations between systems. In this framework, saying two particles "share a state" is already saying something relational and perspectival. The hypothesis's language of "the same event experienced from two locations" has a relational flavor that is not alien to this research program.

- **Block universe / eternalism.** In the block universe picture (which is the natural ontology of special and general relativity), all spacetime events simply exist. There is no "traveling" of anything. Correlations between spacelike-separated events do not require causal connections -- they can be features of the global structure. The hypothesis's suggestion that entangled particles are a feature of temporal structure rather than spatial communication is compatible with the block universe picture, where the correlations are simply built into the 4D structure of events.

**Where it weakens or fails:**

- **Entanglement persists and evolves after creation.** The particles are not frozen at their creation event. They propagate, interact with other systems, and can be measured at arbitrarily different times. Alice can measure her particle on Monday and Bob can measure his on Friday. The correlations hold regardless of when each measurement occurs (provided no decoherence). If the particles were literally "the same moment of time," it is unclear what this means when the measurements happen at different times. The hypothesis must explain time-asymmetric measurement scenarios, and it does not.

- **Entanglement swapping contradicts the "common origin" reading.** In entanglement swapping, particles that have never interacted and have no common origin become entangled through a Bell-state measurement on other particles. Particles 1 and 4 can become entangled even though they were created in completely separate events, at different times and places. The hypothesis claims entanglement is "sharing a single moment of time," but entanglement swapping produces entanglement between particles that manifestly do not share any moment. This is a serious problem for the literal reading of the hypothesis.

- **The phrase "same event experienced from two locations" is metaphysical, not physical.** In physics, an event is a point in spacetime (or a small region). Two particles at different spatial locations are, by definition, at different events. Saying they are "the same event" either redefines "event" in a non-standard way or requires a topological identification (like a wormhole) that the hypothesis does not specify. Without this specification, the claim is poetic rather than physical.

## 2. Internal Coherence

The hypothesis has one significant internal tension and one near-contradiction.

### The tension

The hypothesis says: "Space separates them; time doesn't." But in special relativity, space and time are not independent -- they are components of a unified spacetime, and what counts as "spatial separation" versus "temporal separation" depends on the reference frame. For spacelike-separated measurements on entangled particles (the typical EPR scenario), there exist reference frames in which Alice measures first, frames in which Bob measures first, and frames in which they measure simultaneously. The hypothesis privileges a frame in which the two particles "share a moment," but relativity says no such privileged frame exists. The hypothesis's core distinction (space separates them, time doesn't) is not Lorentz-invariant and therefore clashes with the structure of spacetime it implicitly invokes.

This can be partially rescued: if the claim is about the creation event rather than the measurement events, then there is a natural frame (the center-of-mass frame of the creation event). But the correlations manifest at measurement, not at creation, and the measurements are frame-dependent in their temporal ordering.

### The near-contradiction

The hypothesis says nothing travels because there is nothing to travel between -- "there is only one event." But the two particles produce two distinct, spatially separated measurement outcomes. If there is "only one event," why are there two outcomes? One event should produce one outcome. The hypothesis needs the particles to be identical-as-events (to explain the correlation) and different-as-events (to produce two distinct measurement results in two distinct locations). This is not necessarily a contradiction -- it could be a duality or a complementarity -- but the hypothesis does not articulate how both can be true simultaneously.

## 3. Relation to Known Physics

### ER=EPR

The hypothesis is most naturally read as a verbal approximation of ER=EPR. The ER=EPR conjecture provides the geometric mechanism the hypothesis lacks: entangled particles are connected by non-traversable wormholes, making them "the same place" in the internal geometry while appearing separated in the external geometry. The wormhole is non-traversable, which enforces no-signaling. The hypothesis's intuition that "distance is our misunderstanding" is precisely the ER=EPR insight: the ambient spatial distance is real, but it is not the whole story about the geometric relationship between the particles.

**Key difference:** ER=EPR is a geometric claim (there is a spatial bridge), not a temporal claim (they share a moment). The hypothesis puts the emphasis on time; ER=EPR puts it on a topological feature of space. These are not the same, though they share the family resemblance of "the separation is not what it appears."

### Retrocausal interpretations

The retrocausal interpretation of QM (Price, 1996; Wharton, 2018; Adlam, 2022) proposes that the correlations in entanglement are explained by influences that travel backward in time from the measurement events to the common origin, or by a block-universe constraint that ties future boundary conditions to past ones. The hypothesis's emphasis on temporal structure ("they share a single moment of time") resonates with retrocausal thinking, which also locates the explanation in the temporal structure of events rather than in spatial communication.

**Key difference:** Retrocausal interpretations are precise about what they mean. They modify the causal structure of spacetime in a well-defined way (allowing future-to-past influences subject to specific constraints). The hypothesis gestures at temporal structure without specifying what "sharing a moment" means dynamically. Retrocausal QM is the rigorous version of the intuition the hypothesis is reaching for.

### The no-signaling theorem

The hypothesis is fully compatible with no-signaling. In fact, no-signaling is arguably the hypothesis's strongest ally: if nothing travels between the particles, then of course you cannot use them to send signals. The no-signaling theorem is a *consequence* of the hypothesis rather than a constraint on it. This is a genuine strength.

### Special and general relativity

**Compatibility is mixed.** The hypothesis's language of "sharing a moment of time" appears to invoke absolute simultaneity, which special relativity forbids. However, the correlations in entanglement are Lorentz-invariant -- the joint probability distributions for measurement outcomes do not depend on the reference frame. So the physical content (the correlations) is relativistically kosher. The danger is in the interpretive language, not in the predicted physics. If the hypothesis is read as "the correlations are a frame-independent structural feature of the spacetime, not a consequence of information transfer," it is compatible with relativity. If it is read as "there exists a preferred time-slice on which the particles are simultaneous," it conflicts with relativity.

### Bell's theorem and local realism

Bell's theorem rules out local hidden variable explanations of entanglement. The hypothesis must be something other than a local hidden variable theory. Is it? The claim that the particles are "the same event" is arguably non-local (one event at two locations is a non-local structure). But it is non-local in a way that does not permit signaling, which is the same kind of non-locality that standard QM exhibits. The hypothesis does not conflict with Bell's theorem, but it does not resolve it either -- it redescribes the non-locality in temporal rather than spatial language.

## 4. Hypothetical Testability

This is the hardest section, because the hypothesis is an interpretation rather than a theory. It does not modify the quantum mechanical predictions; it reframes what those predictions mean. This places it in the same category as the many-worlds interpretation, the Copenhagen interpretation, and Bohmian mechanics: all make the same experimental predictions for all currently feasible experiments.

### Could we distinguish it from standard QM?

**In principle, possibly, through the following routes:**

- **Entanglement swapping and delayed-choice experiments.** If entanglement is literally "sharing a moment of time," then entanglement swapping (where particles with no common past become entangled) presents a clear test case. The hypothesis must either (a) claim that the Bell-state measurement retroactively makes the particles share a moment (which is a retrocausal claim and could in principle be tested by looking for retrocausal signatures), or (b) expand the definition of "sharing a moment" to include events connected by a chain of entanglements (which dilutes the hypothesis to the point of unfalsifiability). Delayed-choice entanglement swapping experiments (Ma et al., 2012) have already been performed and the results are consistent with standard QM. The hypothesis does not predict different results, so these experiments do not distinguish it.

- **Gravitational detection of wormhole-like structures.** If the ER=EPR reading is taken seriously, entangled particles are connected by Planck-scale wormholes. These are far too small to detect with any foreseeable technology. However, proposals exist (e.g., Gao, Jafferis, and Wall, 2017) for traversable wormholes in AdS spacetimes that could in principle be simulated in quantum computers. If such simulations confirmed the ER=EPR geometric picture, this would indirectly support the "distance is not what it appears" aspect of the hypothesis.

- **Probing the temporal structure of entanglement.** If the particles truly "share a moment," one might expect the correlations to degrade when the measurements are performed at very different proper times (highly asymmetric time dilation). Standard QM predicts no such degradation (the entangled state is Lorentz-invariant). Testing this is experimentally challenging but not impossible -- satellite-based entanglement experiments (like Micius) in different gravitational potentials could probe whether the correlations depend on proper time differences. Current results show no degradation, which is consistent with standard QM but does not specifically support or refute the hypothesis.

### Honest assessment

The hypothesis is not testable in its current form. It is an interpretive gloss on standard quantum mechanics. To become testable, it would need to be sharpened into a specific model that makes at least one prediction differing from standard QM. The retrocausal and ER=EPR research programs are examples of how this sharpening has been done by others; the hypothesis as stated has not achieved this level of specificity.

## 5. Strongest Version (Steelman)

The strongest version integrates the hypothesis with ER=EPR, retrocausal QM, and the block-universe picture, and drops the claim that entanglement is purely a temporal phenomenon:

> Entangled particles are not best understood as two separate entities connected by an information channel. They are a single, non-local structure in spacetime -- a feature of the global geometry rather than a relationship between local objects. The question "how does information travel between them?" is ill-posed, in the same way that "how does the North Pole communicate with the South Pole to ensure they are always at opposite ends of the axis?" is ill-posed. The correlations are not produced by communication; they are a structural feature of the spacetime in which the particles are embedded.
>
> More specifically: the creation event of the entangled pair is a topological feature that connects the two particles' worldlines in a way that the ambient spatial geometry does not fully capture. This connection may be geometric (as in ER=EPR, where a non-traversable wormhole bridges the particles) or temporal (as in retrocausal interpretations, where the common past constrains the future measurements through block-universe boundary conditions) or both. The key insight is that "spatial separation" is not the right measure of the relationship between entangled particles. The particles are closer -- in the geometric, topological, or temporal sense -- than their spatial separation suggests.
>
> The no-signaling theorem is not a mysterious constraint that prevents entanglement from being useful for communication. It is the natural consequence of the connection being non-traversable (ER=EPR) or acausal (block-universe). You cannot send a message through a structure that does not support directed information flow.

This version is genuinely defensible. It is a synthesis of ideas from Maldacena, Susskind, Rovelli, Price, Wharton, and Van Raamsdonk that serious physicists actively pursue. It avoids the problematic claim about "sharing a moment of time" and replaces it with a more precise claim about geometric or structural connectedness. It is compatible with relativity, Bell's theorem, and the no-signaling theorem. It predicts the same experimental results as standard QM (which is both a strength and a weakness -- it cannot be falsified, but it also does not make new predictions).

The poetic kernel of the original hypothesis -- that the puzzle of entanglement is dissolved rather than solved when you stop asking how information travels -- is preserved and strengthened. The answer to "how do they communicate?" is not "through a channel we haven't found yet" but "they don't communicate; they are aspects of a single structure, and asking how one aspect communicates with another is a category error."

## 6. Fatal Flaw

**The hypothesis explains the easy case and fails on the hard case.**

The easy case of entanglement is the one the hypothesis handles well: two particles created together, measured at spacelike separation, found to be correlated. Here, "they share a common origin and carry that shared identity forward" is intuitive and not wrong.

The hard case is **entanglement swapping**, and more generally, the creation of entanglement between particles with no common past. In entanglement swapping:

1. Source A produces entangled pair (1, 2).
2. Source B produces entangled pair (3, 4).
3. Particles 2 and 3 are brought together and a Bell-state measurement is performed.
4. After this measurement, particles 1 and 4 are entangled -- despite having never interacted and having no common origin.

The hypothesis says entanglement is "two particles sharing a single moment of time." Particles 1 and 4 share no moment. They were created at different times, in different places, by different sources. They have never been in the same location. Yet they are now entangled and exhibit exactly the same correlations as particles that were created together.

The hypothesis must either:

**(a) Claim that the Bell-state measurement on (2, 3) retroactively causes particles 1 and 4 to have shared a moment.** This is a retrocausal claim. It is not incoherent (retrocausal QM exists as a research program), but it fundamentally changes the hypothesis: the "shared moment" is no longer an explanation of entanglement but a consequence of it. The explanation has become circular -- entanglement exists because particles share a moment, and they share a moment because entanglement was created.

**(b) Claim that the chain of entanglement (1-2, then 2-3, then 3-4) constitutes "sharing a moment" transitively.** But transitivity of "sharing a moment" is not temporal identity. It is a network relationship. And calling a network relationship "sharing a moment of time" strips the phrase of its intended meaning.

**(c) Abandon the temporal language entirely and retreat to the structural/topological claim of the steelmanned version.** This is the right move, but it means the original hypothesis -- that entanglement is specifically about *time* -- has been replaced by something else.

Entanglement swapping is not an edge case or a technicality. It is the basis of quantum repeaters, quantum teleportation networks, and much of quantum information theory. Any interpretation of entanglement that cannot handle it naturally is incomplete at a fundamental level.
