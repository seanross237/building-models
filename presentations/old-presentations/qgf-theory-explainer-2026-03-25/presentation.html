Part 1

Why Gravity Breaks Quantum Mechanics

We have two wildly successful theories. They refuse to work together.

General Relativity
Gravity = curved spacetime
Smooth, continuous geometry. Describes planets, black holes, the expansion of the universe. Works perfectly at large scales.
Quantum Mechanics
Forces = particle exchanges
Discrete, probabilistic. Describes atoms, electrons, photons. Works perfectly at tiny scales. Forces are carried by particles.

What goes wrong when you combine them?

The infinity problem. When you try to calculate quantum corrections to gravity — like "what happens when two gravitons bounce off each other?" — the math produces infinities. Not manageable infinities like in other quantum theories, but new types of infinity at every level of calculation, each one requiring a new free parameter to cancel it. You end up needing infinitely many parameters. A theory with infinitely many unknowns predicts nothing.
Analogy: Imagine a spreadsheet where every cell depends on other cells, but each formula also introduces a new undefined variable. You can never close the system. You can never get an answer out.

Why this matters

This is not an academic curiosity. The centers of black holes, the first moments after the Big Bang, the smallest structure of spacetime — these are places where both gravity and quantum mechanics matter simultaneously. Without a unified theory, we literally cannot describe what happens there. The technical term for this infinity problem is "non-renormalizability" — the inability to absorb infinities into a finite number of measurable parameters. Every other force in nature (electromagnetism, the strong force, the weak force) has a quantum version that avoids this. Gravity alone resists.

Part 2

The 5 Rules Every Gravity Theory Must Obey

These are not preferences — they are constraints that nature has enforced in every experiment we have ever run. Drop any one and alternatives open up. Keep all five and there is only one answer.

1
Lorentz Invariance
The laws of physics are the same for everyone. No matter how fast you travel or which direction you face, the rules do not change. There is no preferred speed, direction, or frame of reference. Every experiment testing this has confirmed it to extraordinary precision.
2
Diffeomorphism Invariance
You can label spacetime coordinates however you want — the physics stays the same. Like how a mountain doesn't change if you use Cartesian vs. polar coordinates on a map. This is the mathematical heart of general relativity: geometry is real, coordinate labels are just human conventions.
3
Locality
Things interact at a point, not across gaps. To push someone, you have to touch them. Forces are transmitted through local contact, not by instantaneous action at a distance. Information respects a speed limit (the speed of light).
4
Unitarity
Probabilities always add up to 100%. If something has a 30% chance of happening and a 70% chance of not happening, those numbers must sum to 100%, always, in every situation. This sounds obvious, but many proposed modifications to gravity silently break it — creating scenarios where something has a 110% chance of happening, or a -5% chance. That is physically meaningless.
5
Perturbative Renormalizability
The theory can handle infinities with a finite number of knobs. This is the property Einstein's gravity lacks. In renormalizable theories (like the theory of electrons and light), infinities appear in calculations but can always be absorbed into a finite number of measurable quantities — mass, charge, etc. Once you measure those, everything else is predicted. This is a requirement for the theory to actually be calculable.

Part 3

The Clue: Spacetime Gets Simpler at Tiny Scales

A mysterious pattern keeps showing up across completely different approaches to quantum gravity.

The spectral dimension flows from 4 to 2. We live in 4-dimensional spacetime (3 space + 1 time). But multiple independent research programs — using completely different mathematical frameworks — all find that at extremely tiny scales (near the Planck length, ~10-35 meters), spacetime behaves as if it only has 2 dimensions.

What does "behaves like 2D" mean?

Imagine you release an ant on a surface and track how far it wanders. On a 2D sheet, it covers less area than on a 3D sponge. The "spectral dimension" measures this: drop a random walker on your spacetime and see how its wandering distance scales with time. At everyday scales, the answer is 4. At quantum-gravity scales, it shrinks to 2. It is as if spacetime itself thins out, becoming a kind of 2D sheet when you zoom in far enough.

These programs were built by different people, with different assumptions, using different math. Some use continuous spacetime, some use discrete building blocks. Some keep Lorentz invariance, some don't. Yet they all converge on the same number: ds = 2 in the ultraviolet.

Why this matters: When 7+ independent approaches to a problem all get the same answer using different methods, that answer is probably telling you something real about nature — not an artifact of any one method. This is the kind of convergence that makes physicists sit up and pay attention.

Part 4

The Hard Choice: You Can't Avoid Ghosts

The clue from Part 3 forces a brutal mathematical consequence.

The No-Go Theorem: Any Lorentz-invariant, ghost-free theory CANNOT produce the spectral dimension flow ds = 4 → 2. This is a rigorous mathematical proof, not an opinion. If you want ds = 2 while keeping Lorentz invariance, your theory must contain ghost-like degrees of freedom.

What is a "ghost"?

In quantum field theory, a ghost is a particle that carries negative probability. Normal particles contribute positively to the total probability of all outcomes summing to 100%. A ghost contributes negatively — which means probabilities no longer add up. You might calculate that an event has a -5% chance of happening. That is not physically meaningful. It means reality itself would be inconsistent.

Why ds = 2 forces ghosts (the intuition)

Getting ds = 2 requires the gravitational force to fall off much faster at high energies (technically: the propagator must go as 1/p4 instead of 1/p2). The only way to achieve this while keeping spacetime smooth and local is to add extra terms to Einstein's equation — terms that inevitably introduce a new massive particle with the wrong "sign." That wrong-sign particle is the ghost.

The three escape routes

Break Lorentz invariance
Pick a preferred direction in spacetime. This is what Horava-Lifshitz gravity does. But experiments (gamma-ray bursts traveling billions of light-years) have constrained Lorentz violation so tightly that it would need to kick in at energies above the Planck scale — making it essentially non-viable.
Give up on ds = 2
Abandon the clue. But then you lose the one constructive constraint that actually narrows the landscape of possible theories. You are back to guessing.
Accept the ghost, but tame it
Let the ghost exist in the math — but find a way to prevent it from ever showing up in the real world. Make it "purely virtual": it influences intermediate calculations (like a scaffolding in construction) but can never be directly observed or produced. This is the fakeon.

Part 5

The Answer: Quadratic Gravity + Fakeons

Start with Einstein's equation. Add carefully chosen terms. Apply a new quantization rule. Get the only viable quantum gravity.

Step 1: Modify Einstein's equation

Einstein's equation describes gravity using the curvature of spacetime (a quantity called R). It is "linear" in curvature — R appears to the first power. Quadratic gravity adds terms where curvature appears squared — R2 and a related quantity called C2 (the Weyl tensor squared). These extra terms matter only at extreme energies near the Planck scale and are invisible in everyday physics.

Analogy: Think of it like upgrading a simple linear model (y = mx + b) to a quadratic one (y = ax2 + bx + c). The quadratic terms only matter when x is large — at small x the behavior is effectively linear. Quadratic gravity adds higher-order curvature terms that only matter when spacetime curvature is extreme.

Why this specific modification?

ds = 2 forces 4-derivative terms (curvature squared). Diffeomorphism invariance restricts them to just two possibilities: R2 and C2. (A third candidate, the "Gauss-Bonnet" combination, is trivial in 4 dimensions.) Renormalizability is automatic because the new coupling constants are dimensionless — the infinity problem from Part 1 goes away.

Step 2: The fakeon prescription

The C2 term introduces a massive spin-2 particle with negative probability — a ghost (from Part 4). The fakeon prescription (developed by Anselmi and Piva, 2017-2018) is a specific mathematical rule for how to handle this particle in calculations.

What the fakeon does: It says "this ghost particle can appear as a virtual intermediate step in calculations, but it can never be produced as a real particle." It is purely mathematical scaffolding. Think of it like an imaginary number in engineering: complex numbers appear everywhere in circuit calculations, but you never measure an imaginary voltage. The fakeon is the gravitational analogue — it shows up in the math, influences the results, but has no physical reality.

Part 6

The Uniqueness Proof: 8 Roads, 1 Destination

This is not just "a good theory." Given the 5 rules from Part 2, it is the only possible theory. Eight completely independent approaches all arrive at the same place.

1
Spectral dimension. Start with ds = 4 → 2 as a constructive axiom → forces the quadratic action → forces fakeon quantization.
2
Entanglement bootstrap. Start with the principle that the vacuum is maximally entangled + demand self-consistency + renormalizability → uniquely selects QG+F, including the fakeon prescription.
3
Most general renormalizable action. Write down every possible gravitational action with dimensionless couplings → only one exists (Stelle, 1977).
4
Asymptotic freedom. Demand gravity gets weaker at high energies (like the strong nuclear force) → uniquely arrives at QG+F (Fradkin-Tseytlin, 1982).
5
Lee-Wick gravity. Try a different quantization rule for the ghost → it violates unitarity → the only viable prescription collapses back to the fakeon.
6
Asymptotic safety. Look for the perturbative sector of the gravitational fixed point → it is quadratic gravity (Sen-Wetterich-Yamada, 2022).
7
Classical scale invariance. Demand no mass scales appear in the fundamental action → uniquely selects QG+F (Salvio-Strumia, 2014).
8
Weinberg's asymptotic safety program. Its perturbative realization is QG+F.
The "Quantum Lovelock's Theorem": In classical physics, there is a famous result (Lovelock's theorem) proving that Einstein's equation is the unique classical theory of gravity in 4 dimensions. QG+F is the quantum analogue: it is the unique quantum gravity in 4 dimensions. The space of perturbative quantum gravities has been fully mapped. It contains exactly one theory.

The make-or-break number: r

The tensor-to-scalar ratio (r) measures the strength of gravitational waves left over from the Big Bang, imprinted as subtle swirl patterns in the cosmic microwave background (the oldest light in the universe). The Japanese satellite LiteBIRD, launching in the early 2030s, will measure r precisely enough to test QG+F.

If r < 0.003
QG+F is confirmed. The perturbative theory is correct. The simplest version of the theory wins.
If r is between 0.003 and 0.005
The six-derivative extension (a natural refinement of QG+F) is favored. Still the same family of theories.
If r > 0.005: QG+F in its perturbative form is wrong. One of the 5 foundational rules is broken. This would actually be even more exciting — it would mean one of our deepest assumptions about physics is incorrect, opening entirely new territory.
The Higgs mass: In 2010, Shaposhnikov and Wetterich used asymptotic safety boundary conditions (the non-perturbative cousin of QG+F) to predict the Higgs boson mass at ~126 GeV. Two years later, the LHC measured it at 125.1 GeV. This is the only confirmed particle physics prediction from any quantum gravity framework.

Part 8

What's Still Unknown

QG+F answers the question "what is the quantum theory of gravity?" It does not answer everything.

The cosmological constant problem
The universe is expanding faster and faster, driven by "dark energy." Its measured value is 10-122 times what naive quantum calculations predict. This is the most famous unsolved problem in physics. QG+F solves the "old" version (why the constant is not infinite) using a unimodular formulation, but cannot explain why it has the specific tiny value we observe. No continuum theory can. This is a shared problem across all approaches.
Non-perturbative completion
QG+F is a perturbative theory — it works by calculating corrections in a series, each one smaller than the last, like a Taylor expansion. But there may be non-perturbative effects (phenomena that cannot be seen at any order in the series). The strong nuclear force has this: the perturbative theory (asymptotic freedom) is well-understood, but phenomena like quark confinement require the full non-perturbative picture. QG+F likely connects to "asymptotic safety" at the non-perturbative level, but this has not been rigorously proven. It is the gravitational analogue of the gap between perturbative QCD and the full theory of the strong force.
Where does the Standard Model come from?
QG+F tells you how gravity works at the quantum level. It does not explain why the other forces (electromagnetism, strong, weak) have the specific structure they do — why there are three generations of quarks and leptons, why the coupling constants have the values they have. The gauge group SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) is not derived from QG+F. This is the frontier.
The one-prediction problem
All non-CMB experimental signatures of QG+F are suppressed by 15+ orders of magnitude below current experimental reach. The tensor-to-scalar ratio r, measured from the cosmic microwave background, is the only realistic near-term test. This is not a flaw of QG+F specifically — it is the nature of quantum gravity: the effects live at the Planck scale (10-35 meters), and we probe nature at 10-18 meters at best.
The bottom line: The landscape of perturbative quantum gravity has been fully mapped. It contains exactly one theory. That theory has one confirmed prediction (the Higgs mass), one decisive upcoming test (r, ~2037), and three deep open problems shared by the entire field. Whether r confirms or refutes QG+F, the measurement will reshape theoretical physics.
