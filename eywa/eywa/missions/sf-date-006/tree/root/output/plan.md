### Step 4: synthesize-meta-analysis
Goal: Three complete SF date plans have been designed — one for an adventurous adrenaline junkie, one for a quiet intellectual, and one for a foodie artist. The actual plans will be provided as context. Your job is to compare them and extract reusable principles of great date design.

Produce a meta-analysis with the following sections:

1. **What makes each date cohesive** — For each of the three dates, name the central emotional thread or principle that ties its stops together. What would be lost if you swapped one stop for a random substitute?

2. **Cross-archetype patterns** — What structural or design patterns appear across all three dates, even though the content is totally different? Look for things like: pacing choices, the role of transitions, how stakes are managed, when conversation happens vs. when experience takes over.

3. **3-5 reusable date design principles** — Extract the most generalizable insights. Each principle should: (a) be stated as a clear claim, not a vague platitude ("great dates have a narrative arc" is better than "be intentional"), (b) be grounded in at least one concrete example from the three date plans, (c) be testable — you could use it to evaluate a proposed date and get a real answer.

4. **What separates a great date from a list of stops** — One tight paragraph answering this directly.

Success criteria: The principles are genuinely extractable from the three dates (not invented from thin air) and specific enough to use as a design checklist for a new date.
