## Judgment calls

1. **Where to introduce `rendered_prompt`.** I put the explanation in a mini-subsection under "What a slot is" rather than dropping it inline into the bullet list, because it's a materialization-time concept that deserves two paragraphs and pulling it out reads cleaner than making the `meta_prompt` bullet five sentences long. I kept the `meta_prompt` bullet focused on "what it is" and let the subsection handle "how it becomes the worker's input."
2. **Concrete rendered prompt choice.** I used the Riemann hypothesis task text from the subgraph-instance-contract example verbatim, so the example in this doc is consistent with the contract's example payload. This keeps one canonical example across the docs, which should make the codebase easier to learn from. The `worker_1` slot was the obvious choice because its meta prompt only references `{task.text}`, so the substitution story is cleanest — showing a synthesizer example would have required handwaving the incremental substitution of `{inputs.worker_N.output}`, which I handle in a separate one-sentence note instead.
3. **Escape-hatch visibility.** I mentioned the `just_execute_with_buddy_instruction` pattern explicitly in the "Being honest about the seed library" subsection, naming it and pointing at `02-subgraph-types.md`'s Option 3 commitment section. I didn't invent a new seed bundle — Builder H explicitly flagged this as a Scientist-to-publish pattern, and the guidance said not to invent seed bundles in this doc. I leaned toward being specific about what the escape hatch looks like (variable name `{buddy_free_instruction}`, binding_type literal) because it's better for readers to understand what the pattern looks like than to leave it abstract, but I stopped short of writing a full bundle example.
4. **How to handle the "physics / applications / history" researcher framing.** I chose to flag it directly: the seed bundle doesn't assign these angles, and a richer bundle with `researcher_physics` / `researcher_applications` / `researcher_history` slots would be a Scientist-published variant. I considered softening this (e.g., "the seed bundle is generic; richer variants exist") but the guidance said to be honest, and the honest version is more useful as a learning surface.
5. **The artifact-binding paragraph.** I folded this into the "what a worker's prompt looks like" section as a single sentence after the concrete example, rather than making it its own subsection. It's a small enough point that a subsection felt heavy. The load-bearing claim is "there is no separate Buddy-authored 'referenced artifacts' section — the bundle decides where artifact content lands" and that's captured in one line.
6. **Length budget.** Original 129 lines → final 147 lines, ~14% growth. Well under the 20% cap. I tightened the "being honest" subsection and the rendered-prompt example after an initial pass came in at ~23% growth.

## Things that might warrant reconsideration

- **The worker's rendered-prompt example includes a `[slot role: ...]` header line.** The contract doesn't actually say the runtime prefixes the role label onto the worker's prompt — it just says `rendered_prompt` is the substituted `meta_prompt`. I added the role header as a display convention to make the example readable and to show the reader where `role` shows up, but technically that line is a doc-level convention, not something the runtime is guaranteed to produce. If a future implementation doesn't prefix the role, this example will be misleading. A quieter alternative would be to drop the `[slot role: ...]` line from the example and just have the rendered meta prompt stand on its own — worth flagging for Sean.
- **The "appending a glossary can't be expressed on day one" claim.** Technically Buddy could pick `just_execute` on turn 2, with the task text being the ORIGINAL blog post task, and hope that `worker` does something useful — but in practice `{task.text}` resolves to the run's raw task text, not the prior turn's output, so worker would just redo the blog post from scratch. I mentioned this obliquely in the final paragraph ("In practice Buddy would either declare done… or pick a second `delegate_fanout_synthesize` or `just_execute` turn knowing the workers will have to infer from the task text alone that a glossary is wanted — which they probably won't") but it could be worth spelling out that `{task.text}` is run-scoped, not turn-scoped, if readers find this confusing. I judged that cross-referencing `subgraph-type-contract.md` was enough.
- **The "artifact binding" one-liner.** I say "when a slot's meta prompt includes a buddy-bound variable whose `binding_type` is `"artifact"`, the runtime loads that artifact's content and substitutes it into the rendered prompt just like any other value." That's consistent with what Builder H wrote in `02-subgraph-types.md` and what the subgraph-type-contract says about resolution, BUT the contracts don't currently explicitly say "the runtime loads artifact content for artifact bindings at substitution time" — they say "buddy-bound variables are looked up in `context_bindings` at materialization time" and leave the content-loading step to the runtime's implementation. My one-liner is slightly ahead of where the contract language is. I believe it's correct (it'd be nonsensical for the runtime to substitute an artifact_id string into a meta prompt instead of loading the artifact body) but Sean or Builder H might want to tighten the contract language separately. Flagging for followup.

## Real limitations of the seed library worth flagging to Sean and future Scientist work

1. **No way to reference prior-turn output in a clean Option-3 way without a new bundle.** The "add a glossary to the draft" problem requires either a dedicated bundle with `{prior_turns[0].terminal_output}` hardcoded in, or the `just_execute_with_buddy_instruction` escape hatch. Neither is in the seed library. This means the seed library can express single-turn-quality tasks and parallelizable-then-synthesize tasks, but cannot express "refine the output of turn N in a specific way" cleanly on day one. The bench probably won't surface this as a problem until Buddy attempts a task that genuinely wants refinement, at which point it'll be obvious.
2. **The three workers in `delegate_fanout_synthesize` are not differentiated.** They all say "handle the first / second / third independent piece" and the actual angle differentiation is left to the model's interpretation of "independent piece." This is likely to produce noisy, redundant outputs on tasks where the angles aren't obvious from the task text. A `delegate_fanout_synthesize_with_angles` variant with buddy-bound `{angle_1}`, `{angle_2}`, `{angle_3}` slots (literal bindings) would let Buddy name the angles per turn while still keeping the synthesizer's meta prompt fixed. Or three specific named researchers as Builder H noted. Either is Scientist work, not day-one seed work.
3. **No iterative-critique pattern.** Current super-eywa can do "execute, review, revise" loops through orchestration decisions. Option 3's answer is "nest a host and let the inner Buddy loop iterate," but the seed library doesn't have a `review` bundle or a `revise` bundle, so the inner Buddy would only have `just_execute` and `delegate_fanout_synthesize` to work with — which doesn't express "critique the plan" naturally. First Scientist-published bundle after day one will probably be something in this neighborhood.
