# What Is Time?

---

## The Question Everyone Knows How to Answer — Until They Try

You already know a great deal about time.

You know it flows — always forward, never back. You know you can't unscramble an egg or unsay a word. You know the future is open in a way the past is not: tomorrow could go many ways, but yesterday is fixed. You know that near massive objects, clocks tick slower — a fact so precisely measured that your phone's GPS corrects for it sixty times a second. You know the present feels different from memory and anticipation — sharper, more vivid, more *real*.

These are not separate observations. They are all expressions of a single underlying structure. This essay is about what that structure is.

Physics has been spectacularly successful at *using* time while remaining unable to say what it *is*. The equations describe how things change *in* time. They don't explain time itself.

Over the past century, this gap has sharpened into a crisis. General relativity says time is dynamic — it bends and dilates. Quantum mechanics says time is a fixed background. These two descriptions are incompatible, and when you try to reconcile them directly — applying quantum mechanics to the universe as a whole — you get an equation (the Wheeler-DeWitt equation) with no time variable at all. The quantum state of the universe doesn't change. It just *is*.

This essay offers an answer. It is built from established physics combined with a single philosophical commitment that is clearly identified and honestly assessed. The answer has two layers: a ground floor that any physicist can accept, and an upper floor that requires a specific philosophical choice. The reader decides how far up to go.

The short version:

> **Time is irreversible determination — the ongoing, physically constrained process by which the indeterminate becomes determinate, as encountered from within.**

That sentence is compressed. The rest of this essay unpacks it.

---

## The Wrong War

For most of the twentieth century, the debate about time was stuck in a binary. On one side: the **block universe**, where past, present, and future all exist equally — a frozen four-dimensional block, with no real flow and no special present. On the other side: **becoming**, where the passage of time is real, the present is ontologically special, and the future is genuinely open.

Both sides had serious arguments. The block-universe camp pointed to relativity: since different observers disagree about which events are simultaneous, there is no universal "now." The becoming camp pointed to experience: the passage of time is the most immediate datum of consciousness, and dismissing it as illusion is dismissing the most obvious fact about existence.

This war is a dead end. In 2021, Philipp Höhn and collaborators proved something that dissolves it. Working within the framework of *quantum reference frames*, they showed that for constrained quantum systems — the kind the Wheeler-DeWitt equation describes — the "timeless" description and the "temporal" description are **mathematically equivalent**. Related by a precise physical transformation, the way coordinate systems in general relativity are related by coordinate changes. Neither is privileged. Neither is more fundamental. Asking "is the universe *really* static or *really* evolving?" is like asking "is electromagnetism *really* an electric field or *really* a magnetic field?" The answer depends on your reference frame.

This clears the ground. The productive question is not "does time exist?" but **"why does time have the specific properties it has?"**

Why does it flow in one direction? Why can't we reverse it? Why is the future open? Why one dimension when space has three? Why does it slow near massive objects? What is "now"?

These questions have answers. They trace to the same source: the structure of information processing in a finite universe — and, if we're willing to go further, the nature of what that processing *consists of*.

## The Engine You Can't Shut Off

Start with the deepest practical question: why can't we go backwards?

The textbook answer is the second law of thermodynamics. There are vastly more disordered states than ordered ones, so systems drift toward disorder. This is correct but unsatisfying. It tells you reversal is *unlikely* — overwhelmingly, astronomically unlikely — but not that it is *impossible*. Given enough time, statistical mechanics says even a broken egg will spontaneously reassemble. The irreversibility is statistical, not fundamental.

There is a stronger answer. It comes from information theory, built from three results that lock together like gears in an engine.

**Gear 1: Landauer's Principle** (1961). Erasing information has an irreducible physical cost: resetting one bit requires dissipating at least *kT* ln 2 of energy as heat. This is not an engineering limitation — it is a law of physics, confirmed experimentally (Bérut et al. 2012; Aimet et al. 2025). Erasure is *logically irreversible*: a many-to-one mapping that destroys information.

**Gear 2: Bennett's Reversibility Theorem** (1973). Any computation *can* be performed reversibly — but only if you keep a complete record of every intermediate step. Reversible computation requires saving everything.

**Gear 3: The Bekenstein Bound** (1981). Any physical system of finite energy *E* in a region of radius *R* can store at most *S* ≤ 2π*kRE*/ℏ*c* bits of information. A finite region has a maximum information capacity.

Now watch what happens when these three gears mesh:

1. The universe processes information — every physical interaction is a computation on quantum states.
2. Bennett says reversible processing requires keeping complete records.
3. Bekenstein says storage capacity is finite.
4. Therefore, the universe *cannot* keep complete records. It must erase.
5. Landauer says erasure is irreversible — it dissipates energy as heat.

**Therefore: the universe's computation is necessarily irreversible.** Not statistically. Not probably. *Necessarily.* Any finite universe that processes information must erase information, and erasure is physically irreversible. The destroyed information no longer exists in any recoverable form.

This is the **Landauer-Bennett-Bekenstein engine** — the LBB engine. Not a statistical tendency but a structural necessity. A broken egg cannot reassemble because the information specifying its pre-broken state has been physically destroyed, dissipated as heat into a Bekenstein-bounded environment. To reverse the process, you would need information that no longer exists anywhere.

Each gear is proved or experimentally confirmed. Together they give a different kind of answer than thermodynamics: irreversibility is not about the improbability of spontaneous order but about the *destruction of information* in a finite system.

## Why Time Has One Lane

Space has three dimensions. You can move north, east, or up. Time has one. You can only move forward along it. This is such a basic feature of reality that it's easy to overlook how strange it is. Why one? Why not two temporal dimensions, or zero?

In standard physics, the answer is: that's just how it is. The spacetime manifold has signature (−,+,+,+) — one temporal and three spatial dimensions — and no explanation is offered.

The computational framework offers one.

In 1998, Norman Margolus and Lev Levitin proved a theorem that sets an absolute speed limit on change. The **Margolus-Levitin theorem** states: any quantum system with average energy *E* requires at least a time *t* = πℏ/2*E* to transition to a distinguishable state. The maximum rate of state transitions is 2*E*/πℏ. This is a speed limit on change itself — not on how fast a signal propagates, but on how fast anything can become something different. It was confirmed experimentally in 2021 by Ness and colleagues using a trapped-atom system.

A system that processes information transitions through distinguishable states one at a time. The Margolus-Levitin bound says it can do this at a rate proportional to its energy — but crucially, *one transition at a time*. The computation is sequential. Step by step.

This is why time has one dimension. Time is the axis along which irreducible computation proceeds, and computation is sequential. Space, by contrast, admits parallelism: multiple regions can process information simultaneously and independently. This is why space is multi-dimensional — spatial dimensions correspond to independent degrees of freedom operating in parallel. But for any given subsystem, the computation threads through a single sequence of states. That sequence is time.

This doesn't prove from first principles that a universe *must* have one time dimension. What it does is provide a structural explanation where the standard account has a brute fact.

## Why the Future Doesn't Exist Yet

Imagine you had unlimited computational power — more than any computer that could ever be built. Could you predict the future?

No. And the reason is not merely practical.

**Computational irreducibility.** Some computations have no shortcut. The only way to find out what a computationally irreducible system will do after *N* steps is to run all *N* steps. There is no formula, no compression, no clever algorithm that lets you skip ahead. This is not a conjecture — it is a proven property of many computational systems, following from deep results in mathematics (the undecidability of the halting problem, Gödel's incompleteness theorems). Many physical systems are computationally irreducible.

**Wolpert's impossibility theorem** (2008). David Wolpert proved that no inference device — no computer, no mind, no physical system of any kind — that is embedded within a universe can correctly predict all properties of that universe's future states. This is not about limitations in computing power. It is a mathematical impossibility, structurally similar to Gödel's incompleteness: a system cannot fully model itself.

Together, these two results establish: for any subsystem of the universe, the future is genuinely open. Not merely unknowable in practice — unknowable *in principle*, from the inside. The present is a real computational frontier: everything before it has been computed; everything after it has not.

This is the ground floor of the interpretation — established physics, zero metaphysical commitments. But notice something: these results tell you that you *can't access* the future. They don't tell you *why there's nothing there to access*. Is the future a hidden pattern waiting to be revealed, or is it genuinely undetermined — a space of real possibilities not yet resolved?

The answer to that question takes us upstairs. But first, two more ground-floor results.

## Why Clocks Near Heavy Things Run Slow

General relativity tells us time runs slower in gravitational fields. A clock on Earth's surface ticks about 45 microseconds per day slower than one in orbit. GR describes this beautifully through spacetime curvature — but doesn't say *why* curvature should affect the rate of time.

The Margolus-Levitin theorem provides a physical picture. The maximum computation rate is proportional to energy: ν_max = 2*E*/πℏ. In a gravitational field, energy is redshifted by √(1 − 2*GM*/*rc*²). Reduced energy means reduced computation rate. The system near the massive object is processing information more slowly. Since time is the progression of this computation, time runs slower.

From the local perspective, nothing has changed — local energy and processing rate are the same. Time dilation is relational, showing up only in the comparison between frames. This is exactly what GR says.

This doesn't independently derive time dilation — the ML bound inherits it from the metric. What it provides is a computational reading that connects time dilation to the same framework explaining irreversibility and the open future. From causal set theory, there's a complementary structural picture: proper time along a worldline is proportional to the longest chain of causal elements traversed. Near massive objects, fewer causal elements, less proper time.

## The Ground Floor — and the Staircase

Everything described so far is established physics — proved theorems and experimentally confirmed principles. Together, they explain why time is irreversible, one-dimensional, bounded in rate, and open toward the future.

This is the **ground floor**. It requires zero metaphysical commitments. Anyone — Everettian, Bohmian, instrumentalist, realist — can accept it.

But it tells you how time *works* without telling you what time *is*. What is the process the LBB engine is running? What makes the future different from the past — not just inaccessible, but ontologically different?

To answer those questions, you need to go upstairs. And going upstairs requires a choice.

---

## The Discovery That Changed the Question

Here is something that deserves attention.

Two intellectual traditions — developed independently, by different communities, with different methods and different vocabularies — converge on the same deep structure when asked about time.

**From information theory and computation:** Time is the sequential, irreversible processing of information in a finite system. The LBB engine provides the mechanism. The ML bound provides the rate limit. Computational irreducibility provides the openness.

**From process philosophy and the physics of causation:** Time is the ongoing resolution of possibilities into actualities. Each event inherits its causal past and generates a new fact. The process is inherently directional — from open possibility to settled actuality — and the structure of causation (a partial order on events) naturally produces one-dimensional maximal chains. This tradition draws on Whitehead's process philosophy, the Heisenberg interpretation of quantum mechanics, and the causal-set approach to quantum gravity.

These two accounts were not designed to be compatible. Yet when you lay them side by side, the structural correspondence is nearly total: input state maps to inherited past; computation maps to the event of becoming; information erasure maps to the extinguishing of unrealized possibilities; the computational frontier maps to the present — the locus of active becoming. Point by point, the vocabularies track.

This convergence was not manufactured. The two traditions didn't know they were building the same thing. When independent lines of investigation converge on the same structure, that is evidence — not proof, but evidence — that the structure is real.

This interpretation proposes that the two accounts are not describing different things but **the same process from different angles**. The computational account describes it from the outside — constraints, bounds, information-theoretic properties. The process account describes it from the inside — what the process consists of, what kind of transition it involves.

This is a specific identification claim. It could be wrong. But it earns its keep if the combined account explains more than either alone — particularly on the three questions where each individually struggles: what is the "now," why does time have a direction, and why does it flow.

## The Upper Floor: What Time Actually Is

Going upstairs requires a single philosophical commitment: **quantum possibilities are ontologically real.**

This is the commitment of Heisenberg, who called quantum states *potentia* — real tendencies toward actualization, not mere descriptions of our ignorance. It is the commitment of Ruth Kastner's transactional interpretation, where offer waves and confirmation waves represent genuine potentialities resolved in transactions. It is the commitment of objective collapse theories (GRW, Penrose, Diósi), where physical collapse IS the moment of resolution. And it is, arguably, the natural reading of quantum mechanics before the interpretation debates muddied the waters: the quantum state describes something real, and measurement resolves it.

This commitment is incompatible with Everettian many-worlds (all possibilities realized, none extinguished), pilot-wave theory (definite trajectories, no genuine indeterminacy), and strict instrumentalism (wavefunction as calculational tool only). If you hold one of those positions, the upper floor is not available — but the ground floor remains fully intact.

For those willing to make the commitment, here is what it buys.

**An identification of what time IS.** Not just constraints on a process, but the process identified: time is the resolution of real possibilities into actualities. Each moment, across the universe, quantum potentialities that are genuinely real are being resolved into determinate facts. The LBB engine is the information-theoretic signature of this resolution. The Margolus-Levitin bound is the rate at which it can proceed. Computational irreducibility is why it can't be shortcut. The process is what the ground-floor physics is *about*.

**A deeper grounding for irreversibility.** On the ground floor, irreversibility is about information destruction — the LBB engine forces erasure, and erasure is physically irreversible. On the upper floor, we can say *why* information destruction is irreversible: because the possibilities that the information described have been genuinely extinguished. They are not hidden, not recoverable, not stored somewhere else. They are ontologically gone. Landauer's *kT* ln 2 is, on this reading, the thermodynamic cost of extinguishing a real possibility. This is a substantive philosophical claim — it could be wrong — but it is not merely a relabeling. It provides a deeper answer to *why* erasure is one-way: the possibilities that were erased no longer exist.

These two gains — identifying the process and deepening the grounding — set up the three most important contributions of the upper floor: an account of the "now," an intrinsic direction for the arrow, and a richer explanation of flow.

## What Is "Now"?

This is the question that most interpretations of time handle worst. The block universe says the present is merely indexical — "now" is just wherever you happen to be, like "here" in space, with no special ontological status. The thermodynamic account doesn't address it at all. The ground-floor computational account offers a structural answer — the present is the computational frontier between the already-computed and the not-yet-computed — but struggles to explain what makes any particular frontier *special*.

The upper floor does better.

If quantum possibilities are ontologically real, then past, present, and future have genuinely different modes of being:

**The past** is settled. Its possibilities have been resolved into facts. Some of its information has been irreversibly erased by the LBB engine. It is the domain of accomplished, partially recorded facts — real, determinate, but increasingly inaccessible.

**The future** is open. Its possibilities are real but unresolved. There is nothing determinate there to access — not because we lack the cleverness to find it, but because the resolution hasn't happened yet. Computational irreducibility ensures no shortcut exists. Wolpert's theorem ensures no embedded system can predict it. But these are information-theoretic barriers. The deeper point is ontological: the future is a space of genuine potentiality.

**The present** is active. It is where the resolution is happening — where the LBB engine is running, where the ML bound is constraining the rate of resolution, where real possibilities are being extinguished and new facts are being generated. It is the frontier between the settled and the open, and it is ontologically distinct from both.

This account of the "now" is **local and perspectival**. There is no global present — no cosmic "now" slicing the universe into past and future. Each event of resolution is its own present. Different worldlines have different presents. This is compatible with special relativity because the present is defined by causal structure (which is Lorentz-invariant), not by simultaneity surfaces (which are frame-dependent).

"But why *this* now?" Because "now" is not a property of a pre-existing timeless moment. It is a feature of the resolution process itself. You are always at the resolution event you are currently undergoing. The question "why is this moment special?" presupposes a view from outside the process — as if you could stand above the sequence of events and ask why the spotlight falls *here*. From inside, there is no spotlight. There is just the process, and you are always at your own frontier.

This is not a complete answer to the mystery of the present. The *structure* of the "now" — why it sits between a settled past and an open future, why it is local, why it is the locus of activity — is explained. But the *vividness* of the present — why this moment feels so much more real than memory or anticipation — may be at the boundary of what any physical interpretation can explain. That boundary is the hard problem of consciousness, and this interpretation does not pretend to cross it.

## Why Time Flows

Temporal flow — the sense that time *passes*, that each moment gives way to the next — is the most universal feature of conscious experience. Yet physics has had almost nothing to say about it. The block universe dismisses it as illusion. Thermodynamics reduces it to entropy increase.

The ground floor offers a structural identification. Flow consists of: states succeeding one another in fixed order, the future being inaccessible, no skipping steps, the process genuinely *happening*. Being a bounded subsystem in a computationally irreducible process consists of *exactly the same things*: sequential state transitions (ML bound), inaccessible future (irreducibility + Wolpert), no skipping (each step must complete), outcomes generated step by step.

The ground-floor claim: these are the same thing. Flow *is* what computational irreducibility is like from the inside.

The upper floor enriches this. If the process is not just computation but the resolution of real possibilities into actualities, then flow has a phenomenological structure that mirrors the structure of becoming:

**Retention** — the trailing awareness of the just-past — is the experiential expression of inheritance. Each resolution event carries forward the results of prior resolutions. The past is not gone; it is incorporated.

**The vivid present** — what Husserl called the "primal impression" — is the experiential expression of active resolution. It is the moment where possibilities are being extinguished and new facts generated.

**Anticipation** — the sense of openness toward what comes next — is the experiential expression of the genuinely open future. Real but unresolved potentialities that will be resolved next.

This three-part structure — inheriting the past, actively resolving the present, open toward the future — is not imposed on the physics. It *is* the physics, as experienced from within. The ground floor gives the structure; the upper floor explains why the structure has the *character* it does — why flow feels like becoming rather than mere succession.

The honest caveat: this still faces the hard problem of consciousness. The interpretation explains the *structure* of temporal experience — why it has this three-part form — but not *why* this structure is accompanied by subjective experience at all. That question may be beyond what any physical interpretation can answer.

## Why There's a Direction

The LBB engine explains why individual processes are irreversible. But the *arrow* of time is a bigger question: why does the entire observable universe exhibit irreversibility in the same direction? Why does entropy increase toward the future everywhere, not just locally?

The standard answer has two parts: the engine (LBB mechanism) explains *that* processes are irreversible; the fuel — the **Past Hypothesis**, that the universe began in a very special, low-entropy state — explains *why* the arrow points the way it does.

The ground floor needs the Past Hypothesis just as much as the standard account does. It reframes it computationally — the universe began with nearly empty Bekenstein-bounded storage; the LBB engine has been filling it ever since — but this is re-description, not derivation.

The upper floor adds something genuine: an **intrinsic direction**.

If possibilities are ontologically real, then the resolution of possibilities into actualities has a built-in asymmetry. "Possible" and "actual" are not symmetric categories. You can go from possible to actual — that is what resolution is. But you cannot go from actual back to possible — you cannot un-make a fact. An egg that has broken was once whole among many possibilities; once the breaking is actual, the possibility of wholeness is extinguished. The direction from possibility to actuality is constitutive of the process, not imposed on it from outside.

This intrinsic direction does not replace the Past Hypothesis — you still need an initial condition to explain why the global arrow points *this way*. But it provides something the ground floor lacks: a reason why the process is directional *at all*. On the ground floor, you need boundary conditions to set a direction. On the upper floor, direction is built into the nature of what's happening: possibilities becoming actualities. The arrow is not just a boundary condition; it is an expression of the fundamental character of the process.

What this achieves: an intrinsic direction that thermodynamic accounts lack. What it doesn't: a derivation of the Past Hypothesis. Why the universe started far from equilibrium remains open.

## The Honest Limits

Every interpretation of time must acknowledge what it does not explain. Here are the genuine limits of this one — not soft-pedaled, not buried in caveats, but stated plainly.

**1. The hard problem of consciousness remains.** The interpretation explains the *structure* of temporal experience — why it has the three-part form of retention, vivid present, and anticipation. It does not explain *why* this structure is accompanied by subjective experience. The vividness of the "now" — why the present feels more real than memory or expectation — remains at the boundary of what any physical interpretation can explain.

**2. The philosophical commitment is real and costly.** The upper floor depends entirely on a single commitment: that quantum possibilities are ontologically real. This commitment is supported by the Heisenberg interpretation, by Kastner's transactional interpretation, and by objective collapse theories. But it is not derivable from physics alone. It is incompatible with Everettian many-worlds, pilot-wave theory, and strict instrumentalism. For a reader who holds one of those positions, the upper floor is not available. The ground floor — established physics, no metaphysical commitments — remains fully intact, but every improvement that the upper floor offers is lost.

**3. No novel predictions.** The interpretation generates no experimental predictions beyond what its ingredients individually provide. Its value is explanatory, not predictive. It unifies disparate phenomena under a single framework. It identifies physical mechanisms where the standard account has statistical tendencies. But it produces no new numbers. This limits its status to *interpretation* rather than *theory*.

**4. The Past Hypothesis is not resolved.** The interpretation provides an intrinsic direction for the arrow of time — the asymmetry between possible and actual — but does not explain why the universe began in a low-entropy state. The Past Hypothesis remains an initial condition that must be assumed, not derived.

**5. The identification claim is proposed, not proved.** The claim that information erasure *is* the extinguishing of real possibilities — that Landauer's cost is the thermodynamic cost of possibility-extinguishing — is a substantive philosophical identification. It is not a derivation. It could be wrong. If it is wrong, the upper floor collapses and you are left with the ground floor, which still stands.

**6. The circularity is acknowledged.** Describing time as a temporal process is circular. The interpretation argues this circularity is virtuous — like "heat is molecular kinetic energy," which uses the concept of motion but genuinely identifies heat with something specific. But someone who finds any circularity unacceptable will not be satisfied.

**7. The factorization problem.** The interpretation presupposes that the universe can be decomposed into subsystems. In fundamental quantum gravity, this decomposition may itself be emergent. The interpretation describes time *within* the regime of well-defined subsystems but not the origin of that regime.

## What This Means

Return to the compressed version:

> **Time is irreversible determination — the ongoing, physically constrained process by which the indeterminate becomes determinate, as encountered from within.**

Unpacked: **"Irreversible"** — each step erases information and extinguishes real possibilities. The process cannot be reversed because the information is physically destroyed and the possibilities are ontologically gone. **"Ongoing"** — the resolution keeps happening, each event inheriting results of prior resolutions and generating conditions for the next. **"Physically constrained"** — the ML bound constrains its rate, the Bekenstein bound its storage, Landauer's principle its cost. **"The indeterminate becomes determinate"** — the ontological heart, requiring the upper-floor commitment. **"From within"** — temporal experience is the resolution process from a particular reference frame within it.

The universe is not a place where things happen *in* time. The universe is a process — physically constrained, computationally irreducible, Bekenstein-bounded — of resolving possibilities into facts. Time is what that process is like from the inside. Not an illusion. Not a background stage. Not a brute fact. Time is the shape of becoming in a finite world.
