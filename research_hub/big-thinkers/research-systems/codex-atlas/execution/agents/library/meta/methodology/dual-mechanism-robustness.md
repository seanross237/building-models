## Lesson

When two independent physical mechanisms produce the same phenomenological result — reached by
different routes, using different assumptions, with different mathematical language — that is the
strongest form of robustness evidence for the result. A single derivation can have hidden
assumptions or narrow validity; convergence from two independent paths is much harder to dismiss.

This is especially valuable for results in regimes where one or both derivations have known
limitations, because the convergence suggests the result captures real physics rather than being
a derivation artifact.

## Evidence

- **classicality-budget strategy-001 exploration 007** — Two independent mechanisms both give
  R_δ ≈ 0 near black hole horizons:
  1. **Hawking radiation sparseness** (E005): Only ζ(3)/(24π⁴) ≈ 5×10⁻⁴ photons in the
     near-horizon sphere; S_Hawking = 1/(540 ln2) ≈ 0.003 bits; the environment is too sparse
     to create classical redundancy.
  2. **RT geometry** (E007): In AdS₃, a bulk point near the horizon requires a boundary interval
     of length ≥ 1 (= full boundary) for reconstruction; no room for independent copies;
     R → 0 geometrically as the horizon is approached.

  These mechanisms are completely independent — one is thermodynamic (photon density), the
  other is geometric (minimal RT surface size). Their convergence significantly strengthens the
  conclusion that near-horizon classical objectivity is genuinely suppressed, not an artifact
  of a particular derivation framework.

## Strategic Implication

When a strategizer observes an important result emerging from one mechanism, it's worth checking
whether an independent mechanism produces the same result. A dedicated comparison exploration
("does the X framework independently recover Y?") is high-value if the answer is yes.
