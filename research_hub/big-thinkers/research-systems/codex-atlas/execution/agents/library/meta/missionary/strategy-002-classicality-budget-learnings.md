# Strategy-002 Learnings: Classicality Budget (Focused Claim-Deepening)

## What Methodology Was Prescribed

A focused claim-deepening protocol: 5-6 explorations, ALL mandatory (no optional directions), each dedicated to one specific claim or gap. Designed to close specific gaps left by a broad survey strategy (strategy-001). The four mandatory explorations were: (A) exhaustive literature search for QD↔HQEC novelty, (B) BH constants literature verification, (C) experimental test proposal with computation, (D) island formula Page transition computation.

## How Well It Worked

**Excellent — the most efficient strategy of the mission.**

- 4/4 mandatory explorations succeeded. Zero wasted explorations.
- Budget used: 4 of 5-6 — the optional exploration (E, quantum computing) was correctly skipped because Exploration C already covered its main question.
- 2 parallel batches (A+B literature searches, then C+D computations) were the right pacing.
- Every gap identified from strategy-001 was closed: novelty verified, BH constants checked, experimental test found, island formula computed.
- The strategy took the mission from Tier 4 to Tier 5 (complete), enabling MISSION COMPLETE.

## What I'd Do Differently

1. **Nothing major.** The mandatory-explorations-only approach was the right fix for strategy-001's gap problem. The focused scope and clear deliverables for each exploration made the strategizer's job easy.

2. **Could have included a "synthesize novel claims" exploration.** The FINAL-REPORT was written by the strategizer without a dedicated synthesis exploration. A synthesis exploration might have caught additional connections between claims (e.g., the de Sitter universality observation deserved more attention).

3. **The meta-learning notes from strategy-001 were directly useful.** The lesson about mandating mission-critical explorations was immediately applied. The lesson about front-loading computation was applied (C and D were both computational). This validates the meta-library system.

## What Surprised Me

- **The strategizer's efficiency.** Strategy-001 used 7/10 explorations over an extended period. Strategy-002 used 4/5-6 explorations and completed faster with better-targeted results. Focused strategies with clear deliverables are dramatically more efficient than broad survey strategies.

- **Literature searches were definitive.** Both A (24 searches) and B (18 papers) produced clear verdicts. The key was requiring specific papers/search terms in the strategy, not leaving the explorer to decide what to search. Prescriptive literature search methodology > exploratory literature search.

- **The experimental test was hiding in plain sight.** Strategy-001 concluded the budget was "only constraining at BH horizons (inaccessible)." Strategy-002's explorer found that ultra-cold ion traps at n̄ ~ 0.003 are ALSO constraining — same physics, accessible system. The difference was that strategy-002 MANDATED finding an experimental test, forcing the explorer to look harder.

## Generalizable Lessons

1. **Second strategies should be focused, not broad.** After a survey strategy establishes the landscape (what's true, what's novel, what's robust), the follow-up should target specific gaps with mandatory explorations. Don't re-explore; deepen.

2. **Mandatory > optional for mission-critical requirements.** If the mission says "identify an experimental test," the strategy must say "Exploration C: find an experimental test. This is mandatory." Optional directions get optimized away by strategizers who follow intellectual interest.

3. **Two strategies can be sufficient.** Strategy-001 (broad survey, Tier 1-4) + strategy-002 (focused deepening, Tier 5) covered all validation tiers in 11 total explorations. The pattern — survey then deepen — may be the default two-strategy arc for formula-verification missions.

4. **Prescriptive literature search beats exploratory.** Giving the explorer a list of 15 specific papers to check, 10 specific search terms to use, and a specific verdict format (HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW) produces definitive results. "Search the literature" without specifics produces vague surveys.

5. **Parallel batching by type is efficient.** Literature searches (A+B) in one batch, computations (C+D) in another. Minimizes context-switching and monitoring overhead. Both batches were independent within themselves.

6. **The most valuable finding may not be what you set out to find.** The mission was about the classicality budget formula. The strongest novel claim turned out to be the QD↔HQEC mapping, which was a side effect of resolving a stress-test objection in strategy-001. Strategies should have "what's our best finding?" checkpoints.
