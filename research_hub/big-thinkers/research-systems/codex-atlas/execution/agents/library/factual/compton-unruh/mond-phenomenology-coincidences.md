## Finding

Milgrom's MOND modifies the force law at a₀ ≈ 1.2 × 10⁻¹⁰ m/s². The constant a₀ coincides (within factors of a few) with multiple cosmological acceleration scales, suggesting a deep connection to the Hubble horizon. MOND works brilliantly for galaxies but fails for galaxy clusters and CMB.

## MOND Formula

    F_N = m · μ(a/a₀) · a

where μ(x) is an interpolating function:
- μ(x) → 1 for x ≫ 1 (Newtonian regime)
- μ(x) → x for x ≪ 1 (deep-MOND)

**Interpolation functions:**
- Simple: μ(x) = x/(1+x) — preferred by galaxy data
- Standard: μ(x) = x/√(1+x²) — faster convergence to Newton

In the deep-MOND regime: v⁴ = GMa₀ — baryonic Tully-Fisher relation.

## Cosmological Coincidences

a₀ ≈ 1.2 × 10⁻¹⁰ m/s² lies near several cosmological acceleration scales:

| Scale | Value (m/s²) | Ratio to a₀ |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| cH₀ | 6.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ | 5.5 |
| cH₀/6 (Verlinde) | 1.1 × 10⁻¹⁰ | 0.92 |
| cH₀/(2π) | 1.05 × 10⁻¹⁰ | 0.875 |
| c²√Λ | 9.4 × 10⁻¹⁰ | 7.8 |

Milgrom (2020, arXiv:2001.09729) argues this coincidence demands a deeper theory connecting local dynamics to cosmology ("FUNDAMOND").

## External Field Effect (EFE)

Because Milgrom's law is nonlinear, subsystems cannot decouple from their gravitational environment. An external field (e.g., from a host galaxy) changes internal dynamics of a subsystem even if the external field is uniform — a unique MOND prediction with no Newtonian analogue. Observational evidence reported in 2020 using wide binary stars and satellite galaxies.

## Solar System Constraints

Both the "simple" (μ=x/(1+x)) and "standard" (μ=x/√(1+x²)) interpolation functions are **ruled out** by:
- Lunar laser ranging (anomalous quadrupole effect)
- Cassini radio tracking (Saturn's orbit measured to ~1 m)

Viable MOND functions must converge to Newton faster than both standard forms at high accelerations.
