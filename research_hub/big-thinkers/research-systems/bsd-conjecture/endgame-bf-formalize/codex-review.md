## Overview

The sketch aims to prove two nonstandard claims in sequence: first, that the newly defined BF correlators with insertions generate the higher Fitting ideals of the cyclotomic Selmer module `X`, and second, that each such correlator matches the corresponding Kurihara number `\delta_n`, from which the BSD consequences are extracted in Part (D) of the main theorem (findings.md, §1.3-§1.4, lines 92-154; Theorem BF-Kurihara Correspondence, lines 254-290). The document also states explicitly that both the correlator definition with insertions and the composite identification `BF correlator = Kurihara number` are new to this work, rather than quoted results from the literature (findings.md, lines 851-853).

My overall assessment is that the sketch is not yet a complete proof of `BF correlators = Kurihara numbers`. The core reason is not a minor normalization issue, but the absence of a rigorous comparison theorem between the new BF determinant ratio and the specific Kato/Mazur-Rubin Kolyvagin system used on the arithmetic side; Section IV identifies exactly that comparison as the hard step, Section 4.2 admits the naive determinant argument is “NOT quite right,” and the risk section still lists the same point as a live danger, even though the closing assessment says the theorem is already proved (findings.md, §4.1-§4.4, lines 389-478; lines 861-867; lines 709-710; lines 918-920).

## 1. Logical Gaps and Circular Reasoning

The first gap is the passage from the derived-category definition of the correlator to the determinant of a concrete localization map. In §1.3 the correlator is defined as a torsion ratio of Selmer complexes, and the exact triangle is written as `SC(T) -> SC(T,n) -> ⊕ H^1_s[-1]`, whereas in Part (A), Step 2, the triangle is written in the opposite direction as `SC(T,n) -> SC(T) -> ⊕ H^1_s[-1]`; that discrepancy matters because determinant functor formulas depend on the orientation of the triangle and on the induced long exact sequence on cohomology (findings.md, §1.3, lines 108-119; §III Part (A), lines 300-310). Section 4.2 then explicitly concedes that the naive multiplicativity argument only gives the torsion of the target term and “NOT quite” the localization map itself, after which the text simply reasserts that the torsion ratio “captures” the desired cokernel/kernel data without proving a theorem that identifies the two invariants (findings.md, §4.2, lines 403-444).

The second gap is a citation gap: Proposition 1, §2.2, and §4.3 all use Mazur-Rubin Theorem 5.2.12 as though it directly proved a `\Lambda`-adic statement of the form `F_k(X) = <loc^s(\kappa_n)>`, but that is not what Mazur-Rubin’s theorem says (findings.md, §1.4, lines 140-151; §2.2, lines 211-217; §4.3, lines 450-460). In Mazur-Rubin, *Kolyvagin Systems*, Mem. AMS 168 (799) (2004), Theorem 5.2.12 is the core-rank-one discrete-valuation-ring structure theorem for `ord(\kappa)` and the elementary divisors of the dual Selmer group, while the freeness/uniqueness-up-to-unit statement is Theorem 5.2.10; neither theorem, by itself, is the `\Lambda`-adic higher-Fitting statement claimed in the sketch. The document itself partly admits this later, when it says Theorem 5.2.12 only covers the core-rank range and that higher `k` needs the Burns-Sano refinement, so Part (A) as stated is stronger than the citation given for it (findings.md, lines 254-258; lines 867-867).

There is also a coefficient-level mismatch that the sketch never resolves. The observables `O_\ell` and the Kolyvagin classes `\kappa_n` are introduced at finite level with coefficients in `T/I_n T`, while the theorem to be proved is stated for the cyclotomic Iwasawa module `X` over `\Lambda`, and Part (A) quietly switches to `SC(T \otimes \Lambda)` only after the finite-level correlator has already been defined (findings.md, §1.2-§1.4, lines 65-154; Theorem BF-Kurihara Correspondence, lines 250-258; §III Part (A), lines 298-317). That switch would need either a genuine `\Lambda`-adic Kolyvagin-system argument, for example along the lines of Büyukboduk, “Lambda-adic Kolyvagin Systems,” *IMRN* 2011, or a careful finite-level-to-Iwasawa descent argument; neither is written out here. As written, the proof oscillates between finite-level Selmer groups and the `\Lambda`-module `X` without a theorem connecting the exact determinants that appear on the two sides (findings.md, §1.3-§1.4, lines 117-154; §III Part (A), lines 298-317).

The most serious conceptual gap is in the chain `det(loc^s(\kappa_n)) = exp_p^*(\kappa_n) = \delta_n`. The determinant in the first term concerns singular localization at the Kolyvagin primes `\ell | n`, whereas the dual exponential is a local map at `p`; the document simply labels this step “Dual exponential = singular localization,” but no argument is given because the two maps live in different local theories (findings.md, §2.1-§2.2, lines 182-206). Kato’s explicit reciprocity law, Astérisque 295 (2004), Theorem 12.5, is a statement about the `p`-adic image of Kato’s zeta element, and the more relevant refined derivative statement is Chan-Ho Kim’s May 12, 2025 preprint, Theorem 3.11, which identifies the `p`-local dual exponential of `\kappa_n` with a unit multiple of `\delta_n`; neither theorem identifies `\delta_n` with the determinant of `\prod_{\ell | n} loc_\ell^s`. So the composite equality in §2.2 is not a proved chain but a type-changing assertion (findings.md, §2.2, lines 201-217).

Finally, there is a genuine circularity between Proposition 1 and the later “resolution” of the hard step. Part (A) proves only an ideal-level statement generated by all correlators, but Part (B), Step 4 upgrades that to the statement that a specific correlator equals the specific localization of `\kappa_n`; that upgrade is exactly the gap acknowledged in the risk section, where the text says there is a jump from “some localization data” to “the specific localization data from Kato’s Kolyvagin system” (findings.md, §1.4, lines 128-154; §III Part (B), lines 340-346; lines 863-863). Worse, §4.2 says the image-size statement is “the content of Proposition 1,” so Proposition 1 is being used to justify the very comparison that Proposition 1 itself was supposed to prove (findings.md, §4.2, lines 441-444). That is not a gap of exposition; it is a dependency loop in the proof chain.

## 2. Mazur-Rubin Uniqueness as Alternative Path

Strictly speaking, the sketch is slightly off about which Mazur-Rubin theorem provides “uniqueness.” In the monograph *Kolyvagin Systems* the rank-one freeness and uniqueness-up-to-unit statement is Theorem 5.2.10, while Theorem 5.2.12 is the companion structure theorem describing `ord(\kappa)` and the elementary divisors of the dual Selmer group; the sketch repeatedly invokes Theorem 5.2.12 as if it simultaneously supplied uniqueness and direct higher-Fitting generators (findings.md, §1.4, lines 140-146; §2.2, lines 211-217; §4.3, lines 450-460; lines 863-863; Mazur-Rubin, Mem. AMS 168 (799), 2004, Thms. 5.2.10, 5.2.12). That matters because the cleaner alternative path is really a freeness/uniqueness argument, not an elementary-divisor argument.

With that correction made, a Mazur-Rubin-style uniqueness strategy would indeed be cleaner than the route taken in the sketch. If one could construct from the BF side a bona fide rank-one Kolyvagin system, or at least a distinguished primitive class in the Kolyvagin-system module whose local data recover the BF correlators, then freeness of that module would collapse most of Proposition 1, the ad hoc generator language in §2.2, and the comparison paragraph in §4.3: one would only need to compare the BF class to Kato’s class up to a unit, and then the published reciprocity theorems for Kato’s system would transport the identification to all levels (findings.md, §1.4, lines 128-154; §2.2, lines 199-217; §4.3-§4.4, lines 448-478; Mazur-Rubin, Mem. AMS 168 (799), 2004, Thm. 5.2.10; Büyukboduk, *IMRN* 2011).

But uniqueness does not solve the current hard part by itself, because the sketch never constructs a BF-side Kolyvagin system. The BF object in §1.3 is a determinant ratio of perfect complexes, not a section of the Mazur-Rubin Selmer sheaf satisfying the Kolyvagin recursion from §2.1, and converting the former into the latter is exactly the comparison theorem that is missing (findings.md, §1.3, lines 92-124; §2.1, lines 171-180; §4.1, lines 389-397). So the answer is: yes, a uniqueness route is cleaner, but only after a new BF-to-Kolyvagin-system construction is supplied; it cannot be used as a drop-in replacement for the unproved determinant comparison in the current sketch (findings.md, lines 861-863).

## 3. Hardest Remaining Step

The single hardest remaining step is the proof of a BF-to-Kolyvagin comparison theorem: starting from the new BF correlator with insertions, prove that the derived determinant ratio is represented by the same primitive Kolyvagin system that arises from Kato’s Euler system, rather than merely by some unspecified generator of the same ideal (findings.md, §1.3, lines 92-124; §4.1, lines 389-397; lines 851-853; lines 861-863). This is the true mathematical bottleneck because it simultaneously subsumes the unresolved H1 issue “torsion ratio = localization determinant” and the unresolved H2 issue “localization determinant = Kato’s `loc^s(\kappa_n)`” (findings.md, §4.1-§4.3, lines 395-462).

As of early 2025, I do not think this step is obtainable purely by citation to published results. The arithmetic side was already strong by then: Mazur-Rubin’s monograph was long available, Büyukboduk’s `\Lambda`-adic freeness result was available in 2011, Sakamoto’s *Documenta Mathematica* paper appeared in 2022, and Burns-Kurihara-Sano’s “On Derivatives of Kato’s Euler System and the Mazur-Tate Conjecture” was published on February 14, 2025; but the BF side on which the sketch depends is explicitly tied to Park-Park 2026, and the direct Kurihara-number structure theorem the sketch relies on is Kim’s preprint dated May 12, 2025, both outside the early-2025 published corpus (findings.md, lines 684-690; lines 851-853; lines 861-867; Burns-Kurihara-Sano, *IMRN* 2025(4); Kim, preprint dated May 12, 2025; Park-Park, arXiv:2602.19621).

My judgment call is therefore negative on the narrow question “provable with existing published technology as of early 2025?” The answer is not “no in principle,” because the necessary arithmetic tools were maturing quickly, but it is “no as a matter of the published record,” since the required BF comparison theorem is not present in the literature and the sketch’s own decisive BF and Kurihara inputs postdate the cutoff it asks us to enforce (findings.md, lines 684-690; lines 871-878). In other words, an expert might plausibly prove this step by new work using the 2025-era toolbox, but the step itself is still new mathematics, not a straightforward synthesis theorem.

## 4. Feasibility Assessment

The document’s estimate of “approximately one published paper of moderate difficulty” is, in my view, too optimistic (findings.md, lines 871-878). That estimate sits uneasily with the document’s own admissions that the correlator with insertions is new, the BF-to-localization identification is still a risk, the precise Kolyvagin system seen by BF is still a risk, and the theorem claims full higher-Fitting and BSD consequences; those are not just editorial loose ends (findings.md, lines 851-853; lines 861-867; Theorem BF-Kurihara Correspondence, lines 254-290).

My calibrated assessment, as a judgment call, is that this is a multi-paper program rather than a one-paper cleanup, if one insists on a genuinely rigorous proof from the published state of the subject through early 2025. One substantial paper would have to define BF correlators with insertions in a way that is intrinsic in Nekovar/Burns-Flach determinant formalism and prove the determinant/localization theorem; a second substantial paper, or a comparably large second half of the same project, would have to build the BF-side Kolyvagin-system comparison and connect it to the published reciprocity statements for Kato derivatives; only after that would the extraction of the clean p-adic BSD consequences look like a routine corollary package rather than part of the hard proof itself (findings.md, §4.1-§4.4, lines 389-478; lines 699-705; lines 871-878).

I would not call it a decade-of-work problem, because the underlying arithmetic engines are already powerful and, by February 2025 and after, the Kato-derivative/Kurihara literature was clearly advancing; but I also would not call it “essentially complete,” contrary to the last line of the sketch (findings.md, lines 904-915; lines 918-920). The best calibrated label is “multi-paper program over several years for experts in Iwasawa theory and Selmer complexes,” with the caveat that, if one allows the post-May 2025 and 2026 inputs the sketch itself uses, the program compresses noticeably, but still not to a single moderate synthesis paper (findings.md, lines 684-690; lines 709-710; lines 871-878).

## Summary Verdict

The proof sketch does not currently establish that BF correlators equal Kurihara numbers. The BF correlator with insertions is a new definition, the determinant-to-localization comparison is explicitly admitted to need correction, Mazur-Rubin 5.2.12 is used in a stronger form than it provides, and the crucial jump from an ideal generated by correlators to the specific Kato Kolyvagin system is both unproved and acknowledged by the sketch itself as a live risk (findings.md, §1.3-§1.4, lines 92-154; §2.2, lines 199-217; §4.1-§4.3, lines 389-462; lines 861-867).

The most promising repair is to recast the comparison through an actual BF-side Kolyvagin system and then use Mazur-Rubin/Büyukboduk freeness to compare that system with Kato’s up to a unit; that would genuinely collapse several ad hoc steps in the current draft. But until that theorem exists, the right feasibility label is “multi-paper program,” not “proved theorem” and not “one moderate paper,” especially if the benchmark is what was published by early 2025 rather than what became available later in 2025 and 2026 (findings.md, lines 863-878; lines 684-690; lines 918-920; Mazur-Rubin, Mem. AMS 168 (799), 2004; Büyukboduk, *IMRN* 2011).
