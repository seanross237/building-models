# The Problem of Time in Quantum Gravity

The problem of time originates in the fundamental conflict between the way "time" is used in quantum theory (as an external parameter against which states evolve) and the role it plays in general relativity (as a dynamical, diffeomorphism-invariant aspect of the geometry). This is not merely a technical obstacle but a deep conceptual challenge affecting all canonical approaches to quantum gravity.

## The Core Issue

In general relativity, the Hamiltonian is a constraint that must vanish:

H |psi> = 0 (Wheeler-DeWitt equation)

In ordinary quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian generates time evolution via the Schrodinger equation i*hbar * d|psi>/dt = H|psi>. When the Hamiltonian is constrained to annihilate physical states, there is no external time parameter and no evolution -- the "frozen formalism" problem.

## Facets of the Problem (Isham 1993, Kuchar 1992)

The problem manifests in multiple ways:
1. **The frozen formalism:** Physical states satisfying H|psi> = 0 appear static
2. **The Hilbert space problem:** What is the correct inner product for physical states?
3. **The functional evolution problem:** How to describe change without a background time
4. **The global time problem:** No single time function works globally on the full phase space
5. **The multiple choice problem:** Different choices of internal time lead to inequivalent quantum theories

## Classification of Resolution Strategies (Isham-Kuchar)

**Type I -- Time before quantization:**
Identify a "true" time variable at the classical level before quantizing. Includes matter time (using a scalar field as clock) and hidden/intrinsic time (extracting time from the geometry, e.g., York time).

**Type II -- Time after quantization:**
Quantize the full constrained system, then recover time semiclassically. The WKB/Born-Oppenheimer approach recovers a Schrodinger equation with emergent semiclassical time from the Wheeler-DeWitt equation.

**Type III -- Timeless approaches:**
(a) Conditional probabilities interpretation (Page-Wootters): define probabilities for correlations between observables without reference to external time.
(b) Evolving constants of motion (Rovelli): define "complete observables" as gauge-invariant quantities parameterized by partial observables.
(c) Records theories (Barbour): time is illusory; physics is about correlations in a "timeless" configuration space (Platonia).

## Cross-Cutting Significance

The problem of time affects every canonical approach to quantum gravity (LQG, Wheeler-DeWitt quantum cosmology, causal set theory). Covariant approaches (string theory, spin foams) partially circumvent it but must still address how time emerges from a fundamentally timeless formalism. The problem is intimately connected to the observables problem: in a diffeomorphism-invariant theory, all observables must be relational (non-local).
