## Lesson

When asking an explorer to assess predictions, compare frameworks, or evaluate severity, provide an explicit classification scheme (e.g., DISCRIMINATING/NOVEL/INHERITED, or FATAL/SERIOUS/MODERATE/COSMETIC). Without forced categorization, explorers produce lists that sound impressive but lack honest discrimination.

## Evidence

- **strategy-001 exploration 005** — Severity ranking (for devil's advocate) produced the clearest possible output for strategic decision-making.
- **strategy-004 exploration s4-002** — Classification scheme (DISCRIMINATING/NOVEL/etc.) revealed that only 1 of 12 BH predictions was genuinely discriminating. Without it, the list of 12 "predictions" would have looked impressive.
- **strategy-004 exploration s4-005** — Classification continued to be essential for honest assessment across three domains.
- **strategy-004 exploration s4-006** — Severity ratings (FATAL/SERIOUS/MODERATE/COSMETIC) per attack plus 5-tier validation produced clean, actionable devil's advocate results.

- **yang-mills strategy-001 exploration 001** — The status classification scheme (COMPLETED / PARTIALLY COMPLETED / NOT ATTEMPTED) produced a clear two-tier gap structure that stratified difficulties and made the precise stopping point immediately visible. Classification works not just for severity/quality but also for *progress stratification*.

## Variant: Multi-Dimensional Comparison Frameworks

A related pattern: when comparing two approaches or bodies of work, provide explicit comparison dimensions (e.g., "compare on: propagator, fixed points, RG flow, ghosts, spectral dimension, recent literature"). This structures the explorer's analysis and prevents selective emphasis. Force a definitive conclusion: "same, complementary, or distinct?"

- **yang-mills strategy-001 exploration 004** — 6-dimension comparison framework produced a clear classification table distinguishing numerical lattice evidence from rigorous mathematical proofs. The framework directed attention to the key settling paper and produced the most actionable output.

## Variant: Constraint Checklists

A related pattern: provide an explicit list of constraints to check proposals against (e.g., "check against: graviton propagator, Lorentz invariance, spectral dimension, BH entropy, ghost freedom"). This produces structured, point-by-point assessment that is immediately usable for strategic decisions.

- **quantum-gravity-2 strategy exploration 002** — Asking the explorer to check a framework against specific constraints produced structured, useful assessment. The constraint checklist forced systematic coverage rather than selective emphasis.

## Variant: Verification Scorecards

For computation-heavy explorations, ask for a verification scorecard that classifies every result by its epistemic status (e.g., VERIFIED / COMPUTED / CHECKED / CONJECTURED). This makes it immediately clear what to trust and what remains uncertain, preventing the reader from treating all results as equally reliable.

- **yang-mills strategy-001 exploration 003** — The 4-tier scorecard (VERIFIED: 4 results, COMPUTED: 12, CHECKED: 3, CONJECTURED: 2) made the epistemic status of every finding immediately visible. Without it, 21 numerical results would have been presented with uniform apparent reliability.
- **classicality-budget strategy-001 exploration 002** — Tags COMPUTED/ESTIMATED/CHECKED distinguished calculated values (entropy bounds, R_delta) from estimated inputs (S_T values for "a photograph" or "a letter") from cross-validated results (BH entropy vs. published Bekenstein-Hawking value). The ESTIMATED tag is a useful addition — it flags inputs that required judgment calls, distinguishing them from mechanically computed outputs.

## Variant: Structural vs. Technical Obstruction Classification

When asking about obstacles or barriers to extending results, request explicit classification of each obstacle as **structural** (fundamental incompatibility requiring new approach) vs. **technical** (amenable to refinement/harder work). This prevents conflation of genuinely different difficulty levels.

- **yang-mills strategy-001 exploration 006** — The distinction between structural and technical obstructions in the Adhikari-Cao finite→continuous analysis was critical: four specific obstacles were classified as structural (not technical), immediately ruling out incremental improvement strategies and clarifying that fundamentally new approaches are needed.

## Variant: Category Labels for Report Structure

For catalog/survey tasks, labeling the goal's categories with explicit letters (A, B, C, D) or named sections produces a report that mirrors those categories. The explorer uses the goal's structure as a scaffold for the output.

- **amplituhedron strategy-001 exploration 001** — Comprehensive constraint catalog goal with categories A (structural), B (recovery), C (precision bounds), D (rankings, implications) produced a well-organized, multi-section report matching those categories exactly. Adding explicit success criteria (20+ constraints, mathematical forms, ranking tables) gave the explorer concrete targets — it exceeded all of them.
- **stochastic-electrodynamics strategy-001 exploration 001** — Comprehensive structured goal with explicit categories and quantitative success criteria (target variances, distribution tests, parameter sweeps) produced a well-organized 7-section numerical report.
- **thermal-time strategy-001 exploration 001** — Goal with structured categories (Part 1: catalog, Part 2: TTH formalization, Part 3: discriminating observable, Part 4: computation) plus explicit success criteria (20+ constraints, mathematical forms, ranking tables) produced a well-organized 4-part report matching those categories exactly. The explorer exceeded the explicit success criteria. Third independent confirmation across a different domain and mission.

Note: this is about report *organization*, not severity ranking. The explorer uses goal categories as a table-of-contents template, so well-chosen categories produce a well-organized deliverable.

## Variant: Prior Art Comparison Tables

For novelty assessment explorations, request a "what's already been computed" table that maps each candidate paper against the specific formula or result being assessed. Columns: Paper | Contains the result? | Closest result | Gap from target. This format makes gaps immediately visible and produces a definitive novelty verdict.

- **classicality-budget strategy-001 exploration 003** — A 15-paper comparison table (each checked for R_delta <= S_max/S_T) immediately revealed that Tank (2025) had the structural form while all others lacked it, and that zero papers connected QD to Bekenstein bounds. The table format made the gap between two research communities visually obvious.

## Variant: Explicit Honesty Language in Stress-Tests

When asking for adversarial or weakness assessment, using the phrase **"be brutally honest"** (or equivalent explicit honesty language) in the goal produces measurably more honest assessment. Without it, explorers produce criticism that is formally structured but soft. With it, the explorer commits to genuine severity ratings even when the result is unflattering.

- **classicality-budget strategy-001 exploration 004** — The goal explicitly instructed "Be brutally honest — if it's not a new result, say so." The explorer's Objection 1 verdict was: "The formula IS essentially Bekenstein applied to the information requirements of QD, and a referee would be right to note this." This kind of explicit admission, while self-undermining, is the most useful output for strategic decision-making. The FATAL/SERIOUS/MODERATE/SUPERFICIAL scheme combined with honest-language instruction produced unusually candid assessment.

- **amplituhedron strategy-001 exploration 004** — "Be brutally honest" framing worked for a pure **literature/assessment task** (not just devil's advocate). The explorer explicitly labeled each claim as one of: REFORMULATION, ESTABLISHED, SUPPORTED, SPECULATIVE, or REFUTED. The resulting assessment table was the most strategically useful output of the exploration. Demonstrates that "brutally honest" framing is effective for claim-evaluation explorations, not just stress tests.

Note: honest language works best in combination with an explicit classification scheme (which forces structure) and quantification requirements (which prevent arguments from staying verbal — see goal-design/require-quantification-in-stress-tests.md).

## Variant: Confidence Level for Novelty Verdicts

For literature-search explorations, request an explicit HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW CONFIDENCE NOVELTY verdict. Without a forced confidence level, explorers hedge ("this appears to be novel, but..."). With an explicit three-level scale, the explorer commits to a classification, making the result actionable.

- **classicality-budget strategy-002 exploration 001** — The explicit HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW novelty scale forced a clear "HIGH CONFIDENCE NOVELTY" verdict on the QD↔HQEC connection, rather than vague hedging. The forced commitment enabled the strategizer to proceed with confidence to downstream explorations.

Pattern: "Rate the novelty as HIGH CONFIDENCE NOVEL, MEDIUM CONFIDENCE NOVEL, or LOW CONFIDENCE NOVEL, with explicit reasoning for the rating."

## Variant: Extension Map Tables

For literature surveys of "where does framework X extend to theory Y?" questions, use a 4-column table format in the goal: **Feature | Status | Key Reference | What Breaks**. The Status column (✅/⚠️/❌) provides immediately scannable discrimination; the Key Reference column forces citation; the "What Breaks" column forces the explorer to explain WHY failures occur rather than just noting them. This format produces a comprehensive, scannable extension map in one pass.

- **amplituhedron strategy-001 exploration 003** — A Feature | Status | Key Reference | What Breaks table for 20+ extension candidates produced a comprehensive overview as the opening section of the report. The status icons enabled immediate strategic assessment; the "What Breaks" column systematically revealed structural vs. technical obstacles across all theory classes. The explorer reproduced the table format from the goal and populated it accurately.

## Variant: Claim Evaluation Tables

For explorations that assess the validity of multiple specific claims (e.g., "what has this program actually proved?"), request a table with columns: **Claim | Evidence For | Evidence Against | Status**. The Status column uses an explicit vocabulary (e.g., ESTABLISHED / SUPPORTED / SPECULATIVE / REFUTED). This format:
- Forces the explorer to find evidence on both sides of each claim (not just confirming evidence)
- Requires an explicit verdict on each claim
- Makes the overall picture immediately scannable

- **amplituhedron strategy-001 exploration 004** — An assessment table for 12 claims about the amplituhedron program (e.g., "Does amplituhedron give new predictions for N=4 SYM?" → REFUTED; "Does EFT-hedron give real-world constraints?" → ESTABLISHED) was the most valuable structured output of the exploration. The "Evidence Against" column forced honest treatment of cases where the program is a reformulation, which the explorer was otherwise prone to soften.

Pattern: "For each claim, fill in: Evidence For | Evidence Against | Status (ESTABLISHED/SUPPORTED/SPECULATIVE/REFUTED)."

## Variant: Proof Review Verdicts (CONDITIONAL PASS)

For adversarial proof reviews, request a verdict classification that distinguishes between full pass, conditional pass, and fail. The "CONDITIONAL PASS" verdict — core proof correct but chain to full claim has gaps — was highly effective in yang-mills-conjecture E007: it prevented both rubber-stamping (the proof IS internally correct) and dismissal (the full claim IS not established). The verdict immediately communicates what work remains.

Suggested scale: **PASS** (proof complete and correct) / **CONDITIONAL PASS** (proof correct but with identified gaps or scope limitations) / **FAIL** (proof has errors). The CONDITIONAL PASS category is the most strategically useful — it tells you the proof is worth completing rather than abandoning.

- **yang-mills-conjecture exploration 007** — CONDITIONAL PASS: algebraic proof verified (13 VERIFIED, 5 COMPUTED), but two gaps identified (staggered-only bound, even-L limitation). Neither rubber-stamp nor dismissal; clear about what remains.

## When to apply

Any exploration that produces a list of items that need ranking: predictions, attack vectors, evidence assessments, framework comparisons, or **progress/status audits**. Also effective as **constraint checklists** when assessing proposals against known requirements, as **verification scorecards** for computation-heavy results, as **structural/technical obstruction classifiers** for difficulty assessment, as **prior art comparison tables** for novelty assessment, and as **extension map tables** for framework coverage surveys (Feature | Status | Key Reference | What Breaks). Especially critical for devil's advocate explorations and prediction extraction, where the tendency toward impressive-sounding lists is strongest. Also effective for gap analysis — asking for difficulty stratification (tractable vs. fundamental obstruction) produces cleaner strategic insight.
