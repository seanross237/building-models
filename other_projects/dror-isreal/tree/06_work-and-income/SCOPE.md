# Scope: 06 — Work and income

How an Israeli actually earns a living after the move. This branch is
about the work itself: permission to work, how employment is structured,
how freelancing or remote work is treated in practice, and how to enter
the local labor market.

There is deliberate overlap with `00_eligibility-and-right-to-enter`
because a visa is often the legal gateway to working. The distinction:
`00` covers the pathway to status; `06` covers the work reality once that
status exists or is being used. There is also overlap with
`02_money-and-tax`; `06` covers how income is earned, not how it is taxed.

## What belongs here

- **Work authorization in practice** — whether a given visa or residence
  status allows employment, self-employment, employer changes, part-time
  work, freelance invoicing, or only work for a sponsoring employer.
- **Remote work for Israeli employers** — being paid by an Israeli company
  while physically resident abroad, employer-of-record arrangements, local
  labor-law issues, permanent-establishment risk flags, and when "just keep
  your Israeli job" is not actually lawful or simple.
- **Freelance / self-employed status** — sole trader, freelancer, autónomo,
  Gewerbe, independent contractor, and other destination-specific ways of
  working for multiple clients.
- **Startup and entrepreneur work routes** — where the practical issue is
  not only visa eligibility but business-plan execution, incubator
  requirements, local hiring, and founder work rights.
- **Credential recognition for regulated professions** — medicine, law,
  engineering, architecture, teaching, social work, nursing, psychology,
  and other fields where an Israeli credential does not automatically
  transfer.
- **Professional licensing exams and adaptation periods** — language tests,
  supervised practice, bridging courses, document legalization, and waiting
  periods before full authorization.
- **CV / resume / cover-letter conventions** — what local employers expect,
  what photos or personal details are normal or forbidden, and how Israeli
  experience translates on paper.
- **Job search channels and hiring culture** — LinkedIn vs local boards,
  recruiters, networking norms, salary negotiation, probation periods,
  references, and response-time expectations.
- **Labor-market realities by destination** — high-demand sectors,
  language barriers, public-sector restrictions, and the difference between
  "legal to work" and "realistically employable."

## What does NOT belong here

- The visa pathway itself as a legal eligibility question
  → `00_eligibility-and-right-to-enter`
- Detailed tax treatment of salary, freelance income, stock options, or
  employer benefits → `02_money-and-tax`
- Opening a bank account or getting a tax number on arrival
  → `04_destination-landing`
- Schooling, childcare, or dependent logistics tied to accepting a job
  → `07_family-and-education`
- Emotional adaptation to career loss or status loss
  → `10_emotional-and-identity`

## Language note

Destination-local language sources are often decisive here because
professional regulators, labor ministries, licensing boards, and employer
guides frequently publish only locally. Hebrew matters for the Israeli side
of remote-employment relationships and for understanding how Israeli
credentials are described in source documents.
