# Scope: 10 — Emotional and identity

The psychological, cultural, and identity dimensions of yerida. This
branch covers the human experience of leaving Israel: what it feels like,
how people explain it to themselves and to others, and what support
frameworks help them carry the decision without collapsing under it.

This branch is intentionally different from most of the tree. Much of its
value comes from lived experience, reflective writing, support resources,
and recurring patterns reported by people who have done this. It should be
rigorous, but not pretend that every useful insight looks like a statute.

## What belongs here

- **What to tell family and friends** — scripts, framing options, common
  reactions, stigma management, and the difference between logistical
  explanation and emotional disclosure.
- **The stigma of yerida** — social shame, political judgment, community
  pressure, and how those vary by age, ideology, family background, and
  whether the move is framed as temporary or permanent.
- **Wartime and crisis weight** — guilt about leaving during conflict,
  solidarity questions, fear for those staying behind, and the moral
  language people use when they justify or condemn departure.
- **Dual identity and belonging** — being Israeli abroad, staying Jewish
  or becoming less Jewish, raising children with mixed identity, and the
  push-pull between integration and maintaining Israeli selfhood.
- **Reverse culture shock and return ambivalence** — what happens when a
  person visits Israel, considers returning, or realizes they no longer
  fit cleanly in either place.
- **The first six months** — loneliness, relief, grief, bureaucracy
  fatigue, idealization crashes, language exhaustion, and the specific arc
  many migrants report in the first season abroad.
- **Mental-health resources** — therapists, support groups, expat-focused
  communities, hotlines, peer circles, journaling prompts, and other
  scaffolds that help someone not white-knuckle the whole transition.
- **Frameworks rather than verdicts** — how to think about leaving,
  staying connected, setting checkpoints, and deciding whether the move is
  experimental, permanent, or reversible.

## What does NOT belong here

- Concrete visa, tax, banking, or legal rules
  → `00_eligibility-and-right-to-enter`, `01_exiting-israel`,
  `02_money-and-tax`
- Purely practical community-finding resources
  → `08_community-and-language`
- Detailed family-law or school-enrollment procedures
  → `07_family-and-education`

## Language note

Hebrew is especially important here because much of the relevant discourse
among Israelis happens in Hebrew: essays, interviews, support-group posts,
podcasts, journalism, and therapist commentary. English may surface expat
psychology frameworks. Destination-local sources matter when the branch is
cataloging local support resources or culturally specific integration
programs.

## Trust note

Leaves in this branch may rely more heavily on tier-4 and tier-5 sources
than other branches because lived experience and community writing are part
of the point. That does NOT relax the rule for concrete factual claims:
when a leaf says a service exists, a hotline operates, or a therapist
directory has a policy, it should still use tier-1 or tier-2 sources when
available. Experiential claims should be labeled as experiential, not smuggled
in as hard fact.
